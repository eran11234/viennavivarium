# Inheritance Experiments on Asymmetric Eye-Colouration in Angora Cats.

By

Hans Przibram.

*(From the Biologische Versuchsanstalt in Vienna.)*

Received on 31 August 1907.

*Archiv für Entwicklungsmechanik der Organismen*, vol. 25 (1907).

> **Full translation.** A complete English rendering of Przibram's short inheritance study of asymmetric eye-colouration in Angora cats.

Most papers on animals of bilateral build show the homologous organs of the two sides agreeing, in all characters, in a symmetrical correlation of high degree, so that when one side deviates from the average normal size or normal colour, the other side too exhibits the analogous deviations.

An exception to this is formed by individual, strongly differentiated animal groups in which the limbs of one and the same pair are differentiated for different functions — as, for example, the chelae [Scheren] in a number of decapod Crustacea. Here it happens that the one chela-form is not bound to a definite body-side, so that two cases of asymmetry of opposite kind occur. About the mode of inheritance of this asymmetry, unfortunately, little is known, since the rearing of Crustacea meets with great difficulties. It is, however, clear that the correlation between the right and the left side need not be any less than in symmetrical forms, and that in inheritance the two chelae act as an undivided unit; for otherwise forms with two identical chelae of the one or the other type would make up about one half of all specimens, whereas in reality they are extraordinarily rare and can be traced back to regenerations. By means of regeneration experiments, the interchange of the chelae in one and the same individual has indeed been brought about.

Asymmetries which allow no definite correlation between the two body-sides to be recognized occur in the colouration of the domestic animals, and occasionally also of man. The question now thrust itself upon me whether here the asymmetry is inherited as a whole, or whether each side is inherited for itself. As object of investigation I chose white Angora cats, which fairly often exhibit one blue and one yellow eye.

If the asymmetry were inherited as a whole, then upon crossing an "asymmetrical" cat with a "symmetrical" one — that is, one with two blue or two yellow eyes — there could arise only cats of the same asymmetry and of the same symmetry as their parents and grandparents exhibit. If, on the other hand, in the asymmetrical characters each side can be inherited separately, then in crossings all possible cases may appear, in case the character of one side is able to determine both sides of the offspring, or in case two mutually opposite cases of asymmetry are used for crossing.

### Experiment I. Crossing yellow-blue × yellow-yellow.

Tom (1), yellow-blue (the first-named colour denotes the right eye), out of just such a father, crossed with the own yellow-yellow mother (4); Tom 1 had three siblings, all yellow-yellow (one ♂ and two ♀), in the same litter.

In the year 1903 a litter 1. ♂ × 4. ♀ had occurred, which falls before the beginning of the experiments and therefore offers no complete certainty. There were 1 ♂ blue-blue, 1 ♂ yellow-yellow, 2 ♀ yellow-yellow, of which one on both sides had a tinge toward greenish.

In the year 1904 a litter occurred on 13 May, consisting of 2 ♂ and 1 ♀. All three young exhibited, as soon as they opened their eyes at all, blue-blue. However, as later turned out, the initial colour is not always the definitive one — a phenomenon which is indeed very often observed in human children too. Later the eye-pair of the one ♂ gradually changed to yellow-yellow, in that on 31 July yellow stars appeared, and by 30 September the yellow-colouration had become complete. The ♀ had unfortunately died on 17 July, so that it is doubtful what colour it would definitively have retained.

The result of the crossing yellow-blue × yellow-yellow was thus yellow-blue (1. ♂ himself!), yellow-yellow, and blue-blue; only blue-yellow was lacking out of the four combinations.

### Experiment II. Crossing yellow-blue × blue-yellow.

Tom 1 yellow-blue was paired in 1905 with Cat 3 blue-yellow, who stems from the same mother and from a blue-blue father.

Only two young came into the world on 24 May, 1 ♂ and 1 ♀, both blue-blue. But in the ♂ the right eye soon became darker, on 12 July greenish with a yellow star. On 23 May 06 it was wholly greenish-yellow and is even now (June 1907) still coloured the same. The ♀ had already died on 20 June; but since it showed no indication whatever of any change, it may be reckoned as blue-blue, and would thus exhibit a combination that had been possessed by neither of the parents.

### Experiment III. Crossing blue-blue × blue-yellow.

Tom 2, allegedly a pure-bred blue-blue, paired with Cat 3, the same that later served for Experiment II.

A litter on 1 July 1904: five young, which all were initially blue-blue, but on 30 September each yielded one of the four possible combinations: a) blue with yellow star–blue, b) blue with y. st.–blue with y. st., c) blue-blue, d) blue-blue w. y. st.; a kitten (e) had died on 7 September. Unfortunately the young Angora cats showed altogether a great frailty; on 14 October and on 20 October two further deaths followed (a and c). At the latter date the eyes of b were almost entirely yellowish-brown, likewise the left one of d. On 30 November d died typically blue-yellow, on 5 December b too typically yellow-yellow.

The great frailty and slight fertility of my Angora cats make them appear not a suitable material for the planned experiments. The cats 6 and 7 and the tom 5, which were to be used for a pure blue-blue stock, yielded no offspring at all, perhaps because of in-breeding driven too far.

If I nevertheless hold the sparse results worth communicating, this happens with regard to the fact that the asymmetry does not actually seem always to have been inherited as a whole, but rather that, notably in Experiment III, all possible combinations came about, although the parents together disposed of only a single yellow eye, and among the ancestors, so far as known, only blue-yellow and blue-blue were likewise found. Admittedly the objection cannot be refuted that the various combinations correspond to earlier ancestors than the known ones, and that therefore the asymmetry may yet have been inherited as a whole. A continuation of the experiments with ordinary house cats might perhaps promise more success.

It is of interest that, so far as a testing of the hearing could be undertaken, the blue-blue adult cats were completely deaf, which indeed agrees with earlier observations by Darwin and Rawitz; the asymmetrical eye-colours seem, quite correspondingly, to be afflicted with a one-sided deafness affecting the side of the blue eye. The correlation between blue eye and deafness thus persists also in asymmetrical inheritance.

The blue-eyedness is not to be traced to an absence of the pigment in the eye; the pigment-formation can be demonstrated both by preparation and by the Fürth tyrosine reaction.

About the first origin of the asymmetry I have been unable to learn anything, neither in cats, nor in dogs — where the asymmetrical eye-colouration appears almost regularly in correlation with a definite kind of piebaldness in the colours grey, black, white and red-brown, often further complicated by a coloured division of one and the same eye. Always the asymmetrical animals could be traced back to a likewise asymmetrical parent.

On the other hand, the following case in man — which I owe to the communication of one scientifically active member of the family — offers a welcome supplement to the animal experiments. In this family the father possesses one light-grey and one dark-brown eye; his two parents had symmetrical, light eyes — namely the mother blue, the father probably light-grey — and a brother and a sister two light-grey eyes.

The mother of the family and her siblings have grey eyes (they can no longer remember the exact eye-colour of their parents).

Among the children all possess symmetrical eye-colouration: three grey, one light-greenish-grey, two dark-brown eyes.

There results the pedigree:

| ♂ light-light | ♀ blue-blue | ♂ ? | ♀ ? |
|---|---|---|---|
| ♂ light-light, ♀ light-light, ♂ light-**brown** | | ♀ grey, etc. | |
| ♂ **brown-brown**, ♂ grey-grey, ♀ grey-grey, ♂ **brown-brown**, ♀ light-light, ♀ grey-grey. | | | |

*(Note: the entries are printed in German in the original — hell-hell = light-light, blau-blau = blue-blue, licht-licht = light-light, licht-braun = light-brown, grau = grey, braun-braun = brown-brown, grau-grau = grey-grey; "u. s. f." = und so fort = etc. The brown-brown and certain entries are set in bold in the original.)*

Here, then, the asymmetry seems first to have appeared in the parent-generation, and there can also have been no brown eyes among the grandparent-eyes, since all the siblings of the mother have grey eyes — whence their parents too may have had grey ones, because brown rarely tends to vanish completely. In the child-generation the asymmetry has disappeared again, but two symmetrical-brown appear, alongside one symmetrical light and three symmetrical grey. The latter are mother's-heirs; the others, at least the brown-brown, are however probably to be regarded as heirs of the paternal halves; this paternal half is then to be referred either to a likewise [asymmetrical] or to a brown-brown ancestor.

As overall result of the investigation it may be said:

1) Under hitherto unknown circumstances, the correlation between the colouration of the two eyes of an individual can be lost.

2) This asymmetry can appear not only as a whole, unchanged, again in the offspring (and indeed through several generations, cf. Experiment II), but also in its reversal (Experiment III), and besides this each eye-colour of the asymmetrical parents can recur alone in both eyes of the symmetrical offspring (Experiments I, II, III, and Man).

3) Thereby the correlation between "blue eyes" of the cats and "deafness" persists also for the body-half.

4) As definitively blue-eyed, cats can be recognized only in the half-grown state, since the eyes of most (all?) young kittens are at first blue.
