# The Distribution of Form-Building Capacities in the Animal Body in the Dorso-Ventral Direction

By

Hans Przibram.

(Biologische Versuchsanstalt in Wien [Biological Experimental Institute in Vienna].)

With 4 figures in the text.

Received on 1 February 1910.

*Archiv für Entwicklungsmechanik der Organismen*, vol. 30 Roux 60 (1910).

When, in composing the first two volumes of my Experimental Zoology (Embryogenesis 1907, Regeneration 1909), the opportunity presented itself to me to survey the whole of the available experimental material on the developmental mechanics of primary and secondary regulations in animals, I was particularly struck by the role that the body axes played in the distribution of organ-forming anlagen with respect to their prospective significance and prospective potency.

Since I deal with the role of the oral-aboral principal axis elsewhere, and wish to reserve the role of the lateral axis for a later discussion, the present communication encompasses only the role of the dorso-ventral axis.

I. If one designates the nuclear pole of the egg as the animal pole, the opposite one as the vegetative pole, and the connecting line as the egg-axis, then it may be said: if in an egg the first furrow runs perpendicular to the egg-axis (Fig. 1*a*), then the first two blastomeres, after their isolation, are not capable of mutually replacing one another at full value. It does not matter here whether this equatorial course of the first furrow is the generally valid one for the egg in question (*Ascaris*), or is one of several predilection directions (*Triton*, *Aegineta*), or finally occurs only exceptionally (*Echinus*).

If we follow what becomes of the animal and vegetative half of the egg, we find that the prospective significance of the former lies preferentially in the formation of dorsal parts, that of the latter in the formation of ventral parts (Fig. 1*d*). The potency of the eggs is therefore restricted in the direction of the later dorso-ventral axis in such a way that the prospective potencies are scarcely greater than the prospective significance of the relevant anlagen.

**Fig. 1.**  *(figure not reproduced)*

If the first furrow does not run perpendicular to the egg-axis, but rather through it, then, on the contrary, upon isolation of the first blastomeres, both the dorsal and the ventral anlage will be distributed onto both [blastomeres]. Correspondingly we see that, when the first furrow runs through the later median plane (Fig. 1*c*, plane of the paper; *Rana*, *Triton*, *Cerebratulus*) or in the transverse direction (*Rana*, *Echinus*, Fig. 1*b, e, f*), there indeed at first often arise half, but regulation-capable and regeneration-capable embryos, so that both blastomeres prove to be totipotent. If, through the first, medially running furrow, a left and right body-anlage have been separated, then it now turns out that these — in bilaterally symmetrical forms — are able to replace one another at full value. Likewise, in the second case, anterior and posterior body-anlage are able to replace one another mutually.

If first blastomeres that have been driven apart remain in connection, or even later coalesce again, then twins arise which are assigned to one another in mirror-image fashion (*Echinus* — Driesch 1906, 1908 [Fig. 1*h, k, l*]; *Triton*?). What remains unchanged is the morphological place at which the dorsal anlage, and that at which the ventral anlage, comes to development; yet geometrical rotations can bring it about that the two oral surfaces (Fig. 1*i*) or the two aboral surfaces become turned toward one another. In any case the dorsal parts pass over into the dorsal, the ventral into the ventral parts of the second partner; whereas left and right side stand mutually opposed to one another. Since the sides, in relation to one another — as we saw above — are totipotent, I am unable to see in the reversal of the bilaterality the primary phenomenon; rather I interpret the inversion of the mouth-pole in a twin as the consequence of the rotation of the dorsal and ventral anlagen, which follows from the mirror-image-different stressing in the physical respect and brings with it the arrangement of the bilateral symmetry.

Also the possibility, by means of chemism, of producing radiate forms (*Echinus* — Herbst 1904) in which the lateral organs can indeed be formed, but in greater than normal number, speaks for the fact that here it is a matter of a disturbance of the dorso-ventrality, which exerts its directing influence here. The subsequent regulation to a more or less bilateral arrangement after only brief action agrees with this, whereas this re-ordering of the already completed radiate distribution on the part of the lateral anlagen would appear difficult to understand.

II. As regards the regeneration of already developed animals, no case is known to me in which ventral and dorsal sides could fully replace one another. Most of the experiments of interest to us here were indeed undertaken for quite another purpose, namely for the testing of the influence of the nervous system on regeneration. But it is precisely the most recent exact experiments of Goldfarb (1909) that have brought me the confirmation of the view already advocated by me in the "Regeneration,"

that from the experiments one is **not** to conclude a dependence of regeneration on the nervous system. Let us consider the older experiments of Morgan and the newer ones of Goldfarb on one and the same animal. Morgan cut ventral longitudinal pieces, which contained the nerve-cord, from several segments and then saw the regeneration of the head-end fail to occur, except at the segment-end of the nerve-cord that had been left in place, from which a new head formed. Goldfarb tore the nerve-cord out from several anterior segments and now obtained, in about half of the cases, heads at the anterior end whose ganglion could form far separated from the segment-end of the nerve-cord that had been left in place. If the latter phenomenon proves the independence of the head-building potencies from the nervous system, then it seems to me that the experiments of Morgan now find their explanation in this, that the ventral parts cannot be replaced by the dorsal; thus here too the prospective potency of the dorsal side in respect of the dorso-ventrality will not be greater than its prospective significance. Similarly, but reversed, it stands with the position of the organ-systems in vertebrates, where, for instance, the removed dorsal chorda or the skeleton cannot be produced by the ventral part left in place, as is shown among other things by the experiments of Tornier and Goldfarb on amphibian tails.

Just as little, in my own experiments on *Antedon*, can the destruction of the dorsal nerve-center be held responsible for the failure of the regeneration, since isolated pieces without connection with this central capsule unfolded regenerative potencies, indeed without a part of the central capsule or of the central nervous system being newly formed. We can conclude from this that the dorsal part of the animal, containing the central capsule, can no longer be replaced by the more ventral parts. In and of themselves the more ventral parts are not incapable of regeneration, since mouth and anus can be replaced very well from the ventral disc of the animal; likewise the dorsal parts unfold regenerative potencies very fully, in that the most dorsal cirri can be replaced by them.

An apparent contradiction against the difference of the potential predisposition of dorsal and ventral half meets us in the regeneration experiments on certain worms inhabiting tubes. Thus the sabellid *Potomilla* (Fig. 2*a*) consists, according to Watson's description, of the head-segment bearing the tentacle-crown, then of 8 to 9 anterior segments, in which bristles stand dorsally, hooks ventrally, further of a larger number of posterior segments with exactly reversed arrangement of bristles and hooks. If a worm robbed of the anterior segments is cut into several pieces, then all the transverse pieces transform into whole worms in such a way that from each one merely a new anterior segment together with tentacle-crown is formed, but behind it the arrangement of the hooks and bristles on 5 to 9 segments is reversed. Similarly behaves *Sabella pavonina*. According to this account, in place of ventral hooks the bristles belonging to the dorsal side would sprout, and reversed. If, however, one considers the structure of a sa-

bellid more exactly, one sees in the side view (Fig. 2*b*), just as in the ventral view (Fig. 2*c*), that the bristles on all segments run along the middle of the flank in a line that is only slightly curved and uninterrupted, and that they are separated from both the ventral shields and also from the middle of the back by a considerable interspace. From these interspaces the ventral hook-sacs are filled at the anterior segments, the dorsal ones at the posterior segments, and at these places it appears widened. According to the concordant statements of Vaney and Conte (1899), Orlandi (1906) and Ivanov (1908), who have made experiments on *Spirographis*, the transformation of the abdominal segments into anterior ones proceeds in such a way that the hook-sacs disappear from the dorsal interspace and instead such [hook-sacs] form in the ventral one. The bristles do molt indeed, but do not appear at a foreign place; at most they are thereby somewhat displaced, in that the hook-sacs seek to widen the relevant interspace. One must therefore not speak of a reversal of the dorso-ventrality, but merely of a somewhat enlarged prospective potency of the abdominal flank-parts, which permits these to form hook-sacs not only dorsal but also ventral to the bristle-line.

III. Teratology, which after all offers so great a selection of peculiarly placed parts in animals, likewise seems to know no certainly attested cases in which a ventral organ would come to stand on the dorsal surface or reversed. Just as little do, in multiple formations, dorsal parts pass over into ventral and vice versa. Let us consider from this standpoint

**Fig. 3.**  *(figure not reproduced)*

Bateson's Material for the Study of Variation and Schwalbe's Morphology of the Malformations of Man and Animals. Bateson (p. 187) cites indeed two cases of supernumerary mammae on the back of man, but holds both (Paullinus 1686, Helbig 1693) to be fables. All other cases of supernumerary mammae observed in more recent times belong to the ventral half of the body. Very instructive are the cases, which I have throughout regarded as fracture-triple-formations, of supernumerary limb-pairs with secondary symmetry, as well as those to be characterized as cleft-formations. Among the latter I could find no cases in which the cleavage had arisen through horizontal separation of the dorsal from the ventral surface. Were such cases to lead to regeneration, then the hypergenerates would have to lie one above the other, as is always the case (cf. e.g. the duplication of the right foreleg in a click-beetle, *Agriotes obscurus* — Asmuss 1835, Fig. 3*a—c*). In a single case, in which it really seems to be a matter of horizontal cleavage, namely of the left hindleg-thigh of the carabid beetle *Carabus nemoralis*, there is no regeneration in the dorso-ventral direction to be observed, either on the dorsally split-off piece or on the ventral piece that has remained in connection with the tibia (Hermann 1877, Fig. 3*d*). On this occasion attention may be drawn to the occurrence of otherwise laterally placed appendages on the malformation of the dorsal surface, which points to the capacity of the dorsal body-part to produce, at any place, the dorsal parts of the lateral appendages. Thus there is found, in a dogfish investigated by Grosser and me

**Fig. 4.**  *(figure not reproduced)*

1906 (Fig. 4*a*), a paired symmetrical breast-fin-pair attached to the posterior margin of the parietal pit, horizontally outspread and running parallel with the back toward the rear. Here indeed the skeletal pieces belonging to the dorsal body-half are developed, but not the muscle-portions, which should connect ventralward. Perhaps the duck depicted by Geoffroy St. Hilaire (1837) also belongs here (Fig. 4*b*), with a leg-rudiment springing from the occiput, and a cow (Fig. 4*c*), from whose withers a fused double-leg hangs down in a flabby skin-sac. Not into this category of malformations belong the fragments, occurring ventrally, of a second embryo, which give themselves to be recognized as parasitic twins by the absence of any connection with the skeleton of the host and the presence of trunk-remnants between the legs.

In a fly, *Palloptera ustulata* (Gercke 1886, Fig. 4*d*), there was found in the middle-line of the thorax an upright-standing wing-like scale; it possesses the encircling marginal swelling of the normal wing, but lacks the tracheal opening lying ventral to the normal wing-axis, and with the tracheae also the normal wing-venation.

A reversal of the dorso-ventrality in its position, on the other hand, is shown by additional formations actually appearing at quite foreign body-places; in my view it is here a matter of parts displaced during development (cf. "Homoeosis," this Archiv 1910, there also on the alleged replacement of insect-wings by legs and reversed).

IV. That the secondary symmetry appearing in transplanted extremities, too, is to be ascribed to the dorso-ventral structure in connection with the centrifugal growth-direction of cells freed from tissue-pressure, I have already indicated in the mentioned work on *Acanthias vulgaris*. A work by Bresca soon to appear in this Archiv contains a further indication that in transplantation it depends on whether the transplant used possesses a definite specialization in the dorso-ventral direction. It showed itself, namely, that the dorsal stripe of newts (*Triton*), even of female sex, was able in transplantation to bring forth the male crest, while other skin-stripes proved incapable thereto.

### Summary.

1) In embryogenesis and regeneration of animals, ventral and dorsal body-parts always proceed from separate anlagen.

2) Ventral and dorsal anlagen are not able to replace one another mutually and at full value, neither in the eggs nor in developed animals.

3) The prospective potency of the ventral and dorsal anlage, each grasped as a unity, is therefore, with respect to the dorso-ventral axis, scarcely greater than its prospective significance.
