## I. Introduction.

The summer of the year 1905 could be used for the setting up of a series of experiments at the Zoological Station in Roscoff (Finistère, France). It is a pleasant duty for me to express the due thanks to Professor Yves Delage for the kind provision of a working place at this station, and to the gentlemen Professor Hérouard and Dr. Hérubel for support in my work. Since my stay at the named station passed all too quickly, even before the experiments could be brought to a conclusion, I decided to take the still-surviving experimental animals along with me to the Biological Experimental Institute in Vienna, which also succeeded with very slight losses. The animals used for the experiments — hermit crabs and crabs — then in part held out for a still longer time, so that distinct results could be achieved. For supplementary experiments, which during this time had proved necessary, new experimental animals were also obtained from the k. k. Zoological Station at Trieste, whose flawless arrival is to be thanked to the accustomed circumspection of the Director, Herr Professor Cori.

The experiments were, in the main, concerned with the question of the regeneration of the unequal chelae. In the meantime such a large experimental material has accumulated that I have preferred to publish two preliminary communications about this, one of which was presented before the Morphological-Physiological Society in Vienna on 21 November 1905, the other before the Stuttgart Assembly of Naturalists on 19 September 1906. While the working-up of the quantitative data obtained for the "heterochely" and regeneration of the chelae will still claim a longer time, I here present the detailed account of a phenomenon which had struck me in the course of the regeneration experiments on hermit crabs and seemed worth being made the starting point of a separate investigation. Namely, hermit crabs had been removed from the snail-shells into which they are accustomed to hide for the protection of their soft hind-body, and were kept without the possibility of obtaining a new shell. The intention was the convenient handling during the operations on the unequal chelae and the re-measuring at the moltings, for the purpose of determining the growth of the regenerates and of the other parts of the animal. It then struck me, upon undertaking the re-measurement about a month after the start of the experiment, that the habitus of the hermit crabs had changed, and it turned out that changes had taken place in the hind-body which had taken much of their characteristic appearance away from the hermit crabs. Since in all species, upon the same intervention, similar effects set in, they will be treated jointly.

## II. Material.

Most species of the family of the hermit crabs or **Paguridae** possess a hind-body that, toward the right, exhibits a twisting and asymmetrical development of the appendages. Partly only appendages on the left are developed at all on the abdominal segments; partly the existing ones are more strongly developed on the left (namely on the penultimate segment). The differing development of the leg-pairs of the first two abdominal segments in the two sexes points to a connection of this formation with the copulatory function, yet no observation about this seems to exist. The chelae too, and the leg-pairs following upon the chelae, mostly exhibit asymmetries, which however do not always stand in a definite way in correlation with the abdominal asymmetry.

Perhaps even more than the asymmetry, however, the general habitus of the hind-body strikes one in the hermit crab freshly removed from its adopted shell (Fig. 1, 2, 5, 8), and the same sets itself off from the more or less resistant and pigmented anterior body-section as a swollen, paler sac; the individual segments are scarcely separated from one another by furrows, often a segmentation cannot be perceived at all without examination under a magnifying lens. From within, the asymmetrically displaced viscera shimmer through the thin, taut-stretched skin.

For the experiments, four species belonging to the right-handed genus *Eupagurus* Brandt = *Bernhardus* Dana could be successfully used: *Eu. Bernhardus* L. (Roscoff), *Eu. Prideauxii* Leach (Roscoff and Trieste), *Eu. cuanensis* Thomps. ?¹⁾ (Roscoff), *Eu. pilosimanus* ?²⁾ (Roscoff) in various sizes, and the left-handed *Diogenes varians* Costa (Trieste); *Eupagurus sculptimanus* Lucas and *Paguristes maculatus* Risso could not be kept alive for sufficient time. Among these species, the hind-body stands out most strongly from the rest of the body in *Eu. Prideauxii*, least in *Diogenes varians*.

## III. Methodology.

### a. Unshelling.

It is in most cases not so simple as one might at first think to rob the hermit crab of its shell. The easiest to "dislodge" are those examples for which the shell has already become too small and which therefore stick in it with only a small part of the hind-body. Such examples also leave their shell spontaneously, in order to go in search of a larger one. If the animal does not willingly follow the first pull, one must be warned against an unshelling without further preparation, since upon further exertion of force the hind-body is apt to tear off, leaving behind the calcified end-piece, and the animal thus mutilated perishes after a few days. Also, in the releasing one must not pull at one of the cheliped-legs, since these break off very easily through autotomy, and it is then (even if one does not insist on uninjured animals) very difficult to obtain sufficient holding-points for the fingers. One therefore does well to grasp the two cheliped-feet and, if possible, also a part of the cephalothorax, and, under gentle compression, to pull in an oblique direction which forms the continuation of the snail-coiling. From pulling against the snail-coiling one is also to be advised, because the edge of the snail-mouth easily cuts into the soft hind-body, whereupon the viscera bulge out and a further operating becomes useless.

To move stubborn occupants to leave the shell, four different factors have proved effective, which are still capable of manifold modification.

1) **Gravity.** Some hermit crabs do not tolerate it if one holds the shell with the mouth so obliquely upward (outside of the water) that the crabs come to lie on their backs. They then strive to bring themselves around over the rim of the mouth into an upright position, and in doing so they can be taken by surprise and hauled entirely forth. This method has the advantage of needing no preparations whatever, but it is not applicable with every shell-form and, above all, not with very small examples.

2) **Mechanical means: Cutting-out.** Quite laborious is the cutting-open of a snail-shell so as to keep the hermit crab uninjured. One can either begin at the mouth and break off piece by piece with a forceps; the hermit crab finally withdraws onto an unbelievably small little piece of the last coil, yet then, when one sets it back into the water, leaves the useless shell-remnant. I thought that it would be simpler to cut off transversely, with a strong shears, the tip of the tower-snail-shells [turreted shells], which are often used by hermit crabs, and, by insertion of a needle into the resulting little hole, which leads into the interior of the snail-passage, to move the animal to leave the shell. But it turned out that in this case the animal, upon the stimulus of the needle, nevertheless contracts in the direction of the shell-tip — thus the customary answer to every disturbance merely drives it against the needle! It succeeds, however, through careful breaking-off of the snail-coils lying nearest the shell-tip, finally to move the animal to leave the shell. The method of cutting-out has, as against the disadvantage of the laboriousness, nevertheless the advantage that it delivers a result at once.

3) **Chemical means: Spoiled water.** A batch of hermit crabs in a relatively small water-vessel had been left standing over midday, before I had got around to unshelling all the animals by the previously described methods. In the afternoon I found all the animals fled from their shells, the most of them in a half-lifeless condition. Brought into fresh seawater, a part of them recovered quickly. Thereupon the method was applied more often, of letting the water spoil to the point where the hermit crabs voluntarily left their shells. This method has the advantage that the trouble is a very small one and, above all, that it can also be applied with the smallest examples. On the other hand, it requires a certain time, and it happens, despite attentiveness, that a larger number of animals are brought too late into fresh water and perish. Through the use of carbonic acid or a narcotic, this method might perhaps be better regulated.

*Archiv f. Entwicklungsmechanik. XXIII.* 38 4) **Temperature: Cold.** A consignment from Trieste arrived on 6 December 1905 at a temperature of only a few degrees above zero. It turned out that the hermit crabs were half-numbed and could be taken from their snail-shells without resistance. Gradually brought into the warmth, the animals recovered again, yet some subsequently perished. Thus also in the lowering of temperature a means for unshelling is given.

In their resistance against the unshelling, the various species of hermit crab behave somewhat differently. Relatively the least reluctance to leave its shell is shown by *Eupagurus Prideauxii*. This species has the habit, even when in possession of a house, of burying itself up to the eyes in sand. Driven from its shell, it likewise buries itself very quickly in the sand, so that the hind-body appears quite covered.

### b. Husbandry.

The husbandry of the hermit crabs is not a difficult one. Any arbitrary glass-vessel in flowing-through seawater, or, still far better, an aquarium-jar [Einsiedeglas] with pure seawater, whose salt-concentration is kept constant in correspondence with the evaporation by topping-up with good freshwater, and which is supplied by a continuous aeration, serves as a dwelling. Favorable is some gravel and fine sea-mud, from which the hermit crabs can draw food, which they take up through a continuous swirl produced by the maxillipeds, or through the chelae. But also small worms (if one has no marine animals at one's disposal, our *Tubifex rivulorum* will do), cut-up fish, and small pieces of meat are, especially to the larger animals, very welcome. As with all aquatic animals, one must always see to the removal of the rotting food-remains. For our experiments there then comes additionally into consideration that no snail-shells remain lying within the reach of the hermit crabs. In Roscoff I had set the larger hermit crabs into small glass-vessels connected with one another by siphons, and had housed these in a larger basin which took up the outflow of the last siphon. By chance, one day, some snail-shells remained lying in this outer aquarium. The next morning, to my astonishment, I found a number of the unshelled hermit crabs in their glasses with snail-shells. One was just in the act of climbing the rim of its glass, so that no doubt existed about the origin of the shells. The hermit crabs had therefore not only found the way to the snail-shells, but had also returned again into their own basins! It must hereby be emphasized that the hermit crabs otherwise did not leave the once-adopted location, and the breaking-out, once all the snail-shells had been carefully removed from the surroundings, did not repeat itself again.

The hermit crabs are very well suited for observations on habits. At first very shy, flinching easily at every approach, they leave the meat set before them unheeded. Later they take the cautiously offered meat even from the forceps or the little rod, and finally they even rush up with a certain greed upon the approach of the feeder and hang themselves onto the feeding-instruments. At first the unshelled hermit crabs use all the means standing at their disposal — such as little pieces of gravel, glass-splinters, and the like — to cover the hind-body with them, whereby they make use of the chelae for grasping. Later, however, given a sufficiently long captivity, they give up this habit entirely — likewise *Eupagurus Prideauxii* the burying in the sand. The smallest examples of fully-formed hermit crabs, which in Roscoff were kept in a large bowl with sea-sand, also in part gave up the habit of hiding behind stones. All my animals were withdrawn from pursuit by enemies; among one another the small ones showed themselves peaceable, the larger ones were, as mentioned, kept entirely isolated (in Vienna each one piece in an aquarium-jar of 2 liters). Small crabs were attacked and devoured by the larger hermit crabs without being able to inflict any harm on them.

## IV. Experiments.

### a. Without regard to illumination.

The first series of experiments set up in Roscoff were housed without regard to illumination, since for the intended purpose of regeneration this factor was of no importance. The intensity of the light was, moreover, a very slight one, since the basin with the experimental glasses and the experimental bowl had to be covered during the day with a wooden board, in order to keep off the directly incident sun-rays and to maintain the lower water-temperature of 10–14° C., which is more favorable for the welfare of the crustaceans. For the changes themselves to be described, this temperature is not necessary, since the later experiments set up in Vienna also at 17° C. and a few degrees more yielded analogous results; only there is then always the danger present that the animals, upon exceeding the usual room-temperature by a little (mostly at about 23° C.), perish.

It was drawn from life on 20 Oct. (Pl. XXV Figs. 3, 4). One notices that the abdomen already appears pigmented throughout; the first abdominal segments stand off sharp-edged, while the further, still rounded part of the abdomen is carried folded in against the breast of the animal, which recalls the normal behaviour of the Galatheids (and crabs). ### b. In the Dark and in the Light.

If we ask ourselves about the causes which are decisive for the alterations of the abdomen after the stripping, these could be of two kinds. Either the typical constitution of the hermit-crab abdomen, which distinguishes it from its nearest relatives, is a direct consequence of the surrounding shell, or else the shell merely plays the role of preventing the action of external factors which, in the absence of it, would get the opportunity to play that same role which they could always play in the other crabs.

Since the seawater can penetrate into the shells of the hermit crabs as well, this external factor scarcely comes into consideration. On the other hand, light is in any case prevented from penetrating through the opaque snail-shells, and since light otherwise plays a role at least in pigmentations (cave animals! Cf. Weindl for the olm [*Proteus*]), it was not excluded from the outset that 

at least the reappearance of the pigmentation after the stripping is to be ascribed to the influence of the now freely entering light.

A new series of experiments was therefore set up, with *Eupagurus Prideauxii* from Trieste; namely, animals stripped of their shell on 6 Dec. 1905 of a carapax-length of 13–19½ mm were used. Five animals were brought into the dark-chamber set up in our institute, which is provided with a double door and red manipulation-lamp, as well as a ventilation conduit. Seven control animals were placed, under otherwise as nearly as possible equal conditions (17°), isolated in 2 one-litre hermit-crab glasses with through-ventilation, fed with cut-up fish or horse-meat-flesh, in a bright hall. In this way very great differences in the intensity of the acting light were given: the abdomen of the animals in the dark-chamber received, despite the absence of the shell, only traces of red light, so long as the necessary manipulations, feeding, control, etc., had to be carried out, but was otherwise surrounded by a still stricter darkness than in the snail-shells in nature, which after all are mostly somewhat translucent. On 5 Jan. 1906 there were still three animals each of the dark- and the light-experiment alive, which had in part moulted. In all of them the formerly quite pale-yellowish abdomen was pigmented orange-red. The segmentation had become distinct in the moulted animals. The last dark-specimen, which moulted on 28 Jan. and lived until 1 March, is reproduced in the picture (Pl. XXV Fig. 7). The pigmentation of the abdomen appears no longer weaker than that on the carapax and the extremities (the chelae appear pale, because they have both been regenerated). The abdomen also otherwise again shows the alteration in the direction toward the related, non-shell-dwelling crustaceans.

These experiments make it appear beyond doubt that the failure of the light is not to blame for the paleness of the abdomen in the shell. For *Diogenes varians*, which appears pigmented even in the shell, this was actually probable from the outset. In this it is, however, of course by no means excluded that a long-lasting lack of light is able to exert an unfavourable influence on the development of the pigment of the whole animal.

Besides light, temperature-increase (cf. Kammerer) and oxygen-supply (cf. Faussek) come into consideration for strong pigmentation. A temperature-increase is certainly of no significance, particularly in the case of the stripped hermit crabs kept in the dark. On the other hand the oxygen-supply after removal of the shell is to be regarded as 

a considerably more favourable [factor], especially when we recall to ourselves that the waste-products are left behind by the hermit crab in the shell, *and that therefore the carbon-dioxide content of the fluid surrounding the abdomen will constantly be a high one.* (Only that form which exhibits the strongest pigmentation on the rest of the body too, namely *Diogenes varians*, is able under these unfavourable conditions to produce pigment on the abdomen as well.)

If we can thus trace back pigmentation, and with it also the differentiations occurring within it, the marking, perhaps to oxygen-supply, and thus make oxygen-deficiency responsible for the normal paleness, then there is no sufficient ground to assume the same for the alteration of form. Here the drawing-upon of the direct action of the shell-pressure brings us nearer to known phenomena: it is the oedema of the skin produced by pressure, which, with accumulation of fluid, leads to a taut tension of the lifted-off uppermost layers. For this direct action speak all the accompanying circumstances: the stretching of the first abdominal segments, the development of the keel corresponding to a pressure-ridge of the shell, the smoothness and tautness of the constricted abdomen.

If we grasp the normal constitution of the hermit-crab abdomen as having arisen through pressure-oedema, then it is also explicable to us that, with the use of a preserving fluid of unsuitable osmotic pressure, a lifting-off and taut tension of the epidermis could once again occur on an abdomen already segmented and flat through stripping. The thinness of the skin would be traceable back to the pressure-atrophy, and perhaps to the latter, besides the oxygen-deficiency, an influence on the chromatophores as pigment-formers is also to be ascribed. Directly influenced by the snail-shell is the right-sided rolling-up of the abdomen, which after the stripping is transformed into a folding-over directed toward the breast. On the other hand it may once again be emphasized that an alteration of the asymmetry with respect to the appendages does not take place. The unequal chelae too, on which regeneration-experiments to be described shortly were undertaken, are not modified by the residence outside of the shell (the large chela regenerates directly, as T. H. Morgan already found in 1904).

## V. Theoretical Significance.

The theoretical interest in the described alterations of the delodged hermit crabs lies in this, that in the normal constitution of the abdomen in these crabs a most far-reaching adaptation appears to be present. This example has been used by a whole series of authors for the elucidation of their views about the efficacy of species-forming principles [artbildende Prinzipien].

Usually it is not only the asymmetry and the rudimentary state of the abdomen, but also the unequal development of the chelae and legs, that is regarded as such adaptations. I have already pointed out on earlier occasions that a correlation between difference-of-chelae [Verschiedenscherigkeit] with respect to the chela-development of a particular side and asymmetry has not taken place, inasmuch as both hermit-crab species with symmetrical abdomen exhibit heterochely, and snail-dwelling [species] can be equal-chelaed or can exhibit a stronger development of one chela directed opposite to the snail- and abdominal-winding (cf. Przibram, 1905, Heterochelie). Also the direct regeneration of the large chela, which takes place without regard to the presence or absence of a definitely wound shell, has already been adduced (cf. ibidem p. 235) as proof for the independence of the two asymmetries from one another.

The asymmetry of the abdomen itself, with the asymmetrical atrophy of the appendages, sets in — according to the fine investigations of Millet T. Thompson in the metamorphosis of the individuals of *Eupagurus longicarpus* and *auriculops* — at a certain stage without regard to whether right-wound, left-wound, straight shells, or none at all, were placed at their disposal. These experiments lend a new support (biogenetic parallel) to the supposition expressed by me (cf. Przibram, 1905, p. 236) that in the phylogeny too the asymmetrical atrophy is the primary, the seeking-out of the snail-shells the secondary moment. Thompson himself expresses himself cautiously enough on this (p. 147): "Whether the asymmetry — which is of the right-handed type, with the exception of the genus *Paguropsis* — owes its origin to this use of shells as dwelling, since almost all marine gastropod-shells are right-handed, cannot be determined with certainty until the phylogeny and relationship of the various genera 

are better understood. But nonetheless it is indubitable that the modifications found here stand for a large part in correlation with the residence in right-spiralled shells."

While the asymmetry — at least as far as concerns the arrangement of the viscera and appendages — is altered neither before the taking-up of the first snail-shell nor after a later stripping in the individual life (at least as long as such specimens have lived hitherto), it stands, now, according to my experiments, otherwise with the remaining constitution of the abdomen. Here there confronts us a form induced by external factors — residence in the shell: pressure and metabolic alteration. The dependence of the deficient articulation and of the other development (pigmentation, chitinization) on the residence in the shells is placed beyond doubt. But whether one may designate this as "adaptation" is nevertheless doubtful. For the question is, namely, whether out of this atrophy of the protected abdomen, out of the segment-, chitin-, and pigment-deficiency, an advantage arises. At most in the closer applying of the body to the snail-wall and the thereby-enabled greater resistance against being drawn out could such a one be perceived. On the other hand, in the re-development of a more vigorous skin in the stripped animals there lies a favourable adaptation to the heightened danger of the influence of mechanical and radiation insults.

The rapid return of the original conditions of the abdominal cutis of crabs speaks eloquently for it, that here in the normal case it is only a matter of the inhibition of a still-present capacity for development, and not of a property lost through selection-processes. When Weismann (1902) wants to explain the reduction by panmixia and germinal selection, there stands against this the reactivation appearing — up to a certain degree — at once and in the same individual. Lloyd Morgan (1890) is little more fortunate when he puts, in place of panmixia, a reversal of natural selection; he admits, moreover, himself that this seems alone not to suffice for a complete explanation of the phenomenon.

On the right path, in his critique, was Ludwig Plate (1903, p. 150): "An example recurring several times in Weismann is the soft abdomen of the Paguridae. He sees in it a proof that passive adaptations too, in which functional stimuli (use, exercise) are excluded, can atrophy.

And yet it is quite possible that the external conditions here have brought about the reduction of the chitinous armor. When a crab presses its soft abdomen into a snail shell immediately after molting, a lasting pressure is exerted on the hypodermis of the cuticula, and the abdomen is moreover bent together more or less spirally: The former circumstance acted inhibitingly on the hypodermis cells, which secreted less chitin, so that the body became soft and elastic with the exception of the hindmost body-end, which had more room at its disposal and therefore hardened.«

If we do not wish to say more than the experiments have taught us, then we cannot yet decide the question of whether a hereditary transmission of the abdominal changes is possible. So far it has not succeeded in rearing Paguridae in captivity, and it could therefore not yet be tried whether the unshelled sexually mature animals would be capable of transmitting their abdominal changes to their offspring, and whether these would be capable of attaining a still stronger deviation if they too were denied the shell in their later life. After the experimental demonstrations of the inheritance of acquired characteristics in other animal classes, it does not appear hopeless that already the first generation might exhibit far-reaching changes, since the matter may involve metabolic changes. Perhaps the keeping of individual hermit crabs, continued longer than in the previous experiments (possibly for years), would yet bring about a further progression of the changes in one and the same individual.

## VI. Summary.

1) Hermit crabs of the genera *Eupagurus* (four species) and *Diogenes* (*varians*) can be kept without a shell.

2) These unshelled (dislodged) hermits exhibit, at the next molting — in a less pronounced manner also already earlier, within a month — a far-reaching change of the abdomen in the direction toward the related shell-inhabiting Anomura.

3) The changes consist, in both genera, in the appearance of a sharp segmentation, a more resistant integument, and a shortening and flattening of the abdomen.

4) With *Eupagurus* there also appears the pigmentation and marking on the abdomen, which normally differs from the carapax by its lack of pigment; with *Diogenes* a pigmentation is already present in the shell as well.

5) The pigmentation in *Eupagurus* also appears in unshelled animals in the dark just as quickly as in the light.

6) The normal deformation of the abdomen in the hermits is to be traced back to the direct pressure of the shell (pressure-edema), the lack of pigment moreover to a lack of oxygen (accumulation of the waste products in the shell).

7) The asymmetry of the abdominal appendages, of the large chelae, and other asymmetries of other body parts are, in the unshelled animals, not influenced — at least during several months.

8) The large chela regenerates in the unshelled hermits, just as if a shell were present, on the same side on which it formerly stood, that is, in *Eupagurus* on the right, in *Diogenes* on the left.
