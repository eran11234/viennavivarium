An earlier experiment, regeneration of stigmata, came to mind, which I wanted to take up again. These mutual relationships between two stigmata of one and the same animal, in addition to the further regeneration achievements, gave me hope that, through the removal of the regeneration at least of parts of the stigma, I might attain to recognize, in such half-symmetrical structures, the developmental-mechanical behaviors at developing structures. ## Regeneration experiments at the 1st thoracic stigma.

### Normal stigmata.

In *Carausius (Dixippus) morosus* Br., on each side there are present two thoracic and eight abdominal stigmata¹. The thoracic stigmata lie between the form-elements [Formelemente] of the two adjacent segments, at the level of the pleurae behind the root of the coxae (Fig. 1).

There, between two chitin plates of the body covering, lies an excavated rounded field of thinner skin. Rather sharply demarcated, encircled thickenings of this skin form the outer plates of the stigma closing apparatus.

**Fig. 1.** Position of the 1st right thoracic stigma. *(figure not reproduced)*

There are above all three (Figs. 1, 2): two triangular "closing plates," between which the stigmatic slit lies in the depth. The mutually facing edges, "slit-edges," form the closure of the stigmatic slit, and its size is conditioned by the movement of the closing plates. Ventral of them the small, likewise triangular "anchor plate," at which a part of the stigma musculature originates and through which it is anchored in the soft-skinned surroundings. The Notum stands, in the 1st thoracic stigma, in functional relation to the closing apparatus: the closing apparatus is encompassed by a more or less independent side-lobe of the Notum, engaging on its dorsal side in an arch-

shaped manner, and supported, and is, as it were, suspended in it. This part we wish to assign to the closing apparatus as the "suspension plate" (Fig. 2, 14), with whose condition the development of its form stands in close correlation, as the following experiments have shown. Its ridge-shaped, reinforced ventral edge, the "suspension arch," follows quite exactly the outline of the closing plate, which is completely separated from it by a skin-fold and leans against it, and on its cranial side draws far ventralward. At the 2nd thoracic stigma (Fig. 3, p. 319), which has a different position relative to the neighboring segment, there exists, in place of the suspension plate, a fully independent chitin ridge of evidently different origin. Inner parts: The tracheal bundle forms at its mouth a widening, through which it arches over the stigma from the inside dome-like ("tracheal vestibule" ["Tracheenvorhof"]). The termination of this widening toward the body surface is formed by the oval "tracheal plate," which lays itself from the inside against the two triangular closing plates and in the middle bears a slit, whose edges are fused with the slit-edges of the closing plate and together with them form the stigmatic slit. Each lip of the closing apparatus therefore consists of two walls (outside the closing plate, inside the tracheal plate), between which body-substance is located. Outside is the outer air, inside the breathing-air, so that a connection with the remaining tissue here, for each of the two lips, exists only on the side turned away from the slit and dorsally and ventrally (at the end of the stigmatic slit). Of the closing and opening muscles let it merely be mentioned that some of their origins, respectively attachments, are located at the anchor plate and at the lower slit-edge, as well as in a chitin ridge lying in the soft skin, which in the 1st thoracic stigma lies dorsal-caudal of the posterior, in the 2nd thoracic stigma dorsal-cranial of the anterior closing plate, in the soft skin. The pigmentation of the superficial parts is various and may be lacking, so that its absence alone, at least in green animals, does not yet prove the regenerative origin of the part in question. At certain places warty humps appear regularly, which on the reduction of the plate in question can likewise retreat, but on regenerates of the parts here coming into consideration have come into being. (Around the two closing plates with the anchor plate a strip of the skin remains unhardened, so that the closing apparatus lies freely movable between the remaining skeleton plates.)

Of particular significance for the following experiments on heteromorphotic regeneration is the half-symmetrical behavior of the normal closing plates and the form of the anterior slit-edge, both. ### Experimental arrangement.

In order to obtain regeneration achievements at the thoracic stigma, in the autumn of 1927 three experimental series were set up. In series XVI/1 I removed the *posterior closing plate* of the first thoracic stigma. On 2. XI. 1927 I selected 17 animals which had already shed for the first time after hatching. With the tip of a small scalpel (the so-called nerve-knife [Nervenmesser]) I first made a cut directly behind the posterior closing plate, from above downward, then at the upper and at the lower end of the stigmatic slit one cut each toward the back (see Fig. 4).

**Fig. 4.** Cutting-guidance. *(figure not reproduced)*

Thereby the closing plate was severed from the surrounding articular skin [Gelenkhaut]. Through the following severing of the one half of the tracheal vestibule (or of the tracheal stems) the closing plate, together with the belonging half of the tracheal plate, was detached from the animal. With the small animals and with the unavoidable blood-exit [Blutaustritt] it is sometimes not possible to see the detached piece of tracheal plate after the operation, yet with this manner of operating *its* detachment too is fairly certain. I used only the nerve-knife and a fine forceps (iris-forceps). With some care the wound is not large and the bleeding only weak, and the animals survive the operation very easily. Only on 7. XI. did I find one animal which had come to grief through the shedding that followed too quickly upon the injury. (At all events, operations on the small animals are mostly more loss-laden, because the still soft chitin-

Already earlier I had set up another series, XV, in which the entire stigma with both closing plates had been removed. It shall be discussed further below.

All operations I made under the REICHERT binocular magnifier with use of the strong objective 30. I held the animals fast with the left hand and operated with the right alone only with use of an iris-forceps and the nerve-knife, which after several cuts was always drawn off anew. In this way even small cuts can be applied with great certainty of cut-guidance on soft and lively animals (also in far less simple operations than those described). I did not anesthetize the animals, since it does not always succeed to hit the right dosage, and thereby the mortality is considerably raised. With corresponding treatment the animals often remain calm during the whole operation in the so-called "death-feigning posture" with the legs laid against [the body]. (Incidentally a posture by which one can recognize with certainty that an animal of this kind is not dead.) Directly after the shedding, in quite soft animals, the operation usually succeeds badly. The experimental animals were kept in small tin cages with dense wire-grids. If one wishes to avoid greater mortality after the operation, it is good, especially in the first days, to attend to a corresponding moisture, so that the animals after the blood-loss do not dry out so easily. They can even be given drink individually with water-drops and recover thereupon even very quickly. As food I used mulberry leaves, ivy, and *Tradescantia*. The stage of the animals one determines according to the development of the genital apparatus. The corresponding drawings are found in: LEHMANN, F. E., LEUTZINGER, WIESMANN (1925). Only in few cases are there animals which show intermediate stages in the form of their genital apparatus. For quick selection a little measuring-table with the various animal-lengths renders good service. To be noted thereby is that between hatching and the first shedding there is still inserted a growth-period without shedding, so that the animals in this stage show two different lengths and correspondingly different appearance. Since the size is dependent on various circumstances, the length-determination alone does not always suffice for the determination of the stage.

## XVI/1: Removal of the posterior closing plate.

The animals show in part heteromorphotic regeneration (in one case a double formation), in part transformation and reduction of the non-operated parts.

### a, b, c: With regenerate.
(Figs. 5–8, Figs. 15–17.)

**XVI/1 b 2** (double formation [Fig. 5a]): The animal is full-grown. The left side is, as always, entirely normal. The first thoracic stigma of the right side is preserved. It stands open and cannot be closed.

The suspension arch is normally preserved, as are all the other parts in the surroundings of the closing plates. These themselves, however, no longer have their original form. Instead of two, three plate-shaped parts are developed in this animal, which surround the triangular cleft.

The anterior closing plate (Fig. 5a) was not affected by the operation; nevertheless it has changed. It has indeed retained its old position, but has taken on an abnormal form. It is reduced in size and is reduced especially on the anterior, lower side turned away from the cleft (suspension corner). This region is no longer hardened; parallel to the edge of the still chitinized part, a deep fold runs obliquely within it to the lower end of the cleft. Correspondingly

**Fig. 5a.** Heteromorphotic triple formation: behind the preserved anterior closing plate, two further "anterior" plates in place of the removed posterior one (on the left in the picture) (×72).  *(figure not reproduced)* to this reduction, neither an active opening movement is to be observed, nor can the further opening of the cleft, which is possible in normal formations, be carried out by pressing with the needle on the articular membrane covered by the corner in question. The cleft-edge of the anterior closing plate is also transformed. On the whole it is shortened, and in place of its bulge directed backward a slight curvature in the opposite direction has appeared, whereby the closing plate is narrowed and the cleft becomes still more widely gaping. The cleft-edge is formed by a bulge, but a characteristic differentiation of the anterior cleft-edges is lacking, namely the rabbet-groove (Falznute). Nor is there present on the opposite cleft-edge any cutting-edge that could fit into this groove. Below, the bulge narrows abruptly, similarly as in the normal formation; above, it is separated from the opposite cleft-edge by a knob-like structure of softer skin.

In place of the posterior closing plate a regenerate has developed which resembles such a plate neither in its overall shape nor in its differentiation. It consists of two parts, which are completely separated from one another by a constriction and which enclose an angle with one another and thereby condition the triangular form of the cleft. At each of the two parts there adjoins, at the cleft-edge, a plate-piece, which (especially at the lower one) is small, but hardened and clearly set off, at the upper one even darkly pigmented. The regenerated cleft-edge is developed in both posterior plate-parts as a bulge, even thicker than at the anterior cleft-edge. The two halves of the bulge (and thereby of the whole plate-regenerate) stand at an obtuse, almost right angle to one another, which is open toward the cleft. Neither a rabbet cutting-edge (Falzschneide), such as would belong to the posterior closing plate, nor a rabbet-groove corresponding to the anterior one, are developed. Each half of the regenerate thus shows a differentiation which corresponds to the anterior closing plate, even if not completely, and which differs from the posterior one in that the cleft-edge is developed not in cutting-edge form but in bulge form. Moreover the two posterior marginal bulges, like the anterior one, bear small teeth, the sensory pegs (Sinnesstiftchen), which on the normal stigma are found only at the anterior cleft-edge, but not at the posterior one, and which are therefore well suited for distinguishing the two formations.

At first, to be sure, a noticeable difference seems to exist in the appearance of this bulge and of the normal anterior cleft-edge, in that the latter, in situ, by no means stands out so strongly. If, however, one takes into account the secondarily altered edge of the anterior closing plate, then the similar development becomes clear. For this forms a middle-member between the two; and it becomes clear that the more powerful development at the regenerate is partly only apparently so great, because in this case one namely sees the whole bulge at the always-gaping cleft, whereas at the normal stigma, on the plate seen from the surface, it is partly overlaid by the rabbet cutting-edge. In addition the absence of the groove conditions the thicker and clumsier form. The complete difference from the normal posterior plate is clear.

In consistency the regenerate is somewhat softer than the normal formation, but yet clearly stiffened, and besides less pigmented; it thus behaves just as we generally see in similar regenerates.

At the constriction-point the individual bulge-parts are sharply bounded, also at their ends, with which they approach the anterior cleft-edge. At the lower end the outrunners (Ausläufer) of the anterior and posterior cleft-edge lay themselves alongside one another in parallel course and show between themselves a fine furrow continuing ventralward, at which also the skinstriations (Hautriefelungen) coming from in front and behind set themselves off against one another sharply and in part at an acute angle. At the upper cleft-end too, cleft-edges and skin-foldings meet one another at a sharp angle and show no transition. It cannot, then, be a matter of a unitary walling-around (Umwallung) of the stigma-opening, which would form merely a scar-formation without relation to the form-differentiation of the closing plates, but rather the bulge-portions separated above and below belong to the district of the anterior and posterior closing plate, and have remained clearly separated as such.

Even apart from the bulge, there shows itself a development corresponding to the closing plates, without here a particular similarity to the anterior plate being ascertainable. The fine striation, which normally is found just behind the bulge on the plate and runs roughly parallel to it, is here too developed. The regenerates are surrounded by the membrane, which has remained soft, on the outer side, with the development of the normal concentric fold-courses, and they stand to the surrounding parts, apart from the indentations at the muscle-attachments, in the usual relation. Thus, above the posterior closing-plate district, the membrane forms band-like fold-courses, obliquely toward the back and up.

The whole plate-apparatus is shifted a little ventralward, and thereby the overall picture is somewhat changed. The shifting is to be traced back to the shortening of the cleft and to the change at the muscles.

The anchor-plate (Ankerplatte) is still completely preserved, also in its characteristic form. Only, it now lies in the body-surface and not, as otherwise, more or less perpendicular to it, with the outer side ventralward. The membrane between it and the lower edge of the closing plate is no longer crowded together (zusammengeschoppt), but shows at the lower cleft-end a funnel-shaped depression, there where the insertion-point of the closing muscle lies. Evidently the rest of the muscle-apparatus is no longer in function, and the consequence of this is also the altered position of the stigma-apparatus, in particular the extension in the dorsoventral direction, as well as the sinking-back into the body-wall, whereas otherwise, through the muscle-contraction, the apparatus is bulged forward. For the opening-muscle, correspondingly, an insertion is also not clearly recognizable from the outside; they were doubtless severed during the cutting-off of the posterior plate, without having afterwards stretched themselves out anew.

Active movement, correspondingly, the closing-apparatus does not show. With passive movement the plate-parts can be bent against one another at the interruptions of the marginal bulge, although (because of the absence of a membrane-fold at the lower end) the possibility of movement is strongly restricted.

Through the wide triangular opening one sees into the interior of the stigma. As normal, the striated wall of the trachea shows itself as background at some distance behind the cleft, closing off the inner space transversely, while farther forward, covered by the anterior closing plate, tracheal trunks lead into the depths. Of the tracheal plate (Tracheenplatte), in the closing-plate regenerates, not much is to be noticed because of its narrowness; still, the cavity reaches also under it. At the anterior closing plate the tracheal plate must have remained fully preserved.

Far more typically developed is the regenerate, and the exchanged character of the posterior closing plate, in animal b 1. In it I obtained the clearest development of the unformed regenerate of the posterior stigma-plate, and clearly also the change of the non-operated anterior plate.

**XVI/1 b 1** (Figs. 5b, 16): The animal stands before the last molt. Here too is found a change of the not-at-all-injured anterior closing plate, and in place of the cut-away closing plate a well-developed transformed regenerate.

Above all, even on superficial inspection, the symmetrical overall form of the closing-apparatus is striking (Fig. 5b), in contrast to normal formations with differing form of cleft-edges and plates. The plane of symmetry is the plane of the stigma-cleft perpendicular to the body. This symmetrical behavior is restricted to the immediate surroundings of the cleft; for the peripheral parts of the closing-apparatus have in general remained preserved unchanged.

The suspension arch (Fig. 16, p. 342) has almost completely normal form. The anchor-plate likewise, deviating only through its position, which lies closer to the body. Correspondingly, here too, as in animal b 2, the ventral side of the stigma-apparatus lies somewhat closer to the body than on the non-operated left side. Strongly there appears the pulling-apart of the parts in the dorsoventral direction, which comes about through stretching of the otherwise folded skin. The cause of both phenomena is to be sought in changes in the muscle-apparatus. According to outward appearance the course of the closing muscle is somewhat changed (see below); the dorsoventral opening-muscle (Musculus stigmaticus, larger part), which must have been cut through during the operation, evidently no longer stretches itself out between anchor-plate and the dorsal membranous district; hence the stretching in the dorsoventral direction instead of the bulging-out and pushing-together of the plate-complex. Over the anchor-plate, indeed, the folded membrane is preserved in the old manner, but in the middle of it there forms a still considerably deeper funnel-shaped depression than in b 2 (Fig. 5b). It is here too surely the closing muscle which, after loss of the connection with the lower cleft-edge, attaches itself at the inner end of the funnel and has only now brought about the retraction. Similar formations are found also at other muscle-attachments and lead, in extreme development, to long tendons, which as hollow threads connect the muscle with a small depression at the skin-surface, e.g. at the flexor of the tarsi of the leg of *Carausius*. A form belonging here has regularly arisen, after removal of the eye, in the same animal at the masticatory muscle.

To the mentioned funnel there runs from the lower cleft-edge a longer dark line, at which the structures of the skin-district lying in front of and behind it set themselves off against one another sharply and without transition. Immediately above the upper cleft-end is found a shallow pit; whether it is to be traced back to the somewhat altered fold-course or to the displaced remnant of an insertion of the dorsoventral opening-muscle cannot be said with certainty.

The surroundings of the closing plates themselves are thus not strongly altered; whereas none of these has the normal form.

The anterior closing plate is indeed preserved, but somewhat narrowed. Of the row of knobs (Höckerreihe) present on the left stigma, only a few at the lower end are here preserved. Above all, however, the cleft-edge is transformed (Fig. 5b). In place of its weakly curved and in general convex form a distinct concavity has appeared. The rabbet-groove characteristic of the anterior cleft-edge has completely disappeared. It is somewhat more strongly thickened than normally, its narrowing at the lower end is preserved. Despite the change, it is surely to be distinguished from a normal posterior cleft-edge.

The posterior closing plate gives a strikingly similar picture, but arranged in the reverse direction. Cleft-edge and plate-portion are developed. There is here only a single posterior plate present, hence the relation to the anterior one becomes much clearer than in b 2. As in it, the cleft-edge, in contrast to the cutting-edge form of the normal formations, is developed in bulge form; the absence of the groove distinguishes it from normal anterior cleft-edges, as does the concave curvature. In both characters, however, it agrees with the altered edge of the anterior plate, to which it is exactly symmetrical, namely with respect to the transverse plane that can be laid through the stigma-cleft. The agreement goes still further: the dorsal larger concavity, the back-curving and the parallel course of the ventral bulge-ends agree exactly in both edges, and finally the continuation of the furrow, which separates these lower ends, forms that dark line, which from there runs into the deepened insertion-funnel at the anchor-plate, and at which also the structure of the anterior and posterior skin-districts sets itself off sharply against one another, as it were so as not to overstep the boundary between the two formations placed against one another and oppositely directed; it even forms the symmetry-line for two oppositely directed striations of the skin-surface. A difference between the two edges exists only in the somewhat more irregular form (somewhat convex in the middle), the greater softness and thickness of the posterior one. This softness and the smallness of the adjoining plate-part (which in any case departs still further from the normal constitution of the posterior closing plate) are, however, evidently in connection with the fact that the posterior plate represents indeed a younger formation, only afterward grown out, and, in consequence of its origin from the cleft-edge backward (see younger stages), the plate is still comparatively imperfect. It has, however, no widening ventrally, such as would just correspond to the posterior plate, although it is otherwise sufficiently developed to show hardening and surface-striation (young regenerates are soft and smooth). Its suspension takes place at the normal, band-like skin-fold, above, behind.

The two closing plates approach one another at the upper and lower cleft-end with their marginal bulges, and the manner in which the two formations enter into relation with one another is of importance for their morphological assessment; for if it were a matter, in the posterior bulge, only of a scar-formation, then it would be to be expected that it grasps around the cleft and adjoins directly the preserved anterior closing-edge. If one wanted to regard also this latter as a new formation (possible degeneration of the parts neighboring the wound, its altered form), then a coherent ring-wall all around the stigma-opening would be more probable. Apart from the sharp boundary, the form repeating itself again and again in a series of cases, and the symmetry between anterior and posterior edge, now also through the presence of an articulation of the two bulges to one another this possibility is excluded. At the upper end they stand opposite one another in an angle with rounded ends, which are connected with one another, easily movable, by a small articular skin (Gelenkshaut). At the lower end they narrow and lay themselves with their ends in parallel course (in the dorsoventral direction) against one another, yet without fusing, but separated by a narrow furrow, the beginning of the mentioned dark line. Along this straight incision they are well movable against one another; but not laterally displaceable, which stands in contrast to the connection of the two closing plates at the normal stigma, but is not surprising, since there indeed not two marginal bulges stand opposite one another, but a bulge a cutting-edge.

An active movement, an opening or closing of the cleft, I could not observe.¹ To be sure, there showed themselves, especially with illumination by a warm lamp, twitchings at the closing-apparatus, especially in the region of the anchor-plate, which are seen also at the normal stigma during the closing-movements. The cleft remains, however, open, although it is passively movable. By pressure with the needle on the lower cleft-end a narrowing is to be achieved, to be sure no complete closure of the concave edges. A further opening than normal (by denting the membrane in front of or behind the closing plates) is not to be achieved, not because the cleft were maximally opened (it can otherwise be unlocked still further), but probably in consequence of the narrowing of the closing plates and of the loss of the widely projecting lower suspension-corners, which otherwise serve as levers in the opening-process. An immediate connection between this reduction and the cutting-through of the opening-muscle cannot indeed be asserted with certainty, but is not improbable.

Even in these unchanged animals the anchor plate remained unchanged. Nowhere did the typical bulge-form arise, but only the stigma extending downward (Abb. 22, p. 342).

By 14 March 1928 there was no anchor plate either. Nor was the operation site at all changed. Once again the anchor plate took its place; the closing plates remained, as before, in the same way somewhat further inward than in animals with normal stigma, but here the soft chitin corner of the protuberance was no longer grasped, just as in the normal case. The pull of the muscle insertion was no longer effective; here therefore it weakened, and the connection with the spalt no longer lay at the soft joint plate. The function of the stigmal muscle could not set in here.

Result, accordingly: After removal of the entire actual closing apparatus, no regeneration set in, but only a change of form (reduction) of the anchor plate (in particular of the anchor ridges), and a partial hardening — which, however, is not to be regarded as such — of the joint plate that was no longer used.

Its disappearance is possibly to be explained purely mechanically; for perhaps it otherwise comes into being, in the soft moulting state, through the resting of the rabbet-edge upon it, and is leveled out by the blood pressure in the absence of the latter. To be sure, in one case a weak furrow was present even after the operation (XVI/1 e 3); there the front closing-edge too was indeed changed, but had not become concave. On the other hand, one can also observe an absence of the groove in normal formations, and only when one presses on the slit-edge does it then become distinct again. That the slit-edge, and sometimes with it the whole plate, is strongly shortened at the ventral end must surely be explained from the shortening of the regenerating hinder edge; for [after] two moultings following the operation, in XVI/1 a 1 the hinder slit-edge has regenerated only a small distance, and in front of the slit the front plate [stands] almost in its original size, with the whole slit-edge, but free only in its upper part, where it stands opposite the hinder one, while at its lower part it is fused with the opposite skin (Fig. 12, p. 337); in the next stage the front one too is shortened, equal to the hinder one, and only in the last [stage] again somewhat elongated together with the latter (Fig. 5c, p. 329). The shortening of the front closing-plate thus evidently results from the growing-in of its lower part into the surrounding skin. It is probably also this process which sometimes causes it to merge completely into its surroundings, when, in place of closing-plates, only a larger hardening is any longer to be found, which either still shows the slit as a small fissure or wholly occupies the place of the former stigma (Fig. 9, p. 335). It also happens that it disappears without trace and not even any hardening indicates its original position. There then remains only a soft skin-fold, which is nothing other than a part of the articular skin which connects the two body segments with each other (Fig. 10, p. 336). Sometimes the front closing-plate is merely reduced in size and lacks the suspension-corner (front-below), which may correspond to the reduction of the suspension apparatus and of the anchor-plate. In some cases the plate is not only smaller, but also smoother, without bosses, translucent and less pigmented, so that one has the impression that it is a regenerate, after the plate had been lost during moulting. In this way one could then also explain the complete reduction of the stigma. Besides, there are also, among the transformed front closing-plates, some which are rough and dark and do not correspond at all to a regenerate; and finally, according to the experimental series XVI/2 and XV, no regeneration is to be expected upon loss of both plates or even only of the front closing-plate. Here too, therefore, it is a matter of a correlation between the presence of the hinder plate and the form of the front one, or expressed differently: upon loss of the hinder plate, the front plate is transformed or reduced. In this connection it is to be noted that the two plate-regions are connected only at their upper and lower ends, while between them, and within them, lies the air-space of the trachea, so that they are widely separated and only at the outermost ends is an immediate bridge of organic substance present.

In view of the compensatory hypertypy of the crab-claws mentioned further below, let the simplification of the uninjured dactylopodite after injury to the corresponding limb of the opposite side, which was observed in *Carcinus*, be cited as an example of a similar correlation (PRZIBRAM 1908). With this, however, nothing is meant to be said about the relationship between the transformations at the operated stigma and compensatory hypertypy.

In the overall form of the stigma, besides the new symmetry still to be discussed, the position of the slit has also changed, insofar as a wholly altered appearance has not resulted from its reduction. Whereas normally the slit is directed almost dorso-ventrally, somewhat toward the rear-below, after healing it runs more from the rear-above toward the front-below (Figs. 6–8, p. 331 to 333); and in the most complete formations there stands, corresponding to the presence of an existing formation, a symmetry line perpendicular [to it] (Figs. 5a, b).

## Heteromorphotic Regeneration.

*General description.* With the mention of a symmetry line, which halves the slit dorso-ventrally, what is essential is already indicated as to how matters stand with the regeneration of the hinder closing-plate. In some measure the symmetry is worked out completely only in a few cases, but it applies almost entirely to the slit-edges of many formations. Caudally from the slit lies, by way of conclusion of the regeneration, a chitinous piece which corresponds in its form to the one located cranially from it (the front closing-plate), but behaves toward it as the left hand to the right. Not always has this chitinous piece been formed at all. Sometimes no regeneration whatever has set in. Then the slit is lacking, but also both closing-plates. After the first moultings following the operation, some animals still had the front plate and the slit, without the hinder one having regenerated up to that point. The hinder boundary of the slit then formed merely a soft skin-fold. Upon removal of merely the front closing-plate, the reverse behavior set in. The slit had then, to be sure, vanished; from the hinder closing-plate an inwardly grown remnant had been left behind, the front one had not regenerated; or here too both plates, together with the slit, had vanished. Here no regeneration of the removed plates had set in. Nor [had it] upon removal of both plates.

In no case was the uninjured plate completely reduced, when at the same time the operated one was at least in part regenerated; in other words: from the *uninjured* plate (in both experimental arrangements the front closing-plate) the hinder one always regenerated, and indeed as a *regenerated* (hinder) *closing-plate* [sometimes on both slit-edges, which then again is embedded [as a] common, hard plate, and which might correspond to an uninjured closing-plate].

It happens that behind the slit a whole closing-plate is regenerated, which, however, is always smaller than the already normally smaller, front [one]. For the plate-part is always only incompletely regenerated, the front one normally developed, whereby both plates at last develop equally large, whereby, however, often both plates [are] normal, hence the front one too develops normally, whereby the normal one has also shortened the front one (Figs. 5, 6, p. 326, 329, 332).

Or it is that on the slit-edge alone a re-formed plate could have been formed (Figs. 6, 8).

Never, however, was a formation encountered which could have corresponded to the hinder closing-plate; always a *reversed counterpart of the front one* developed.

In the simplest case of regeneration there appears at the hinder edge of the slit a wall. In the normal animal there is here a sharp thin cutting-edge, while at the front slit-edge it is wall-shaped. Whereas the cutting-edge comes about through the tapering of the plate toward the edge, the wall sets itself off distinctly from the plate, and does not come about merely through a gradual increase in thickness, but constitutes its own formation, which is well suited to make the regenerate behind the slit appear morphologically equivalent to the front closing-plate. The wall is, however, hardened somewhat more strongly on the outside, both within and on the side turned away from the slit, both in the normal front [edge] and in the regenerated hinder slit-edge. A rabbet-groove [Falznute] is, however, not found. Sometimes, however, a furrow appears when one presses with the needle on the soft side of the wall, similarly to [what occurs] in the transformed ones and sometimes in the normal front slit-edge lacking the groove. A differentiation otherwise belonging *only* to the front slit-edge consists of the small spines, the sense-cones, which stand in a row on the inner side of the marginal wall. In one case I have seen spines on the regenerated hinder marginal wall even before the last moulting (XVI/1 c 3), otherwise only in the adult animals, where the hinder slit-edge regenerated harder (Fig. 5a, p. 329). I had not expected the regeneration of these sense-spines, since in another heteromorphosis, on the antenna-leg [Fühlerbein] of *Carausius morosus*, sense-spines at the base of the tibia were not formed, in contrast to the normal leg (BORCHARDT). So here they stand on the slit-edge just as regularly as normally, sometimes only singly, sometimes in several groups; yet on the altered front slit-edge too they no longer always appear in a closed row, or may even be lacking there.

In contradistinction to the normal front [edge], the regenerated hinder wall is not convex toward the slit. It corresponds in its curvature mostly again to the altered front marginal wall, in that it (in symmetry to it) likewise runs concave toward the slit, or also leans against [it] and runs just straight (Figs. 6, 7, p. 331, 332). (In XVI/1 a 1 it is convex, similar to a normal front slit-wall, Fig. 5c, p. 329.) Above, the two are, in the best-developed regenerates, curved toward each other, below they lie running parallel against each other, most beautifully in XVI/1 b 1 (Fig. 5b), (only in one case, in XVI/1 c 3, does the hinder edge have a concave form, while the front one has remained straight or even somewhat convex, Fig. 8, p. 333).

From the plate-shaped part not always is something regenerated (Figs. 6, 7, p. 331, 332); there sometimes follows directly the articular skin behind the wall, or a larger hardening, when both slit-edges are embedded in a common plate. But when an independent plate adjoins the wall immediately (Figs. 5a, b, c, 7), then it is only very small and lets itself, in its outlines, be no longer assigned with any certainty to a particular other closing-plate. On its surface there are found striations [grooving], which are not unlike the dorso-ventral striations which on normal closing-plates follow directly behind the slit-edge. Never have I been able to find bosses on these plates; sometimes they were only weakly pigmented and dark, and through this had the general appearance of a regenerated chitin-part. Sometimes one could see skin-folds, which run obliquely, as also the plate-regenerates run out from the corresponding side of the normal hinder closing-plate, which always shows this manner of suspension. From this, naturally, no conclusion can be drawn as to the character of the closing-plate as "front" or "hinder," since the matter concerns the relation to the suspension apparatus, not the differentiation of the plate itself. In spite of the little characteristic form of this plate-shaped part, it is nevertheless possible, on the ground of the slit-marginal wall with its differentiations, to recognize the whole plate-regenerate as a front closing-plate appearing heteromorphotic, which adjoins the place of the formed hinder one, and which the mentioned mirror-image behavior toward the more or less still existing [hinder one] remaining shows.

As already mentioned, the symmetry line runs through the slit in dorso-ventral direction, in well-developed cases with a certain geometric completeness. There it becomes visible through the furrow indicated (Fig. 5b), in which the closing-plate edges abut one another; at the lower slit-end it continues from the parallel-running ends of the slit-edges further ventral-ward through the articular membrane as a line, which ends in the funnel-like drawn-in insertion of the closing-muscle, and against which even the striations of the soft skin set themselves off sharply. The anchor-plate takes no more part in the formation of the symmetrical figure, just as little as the suspension-plate.

In one case both plates surround a triangular stigma-opening. All turn toward it a marginal wall, which bears the sense-spines. The front, larger one [preserves] the transformations occurring as a rule, the two smaller, hinder ones, lying at an angle to one another behind the slit, are regenerates. The ends of all three marginal walls touch one another, so that the triangle is closed, but without the walls passing over into one another. Also the plate-parts are well separated from one another. The formation thus corresponds in everything to the otherwise occurring heteromorphosed stigma-closure, merely that, in place of the hinder closing-plate, two heteromorphotic plates regenerate (Fig. 5a).

In the juvenile stages there are found nothing but formations which are not as pronounced as in the adult animals, but already point to the later attained form, in any case in the direction of their development they do not deviate from it (Figs. 11, 12, p. 337).

### Theoretical Consideration.

For the interpretation of the described, altered re-formation of the hinder closing-plate, let us call to mind the various kinds of heteromorphoses. Three groups can be distinguished: Homöosis, polar heteromorphosis, and compensatory hypertypy (see Schema p. 350). By *Homöosis* (in the wider sense) one understands the phenomenon that body-sections lying one behind the other in the series take on the characters of a member of the series of another member of the body (after BATESON 1924, p. 84). According to PRZIBRAM, three kinds can be distinguished:

a) Addition-Homöosis (adventive Homöosis, Heterotopy). Here there appears, besides the normal number of formations, a still supernumerary one with the form of a normally elsewhere standing body-section.

b) Displacement-Homöosis (Translation, Heterophory). A normally appearing formation at a particular place has appeared at another place, while it is lacking at the original place.

c) Substitution-Homöosis (Homöosis s. str.). A lacking formation is replaced by another, which assumes the form of a formation standing at another body-section (or also existing). Among others belongs here the appearance of antennae in place of eyes or of legs in place of antennae.

In *polar heteromorphosis* there arises at one pole of an axis a form which normally stands only at the other pole of the axis. There are, namely, two cases to be distinguished:

Whether the appearance of the heteromorphosis is dependent on outer influences. There belongs here the regeneration of a head in place of the foot-end in *Tubularia*, for which J. LOEB originally coined the expression "polar heteromorphosis." The head arises here when the relevant pole is washed around by water (probably in connection with the stronger O₂-supply), while the development of the foot-end is called forth by the planting-in [of the cut surface].

Another form of heteromorphoses we find when, on differently-poled, like-poled formations arise, [which] let themselves be traced back to proximal regeneration on inner causes alone. Partly quite cut-off distal parts have more the capacity to replace the proximal parts; likewise after the proximal [parts], from which a regenerate with the form of the distal part [arises], and indeed with reversal of the main axis, the mirror-image of the distal one, of which we have spoken. So there arise, e.g., from cut-off planarian-heads, double-heads turned away from one another. At lateral, paired formations there occurs the case that, in that the longitudinal axis of the relevant formation is reversed, [the form is changed]. Cut-off [pieces] of extremities (insofar as they are firmly directed at the basal cut surface), under maintenance of the remaining axes, [yield] reversed, mirror-image extremity-ends in proximal direction; in these cases the regenerate looks the same as the corresponding appendage of the other body-side. Also the middle component of the fracture-triple-formations [Bruch-Dreifachbildungen] behaves mirror-imagedly and is traced back to the same proximal-regeneration process.

Another form of heteromorphoses we find in the so-called *compensatory hypertypy*. When, with unequal development of paired anlagen, the more developed one is removed, then on the one hand the more weakly developed one can take on the form of the removed formation of the opposite side; on the other hand, in place of the removed one, the more weakly developed form can be regenerated, so that finally the interchange of the forms of the two body-sides results. An example is furnished by the heterochelous (differently-clawed) crabs: if the larger "crusher-claw" ["Knack-Schere"] is amputated, then a small "pincer-claw" ["Zwickschere"] grows in, while on the opposite side the retained pincer-claw transforms itself into the larger crusher-claw. In later age-stages the capacity for claw-reversal can cease, and in an intermediate stage a changed re-growth of the removed form without reversal of the opposite-lying one can take place, so that equal-clawed specimens arise. Thus, according to circumstances, on each of the two body-sides the one or the other of the two possible forms can come to development.

With Substitution-Homöosis the compensatory hypertypy has in common that the body-parts concerned prove to be in possession of two different formation-possibilities (antenna : leg, eye : antenna, crusher-claw : pincer-claw).

With which of the listed heteromorphoses can the case be compared?

The Addition-Homöosis falls away, since it is not a matter of the addition of a formation to the normal number of parts.

Just as little does Displacement-Homöosis come into consideration, not merely because a translation would be expected only in the embryonic stage, but also because all the plates that come into question have remained at their original places.

Substitution-Homöosis would come into question as an explanation insofar as the form that has arisen resembles in its basic features the hinder closing-plate of the second thoracic stigma. Thus one might assume that to the hinder region of the first thoracic stigma there belongs, besides the form-potency for a cutting-plate, also the potency for such a form, which corresponds to the (hinder) wall-plate of the *second* thoracic stigma, and so up to the replacement of a part of the first segment by the corresponding part of the second segment.

and it would thus have come to a replacement of a part of the first segment by the corresponding [part] of the second segment. But the strong development of the wulst [bristle-ridge], the pointed upper end of the plate, and its size — in general, its greater resemblance to the plate of the first stigma — speak against this interpretation. The double-formation, too, finds no explanation in this way. — That it is a matter of a replacement by a formation corresponding to the equally-formed and congruent anterior closing-plate of the *opposite side* (left side), one will probably hardly assume. (Rather, agreement with the opposite side as regards symmetry is found also in proximal regeneration.)

When, however, we do not wish to draw upon the left side or the second segment, but quite generally to seek the form-potency of a wulst-plate alongside that of the normal cutting-plate in the anlage of the posterior closing-plate, then we arrive already at the second of the interpretations set out in detail below, which rests upon the analogy to individual phenomena that occur in the *compensatory hypertypy*.

The comparison with the anterior closing-plate, which is mirror-image-equal to the regenerate and [belongs to] the same side and the same segment, leads to the interpretation through *proximal regeneration*.

That *polar heteromorphosis* which (as in Tubularia) is evoked through *external influences* upon the pole to be altered drops away here, since in the regeneration before us no such external influences can be demonstrated.

Before the attempt to explain the closing-plate heteromorphosis through the comparison with the heteromorphoses that come into consideration, the concepts important for it shall yet be delimited.

A distinction must be made between observations which we can make immediately on the closing-plates, and properties which we ascribe to the plates hypothetically.

If we admit only displacements that take place in the principal axis, and in doing so exclude that a plate turns its margin differentiated as split-margin away from the split, then these conditions still always apply to all observed cases (with the exception of the double-formation, which, however, is not of importance here and will be discussed later). We can then, with the following two observations, unambiguously determine the position and form of any given closing-plate of a definite thoracic stigma:

1. Does the plate lie before or behind the split?

2. Is it a matter of a wulst-plate or of a cutting-plate (form-quality)?

Thus, for example, for the regenerated plate of the 1st thoracic stigma, form and position are given by the indications "behind the split" and "wulst-plate," since, according to the restriction made, it turns its split-margin toward the split. At the same time, we have refrained from every hypothetical statement about their character. Moreover, the symmetry-relations between the plates are of significance.

In the schema Fig. 23 (p. 353) the split is indicated by a small transverse stroke, the closing-plates by triangular figures whose broad end signifies the split-margin [Spaltrand]. The form-quality is given by the contour, in that notched triangles represent wulst-plates, but entire-margined ones represent cutting-plates. We see at the same time how the experimental-plate-complex of the 2nd stigma lies symmetrically to that of the 1st stigma (with respect to a transverse plane through the mesothorax), while the closing-plates of one stigma are among themselves symmetrical merely in position and, by and large, in contour, but in form-quality are thoroughly different.

Proceeding from direct observation, we find at each plate the anterior side different from the posterior, and can accordingly ascribe to it — as to the whole body and to others of its parts — a polarity. We can think a polar axis laid into it in the longitudinal direction, since alterations occur precisely in this longitudinal direction. The polar difference from which we thereby proceed is designated by the split-margin (and by the contour, which, however, always goes conformably with the position of the split-margin). Thus, for example, at the first stigma a plate which originates from the region lying before the split normally has the split-margin behind; if it has it in front, then we can, in accordance with the definition, say that its polarity is inverted. (Such a plate would then, in order to turn its split-margin toward the split, have to be located behind the split, although it originates from the anterior region.) The reverse holds for a plate which originates from the region lying behind the split, and at the second stigma everything would be inverted [as compared] to the first.

In order to make for ourselves a picture of the developmental-mechanical connections which have determined the formation of a closing-plate, we will therefore inquire after two characters that are not externally recognizable, but are to be hypothetically assumed for the plate:

1. Has the heteropolar axis to be thought into the plate retained its normal direction, or is it inverted? (*Direction-polarity* [Richtungspolarität] of the plate.)

2. Does the plate, according to its origin, belong to the region of the normal anterior or to that of the normal posterior closing-plate? (According to the *layer-polarity* [Schichtungspolarität] of the body, Przibram 1910, 1926.)

For its interpretation, two possibilities present themselves, according as we judge it by layer- and direction-polarity:

On the one hand, it agrees in its form-quality (wulst-plate) with an anterior normal plate, and we can accordingly ascribe it, with regard to the layer-polarity of the anterior region, that is, grasp it as an altered "anterior" closing-plate (Fig. 25/1). But since such a [plate], in the normal case with upright direction-polarity, has its split-margin on the posterior side, we must therefore ascribe to the regenerate an inverted direction-polarity (Fig. 25/2).

On the other hand, its position behind the split speaks for [the view] that, with regard to the layer-polarity, it is to be placed on a par with a "posterior" closing-plate (Fig. 26/1). Then the position of the split-margin at the anterior end corresponds to the normal behavior, that is, its direction-polarity has remained upright (Fig. 26/2).

In the first case we can range the newly arisen plate in the series of the proximal regenerates, of which we know that the re-produced part has inverted direction-polarity relative to the preserved one and is accordingly mirror-image-symmetrical, but otherwise shows a formation of the same kind as it. The normal anterior closing-plate lies further toward the body-end (head-end), thus more distally; moreover it lies further removed from the transverse plane through the mesothorax, which forms the symmetry-plane for stigma 1 and 2 (the abdominal stigmata can here, on account of their wholly different structure, play no role). The normal posterior closing-plate at stigma 1 is then to be designated as the more proximal one. Accordingly, the proximal half of the stigmal apparatus would have been removed. According to our first assumption about the layer-polarity, the regeneration would then proceed under the form-building influence of the anterior, that is, distal plate; but in inverted direction, which is in general required by the position of the cut-surface at the preserved part, here in addition by the position of the closing-plate relative to the split. As always, the distal part [Distand] no longer has the capacity to replace proximal parts, but can only still form distal-forms, and the result would correspondingly be: situated proximally to the part that remained preserved, a regenerate with the form and the inverted polarity and symmetry of that still-present distal end. In so far, the result would correspond to the already known cases in which extremities regenerate at the proximal cut-surface or (apart from the enantiomorphy that falls away through the median position) to the Turbellarian heads regenerating at the proximal cut-surface, which all bring forth their own mirror-image in symmetrical arrangement to themselves; or to the likewise mirror-image middle components of the double- and triple-formations likewise traced back to proximal regeneration (in insects hitherto the only examples of proximal regeneration). *The anterior closing-plate has, toward the back (here proximad), formed an inverted anterior closing-plate corresponding to the inverted direction of growth, for which alone it was still capable as a distal part [Distand].*

The second interpretation would not require us to grasp the anterior closing-plate as the form-builder of the regenerate. For the regenerate is, with regard to the layer-polarity, ascribed an affiliation to the region behind the split-margin. Direction-polarity (split-margin in front) and position behind the split agree with its interpretation as a "posterior" closing-plate; only the form-quality appears altered.

Since merely the two otherwise same-kinded thoracic stigmata themselves show a mirror-image formation, but not their surroundings — these rather being, with respect to the longitudinal axis, same-directed in both cases — it therefore lies near to assume, for both formations too, same-kinded and same-directed and indeed in-themselves-symmetrical structural plans [Blaupläne], at least according to the anlage. To each of the two plates of one stigma would originally belong the possibility for the formation of both forms, the wulst- and the cutting-bearing plate. In the course of development, then, through whatever circumstances, at the first stigma the posterior [plate-anlage], at the second [stigma] the anterior plate-anlage would suppress the potency for wulst- and small-tooth-formation [Zähnchenbildung], while the cutting-potency would come for formation — and vice versa. Which cause would be decisive for the selection of the potency remains undetermined; possibly this depends on the displacement against the neighbor-segments or on the plate-size, [in] that the plate lying toward the (functional) intersegmental fold, the more freely movable [plate] turned away from the suspension-arc [Aufhängebogen], in each case carries the blade [Schneide] (in spite of the oppositely-set position with regard to the split at stigma 1 and 2).

After removal of the cutting-plate, the other of the two potencies competing at its formation-site could now, in its regeneration, break through and crowd back the previously effective one. The ground for this would be the altered relationships, since the relationships of a newly regenerating plate to grown-out parts of the surroundings must of course be others than the relationships under parts of the same developmental stage. Thus the regenerating plate must grow more quickly. It too is, over against the preserved [plate], naturally smaller, just as in ontogeny the likewise small-tooth-bearing anterior [plate] is over against the posterior plate. After the first moultings following the operation (Fig. 12), and also still later, this altered size-relationship is distinct, and only before the conclusion of growth does the regenerating split-margin attain the same length. In so far, then, as at the formation of each individual plate both form-potencies stand at disposal, *the posterior closing-plate would have been regenerated as such, yet of the two competing potencies the one for the formation of a wulst-plate has prevailed over the other (the one for the formation of a cutting-plate).* Under these circumstances we should then have a partial parallel to the phenomena in the compensatory hypertypy of the crabs (see above), in which, according to circumstances, the snapping-claw or pinching-claw possible according to the anlage on each of the two sides comes for formation; namely (apart from the outgrowth of the pinching-claw to the snapping-claw) the removed snapping-claw is replaced through the form of the otherwise opposite-lying pinching-claw. Only here these two half-symmetrical forms lie at the same body-side opposite each other, while at the crab-pincers they are distributed onto the left and right side.

In what follows, a juxtaposition of arguments for and against each of both interpretations shall be given.

How form-quality, position, and in particular the mirror-image symmetry of the regenerate to the anterior plate can be united with the conception as a proximal regenerate has already been discussed. The corresponding direction- and layer-polarity, too, can be ascribed to the plate-[system] under consideration. — If the regenerate is to be formed from the anterior closing-plate, then there would have to be found on the latter a free proximal cut-surface from which the regeneration proceeds. But against this there seems to stand in contradiction that here the regenerate is connected with the original distal part [Distand] only at its outermost upper and lower end, while the principal part of the cut runs in a quite different region behind the regenerate. Moreover, it is separated through an open air-space (tracheal forecourt) also from the tissues in the vicinity of the distal part. But the double-formation XVI/1 b 2 points precisely to those two small cut-surfaces at the ends of the marginal wulst (Fig. 4, p. 321) as starting-point for the regeneration; the two part-regenerates, same-kinded among themselves and corresponding to our anterior closing-plate (Fig. 5a, p. 323), seem there in fact to have proceeded from those sites, while their other two ends collide [zusammenstoßen].

That, moreover, it is here, as in all other cases, a matter of self-standing plates and not, in some manner, of a scar-like, gap-surrounding formation, the entire form distinctly shows, and the sharp separation of the three margin-wulsts provided with sense-cones [Sinnesstiftchen]. Only the proximal regeneration furnishes a good explanation for the double-formation; in the other case one would have to assume a double-formation caused by accidental injury through "splitting" — and probably in the other cases too, with only a single simple regenerate, the new formation will have proceeded from both (or merely from one of those) cut-surfaces. — That no regeneration any more set in when, besides the posterior, the anterior closing-plate too had been removed, would likewise speak for [the view] that the anterior plate is to be made responsible for the arising of the regenerate. Perhaps the surrounding tissues no longer have the capacity at all to form closing-plates, and therefore the opportunity is given to the anterior closing-plate alone to replace the posterior. In any case the regeneration thereby shows itself dependent on the existence of the anterior plate. The anterior closing-plate too, incidentally, was, after its removal while leaving the posterior, no longer regenerated by the surrounding tissues. Why the posterior plate was not in a condition to do so cannot, to be sure, be said. — Questionable is the agreement with the ordinary proximal regenerates with regard to the designation "distal": the conception of the plate removed from the symmetry-plane between the two stigmata as a distal part deviates from the customary use of this relation, and its position approaching the anterior body-end too does not unconditionally speak for it; for the perpendicular distance from the body-longitudinal axis could namely also be thus designated, or here the free split-margins could count as distal. Moreover, with the tracheae, whose entrances the stigmata form, it is not even certain whether, on account of the ectodermal nature, the ends projecting into the body are not to be conceived of as distal. Nevertheless, no contradiction results from the conception here applied. — Under avoidance of the concept "distal," the same explanation can also be given in attachment to the experience that under circumstances the two normally different margins of an extremity too can become mirror-image-equal (Brandt 1925), whereby one can imagine that the differentiation proper to the relevant margin failed to occur, but the opposite-lying margin was in a condition to furnish a differentiation in its place — in inverted direction (Przibram 1921, 1926, proximal regeneration in the transverse direction). The two closing-plates would then correspond to the two extremity-margins. (Admittedly, here the posterior has been replaced through the anterior, while in vertebrates, in which this observation has been made, mostly the anterior margin is replaced through the posterior.)

In the case of the second interpretation, likewise to be derived from position and form (competing potencies), the distinction between distal and proximal is unimportant, and with that this difficulty falls entirely away.

A gap remains in the explanation, insofar as we indeed do not know which causes it actually is that decide the predominance of this or that form-building potency. In the case of compensatory hypertypy it is natural to make responsible for it the speed with which a form can be attained, and the height of its differentiation, inasmuch as the transformation of the small pinching-claw into the larger snapping-claw can be conceived of as a continuation of growth and of differentiation, while on the opposite side growth and differentiation come merely as far as the attainment of the smaller and simpler pinching-claw form. By contrast, the anterior closure-plate corresponding to the pinching-claw is indeed likewise smaller, but evidently more highly differentiated. — But to compensatory hypertypy one also reckons the (admittedly less common) phenomenon that echinoderm larvae, upon general injury, form the hydrocoel on both sides (Oshima 1921), and this is explained by the fact that, as a consequence of the inhibition in the differentiation of the favored side, a compensatory further development of the opposite side now sets in. These cases of compensatory hypertypy would provide a good parallel for the heteromorphosis of the closure-plates, provided one takes into account the decrease of differentiation and size of the uninjured anterior closure-plate (loss of the fold-groove, transition to the concave form demanded by surface tension, retardation in growth even relative to the normal body parts, whereby it finally comes to an equal length of the remaining and the regenerated plate). Compensatorily, the rapidly regenerated posterior plate receives a higher differentiation than is normally the case (hypertypy). The result here agrees even better in both cases: two opposing structures of the same kind. But naturally neither of the two comparisons (crabs and echinoderm larvae) can be carried through completely. — For the double formation (XVI/1 b 2) one obtains no explanation in this way. One would have to assume an accidental injury during the molt at the (admittedly weakened) spot. (Even in a repetition of the experiments a scar has lately been found at this spot.) — Likewise the failure of regeneration in the other two experiments (removal of the anterior plate and of both plates) finds no explanation in this way, without, however, furnishing an argument against it.

In the case of this second explanation of the described phenomenon, then, a series of imperfections remains, especially since the known heteromorphoses that come into consideration for it furnish no immediate parallel.

Nevertheless, from the present facts no decision can be made between the two explanatory possibilities of the heteromorphosis — either as a proximal regenerate impoverished in potencies, or through the presence of two competing potencies. The transplantation otherwise drawn upon for such purposes promises no success in the case of the external parts of animals with a chitin covering. Also a repetition of the same experiments on the second thoracic stigma cannot be conclusively probative, since here a heteromorphosis at the anterior closure-plate is just as ambiguous as at the first stigma, while from normal regeneration nothing can be concluded here either. The same holds for any possible heteromorphosis of the bulge-plate (posterior plate) of the second thoracic stigma, since the concept "distal" can probably no longer be applied to it with certainty. — More promising is perhaps the following procedure: after (heteromorphotic) regeneration of the posterior closure-plate of the first thoracic stigma, a removal of the anterior plate; if then a re-production of this anterior plate sets in, that signifies for a second time regeneration in the reversed direction, this time already from the regenerated posterior plate. (Regenerating regenerates are also known otherwise, e.g. *Amphiglaena*, Driesch 1906; and *Lumbriculus*, Morgulis 1907.) Presupposed thereby is that the non-regeneration upon simple removal of the anterior plate is also confirmed further on. Under these circumstances, at least in the case of the second regeneration, the possibility of a regeneration in the reversed direction from the remaining plate would be shown. Another way is the removal of merely parts of the closure-plates, in order to test their regeneration also independently of the region of the other (uninjured) closure-plate. But experiments pertaining to this have so far still led to no conclusive result.

## Other examples in *Carausius morosus* itself for axis-reversal and competition of the potencies.

There still remain to be adduced a few occasional observations on double- and malformations on the extremities (leg and antenna) of *Carausius morosus* itself, which furnish a support for the presuppositions of the explanations given of the stigma-plate heteromorphoses. For the first explanation, there must be able to occur a segment with reversed direction of the principal axis — and indeed even between normally oriented parts; the second requires the possibility that at one and the same place two potencies can be present and can outstrip each other in rank.

XII/2 (Fig. 27a): (This case corresponds to No. 145 in Przibram's fracture triple-formation, p. 296, Mocquerys 1880.)

The animal was not fully grown; at the last molt that took place it shed the malformation with the skin. On the left middle leg the first tarsal segment is normal. The second and third segments are very short, but distinctly developed as independent tarsal segments. There follows a large roller-shaped piece that does not have tarsal form. From the distal margin of the third segment and from the following segment it is set off, tapered at both ends, and in the middle it has an incision on the dorsal side, which divides it into two segments and out of which a segment bearing two claws has grown perpendicular to the axis. Both segments of the roller-shaped piece, arising through the incision, are of equal size and equal shape. The ventral side has no little soles. The pilosity, especially developed dorsally, points distalward on the proximal segment, proximalward on the distal one, that is, from both sides toward the claw segment springing from the incision. Upon the roller-shaped piece there follows distally a tarsal segment still to be discussed more closely (with two connected pairs of little soles, whereas normal tarsal segments always have only one pair) and then (5) a normal claw-bearing end segment.

The small lateral branch springing perpendicularly out of the incision of the roller-shaped piece dorsally consists of a single segment, which is distinctly set off proximally by an articular cushion and is thereby characterized as an independent segment, and bears two claws at its distal end. Although it has an abnormal, roller-shaped form and lacks an adhesive pad, one must regard it as a tarsal end segment. Its "volar side" it turns toward the outer (or posterior) side of the leg; its volar plane lies approximately in the dorsoventral plane of the leg. Toward the latter's distal and proximal part it has equal inclination.

Since the whole leg up to the second tarsal segment, and also the end segment, are normally developed, it will not be a matter of a regenerate; there are also, apart from the roller-shaped piece and the lateral branch, five segments developed. To regard it as a regenerate with four segments, in which the third, severed, continues to exist in two parts at both ends of the roller-shaped piece, is not possible because of the size of the end segments. Therefore, as the cause of the slight size and regenerate-like development of the second and third tarsal segments, only a deformation and regeneration of merely the middle tarsal segments will have to be assumed. In tin cages with an overlapping lid, an occasional crushing of the tarsi easily occurs. In this case, in the animal discussed, the end part of the foot probably remained hanging on the leg, and in the healing and regeneration of the shattered middle tarsal segments (2, 3) reduced structures arose. Besides, the crushed mass — supported by chitin — did not perish, but only formed a new skin skeleton. It represents the roller-shaped piece.

So now the proximal three tarsal segments — since the distal parts were indeed still present, but were spatially separated from them by the remains of the crushed parts — could, from the extensive woundspot, furnish for themselves a continuation and completion. On the other hand, however, the distal part still nourished by the animal also had the opportunity to grow out, but on the proximal side and in the reversed direction. The result is the common formation of a claw-bearing end segment in the form of the aforementioned single-segmented lateral branch. In this way the three original tarsal segments springing from the tibia have completed themselves to the number four normal for regenerates, while the fourth segment lying on the distal side has, in the proximal direction, likewise furnished the next distal segment (end segment) coming into question.

Apart from its perpendicular position, the end segment does not, indeed, itself show clearly its descent from two different stumps. It is expressed only in this, that its development is inhibited — a phenomenon which, however, in double-formations occurs not infrequently, perhaps as a consequence of the hindering of two components taking effect at the same places. The form is correspondingly simplified, the claws are crowded close together, and despite their presence the adhesive pad, otherwise running ahead in development in regenerates, has remained absent. (In contrast to XII/1, where on the end segment the components already step apart and an enlargement and supernumeraries make themselves noticeable, and only the middle claw, probably still formed of two coinciding components, shows itself inhibited.)

Much more distinctly than on the end segment itself, however, its descent from two components growing in opposite directions becomes apparent on the fourth segment and on the roller-shaped intermediate piece. It was already mentioned that the direction of the bristles on the two segments of the roller-shaped piece is opposite and that they point from both ends toward the lateral branch. Normally, however, on parts so far distal the bristling is always directed distalward. And most characteristic is the behavior of the fourth, penultimate segment, which stands between the roller-shaped piece and the end segment of the main stem. Here the reversal of the bristling is still more distinctly marked, in that it occurs only at the proximal end. At the distal end the individual bristles point distalward (with respect to the whole leg), and if we follow them further in the proximal direction, they flip over rather abruptly and point proximalward. Besides, the fourth segment is considerably longer on the ventral side than on the dorsal, i.e. it slopes off evenly toward both sides, just as otherwise only toward the distal side, and looks the same in front and behind. We can therefore designate its (with respect to the whole leg) proximal half as a distal end in terms of form-building, and we find correspondingly: on the ventral side also a second pair of little soles at this pseudo-proximal end, whereas otherwise little soles always occur only at the distal end, and we can even recognize the reversed direction of the little soles to some extent by their form.

From this it can be recognized how far the wounding beginning at the first tarsal segment must have reached, namely evidently up to the middle of the fourth tarsal segment.

With this, the whole formation can be understood as follows: as the basis for the formation of the lateral branch, two components directed against each other and at first growing against, finally with, one another are to be assumed, which furnish equivalent structures; the proximal segments (themselves regenerates) furnish, namely, as fourth segment an end segment; while the original fourth segment, after completion of its destroyed proximal half by a reversely directed distal one, must likewise let an end segment follow.

The interesting thing about the fracture triple-formation (and with such a one we have, according to what has been said, to do here) is that the apparently only simple lateral branch is furnished from two spatially completely separated stumps. Thereby not only does the assumption that double-formations often arise through fusions of two components of a fracture triple-formation receive a quite distinct proof, but we recognize here, in an arrangement not usually occurring in other malformations but especially distinct, namely linearly one behind the other, a reversal of the longitudinal axis between correctly oriented axis-parts — in the same way as in the origination of a reversed anterior closure-plate in place of a posterior one in the first thoracic stigma.

### Competition of the potencies in the leg-antenna heteromorphosis.

Further, cases were found from which the presence of different potencies at the same time at the same place emerges more or less distinctly, and which I will here adduce with reference to the stigma-plate heteromorphosis and the corresponding attempt at explanation.

XII/5 (Fig. 28, 28a, 29): The animal is described after the fourth molt (Fig. 28, 29). The peculiar thing about the regenerate is the alternating occurrence of antenna-, leg-, and again antenna-segments on one and the same regenerate of a right antenna. It consists of nine distinctly separated segments with a total length of almost 4 mm. Przibram observed a similar behavior in *Sphodromantis bioculata*; the leg regenerated on the shaft of a cut-off antenna is, at further molts, again displaced by the antennal form.

Recently Borchardt has published cases of *Carausius morosus* in which, likewise after antenna-amputation, mixed formations had appeared which later again approached the antennal form. She connects this with the Johnston's organ situated in the pedicellus, which — in accordance with Herbst's view of the form-determining influence of the ganglia — is supposed to be decisive for the antennal form, and whose merely partial injury she assumes for the explanation of the mixed formations.

Przibram's explanation of the cases of *Sphodromantis* proceeds from the assumption that in the formation of an antenna-leg — as in form-formation generally — several potencies stand in competition with one another, whereby the most rapidly acting one at first determines the form. After removal of the antenna, the leg potency, which is faster in the regenerating animal, overtakes the antennal potency, but is also exhausted earlier (just as, in chemical reactions proceeding side by side, the faster ones exhaust themselves earlier than the more slowly running ones), and at further molts the still unused antennal potency thereby comes anew into effect.

But it is not the individual phases of the origin of this extremity that need interest us for the explanation of the stigma-plate heteromorphosis, but merely the phenomenon that here two potencies compete at all and, according to circumstances, the one or the other comes to effect and, accordingly, the one or the other form comes to realization. And this is shown still more distinctly than by the ordinary leg–antenna regenerates precisely by such a case (occurring in *Carausius morosus* itself), since here, simultaneously on the same extremity and in the same stage, the various results of this competition appear several times. At the very least, however, we must in its case assume more than one potency in the same extremity not only in the scapus and pedicellus, but right out to the last distal segment, not only because in general we find an impoverishment of the potencies only toward this side, [yet] upon the leg segments there still follows an antennal segment, but above all because the leg-antenna, by its approach to the antennal form after the last molt undergone, has shown its antenna-forming capacity even in the tarsus-shaped distal segments.

Therewith, however, the presupposition is fulfilled, that in one organ two potencies can be contained which, at different times or at different stages (at successive molts; above all, however, in regeneration as against embryonic development), can bring about qualitatively different form-formation.

*XII/3:* A formation similar to the animal XII/5 — with only very indistinct leg-character of the middle segments — was found in one of the very rarely occurring males¹. The animal was isolated before the last molt; up to the adult stage the malformation did not change distinctly any more. It was over 5½ cm long (or rather, beforehand, 4½ cm), the malformation standing in place of the right antenna 4½ (or rather, 4) mm.

The two basal segments are developed as in a normal antenna, the second somewhat deformed and distally notched. Since they are not shorter than those of the normal side, the original antenna was probably lost through an injury to the second segment. (Borchardt obtained her transitions between antenna-regenerate and leg-shaped heteromorphosis at a cut between the second and third segments.) The third segment is elongated and gnarled and much too thick for an antenna. The fourth is not very sharply separated from it, equally long, but much thinner and smooth. At the end there is set a forward-and-upward-projecting structure, which also projects somewhat beyond the preceding segment behind below. Its distal part forms a large round soft knob with hairing, but little pigment, and has at its base two small humps. Immediately proximally beneath it there arises an elongate segment of quite the same form as the antenna-like part of the terminal segment of XII/5, only still more slender.

The third and fourth segments are, on account of thickness, length, and form, certainly not antennal segments; as leg segments they are not distinctly expressed, but can (on account of the otherwise regular leg–antenna heteromorphosis) most readily be conceived as such. The knob at the end, together with the two small humps, will represent a tarsal segment, or only its adhesive pad with the rudiments of the claws. The antenna-like terminal segment is quite distinctly developed. Since the doubtful segments are wholly dissimilar to antennal segments, but many transitions between such segments and the leg-form are known, and above all in view of the on the whole very similar structure of XII/5, we can assume leg-character for them, and give a similar interpretation as in XII/5:

The normal abdominal stigma appears externally, as Fig. 32 shows, as a small chitinous plate (peritrema) with an oval hole, in the depth of which the actual stigma-cleft [Stigmenspalt] is closed by two lips that are movable through a special apparatus.

The most stigma-like form (Fig. 33), which was very frequently to be found and showed great regularity in development and position, consisted of an oval hole in the chitinous plate of the head and, lying beneath it, two lips, between which a cleft led into the depth — just as the open window of the peritrema and the two closure-lips do at the abdominal stigma. The direction of the cleft and of the longitudinal extension of the window, too, was almost always similar to that there, slightly inclined from upper-posterior toward lower-anterior; only the lips were more bulging, their sinking-in beneath the rim of the window not always so deep. For the rest, the similarity extended even to the appearance of folds at the rim of the window, which in places led down into the pit, as at the posterior lower rim of the peritrema. Closure-levers were not present. (At the inner closure apparatus of the normal stigma there are two such.) In some cases, where no lips were developed which would close the tube opening there, one saw at the entrance its wall stiffened by regular dense annulation (Fig. 36). Usually the whole formation was considerably larger than an abdominal stigma. Sometimes two such entirely similar formations lay in a line one behind the other (Fig. 34). A twitching or a regular movement was in some cases clearly recognizable, without, however, the cleft opening.

Mostly at least the larger movements ran synchronously with the movements of the mandible of the same side. The connection of this whole formation with the mandible became clear through some such cleft-formations lying farther forward. For it happened, namely, that in the direction of the cleft the whole apparent stigma had advanced up to that point of the mandible at which the opening-muscle attaches to it. Then sometimes the anterior rim of the window tore during the molt, and one saw the cleft and the cleft-lips continue up to the muscle-insertion site.

On the other hand, a connection with the origin of the mandibular muscles reveals itself in that the formation lies at the corresponding place of the head-capsule (or at least follows a line along which the muscles run to the mandible). In some experimental series set up in June and August 1927 I had, after the first or after the second molt, cut out rectangular windows above and behind the eye; they were in part kept until full growing-out, but showed only small inpushings of soft skin at the operation site, or were entirely healed. Only under and in front under the eye had, in the blinded series, organomorphoses sometimes appeared, and indeed, as mentioned, only along the course of the mandibular muscles.

The dissection and the histological investigation showed that the tendon of a mandibular muscle, which otherwise arises immediately behind the eye at the site well characterized by black pigmentation — at the same site where usually the posterior end of the cleft-formation lies — is now, without the mediation of the muscle, grown onto the head-capsule, and leads from there as a flattened tube to the mandible. It opens in the region of the former eye, or under the eye and its regenerate, slit-like toward the outside and simulates the "stigma-cleft"; the lips are the thickened places at which the walls of the tube pass over into the surrounding skin. Since they are connected with the window-rim, behind which they are lodged, only by soft skin, one sees, during the movement of the mandible, the cleft-lips move along with it. The muscle remains preserved and attaches itself, as normally, coming from the wall of the head-capsule, from both sides onto the tendon. Sometimes no external window and no lips are developed, but instead the tendon forms an immediate funnel-like inpushing of the head-wall.

Funnel-like inpushings caused by tendons are found, besides in the normal animal (foot-tendon [Fußsehne]), also in the soft-skinned region at regenerated thoracic stigmata, when the stigma-muscles abnormally attach at places not preformed for that. Only here the depression passes all around into uniform soft skin, whereas there it is sharply set off against the surrounding head-capsule wall.

The essential similarity between the stigma-pit of the abdominal stigma and the operatively produced organomorphose consists in this, that in both cases a tube opening at the body surface (trachea; hollow tendon) is closed by two lips, which are movably mounted under an oval window of the outer chitinous wall.

A formation similar to the one appearing in place of the eye seems also to be present in the forelegs of *Sphodromantis bioculata* described by PRZIBRAM, wherefore the interpretation of the author as a doubling [Doppelbildung] of joint-facets seems questionable. ## Summary.

Three circumstances — the appearance of a formation externally similar to an abdominal stigma at the wound after removal of the eye; the negative outcome of a regeneration experiment undertaken in consequence thereof upon removal of an entire thoracic stigma closure-apparatus; and the observation of an enantiomorphy in structure between the closure-apparatuses of the first and second thoracic stigma of the same body-side — gave occasion, in *Carausius (Dixippus) morosus* BR., to an experimental series on the regeneration of individual parts of the closure-apparatus, which promised an insight into the developmental-mechanical behavior of such half-symmetrical formations.

Each stigma itself consists of two enantiomorphic closure-plates, dissimilar to one another in certain characters. The second thoracic stigma is as a whole enantiomorphic to the first, so that the plates appear reversed and interchanged.

1. Three experimental series on stigma-regeneration were set up, namely at the first thoracic stigma:

After removal of the whole first thoracic stigma (excision), nothing was regenerated.

After removal of the anterior closure-plate of the first thoracic stigma, likewise nothing was regenerated, and, as in the preceding experiment, the cleft was closed.

After removal of the posterior closure-plate of the first thoracic stigma, there follows, in varying degree, either

a) Reduction of the whole stigma: the retained parts of the closure-apparatus including a special section of the pronotum (suspension-arch [Aufhängebogen]) serving for its suspension can be more or less completely reduced, the stigma-cleft can close; or

b) Transformation of neighboring parts: if the cleft remains open, then, besides the reduction of the suspension-arch, a transformation of the anterior closure-plate follows, whereby it loses characters that had stood in connection with the suspension and the posterior closure-plate, and that in part had distinguished the anterior closure-plate as such over against the posterior; and

c) Heteromorphotic regeneration of the posterior closure-plate, in that in its place a formation appears with characters which normally belong exclusively to the anterior closure-plate (bulge-shaped rim toward the stigma-cleft with sensory pegs [Sinnesstiftchen]); the cleft remains open. There thus now lies, opposite the original anterior closure-plate, a mirror-image-reversed "anterior" closure-plate, at the place where formerly the posterior one was situated. (This plate-regenerate also corresponds — apart from the segment — in position and differentiations to the posterior closure-plate of the II. thoracic stigma.) The regenerate can also appear as a doubling, so that three (similar) plates surround the cleft. For the explanation of the heteromorphose can be drawn upon

either reversal of form-formation in the longitudinal axis (*proximal regeneration*); the anterior closure-plate forms in the caudal direction its mirror-image, which here appears inserted, with reversed axis-direction, between parts with normal axis-direction,

or the presence of two *competing form-formation potencies* for the region of the posterior closure-plate, whereby in ontogeny and regeneration in each case a different one can come to effect.

2. Correspondingly, some doublings and heteromorphoses were observed at other parts of *Carausius morosus* among the non-operated material, which can be traced back to the same developmental-mechanical principles as the heteromorphose of the stigma-plates:

at the tarsus of various legs supernumerary side-branches, which themselves represent fusions of two components; they seem to consist each of a distal regenerate and a proximal regenerate fused with it (fracture-triple-formations [Bruch-Dreifachbildungen], *proximal regeneration*). Therein parts with reversed axis-direction can be inserted, in proximo-distal succession, between such with normal axis-direction;

in place of the antennal formations, in which not only members with leg-character follow upon the antenna-shaped basal members, but at the tip antenna-form again appears, which points to the presence of two competing potencies in the same body-part. A progressive reduction toward the antenna-form (PRZIBRAM 1917) could here too again be observed.

3. Occasionally, in another experimental series, in place of a cut-out eye or ventral to its regenerate, one to two formations had arisen, which are externally similar to an abdominal stigma. They are explained by the growing-on of the hollow tendon of one of the mandibular muscles onto the wall of the head-capsule, whereby the tendon-tube is closed off toward the outside by two movable lips, which, under the window of the body-covering that persists after the operation and rounds itself oval, simulate the lips of the similarly sunken-in stigma.

4. Besides these formations, or independently, some eyes removed by excision (as in HLOBIL's experiments with cauterization, 1925) were regenerated, whereby the regeneration proved itself to be independent of whether the ganglion had been removed or retained.
