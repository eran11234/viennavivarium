## I. Introduction.

In the entire animal kingdom an order could hardly recur whose representatives are suited to the experimental testing of the theories of development and descent in the most far-reaching measure as are the urodele Amphibia. Their capacity for adaptation is so great that, defying even the most abnormal conditions of life, they gradually alter their habits, their reproduction, their form and color. For the carrying out of such alterations, however, in many cases not even several generations are required; rather, these occur already within the life of one and the same individual.

One should now expect that, with the recognition of this fact, the experimental morphology and developmental history of the urodeles must have found manifold treatment and yielded numerous results. This, however, has hitherto not been the case; rather, the foundational experiments of Marie v. Chauvin on Amblystoma mexicanum Cope [8]¹, Salamandra atra Laurenti [9] and Proteus anguinus Laurenti [10, 11] still stand today almost alone.

In particular, the investigations of the named researcher »On the adaptive capacity of the larvae of Salamandra atra« [9] have apparently found no emulators at all; at least not a single later report has penetrated into the literature from which it would emerge that even tentatively an attempt had been made to repeat her experiments and to bring about similar results.

Two circumstances evidently bear the blame for this conspicuous restraint: on the one hand, the keeping and breeding of living animals is carried on either only for practical purposes, or by private persons out of fondness, or finally in zoological gardens and aquarium-show establishments; in the first two cases a lack of corresponding theoretical training, in the last case a lack of the time required for the close study of individual objects, prevents the scientific exploitation of the observations gained from living animal material. On the other hand, one rather expects, precisely in the case of the mentioned experiments on urodeles, to find more obstacles than actually present themselves in the carrying-out. A reading of v. Chauvin's writings is indeed apt to heighten this expectation; the authoress is often able to report difficulties which — if at all — can be overcome only by »laborious measures demanding the greatest care and attention«. Meanwhile, many of those difficulties, thanks to the advances of modern aquarium and terrarium technique, are today no longer present, or at least more easily eliminated than at that time. It shall not thereby be denied that, for the success of the experiments, care in the setting-up and punctuality in the tending of the experimental animals are unconditionally necessary.

Encouraged by manifold successes in the field of animal husbandry, I undertook the venture of following the paths blazed by Fräulein v. Chauvin and, where possible, of extending them still further. In particular, the question interested me whether it might not be possible, by such an experimental path (after v. Chauvin's pattern), to discover, in closely related forms, the influences which cause the transformation of species.

Two such closely related urodele forms are now given in the two European species of the genus Salamandra, the spotted fire salamander (Salamandra maculosa Laurenti) and the black alpine salamander (Salamandra atra Laurenti); although both are very sharply distinguished from one another by a series of morphological characters, by their differing geographical — in particular vertical — distribution, as well as most of all by their mode of reproduction, the assumption of an immediate relationship lies nonetheless so close, on various grounds, namely on account of the anatomical structure that agrees so well despite all outer differences, that numerous, more or less far-reaching conjectures about this are quite frequent in the literature.

As regards the differing geographical distribution, this speaks rather for than against the close relationship of the two forms: it is a phenomenon documentable by countless examples in the animal kingdom that very closely related species can indeed be neighbors in geographical respect — as is the case with our salamanders — but nonetheless strictly exclude one another and at most exceptionally are to be met with intermingled at one and the same locality. This finds its explanation, on the one hand, in the fact that in the same region, under the same conditions, there is in general no occasion for a change of form, and that an originally quite different disposition (not present in close relatives) must already be given for different species to develop; on the other hand, because, of two or more closely related species, one suffices to play, in one and the same region, the role devolving upon it in the household of nature (often obscure to us), wherefore the species in question also for the most part mutually contend with one another, and as a further consequence the weaker, displaced by the stronger, must emigrate from less favorable areas and there adapt themselves to the new conditions.

Most strongly the difference in the reproduction of the two salamander species appears. Salamandra maculosa, as is known, brings forth a considerable number of young, according to Freyer [28] up to 72, into the world, and these are deposited in the water as gill-bearing larvae, where they develop further, in order finally to lose the gills, to crawl onto land, and thus transform themselves into the finished land salamander. Otherwise with Salamandra atra: here too an approximately equally great number of eggs as in Maculosa passes from the ovaries into the oviducts, where indeed they become fertilized, but only one single egg on each side develops into an embryo, which undergoes the entire further development and transformation within the maternal fruit-holders and is born only as a finished land salamander; all the remaining eggs do not develop further after the first cleavage stages, but flow together into a yolk-ball and serve the embryo as nourishment during its intra-uterine life.

With a view to the nearly equal number of eggs which in the ovaries of both salamander species attain ripeness at each ovulation and pass over into the oviducts, those seemingly abruptly contrasting differences could not, however, shake the assumption of a close relationship between Salamandra maculosa and atra. Rather, already v. Schreibers, the discoverer of the reproductive conditions of Salamandra atra [55, 56], recognized in this nothing other than an adaptation phenomenon on the part of the latter, the species occurring in the high mountains, to the harsher conditions of life prevailing there, in particular to the lack of suitable waters for depositing the larvae. A view here and there obscurely indicated in the literature, but nowhere established in a satisfactory manner, goes still further, in that it asserts that Salamandra atra descends directly from Maculosa and — as Schwalbe [57] more precisely expresses it — represents a »specifically fixed stunted form« (Kümmerform) of the latter.

Schwalbe has occupied himself in detail with the question of the relationship of the two species and has endeavored to bring this question, as well as the question of the »processes« which gave occasion to the transformation of species, closer by statistical means, while M. v. Chauvin had taken the experimental path. Both, however, agree as regards their method in this — that, for the attainment of the goal, they found it above all necessary to demonstrate that the abrupt differences in the reproduction of the two species are not differences of principle, but only of degree.

The two works of M. v. Chauvin and Schwalbe on this subject have become foundational for my own investigations. It was my idea that both the experimental and the statistical method, independently of one another, must yield the same theoretical results and justify the same conclusions. To the experiments of M. v. Chauvin I could link in so far as the authoress draws only the one species, Salamandra atra, into the circle of her considerations; to the more recent work of Schwalbe's in so far as he expresses the much-promising character of the statistical method, applied to the special case of the two salamander species, properly only at the conclusion, in the form of a hypothesis, and bases it upon the results he had obtained regarding the nourishment of the embryos.

The biological experiments necessary for the working-out of the experimental part of my work reach back into the year 1891; they were carried out in my room-terraria and -aquaria — to be described in the technical section — and continued with slight interruptions up to the present day; in part they were repeated during the winter semester 1902/1903 in the II. Zoological Institute of the Imperial-Royal University in Vienna, as well as in the newly arisen Biological Experimental Station (Vienna, Prater, Vivarium building), and there provisionally concluded in March 1903. — The anatomical investigations necessary for the working-out of the statistical part were begun on a large material (to be demonstrated in the following section) in the year 1898 — apart from earlier, isolated dissections — and were carried out partly privately, partly in the II. Zoological Institute, partly finally during my study-sojourn at the Imperial-Royal Zoological Station in Trieste in the autumn of 1902. They were concluded, so far as the present work is concerned, in July 1903 in Appenzell.

## III. Technique of keeping and rearing.

For the success of the experiments described in the present work, it does not suffice to be supplied amply and often with fresh material; rather, a not inconsiderable part of the old experimental animals must constantly be offered such favourable conditions of life that, even after years, they are in a state of health which differs in nothing from that in free nature. To see the animals thus thriving, feeding, indeed even regularly proceeding to reproduction, and to preserve for them their native power of resistance in such a way that, exposed to various abnormal influences, they do not perish but adapt themselves to the new conditions — all this is possible only with an uncommonly careful and appropriate tending, namely in containers whose internal arrangement comes as close as possible to the conditions in nature, in free life. Let me therefore be permitted to devote a broader space to the section "Technique" than is otherwise customary and necessary in scientific works. The more detailed treatment of this section may here prove all the less superfluous, since opportunity is after all to be given to others to check my experiments and to carry their results further.

Adult earth-salamanders [Erdsalamander = land/fire salamanders] are best housed in terraria. Since I kept the material strictly separated according to locality and vertical altitude of the find-spot, several of those room-glasshouses were always stocked with salamanders.

If it is only a question of preserving, in a suitable manner, material intended for anatomical purposes for a longer time — that is, for several months — then for this purpose simple terrarium containers are very much to be recommended, such as I used in the 2nd Zoological Institute of the Vienna University: these are 100 cm long, 60 cm broad and 60 cm high; built of concrete and closed above with an overlapping, removable lid of wire mesh, only the one long wall facing the observer consists of a (withdrawable) glass pane, which, however, does not reach quite down to the floor but ends 25 cm above it; the remaining part of the front wall, between glass pane and floor, is made up of a 10 cm high sheet-metal strip and a 15 cm high strip of wire mesh.

The space up to the height of the sheet-metal strip (10 cm) serves for the introduction of the floor covering — for Salamandra maculosa simply fresh forest moss, for atra moistened loam soil with turf cushions on top of it. The wire-mesh strip provides for direct ventilation of the moss or turf surface, for which the mesh lid does not suffice. That the container, with the exception of its front wall, consists of concrete offers two advantages: it receives only subdued light, and the temperature, even in high summer, remains tolerably cool. The arrangement is completed by a shallow water basin as well as by several randomly scattered stones and pieces of bark offering hiding places.

The tending in such a terrarium is confined to keeping the water in the water basin always clean and fresh, sprinkling the moss or turf cover daily by means of an atomizer, and renewing it should it, despite the sprinkling, no longer show its original fresh-green colour. Now and then a handful of earthworms is thrown into the container.

On journeys I have often employed even simpler prisons, e.g. wooden boxes and earthenware troughs, with good use. Unsuitable are only such containers as consist wholly or for the greatest part of metal, like the not infrequently used zinc boxes; these are subject to considerable temperature fluctuations and, especially for earth-salamanders during the summer, much too hot. Earthenware vessels, however, closed above by a wire mesh, as well as wooden vessels provided with a few grated windows and covered with a glass pane, are suited in all forms and sizes quite well for the keeping of the investigation material, provided they are furnished with an internal arrangement such as described above. — Wooden boxes, loosely filled to the rim with fresh, moist moss, also serve furthermore for the transport of the living material, whether as hand luggage or by postal or rail consignment.

Considerably more care is required by the treatment of salamanders intended for biological experiments. For their complete, years-long well-being the introduction of living foliage plants is indispensable; therefore the containers too must satisfy two requirements which need not be fulfilled in the case of the mere storage magazines: those must, firstly, be accessible to light from all sides and, secondly — on account of the watering of the plants — be provided with a well-functioning device for drainage of the soil. In general I followed in this the instructions of v. FISCHER [20] and ZERNECKE [76], but with manifold modifications, which relate especially to the not exactly undemanding earth-salamanders.

The framework of the four-cornered box-terraria, made to my own specifications, consists of iron, which is sufficiently protected against rusting by an inner and outer, repeated oil-paint coating. On all four walls there are glass panes, but only on one long and one broad side undivided, while one half each of the two other sides is faced with wire mesh and at the same time arranged as a door. The roof consists of not too close-meshed wire lattice and is wholly removable. The floor is especially load-bearing and slopes slightly on all sides towards the centre, where it opens into a short discharge pipe; it accordingly has the form of a shallow funnel. On account of this floor form, the terrarium comes to stand on a table whose top is sawn out except for the edges, where the periphery of the terrarium rests. In the middle, beneath the table, on the floor, a tub receives permanent placement, into which the ground water of the terrarium collected in the discharge pipe drips.

The dimensions of the salamander-houses may vary according to the number of captives, but should keep approximately to the ratio of length, breadth and height of 8 : 6 : 5.

For the table together with the terrarium, a bright but shady standing place is to be chosen.

The internal arrangement is first of all devoted to the drainage device: the discharge pipe is covered with a few potsherds; on these comes to lie a layer of coarse gravel, then a second layer of fine gravel. A third layer consists of earth, and indeed both salamander species thrive by far the best on yellow loam soil.

Then one proceeds to the setting-up of the water basin. As such, the most advantageous is a rectangular, earthenware, porous dish, which may take up about a quarter of the total floor area of the terrarium; its length is made equal to the breadth of the terrarium, so that it can be pushed close against one broad side of the latter; its height should be 6 cm. Into the bottom of the dish an opening is bored, from which a separate discharge pipe, grated on the inside and provided on the outside with a tap, leads through the terrarium floor. Onto the edges of the dish, pieces of tuff are cemented, which makes the climbing in and out easier for the animals, as well as making the business of reproduction more comfortable for the pregnant females of Salamandra maculosa, which like to wedge their trunk between stones for the mechanical facilitation of giving birth. The bottom of the water basin is finally covered with small, clean-washed brook pebbles, to which a few coarser, randomly distributed stones are added.

For the completion of the terrarium arrangement, there now remain only the hiding places and the plants. Here one must proceed differently for Salamandra maculosa and atra.

For the former species, pieces of bark, laid with their hollow side onto the ground, serve as hiding places; in large containers, additionally, a rotten, hollow tree stump, which is posted in a corner of the background. — For the latter species one chooses, more in keeping with nature, flat stones, which are partly pressed singly onto the ground at various places, partly — likewise in a corner of the background — assembled into a larger rock group, and in this way form hollows between one another.

The plants one can either cultivate in garden pots and sink these up to the rim into the soil filling, or else set them — which holds especially for turf-forming growths — freely into the earth; to the infertile loam soil, in this case, some black garden or forest earth is admixed at the places concerned. One must beware of planting too densely, because this impairs the survey and easy accessibility of the whole; in any case a roomy clearing must be left free of plants in the foreground.

For Salamandra maculosa, ferns are to be preferred to all other plants; most successfully I have grown Scolopendrium vulgare Sm., Osmunda regalis L., Blechnum spicant L. and Asplenium ruta muraria L. (the last rampant in crevices of the tree stump). Of phanerogams I used, at most, on account of the broad leaves, Tussilago Farfara L., Cyclamen europaeum L. and Hedera helix L. (the latter clothing the tree stump with a dense net of tendrils). All these plants of the fire-salamander terrarium are very enduring and easy to procure.

Much more difficult was the selection for Salamandra atra, since I wished to use only such plants as occur in the open with a certain regularity at the abodes of the alpine salamander. Of these, however, most are not obtainable in commerce and are hard to accustom to room air; a further difficulty lies still in the short growing period of the high-alpine flora. — After many a failed attempt I finally brought together a number of species whose cultivation in the terrarium is possible; the requirement I covered by gathering with my own hand. As regards the brevity of the vegetation period, the alpine plants were in this very respect more adaptable than I might have expected, in that, namely, most of them — probably chiefly in consequence of the higher temperatures — withdrew much later in autumn and began to sprout again earlier in spring than is the case in the open. Thus, for example, the growing period of the dwarf-sandwort (Cherleria sedoides L.), which in the open lasts only from July to September, was prolonged in the terrarium from May to October inclusive. To these successes the application of nutrient solutions must have contributed not insignificantly. For the alpine-salamander terrarium I recommend, besides the plant already named, the following: Botrychium lunaria L., Selaginella selaginoides L. (turf-forming!), Lycopodium Selago L., Empetrum nigrum L., Draba aizoides L., Silene acaulis L. and Saxifraga Burseriana L. (the two latter turf-forming), furthermore, for clefts between the rock, Sempervivum montanum L., Sedum alpestre Vill. and Daphne petraea Leyb., which spins about the rock group in the background with a net of tendrils and at the same time lends it the necessary firmness.

Now that, after the installation of the water basin, the hiding places, and the plants, the bare soil still comes to light, in the fire-salamander terrarium it is to be clothed with a **moss cover** (various foliage mosses are recommended, such as *Hypnum filicinum* and *molluscum*), and in the alpine-salamander terrarium with a turf cushion consisting of short-stalked grasses of the alpine pastures.

The fully furnished container is now set up in a cool place which, with a view to the thriving of the plants, should indeed be light, but at the same time thoroughly shady. As a population, up to 100 head of *Salamandra atra*, or up to 60 head of *maculosa*, per square meter are permissible.

It is not out of the question that even a far more primitive arrangement than the one just described will bring its successes; but if one wishes afterwards to be sure of one's ground in the experiments, and if the capacity of the experimental animals to adapt themselves to unaccustomed conditions is not to suffer, then it is to be advised that each of the aforementioned points be fulfilled with conscientiousness.

The care and maintenance of the furnished, planted terraria populated with salamanders turns out to be exceedingly simple:

1) The water basin is emptied completely once daily by opening the drain tap and is refilled fresh; the new water should have the same temperature as the old, or at most (in high summer) be a few degrees cooler.

2) The moss cover, or turf cover respectively, as well as the rest of the vegetation, is sprinkled once daily by means of an atomizer; for the sprinkling — which is best carried out early in the morning or in the evening — one should use rainwater, but, in the absence of it, distilled water which one has beforehand aerated quite copiously for several hours by means of compressed air. The temperature of this water should be the same as the current temperature of the water basin.

3) The feeding is done in such a way that from time to time a number of medium-sized earthworms are released into the terrarium. The salamanders know how to find these, even though they at once creep away. A warning must, however, be given against an earthworm species living chiefly in refuse heaps (*Allolobophora foetida* Dugès), which, when roughly touched, lashes about like mad and which is therefore, and still more on account of its yellowish, foul-smelling body-cavity fluid that simultaneously exudes between the segments, not taken, or taken only reluctantly, by the animals. — For variety in the diet it is good occasionally to put a handful of field slugs (*Limax agrestis* L.) into the container and to set up larvae of the mealworm beetle (*Tenebrio molitor* L.) therein in small, shallow porcelain dishes.

4) The cleaning is restricted to an occasional polishing of the glass panes and the removal of any dead plant parts. The animals deposit their excrement regularly into the water basin, which, as stated, must be refilled fresh daily, and so a thorough cleaning involving an elaborate refurnishing of the whole container can be dispensed with for years on end, and is necessary only in the case of unforeseen disturbances, e.g. epidemics.

5) With the onset of winter, it is best to place the entire terrarium — without altering the least thing in its arrangement — in an unheated, half-dark room that is, however, inaccessible to frost (cellar!), whereupon the plants withdraw and the salamanders creep even deeper than usual, in order to lapse into lethargy until the early spring — unless one wishes, for particular purposes, to keep a portion of the experimental animals awake, in which case the terrarium naturally remains in a moderately heated room.

The measures hitherto have applied to the adult salamanders; but some instruction is still necessary for the care of the larvae and the rearing of the young, just-transformed salamanders.

Quite young salamanders and the larvae deposited into the water basin must first be separated, since otherwise they are eaten up by the older ones.

The larvae — and indeed both those brought to the light of day by natural birth and those brought by operation — are best kept in large, shallow porcelain dishes without any furnishing, apart from a few flat stones which one places inside and out of which, by suitable grouping, one creates on the one hand dark hiding places under water, and on the other hand — by letting the stone groups project above the surface — opportunities for leaving the water during the period of metamorphosis. This is said on the assumption that the larvae are to develop and transform normally; if, on the contrary, they are selected for experiments — if, for instance, the transformation period is to undergo some shifting or other — then the choice of the receptacles and their arrangement will have to be a different one. But of this in the two following sections!

A frequent change of water is not necessary, indeed not at all to be recommended; only when the water is cloudy or shows a deposit at the bottom is it to be drawn off by means of a siphon and replaced by new [water] of the same, or (in high summer) a few degrees cooler temperature. If, on the other hand, one has at one's disposal a water supply of an approximately constant temperature of not below 10° C., then it is best to set up the basins for continuous through-flow.

In the case of a heavy population of the basins, and on the occasion of various experiments, an aeration of the water is indicated. For this purpose I used the SIMON aeration apparatus for compressed air. It consists essentially of an air pump, an air reservoir, and outflow bodies. The reservoir bears a spring manometer as well as several connecting tubes for rubber hoses, which establish the connection on the one hand to the air pump, on the other hand to the outflow bodies. As such, the ZWIES ring-aerators are unsurpassed, since by no other system is so fine a distribution of the air bubbles possible. Should one occasionally wish for larger air bubbles, it suffices to screw the closely interfitting, somewhat grooved metal rings more loosely by means of the appropriate adjusting key. The rings can also be taken completely apart for the purpose of cleaning. — The intensity of the outflow is regulated by a reducing valve.

For the feeding of the larvae I like best to use a small, red freshwater annelid (*Tubifex rivulorum* d'Ud.), in an emergency also cut-up earthworms and fibers of lean beef, the latter of which one must hold close in front of the larvae's snout by means of a fine pair of forceps. Water insects and crustaceans are taken less willingly and die off easily, whereby occasion is given for rapid spoiling of the water and the formation of fungus.

The young, just-transformed salamanders are reared in terraria which are furnished similarly to those intended for the adult salamanders, but are on the whole simpler and smaller. For nourishment the same food animals serve as for the old ones, but on a reduced scale (small earthworms, small slugs, small beetle larvae). The feeding consequently turns out often quite laborious. Since the searching-out of the tiniest mealworm-beetle larvae from the cultures is too time-consuming, it is advisable to establish, alongside, also a culture of *Alphitobius diaperinus* Pz., which likewise belongs to the Tenebrionidae but is much smaller. Besides this, the young salamanders can be accustomed to a substitute food in the form of raw meat fibers, which one offers them skewered on a thin wire and moves gently to and fro before their eyes. The wire is, as soon as the little animal has seized the morsel, withdrawn by a cautious twist.

The description of the experiments in the two following sections will yet repeatedly give occasion for special technical remarks. On the other hand, it may already be in place here, in so far as it appears necessary, to enter upon the **pathology of the salamanders**.

As regards first the adult salamanders, these suffer in captivity preponderantly from two diseases, both of which occur epidemically. The one manifests itself in an abnormally strong secretion of the **glandular secretions** and simultaneous violent **cramps**, in which the whole body of the animal curves together so far dorsalward that head and tail-tip touch one another. Cause of the disease: the poisonous effect of the glandular secretions in the case of massive, close penning-together of the material: the amphibians are namely by no means, as one might expect, immune to the skin secretions of other individuals of their own species. Other substances too, e.g. strychnine (cf. FISCHER-SIGWART [21]) and the for salamanders extremely pernicious sodium chloride, brought into contact with the skin, produce the same disease-manifestations. Cures I achieved — making use of an experience of FISCHER-SIGWART's [21] — by washings of the diseased animal in ice-cold water; I held it directly under the water tap, whereupon the cramps usually ceased after a few minutes and the animal was seized by such lassitude that it lay motionless. In this condition I buried it in fresh, moist garden soil, so that only its head looked out. In this way I succeeded in saving several valuable experimental animals. The preventive means is, of course, as sparse as possible a stocking of the terraria and especially of the transport crates. — The second disease consists in the appearance of **ulcerous wounds**, most frequently at the edges of the jaws, on the throat, on the crown of the head, at the tips of the toes, in the region of the anus, and at the tip of the tail. The ulcers spread rapidly and extend not only over the surface of the skin but also penetrate into the depth and devour all soft parts, so that the bone comes to light. The extremities and the tail sometimes rot away literally, so that at the death of the animal only stumps remain. The disease can derive from two causes standing in direct opposition to one another: either the terrarium lacks the necessary air and soil moisture, in which case the skin becomes brittle and cracks open; the slightest contamination of the raw spots thus arising then suffices to transform these into ulcers. Or the terrarium is excessively wet, the water basin unclean, the air spoiled, stuffy, with numerous decaying plant remains and other putrefying matter present at the same time; here too wounds form and pass over at once into suppuration. A sure prospect of healing lies in **antiseptic treatment** of the wounds; these namely heal very quickly as soon as their ulcerous character has been taken from them. At the first appearance of the ulcers, brushing with 5% boric or carbolic acid suffices, indeed even with 50% alcohol, especially when the drying process is accelerated by sprinkling with salicyl powder. But even larger wounds scar over quickly with daily repeated brushing with lignosulfite, to which, in obstinate cases, dabbing with iodine tincture may be added. If, however, the ulcers have already attacked noble organs or devoured whole limbs, then of course every attempt at healing is in vain. The preventive means are naturally strict cleanliness of the container, especially of the water basin, as well as moderate moisture and good ventilation.

Of other disease-manifestations I frequently observed the appearance of **entoparasites**. At times the gut of *Salamandra maculosa* is crammed full with a *Distomum* species. In the gut of *Salamandra atra* I once ascertained microscopically small **nematodes**, which were boring through the walls of the cloaca and were probably about to migrate from here to other parts of the body. Particular disease-symptoms I could not perceive in the salamanders afflicted by parasites.

In the **salamander larvae** I observed two kinds of diseases, of which the larvae of *Salamandra maculosa* are visited only rarely, but the much more sensitive larvae of *atra*, obtained by artificial premature birth, on the contrary quite frequently. The one of these diseases corresponds, in its outer cause and its symptoms, exactly with the so-called "swim-bladder paralysis" of fishes: if, on the occasion of a change of water, one proceeds incautiously, so that the new water is considerably colder than the old, then the animals lose not only the capacity to rise and sink in the water and to maintain themselves suspended in it (a capacity which healthy larvae very well possess!), but they forfeit altogether every equilibrium and hang about in all manner of unstable positions, mostly in the vicinity of the water surface, occasionally also at the bottom. In this connection it must, however, be emphasized that it is not, say, the static sense that is affected, for the animals evidently feel their abnormal position and strive vigorously to regain the normal posture; they only then become quiet when they are exhausted by the many fruitless efforts. The only preventive means rests, of course, once again upon avoidance of the cause of the disease: the abrupt change of temperature. But if the mishap has nevertheless once occurred, then the healing usually sets in of itself, in the course of several days, after the water has warmed up again to the original temperature. — The similarity of this disease to swim-bladder paralysis first brought me to the thought whether it might not be traceable to internal organ disturbances analogous to the latter, and whether the **lung of the Urodela**, as long as gill-breathing predominates in them, might not exercise the function of a swim-bladder. In point of fact — as was also noticed by v. SCHREIBERS [56] in olms and in partially neotenic larvae of *Salamandra maculosa* — the lungs of the gill-breathing Urodela, larval forms as well as Perennibranchiates, are not so cellular as those of the air-breathing forms, but smooth-walled and — as soon as they have at all attained a certain development — quite frequently distended taut with air. Yet I permit myself in this regard no conclusive judgment, since my investigations in that direction are still in progress.

The second disease of the salamander larvae is of ectoparasitic nature and consists in the appearance of the **water-mold** (*Saprolegnia*). Vigorous specimens are attacked by it only exceptionally and only in heavily contaminated basins, but weakly or injured individuals very easily; in particular, the exceedingly delicate embryonic gills of *Salamandra atra*, which become brittle in the water, are a fertile nutrient soil for those Oomycetes. Still more inclined to it are those larvae on which, for the purpose of regeneration experiments, an amputation has been carried out. Any raw body spots cover themselves always first with the whitish-gray fungal turf, which then spreads further only from here onward. — Already M. v. CHAUVIN became acquainted with this disease in her experimental animals and suffered through it grievous losses. In vain she sought a means capable of rendering the fungi harmless. "Antiseptic means," writes the [female] researcher on p. 348 [9], "such as salicylic acid and spirit of wine, seemed to me too severe, and so I tried to combat the evil by reducing the temperature of the water." "By means of ice I cooled it down to 6° R. and then placed the larva into it." "On the following day all the fungi were destroyed and removed from the larva." — In and of itself, then, there would be nothing to object to against the use of cold water (and of diligent water-changing in general); for another reason, however, this means is not to be recommended, despite its one-sided success, and indeed for the reason that the larvae do not tolerate an abrupt change of temperature. If they are subjected to such, then namely there usually appear at once the paralysis-manifestations described above. On the other hand, I found two other, likewise non-antiseptic means excellently suited for the healing of the saprolegnia disease: inasmuch as cold water is richer in air than warm water, but air-rich water is uncongenial to the saprolegnias, both means rest in part upon the same principle as that recommended by M. v. CHAUVIN. Since, however, in addition the bright daylight, possibly in combination with direct, not too hot sunbeams, acts inhibitingly upon the growth of the saprolegnias, I sought to combine both factors, air and light, in the cure.

1) At the windows I always have a number of aquaria standing, in which, thanks to the ample illumination and insolation, a luxuriant algal vegetation has developed. For the present purpose, those vessels in particular come into consideration whose water, in consequence of countless, freely floating algae (predominantly *Chlorella*, *Raphidium*, and *Scenedesmus*), has become quite light-green and almost opaque. Into such vessels I now placed the larvae diseased with *Saprolegnia*. Already after a few hours I observed that small gas bubbles (which evidently contain pure oxygen produced by the algae) had attached themselves in such quantities to all body parts of the animals, especially to those most strongly attacked by the saprolegnias, that the latter were thereby lifted up to the water surface and, in spite of all exertions, could not submerge. Within a few days I could always confidently transfer the larvae back into their original dwelling-containers: the fungal turf had completely fallen off, and no trace of it was any longer to be noticed. This means was applied with the same success and the same accompanying phenomena by MARSSON [44] against various ectoparasitic fish diseases.

2) In the temporary absence of green algal water, I placed the larvae into high, cylindrical glass vessels of small diameter, set these in the brightest spot at the window, and let the Simon aeration-apparatus work. The metal rings of the Zwies aeration-body I did not in this case screw quite tightly together, so that the escaping air-bubbles were of moderate size and the water was set into rather vigorous whirling motion. The larvae were of course carried along by the whirl and, since the vessel was comparatively narrow, were constantly bathed by the air-stream. Presently an immense quantity of tiny gas-pearls (particles of the bubbles coming from the aeration-body) settled here too over the whole body of the larvae; yet it took some days longer before they had destroyed the fungal turf, presumably because the gases here brought to application were atmospheric air (not pure oxygen). In consequence of the fact that the larvae were for days on end exposed to a fairly vigorous water-whirl, and thus withdrawn from gravity, there showed itself in them a transient suspension of the static sense, in that they remained quietly at rest in any position whatever. On the advice of Prowazek [48], who has observed concordant phenomena in *Maculosa*-larvae, I made application of this method in experiments on lens-regeneration, which succeeded repeatedly in *Maculosa*, but in *Atra* — as may be remarked here at once in anticipation — have up to now yielded a completely negative result.

Besides the two biological remedies already described, in more advanced cases I tried, with frequently favourable success, still another antiseptic, namely hypermanganate of potash (potassium permanganate), which the fish-breeders too employ against *Saprolegnia* (cf. Marsson [45]). After many preliminary experiments, in which I deliberately infected larvae of *Salamandra maculosa* with *Saprolegnia*, I arrived at the result that, of this species, an 8-per-million solution (8 mg of the salt to 1 litre of water), and by the *Atra*-larvae a 6-per-million solution, was well tolerated. Both solutions sufficed to destroy the densest fungal turf, provided the larvae were placed every two hours for a quarter-hour each time into a potash-bath of the said concentration. During the bath the original dwelling-vessels are thoroughly cleaned with hot water and a disinfectant. This procedure is continued as often as until the *Saprolegnia* are completely eliminated; on average three to four potash-baths suffice for the cure.

## IV. Experiments on Salamandra atra.

In order now to approach more closely the question as to the laws in accordance with which, in the special case of *Salamandra atra* and *maculosa*, the transformation of species was accomplished, it is first of all a matter of demonstrating by the experimental way that in the species named first we may discern, in its form of propagation which deviates so greatly from that of the latter, in fact only an adaptation-phenomenon.

Two moments allow this to appear from the outset as probable: 1) the fact, already mentioned in the Introduction, that in both species approximately the same number of ripe ovarian eggs reaches the oviducts before each gestation-period, — 2) the circumstance that the two embryos of *Salamandra atra* alone destined for development are, during their intra-uterine life, distinguished by the possession of mightily developed gills, which indeed, according to the biogenetic fundamental law, points to the fact that these organs in an earlier epoch of time served the same purpose as do still today those of the *Maculosa*-larvae, namely water-breathing.

Already v. Schreibers [56] and Czermak [13] did not let themselves be content with these two moments, but had recourse to experiment, in that they (and indeed with favourable success, so far as can be gathered from the brief indications concerning it) reared the embryos of *Salamandra atra* outside the maternal body. Unfortunately one misses in the two authors named a closer exposition of the observations made in the course of the experiment; v. Schreibers does not even mention whether he had reared the embryos taken from the uterus in the water or on dry land. The latter conception of the passage in question finds itself, for example, laid down in Brehm's *Thierleben* [7, p. 750], namely that the embryos do not need water for their thriving. This conception is not devoid of a certain probability, for v. Schreibers remarks expressly that the embryos he used were of the last stage and therefore perhaps already possessed the capacity for lung-breathing. — Czermak observed, on his test-animal kept in the water, which he had cut out of the uterus at a time when as yet little yolk-pulp was present in it, a regressive development of the gills, and draws from this the false inference that the same must likewise take place also in the uterus, from about the half of pregnancy onward; into the same error has also fallen, perhaps misled by Czermak, Schwalbe [57], and was therein strengthened also by the result of his — as he himself says, still too sparse — comparative gill-measurements. In truth the regressive development of the gills which Czermak saw is an adaptation-phenomenon to respiration in water (partly resorption, see pp. 191 and 202 ff. of this work), whereas the gills of the embryos remaining in the uterus increase steadily in length until shortly before birth and only suddenly reduce a few days before the latter. Neither v. Schreibers nor Czermak give thought, moreover, to the deeper theoretical significance of their experiments; this was first appreciated by v. Siebold, who, by the way, had experimented with negative success [61]. At his instigation Marie von Chauvin finally undertook — apparently unacquainted with the preliminary experiments of Schreibers and Czermak already carried out — the same experiment, and to her we owe the first, hitherto only detailed presentation of this subject. Fräulein v. Chauvin's often-cited experiments are so well known that it is scarcely worth the trouble to describe them here once more in brief words: the said researcher took the embryos from the uterus of pregnant *Salamandra atra*-females and placed them in the water, in order thus to force upon them the same course of development as that which the larvae of *Salamandra maculosa* normally pass through. Although among all (56) embryos only two specimens remained permanently alive — that is, after completed development metamorphosed into the land-salamander — and of these again only the one embryo ("Larva I") spent a longer time, namely 14 weeks, the other, however, only 19 days, actually in the water, yet the proof was brilliantly furnished that the embryos of *Salamandra atra* are very well capable, at a comparatively early stage of development, long before their natural birth, of living independently in the water. And the fact that they, transferred directly out of the maternal uterus into an element which, as one should think, is hostile to them, there at once felt at home and took nourishment to themselves, can only contribute to the view that this forcibly induced course of development was, in agreement with the conditions still existing today in *Maculosa*, likewise peculiar to them in an earlier epoch of time. Admiration is therein aroused only by the enormous capacity for adaptation of these animals, which after such long periods of time, within which their entire organization and mode of life had changed from the ground up, did not entirely lose the capacity for aquatic life, but, compelled by circumstances, were able to fall back upon it.

Of the accompanying phenomena under which the adaptation of the embryos to the water took place, decidedly the most remarkable is the complete shedding of the intra-uterine gills and the regeneration of monstrous gills. M. v. Chauvin observed this phenomenon in both cases where the experiment succeeded up to transformation into the land-salamander, and draws from it the conclusion that the intra-uterine gills are unsuited to water-breathing, and that therefore the formation of new gills is necessary for the preservation of the individual, all the more so as all those larvae in which the shedding of the intra-uterine gills did not take place in good time, or only piecemeal, became sick sooner or later (mostly attacked by fungi) and did not reach the goal of their development.

My endeavour now went toward not only repeating the experiments of M. v. Chauvin, but also extending them further; on the one hand, in that I took pains to rear also such foetuses as were still at a much earlier stage than those used for this by M. v. Chauvin; on the other hand, in that I set myself to bring nearer to reality the possibility, already envisaged by v. Siebold and M. v. Chauvin, of rearing from the larvae of *Salamandra atra* an "axolotl-like creature," that is, of holding the latter back from metamorphosis and accustoming them permanently to life in the water.

**Experiment A:** Fertilized eggs of *Salamandra atra* were taken by means of a small horn-spoon from the oviduct and uterus and laid carefully into a porcelain dish with physiological common-salt solution (0.7 g of common salt to 100 g of distilled water), and indeed, following the procedure of Born, who carried out the same experiment on *Maculosa*-eggs [6], in rows upon a grid of glass capillaries. The common-salt solution was not changed, but was aerated with the help of a Simon apparatus. The metal rings of the Zwies aeration-body were screwed together as densely as possible to one another, so that the air-bubbles emerging in between were distributed as fine as dust and were scarcely able to alter the fluid. — By keeping to these measures it succeeded in keeping the eggs in this state for up to twelve days, so that their cleavage took its further course. After this time the eggs swelled up strongly, but showed for a further two to three weeks no trace of decomposition. v. Siebold too had the eggs lying for twenty days in a glass bowl filled with fresh water, without noticing in them — with the exception of the swelling-up — any change (turbidity or decomposition), and surmises that they have a quite specific chemical composition not subject to putrefactive fermentation [61]. — The experiment, however, yielded a negative result in so far as I could not carry the eggs as far as the formation of embryos.

**Experiment B:** On 23. VII. 1902 I took from the left uterus of a female of Series VII (cf. Material-evidence) the egg, in which the embryo had already developed distinctly. According to the proposal of Schwalbe [57] I call that developmental period of the embryo during which it still finds itself within the egg-membrane the first stage; the period during which it moves freely in the yolk-pulp surrounding it, the second stage; the last period, finally, in which the yolk-pulp deriving from the remaining eggs is consumed, the third stage.

The mentioned embryo, which already fulfilled the first stage, showed on the day of its artificial birth as yet no trace of extremities, but indeed very short gill-rudiments in the form of three little knots on each side of the neck. I transferred it, serving myself with the horn-spoon, together with the egg-membrane enclosing it, into a glass dish with physiological common-salt solution, and set the Simon aeration-apparatus into operation.

My diary now records the following developmental stages: already on 25. VII. small swellings appear in the region where the forelegs are to come to view; the gill-rudiments have grown and have the form of threads 1½ mm long. On 28. VII. the foreleg-stumps protrude in the form of two pegs from the body, and on the gills one can already distinctly perceive the branching. On 3. VIII. the development of the forelegs is completed; the gills have reached 3 mm in length. The egg-membrane, which already some days before had no longer retained the spherical form and had shown a tattered appearance, is wholly rent apart by the movements of the embryo, — thus strikingly early in comparison with *Maculosa*-embryos, in which the leaving of the egg occurs only when already both pairs of legs are completely developed. — 7. VIII.: the embryo lies, since it burst the egg-membrane, almost without movement on the bottom of the vessel, now in the belly-, now in the back-position; the hindlegs appear in the form of short little stumps. — On 8. VIII. the embryo was dead.

C. Experiments on embryos of the second stage: 1) On 8. VIII. 1902 I took from the left uterus of a female of Series VII (cf. Material-evidence) the embryo, which already swam freely in the yolk-pulp and showed all four extremities, the hindlegs however still in stump-form. Its hindmost gills, of uncommonly delicate constitution and pale-red colour, were 9 mm long (at a total length of 21 mm, of which 5 mm fall on the head, 8 mm on the trunk and 8 mm on the tail). Before cutting open the uterus I had let it lie for a quarter-hour in the water and only then carefully shaken the embryo out. The latter was now carefully freed by means of a fine horn-forceps from the tough yolk adhering to it. At every touch it made vigorous floundering movements, but always took the back-position again. Yet already after 20 minutes it had definitively exchanged this for the belly-position.

In accordance with the observations of M. v. Chauvin, I now expected first of all, should the animal remain alive, deeply intervening changes in the gills.

Such did indeed set in, but in another way than the expected one. Already on 10. VIII., exactly two days after the artificial birth, the gills were only 4 mm, on 13. VIII. only 3 mm long. A partial shedding of the gills had, however, not taken place, otherwise I should have observed it or at least have had to notice remnants on the bottom of the vessel. Evidently a considerable resorption had gone on! Originally pale-red, the gills further were, on 13. VIII., dark-grey with a scarcely perceptible rosy-red tinge. The gill-filaments were short, yet still very pure; the gill-body, on the other hand, was not only shortened, but also strikingly thickened. The whole picture was very similar to that of a *Maculosa*-gill! That the gills thus transformed actually exercised the function of water-breathing was proved by their regularly pulsating movement, in that namely every 1—2 minutes they were with a jerk laid against the body-sides and then immediately spread out again. The analogous phenomenon is also easy to observe in every other gill-breathing tailed amphibian.

Up to 17. VIII. no kind of intake of nourishment had taken place. My efforts to induce the foetus to seize small pieces of Tubifex held out to it by means of a fine forceps had hitherto failed. On 17. VIII., however, I noticed suddenly that the little animal voluntarily snapped up a small Tubifex left behind in the dish and, not without trouble, swallowed it. This meal, however, was to become fateful for it. The intestine, clearly visible through the transparent outer integument and filled tautly with yolk, was namely in this early stage not yet equipped for such coarse fare. On 18. VIII. one recognized in it the swallowed worm, which was still by no means dead, but bent itself back and forth. Thus it may have torn the delicate connective-tissue wall of the intestine, for on the same day haemorrhages set in, and on 20. VIII. the foetus was dead. During the 12 days of its aquatic life it had grown by 1 mm. At the last it had spewed out yolk in masses, had lain constantly with open mouth in the back-position, and had moved only somewhat when one nudged it. The gills had become entirely white, dead.

2) On 13. VIII. 1902 I took from the right uterus of a female of Series VIII (cf. Material-evidence) the embryo of the second stage and placed it into the water in the manner already indicated. Here too I cleaned that one by careful plucking-off of the tough yolk-mass adhering to it. It possessed forelegs which were developed up to somewhat beyond the elbow-joint, but still no hindfeet. Its pale-red gills possessed the maximum length (measured at the hindmost gill) of 6 mm, at 19 mm total-length, of which 4 mm come on the head, 8 mm on the trunk, 7 mm on the tail. This embryo showed at touches only very weak mobility and persisted even after the lapse of 8 hours in the back-position; only on the next day did it take the belly-position. I now left it for a full week quite undisturbed, within which time the gill-resorption had set in in the same way as in the embryo previously discussed. On 21. VIII. I made the first feeding-attempt, but again not with Tubifex, but with a little yolk-pulp which I had taken from the uterus of another salamander-female. As soon as the yolk-pulp comes into contact with the water, it forms fairly consistent threads, which can be grasped with the forceps and held out to the animal. Against my expectation the latter snapped at once after it, and it succeeded in getting it, after much missed snapping, to gulp down such a yolk-thread. This procedure was from now on continued daily, and the little animal thrived visibly thereby. — On 24. VIII. the forelegs were already complete, the hindlegs developed as small stumps. This foetus withstood without further ado my journey from Rohitsch to Triest, which took place on 29. VIII., whereas that journey proved the ruin of several far older comrades. On 7. IX. the hindlegs too were completely developed. The total length amounted in these days already to 30 mm (4 mm maximum gill-length). At the same time the feeding with Tubifex too began, which now, since the intestine had in the meantime attained the requisite power of resistance, proceeded without objection. The further behaviour of the foetus was that, to be described in the following experiments, of the embryos of the third stage. Its metamorphosis took place, after it had withstood a second journey (from Triest to Vienna), on 10. XI. 1902. My chance idea of nourishing it at first with yolk-pulp instead of with Tubifex had thus brought it about that this embryo, which at its artificial birth was younger than the one previously discussed, remained alive and, in comparatively short time, passed through a quite considerable epoch of its development in an element foreign to it.

## D. Versuche mit Embryonen dritten Stadiums: [D. Experiments with embryos of the third stage]

In all, up to now I have taken 83 embryos of the third stage from the uteri of *Salamandra atra* and raised them in water. Of these, 28 were tended through to their transformation into finished land salamanders and beyond; 30 were preserved at various developmental stages; the remaining 25 specimens perished sooner or later, yet only 14 from illnesses of some kind or other accidents, the other 11 in consequence of the experiments carried out upon them (regeneration of the gills, extremities, of the tail-seam, of the tail-tip, as well as of the lens; neoteny, influence of temperature and light-intensity, etc.).

The whole external bodily structure of such third-stage embryos cut fresh from the uterus points to the uniform, protected-from-all-dangers, motion-poor life within the maternal body: the trunk is very compact, almost cylindrical (about ½–1 mm broader than high) and, owing to the massively taken-up yolk, mostly bulged out in an unshapely manner in the belly region. In proportion to this powerful development of the trunk, all the remaining bodily parts appear, with respect to size and strength, retarded: the legs are very feeble; the tail, while retaining its larval character, is laterally compressed but clothed with a scarcely perceptible skin-seam; in the length-relation of the tail to the trunk there is documented, as I was able to establish, at an early stage an external sexual character that holds good also for the adult animals, namely that in male embryos it [the tail] is just as long or somewhat longer, in female embryos shorter, than the latter [the trunk]. — In comparison with the trunk, finally, the head too appears small and narrow; the jaws are weak and poorly suited for the seizing of other, coarser food than that which ordinarily presents itself to them in the form of the soft, viscous yolk-pulp. All these features stand out the more, the more one calls to mind approximately equal-aged larvae of *Salamandra maculosa*, in which the trunk is slender and laterally compressed, the rudder-tail in its capacity as the chief organ of locomotion is provided with a broad skin-seam, and the broad skull is equipped with powerful predatory jaws reminiscent of those of a catfish [Wels]. — Only in one point concerning the external bodily structure do the larvae of *Salamandra atra* surpass their age-mates of *Salamandra maculosa*: in the powerful development of the delicately feathered gills, which during a considerable portion of the intra-uterine larval life are so long that they reach to the root of the thigh and "envelop, as with a veil," the whole trunk, to which they closely cling (Fig. 1, Pl. XIII). When HYRTL [31, p. 56] mentions larvae of *Salamandra atra* whose gills reach to the end of the tail, this can nonetheless only be regarded as a most rare monstrosity.

As regards the colour of the intra-uterine larvae of *Salamandra atra*, this too is not calculated for the struggle for existence: from the very beginning only dark pigment appears, which, presenting itself at first sparsely, makes the animal appear uniformly light grey or brownish-grey, but accumulates early on the upper side and, with advancing development, passes through all gradations of grey-brown to dark-black, while the underside remains nearly pigment-less until shortly before the transformation and appears at first whitish-yellow, then flesh-coloured. As soon as the embryos have entered the third stage, the colour of their upper side is only a little lighter than that of the finished land salamanders, namely dark blackish-brown. A pronounced spot-marking I have never perceived; at most that quite narrowly bounded, punctiform spots, especially on the head, as well as the furrows on the sides of the body and the vertebral line appear, in consequence of poorer pigmentation, lighter — which, however, becomes noticeable only on close inspection. From the body, which thus presents itself uniformly dark, stand out sharply the nearly pigment-less gills, which appear vividly red in consequence of the innumerable blood-vessels: out in the open, in some body of water, the animals would naturally at once and at a great distance attract attention and thereby be exposed to many persecutions. By contrast, the colouration of the *maculosa* larvae, which immediately after birth are mostly still very dark and almost uniformly coloured, adapts itself with extreme rapidity to the surroundings and takes on, corresponding to the particular ground of the dwelling-water, manifold, sometimes lighter, sometimes darker spots, bands, and marbled markings. The garish dress of the fully developed fire salamander appears only just before the metamorphosis, when at the same time the sharp gland-juices have also become fit for use.

I must here interpolate that v. BEDRIAGA [4, p. 369, 370] describes the colouration of the embryos of *Salamandra atra* not in agreement with me: "The young larvae appear, during the period in which the hind limbs are not yet formed and the tail is still laterally compressed, above greenish-grey and marbled, below pale yellow." Such coloured embryos I have never seen; nonetheless it is not to be doubted that the description of the keen-witted investigator is correct. I conjecture that it is a matter of a local or individual deviation, in which, however, the circumstance is interesting that the described colouration approaches that of many *maculosa* larvae — the biogenetic fundamental law thus perhaps here extending also to the colour!

The foregoing description applied to the exterior of those embryos of *Salamandra atra* which run through their development in the natural manner within the maternal body. There now follows the description of the changes to which such an embryo is subject when it is made by operative means into a "larva" living freely in the water.

As regards, once again first of all, the colour of the upper side of the body, in the larvae this always lightens itself more or less, provided that they are not kept entirely in the dark, and that they are not just before the metamorphosis, i.e. at so advanced a developmental stage that the colour is already definitively fixed. The series of gradations from light grey, grey-brown, blackish-brown, etc., up to dark-black, which the embryo passes through hand in hand with its progressive growth and the increasing accumulation of pigment in the uterus, now, in the water, runs off in the reverse sequence, only much more quickly, so that as a result of it the larva, already after about a week (sooner or later according to the illumination or darkness of its location), has gained a fairly light grey-brown ground-colour with inconspicuous, somewhat lighter grey spots and cloud-markings. The vertebral line, which originally was lighter than the rest of the body-colour, now on the contrary frequently becomes darker than this; on either side of it many specimens bear one longitudinal row each of indistinctly bounded grey-black spots. Tail and toes are more or less distinctly and at regular intervals barred transversely with blackish-grey. It can happen that the larvae in certain intermediate stages, although their colouration and marking consists exclusively of dismal tints, attain an almost variegated appearance, which I never observed in the equal-aged embryos that had remained in the uterus. In the course of aquatic life there then appear numerous fine black dots, which, when the time of transformation approaches, come to stand so densely together that they, fusing with one another, subject the upper side to a renewed darkening and finally lend the whole animal the pitch-black colour of the adults (see Fig. 2 and 3, Pl. XIII).

The colouration of the under side does not change in the water. — Self-evidently the gills too take part in the recolouration: in consequence of strong increase of the pigment they lose their luminous red. This recolouration of the gills is to be treated further below in more detail.

With respect to morphological changes of the water-living larvae of *Salamandra atra* it is to be noted that the form of the trunk, of the head, and of the extremities — in a word, the general habitus — shows itself scarcely variable: only the trunk acquires a more slender appearance through the fact that the yolk present in masses in the gut is either gradually digested, or removed by a spitting-out not seldom occurring especially at the moment of transference from the uterus into the water. Thereafter the body-girth, even with still so abundant feeding, never again attains the original, almost unshapely-to-be-called dimension. — All the more far-reaching transformations are undergone by two other external bodily components: the gills and the tail-seam.

The gills of the embryo of *Salamandra atra* are decidedly one of its most important organs: in them two functions are united: the respiratory and — at least during a very considerable fragment of the intra-uterine life — also the nutritive function. The former presents itself, in conformity with nature, under the conditions given in the uterus essentially otherwise than in the water, and the latter falls away entirely in independent aquatic life, where the nourishment is taken in only through the mouth. For this reason one may expect from the outset that it must be precisely the gills which, upon a violent intervention in the intra-uterine life of the foetus, undergo the most far-reaching changes. So M. v. CHAUVIN expected it, before she proceeded to the execution of her well-known experiment, and her expectation was confirmed in the course of the same. In the single case where her experiment may be designated as completely successful ("Larva I"), the intra-uterine gills proved completely unfit for water-breathing; they were therefore cast off entirely and at a single stroke, and in their place new ones formed, which however had a form quite and entirely deviating from the original, a monstrous one: they were much shorter and smaller, asymmetrical, thickly bladder-shaped, provided only with isolated, thick, irregularly distributed gill-filaments, and stood out stiffly from the head.

In several other cases, where the experiment did not succeed, or succeeded less completely, the endeavour to cast off the gills was distinctly recognizable; indeed, in a second case ("Larva II") there were formed, after the casting-off of the intra-uterine gills — accomplished here not so suddenly, but in the course of several days — rudiments of new gills, which "distinctly let one perceive an agreement in their formation with the gills of Larva I."

In a third case M. v. CHAUVIN found already in a foetus cut fresh from the uterus a malformation of the gills, which strongly recalled that which had formed in Larvae I and II in the course of aquatic life as a replacement for the normal gills. CLEMENS holds this malformation too to be a regenerate, caused by an injury of the gills in the uterus during the transport, and uses this conjecture of his as a support for the assertion that the differentiation of the intra-uterine gills of the *atra* embryos for special functions of embryonic life is here carried too far to permit an unaltered reproduction at all. "The respiratory need of the animal is satisfied by a quite clumsy, provisional, downright-to-be-called-atypical formation" [12, p. 104 and 105].

From the adduced facts M. v. CHAUVIN and CLEMENS, who from the investigator's experiments draws the further conclusion mentioned, were no doubt justified in assuming that the adaptation of the intra-uterine, at once respiratory and nutritive gills for the exclusively respiratory function in the water takes place always in the same manner, as soon as the embryo was forced into aquatic life: namely through total casting-off of the old respiratory organs and regeneration of monstrous ones.

Nevertheless my experiments yielded the result that such a transformation belongs to the rare exceptions. Unmistakably there exists a certain inclination in the constitution of the gills toward that radical process, and the more vigorous the embryo is, the more it will incline thereto. Only thus is it explicable that M. v. CHAUVIN was able to establish the new-formation of monstrous gills in two cases, and that in my experimental material too two examples of it are found.

1) The one case resembles at first entirely that of Larva II in M. v. CHAUVIN: the casting-off of the gills namely took place not at one stroke, but piece by piece; it began already on the day of the artificial birth (18. VIII. 1902, right uterus of a female from Series X) and lasted full ten days, until the 28. VIII.! On the right the last traces of the old gills had already disappeared on the 24. VIII., on the left therefore only four days later. Consistently, the regenerates on the right side too came to development sooner than on the left and there attained a more considerable length-unfolding. At their first appearance they had the form of small globular, in the further course of growth elongated-egg-shaped vesicles. Earlier than in Larva I of Fräulein v. CHAUVIN (if one reckons from the day of casting-off, not from the day of the artificial birth) the first gill-filament appeared here, namely already on the 10. IX., and indeed it proceeded from the middle gill of the right side. From now on the form of the regenerates became ever more similar to the end-results of v. CHAUVIN's material, in agreement with the description and the figure on the plate in v. CHAUVIN's treatise. Only the distribution of the gillfilaments growing out from the egg-shaped-bladder-like gill-bodies was a different one. My specimen resembles, however, "Larva I" in M. v. CHAUVIN in the following essential points:

a) The foremost gill-bodies were the most weakly developed and had remained quite without gill-filaments.

b) The hindmost gill-bodies were the most strongly developed and possessed the most gill-filaments.

c) The gill-bodies of the right side of the head were not merely larger overall, but also more abundantly beset with filaments than those of the left side.

d) The pigmentation of the new gills was a considerably stronger one than that of the original, so that the entire organ no longer looked blood-red, but dirty brown-red.

Unfortunately the animal now transformed itself very rapidly, considerably more quickly than M. v. CHAUVIN's "Larva I," and before I could have it drawn, into the finished land salamander: already on the 2. X. there set in quite suddenly a resorption of the gills, which must have been accomplished within a few hours, during which I could not be present for observation. On the 3. X. the animal crawled onto dry land.

2) The other case differed from the one described in v. CHAUVIN in that no spontaneous casting-off of the gills was present; rather I had, immediately after its artificial birth (1. VIII. 1902, right uterus of a female from Series VIII), cut off all the external gills of the embryo in question smoothly at the root. The animal showed scarcely any discomfort: only in the moment of the operation (with an extremely fine and sharp pair of scissors, beforehand carefully cleaned and disinfected) did it struggle a little and swim away with a powerful thrust. Likewise the bleeding was a minimal one: only for some few seconds did drops of blood emerge, then they ceased entirely. Only about half an hour after the amputation did the consequences thereof begin to show themselves in the behaviour of the animal: it strove violently to get out of the water and, when this did not succeed, sat for hours on end with gaping mouth, without stirring. After two days it had become quite motionless and was seized by a kind of rigor, since even to fairly energetic prodding it reacted only with weak movements of the legs. Furthermore this specimen disdained for a long time the taking of nourishment; it did not even snap, by way of trial, at the worms held out to it with the forceps — worms, which otherwise all larvae are indeed wont to do, even when they are too weak for the actual seizing of the morsel.

On 11 August a turn for the better finally occurred in the larva's behavior: for the first time a feeding attempt succeeded, and the peculiar rigidity also ceased. The cause of the crisis that had been weathered was not hard to guess: in place of the amputated gills there appeared (on both sides of the head, in completely equal development) the first rudiments of new respiratory organs in the form of three globular vesicles each! These increased in size with each day, and by 16 August were already more egg-shaped in form.

Since the larva in question could already be regarded, at its artificial birth (1 August), with a length of 49 mm, as nearly carried to term, the regression of the gills and the metamorphosis then set in as early as 23 August, even before it had come to the formation of gill-filaments on the elongate egg-shaped gill-bodies.

All the other cases that came under my observation showed an essentially different character. To be sure, I observed a partial or even complete shedding of the gills as something quite ordinary; nowhere, however, did the process advance as far as the regeneration of monstrous gills.

M. v. Chauvin speaks, on pages 345 and 346, of a series of larvae obtained by operation, on whose gills the water had not exerted the same influence as in the case of Larvae I and II, but had left them "quite unchanged." "On the third day the gills of all the larvae had become somewhat curved, but had lost nothing of their lively color, and only on the following day did a dying-off of the gill-tips show itself in some individuals, namely at such places where they had been injured. The process proceeded essentially otherwise than in Larva I, where the gill-tufts first faded uniformly, then shrank, and all six died off simultaneously. On the fifth day individual little pieces of the dead gill-tufts fell off, yet without separating completely from the healthy gill-parts. This circumstance was alarming, because fungi were bound to form on the dead parts, which would, as a matter of course, also have spread over the healthy parts of the body. To make matters worse, an unusual heat prevailed at that time, so that the water too could not be kept at the required coolness, and indeed on the sixth day fungi appeared on the dead gill-parts. One of the oldest larvae died, the others fed no more and became sick. The gills had in most of them already died off by half, but in some larvae looked, except at the tips, wholly red and healthy." Finally, however, all the larvae except two — whose lungs were already fit for air-breathing and which were brought onto land in time — fell victim to the fungal disease repeatedly mentioned by M. v. Chauvin (Saprolegnia!).

M. v. Chauvin draws the following conclusions from this: 1) The immediate cause of disease and death is the untimely dying-off and shedding of the intra-uterine gills. 2) The cause of the delay in the shedding of the gills on the one hand, and of the rapidly proliferating fungal turf on the other, is the excessively high temperature of the water (over 18° by day, 20–22° R. at night).

But the situation is nevertheless to be construed otherwise. In that M. v. Chauvin possessed larvae whose intra-uterine gills remained "unchanged" in water, she was on the point of essentially modifying her experiences regarding the adaptation of those organs and of avoiding the generalization of the two cases in which she observed monstrous regeneration.

Only to an unfortunate accident, namely the Saprolegnia disease, is it to be ascribed that M. v. Chauvin was prevented from the enrichment of her experience just mentioned; for it is, as a matter of course, an accident — though one that occurs easily and frequently — that the spores of those Oomycetes got into the vessel where the larvae were kept; then, to be sure, the high water-temperature and the injuries to the gills (not the persistence of these) favored a rapid spreading of the disease. Had M. v. Chauvin known a means of destroying the Saprolegnia, it would probably still have been possible for her to save the larvae. The removal of the Saprolegnia plague was already discussed in the technical section. Let it, however, be remarked once more that there exists absolutely no unconditional necessity why the larvae in warm water — even when the gill-tips crumble away — must be attacked by Saprolegnia. Often, in summer, the temperature of the water in the porcelain dishes, where I kept the larvae, reached as high as 20 and 25° C., and moreover the gills were injured or intentionally amputated; nevertheless the larvae remained free of Saprolegnia, which, on the other hand, in turn appears in much colder water and renders the success of the experiment sometimes questionable. All the more was I able to convince myself that M. v. Chauvin was in error when she designated the retention, or as the case may be the non-shedding, of the intra-uterine gills as the first and immediate cause of the disease.

To be sure, the intra-uterine gills are, in their original structure, always unsuited to water-respiration; but the inference that their adaptation to the new medium must necessarily always proceed by way of their complete dying-off, shedding, and monstrous regeneration was nonetheless premature. The adaptation can rather proceed in manifold, quite various ways.

By far the most frequent kind can be characterized by the two catchwords "partial resorption" and "molting."

Two cases of this kind of adaptation were already mentioned in connection with the experiments on embryos of the second stage, and it was thereby noted that the originally exceedingly delicate gill had become shorter and thicker, that its pale-red color had changed into dark-gray with a faint rosy tinge, and that the pigment had therefore (as is easily established under a strong loupe) increased to a considerable degree. In analogous fashion the adaptation now also took place in the embryos of the third stage.

The first change, setting in — according to the state of vigor of the animal — within a few hours, or only after 2–5 days, consists in this: that the gills lift away from the sides of the body, where they seemed to cling in a manner evidently irksome to the animal and impeding its movement, at a gradually increasing angle, and finally stand off almost horizontally from the neck. Almost simultaneously, or somewhat later, the strong adhesion of the gill-filaments to one another, which produces the impression of stickiness, is also lost, so that they are henceforth carried beautifully spread out and acquire the appearance of a fern-frond.

The remaining changes now set in very rapidly and all simultaneously. To specify numerically the number of days within which this occurs is not feasible here, since it is, according to the state of vigor of the animals, subject to very great fluctuations. — The changes, which are perceived partly already with the naked eye, partly only under the microscope (and indeed either on the living animal or on whole-mounts prepared in the simplest way, fixed with sublimate–glacial acetic acid and stained with boraxcarmine), consist essentially in the following:

a) The gills shorten very strongly, either through pure resorption, or in addition through this, that they roll up inward from the tip, fade, and fall off piecemeal. The hindmost gills are most strongly afflicted by the shortening; whereas in the uterus they far exceeded the middle and front gill-pairs in length, after the resorption they are scarcely a little longer than the middle gills.

b) The richness in blood-vessels decreases, so that the blood-red color of the gills becomes somewhat more matte. The wall of numerous capillary vessels bursts, the blood-corpuscles emerge in droves and disintegrate; upon the re-closing of the blood-pathway those capillary vessels are switched off.

c) The dark pigment, originally only sparsely represented in the gills, gains the upper hand to a considerable degree, so that these acquire a dirty reddish-brown appearance. The gill-body itself is often distinguished by a still darker, black-red coloration. Most intensively, injured places pigment themselves: where larger pieces have crumbled off at the tip, the breakage-places bear a large pitch-black point. I surmise that it is the disintegrated blood-corpuscles, having emerged from the burst capillaries, that bring about the recoloring of the gill, in that they transform themselves into pigment-cells; thus it is also explained that at injured places, where the strongest bleeding took place, the strongest darkening sets in at the same time.

d) Beneath the original, exceedingly delicate gill-epithelium a new, stronger one is formed, as a result of which neoformation the whole gill comes to appear thicker and more robust, the gill-body broader, and the gill-filaments no longer fine, but slender and finger-shaped. The old epithelium dies off and is cast off in countless tiny particles, which are to be noticed as a mealy sediment in the vessel. Thus a regular molting has been accomplished at the gills.

The advantages which all these changes yield for the animal are very obvious: the formation of a stronger epithelium and, in it, of a stronger pigmentation undoubtedly raises the resistance-capacity of the gill against external influences; the pigmentation in particular counters too great a light-intensity and at the same time makes the animal less conspicuous to possible enemies; the diminution of the extent of the whole organ, the resorption and the reduction of the vessels, is connected with the fact that the respiratory conditions in water are certainly more favorable than in the uterus, and that the nutritive function falls away; moreover the shortening and the altered posture of the gill are to the advantage of the unimpeded mobility of the larva.

While the adaptation-process is accomplished in vigorous animals within 8–10 days, at the longest two weeks, in sickly or poorly nourished larvae it requires much more time. To be sure, the reduction of the vessels, and in connection therewith the increase of the pigment, takes place promptly even in these, but the neoformation of the epithelium is delayed, on which account the pigment-cells — which in my opinion have arisen from the disintegrated blood-extravasations — settle themselves in the original, delicate gill-epithelium, an epithelium which possesses for water-respiration at most a provisional significance. In the absence of a stronger epithelium, the gill-bodies and -filaments remain at first as thin as at the beginning, and also lose but little of their length, inconvenient for the larva. Of the gill-filaments, some die off and break; their removal is probably also hastened by the fact that the larva continually entangles its forelegs in them and treads off individual filaments that have become brittle, and further by the fact that the more vigorous, voracious comrades now and then snap at the gills and bite pieces off them. Thus the gill of a weakly larva acquires roughly the frayed appearance of a bird's feather that is partly robbed of its vane. — The posture of the gills is, in vigorous individuals, as mentioned, a quite horizontal one, sometimes even somewhat curved upward, beautifully spread out: the foremost gills are then directed forward, so that they form an acute angle to the head, the complementary obtuse angle to the trunk; the middle gills stand perpendicular to the longitudinal axis of the body (and thus form both to the head and to the trunk a right angle); the hindmost gills, finally, stand obliquely backward and form to the trunk an acute, to be sure, but fairly large angle (ca. 60°), to the head the complementary obtuse angle. — Somewhat less vigorous individuals carry the foremost gills perpendicular to the body's longitudinal axis, while the middle gills form a large, the hindmost gills a small acute angle to the trunk. — Weakly individuals, finally, carry all three gill-pairs directed obliquely backward, in such a way that the hindmost gills stand off only very little from the trunk, and this state does not yet differ strongly from the original positional relations of the gills in the uterus. Also, the gill-carriage in weakened larvae is no such beautifully horizontal, freely-floating one, but rather a more downward-hanging, dragging one, and the tips are (namely in the fore and middle pair) mostly somewhat rolled inward.

Not always (though most frequently) does the adaptation of the intra-uterine gills for water-life set in through molting and partial resorption. The crumbling-off of smaller or larger pieces at the distal end of the gills, which often takes place already as an accompanying phenomenon of resorption, sometimes attains such an extent that the gills have already become too short to supply their bearer with the necessary breathing-air; indeed it comes to pass that the gills are shed altogether, and I observed both cases of simultaneous, sudden shedding (as in M. v. Chauvin's "Larva I") and such of quite gradual, piecemeal shedding. In all these cases a regeneration of the lost gills and gill-pieces is necessary for the preservation of the individual. The regeneration, however, as has already been set out, yields only in exceptional cases monstrosities; as a rule the regenerate rather resembles in its outer form the original gill, only there have already proceeded in it the changes necessary for water-respiration: it is clothed with a comparatively thick, pigment-rich epithelium, is poorer in blood-vessels as well as shorter than the original respiratory organ, and is carried in the described, horizontally spread-out position. The completion of such a regeneration-process takes 4 to 6 weeks.

M. v. Chauvin emphasizes, on pages 337 and 338 [9], a difference with regard to the disappearance of the embryonic and of the monstrous water-gills of her "Larva I" in metamorphosis: the former shrink together, because the blood steps out of them, and then fall off; the latter, however, are not cast off, but, as in other Urodele larvae, are entirely absorbed. The same difference I can also confirm for the normally formed water-gills as against the intra-uterine ones.

Amputated gills and gill-pieces replace themselves quickly again; on these neoformations too, the properties required for water-respiration are then always already present. The same holds of cut-off, individual gill-filaments; these are, in well-nourished individuals, often already grown again within a week, so that it can come to pass that the regenerated gill-filament is already adapted to the water while the rest of the gill is not yet. Larvae whose gills have been amputated on one side lose their equilibrium-posture for as long as the regenerate is not finished. The powerful gills form, as it were, a balancing-pole, and when they are missing on the one side, the center of gravity of the animal is displaced toward the opposite side.

In however various a way the adaptation of the gills may also proceed — whether through resorption and molting, whether through shedding and unchanged [replacement], or through shedding and monstrous regeneration: never did I observe that these differences appeared within an individual, that is, that for example on one and the same individual the gills of the one side, or a single gill, were shed and regenerated, while the others, by contrast, were merely resorbed and molted. But two adaptation-kinds, regeneration and resorption, do indeed occur together on all the gills of one and the same individual, as soon as, as described, too large pieces fall off and the persisting little stumps can no longer cope with the respiratory function; in this case the latter adapt themselves through resorption and molting, while the fallen-off pieces are replaced through regeneration.

To a quite similar adaptation as the gills the tail-seam is subject. According to Schwalbe [57, page 378] and others it is not out of the question that it too participates in respiration in the uterus. Of high importance for the embryo, however, this participation cannot be, for the seam is exceedingly narrow (only in fetuses of the second stage does it sometimes attain a certain breadth), indeed it not seldom is entirely lacking, in both older and younger developmental stages. Its epithelium is delicate and thin, on which account it is transparent to the unarmed eye; its pigment is sparse, on which account it appears colorless. With regard to its adaptation for water-life I have little to add to the observations of M. v. Chauvin. The intra-uterine tail-seam is within 6–10 days always completely absorbed and, after a further 6 to 8 days, replaced by a broader seam covered with a thicker, more strongly pigmented epithelium. This comes to development in the same length of time also in such larvae as had in the uterus no skin-seam at all to show around the tail, provided that those were not already very near to metamorphosis. With regard to the stronger pigmentation it is to be noted that the latter does not appear simultaneously with the appearance of the new tail-seam; rather, in the first days of its full development this is still quite white and stands out sharply from the rest of the dark-colored tail. Only gradually do dark-gray or gray-black spots appear, and indeed often at regular intervals, on the seam too. — Probably the tail-seam loses in water its possible respiratory embryonic function, and its broadening and enlargement serve only to create an ample rowing-organ.

If, as soon as the embryo has been removed from the uterus, one cuts away with a fine pair of scissors the intra-uterine skin-seam all around the tail, then in the course of 6–8 days a regenerate forms which already brings with it the properties necessary for the motor function of aquatic life, namely a greater breadth-extension and a more robust structure. Here too the stronger pigmentation follows only some days later.

In the transformation of the embryonic organism for the sojourn in the water, finally the entire outer integument also takes part. That its pigmentation changes has already been discussed; as for entering into the remaining adaptation-phenomena of the tissue, I reserve this, however, for a special anatomical-histological work, in which still other details, in particular also the question of fertilization and the nutritional relations of the salamander embryos, are to be examined more closely.

In connection with the regeneration-relations of the gills and of the tail-seam, mention may at once be made of the few other regeneration-experiments which I have undertaken on the larvae of Salamandra atra. In general one may say that they possess a reproductive capacity which, though not insignificant, nevertheless stands below that of the Maculosa-larvae, since indeed in general the more highly differentiated species always seem to be less capable of performance in this respect than the phylogenetically younger ones. The failure of the experiments on lens-regeneration has already been mentioned in the technical part. Amputated limbs and tails replace themselves again: in complete fashion if the larva is still young, in incomplete fashion if it was near the metamorphosis. The limbs replace themselves only if they were not cut off quite close to the shoulder- or pelvic-girdle respectively; the tail, if no more than its hindmost third was amputated. What an important organ of movement the latter constitutes in the motor apparatus of the salamander larva, one recognizes at once on the occasion of such an amputation: even when only the outermost tip was cut off, the movements of the larvae become extremely uncertain, indeed they lose every secure hold and seek laboriously to preserve their equilibrium by firmly bracing the legs. — Large skin-wounds at all possible parts of the body (on the neck, on the back, indeed even on the breast), such as I had inadvertently inflicted on some larvae during the cutting-out from the uterus with the scissors, heal closed with surprising speed. In all operative interventions the bleeding is minimal, the closing of the wound enormously rapid. The animals show hardly any discomfort.

All regenerates, as well as the epidermis newly formed over the skin-wounds and over the gill-slits upon their growing-together (cf. v. Chauvin [9], pag. 337), are distinguished at first by a white color (lack of pigment). It takes several weeks until this passes over into light-grey, grey, dark-grey, and finally again into deep-black.

Now may a few further observations on the otherwise biological behavior of the Atra-larvae find a place here. That these are little sensitive to cold and warmth, whereas they tolerate a change of temperature badly, even when it amounts to only a few degrees, can already be gathered from several passages of what has been said so far. Between 8 and 25° C. any constantly remaining temperature is agreeable to them, without any particularly conspicuous influence becoming noticeable in their outer appearance; at most one can, in agreement with the observations made on Maculosa-larvae by Fischel [19], Flemming [23, 24], and Prowazek [48], establish that the Atra-larvae kept in cold water retain the original dark coloration, whereas in warm water they tend toward a lightening of the coloration of their upper side. Larvae which had been kept in warm water and had there become lighter become dark again if one gradually transfers them back into cold water. Young larvae prove in this respect more changeable than old ones; larvae standing immediately before the metamorphosis no longer let any influence of the temperature on their color be recognized at all. — The larvae feel most comfortable at a mean temperature of 14–16° C. and are then also the most mobile and the most eager to feed.

An influence just as slight as the temperature is exerted by the light-intensity: most natural for the larvae is, of course, darkness; they seek it out when they have the opportunity to do so, by creeping away among stones and the like, but on the other hand let no sign of discomfort be recognized in glaring light, indeed even in direct sunbeams — provided that these do not suddenly and not above 25° C. warm the water. — In agreement with what Flemming and Prowazek l. c. [found], yet in contradiction with the observations made on Maculosa-larvae by Fischel l. c., I found that the Atra-larvae in the dark retain their primary, uniform black coloration, whereas in the light a lightening and spot-marking sets in. In white porcelain dishes this takes place to a stronger degree than in glass dishes, as a result of the reflecting of the light-rays. If one keeps the larvae in dishes of differently-colored glass, then no direct influence of the color of the glass shows itself; rather the larvae only become lighter by a few shadings or remain darker, according as the latter is more or less suited to letting the light through: they therefore remain fairly dark in blue and green ones, they fade fairly strongly in yellow or red glass dishes. Larvae of Salamandra atra which have once become light in the light do not, when placed back into the dark, return any more to their primary dark coloration; they show themselves in this less changeable than the larvae of Maculosa. Just before the metamorphosis likewise no influence of the light-intensity on the color is any longer noticeable.

Not uninteresting are the movements of the Atra-larvae. In the uterus there is of course granted to them in this respect only a very slight room for play, yet they make use of this for various changes of position, which can go as far as complete turnings-about, so much so that a larva which had just been lying cranially (with its head turned toward the head of the mother-animal) suddenly takes up the caudal position. Moreover they change their posture: the extremities, which are stretched now forward, now backward, now braced sideways against the uterine wall; the gills, which, so long as yolk-mush is still present, dip up and down in it, later adhere now to the body itself, now to the uterine wall; finally the tail, which is seldom stretched out, but mostly carried in a single to threefold bend. If one cuts the uterus open carefully, this seems at first to disturb the larvae little, for they mostly remain quite still; only at the moment when they are transferred into the water do they wriggle violently. In the water there then usually sets in once again rest, indeed a kind of rigidity, and the larvae, just as they sink to the bottom of the dish, remain as a rule in the dorsal position; the legs are stretched straight out backward. After ¼–½ hour the larvae make exertions to turn themselves over into the ventral position, yet earlier already if one nudges them gently with the finger. If the normal ventral position is happily attained, then there follows as a rule once again a longer, almost motionless lying-still: only the tail performs with its rearward half undulating movements, and analogously the legs — still stretched backward and held asymmetrically even now — move now and then in a peculiar manner to and fro at the wrist-joint. These movement-phenomena of the extremities, which one can sometimes still observe in fairly old larvae, presumably derive from the embryonic life, where they served to push the foetus into place in the uterus until it had found a position agreeable to it. — After one to two hours the larvae set into the water begin with clumsy walking-attempts, in which, however, they soon perfect themselves greatly. Some chance, a jolt, or other fright, or when the larvae touch one another during their walking-attempts, then usually causes them to try out for the first time their swimming-ability in the dwelling-element new to them: the test always turns out surprisingly well, and from that moment on they behave almost as skillfully as if they had been at home in the water all along. Only they are less lively than Maculosa-larvae and therefore make use of their proficiency in swimming only on special occasions. At an advanced stage, when the lungs already enter into action, they come here and there to the surface, in order to expel air-bubbles laden with carbonic acid and at the same time to draw in fresh air. The gills do not move so long as their adaptation for aquatic life is not completed; but then their regularly pulsating movements prove that they are exercising their respiratory function. I possessed two Atra-larvae which now and then ran themselves through the gills with the fingers of the forelegs, as if to comb them, an observation which in Brehm [7, pag. 794] is also stated for the furrow-newt (Menobranchus lateralis Say).

Sometimes a part of the yolk-mass taken up in the uterus is spat out when one transfers the larva into the water; more often, however, this fortunately does not occur, indeed I not seldom observed that larvae from whose mouth long yolk-fibres were hanging out drew these fibres in by chewing-movements and gulped them down, as soon as they had lain only a little while in the water and had taken up the normal position (with the belly downward). Since the larvae are very greedy, their nourishment is not so difficult as one would suppose. On one occasion I observed how the one of two larvae which had just been let out of the foetal membranes into the water at once attacked its companion and bit it in the gills. Yet this is after all an exceptional case; for the time being the main thing remains that the larvae snap at all at the food held out to them, which already, as a rule, occurs in the first hours of their aquatic life. Their frequent mis-snapping, the clumsiness, indeed inability in seizing the prey — the jaws are, after all, very weak, because calculated only for the soft yolk-mass — probably tire first the patience of the keeper, but their hunger is so great that they nevertheless soon attain the necessary practice in feeding. Older larvae, of 45–49 mm length, I brought to metamorphosis several times even without feeding: the yolk-store still present in their gut suffices to let them, with difficulty, complete their development.

The sensory and intellectual capacities of the Atra-larvae are to be rated low, both in and of themselves and also in comparison with those of the Maculosa-larvae. A prey-animal they notice only when it moves in the most immediate proximity, indeed often only then, when it touches their snout. In the selection of the prey, the sense of taste apparently does not come into consideration, but only whether the former lets itself be seized more or less easily with the feeble jaws: for this reason the soft-skinned worms are preferred to the chitin-armored, hard-shelled insects and crustaceans. Well developed, on the other hand, the sense of touch seems to be, for already a slight touch or shaking of the surrounding medium as a rule releases wild flight-movements. The static sense proves to be in a certain respect stunted: such larvae, namely, as were compelled for a longer time, through some external circumstance (e.g. asymmetrical gill-development or one-sided limb-amputation), to persist in a slanted, abnormal position, often do not give this up even then, when that equilibrium-disturbing factor has long since again disappeared (the damage repaired), indeed they move and even swim outside the natural equilibrium-position. Thereby, however, they are quite healthy and in good condition, as is sufficiently proved by their uninterruptedly active feeding-eagerness. — In voluntary changes of place the larvae are guided less by a pronounced, genuine place-sense than by the endeavor always to seek out the darkest spot in the vessel; therefore they always find their way back again into hiding-places, which for this purpose had been constructed out of stones laid one upon another.

Experiments on Neoteny:** The foregoing table indicates, under No. 21, the developmental data of a larva of *Salamandra atra* which comes closer to the v. Siebold and v. Chauvin problem, namely whether it might succeed in rearing "axolotl-like beings" out of such larvae. By the expression "axolotl-like being" v. Siebold understood a urodele-larva which, in analogy with the best-known example of this, the Mexican axolotl (*Amblystoma mexicanum* Cope) — whose perennibranchiate larval form was for a long time regarded under the name "*Siredon pisciformis* Shaw" as an independent species — retains lifelong its gill-breathing, water-dwelling larval form and becomes sexually mature as such, to which there thus belongs that property which Kollmann has aptly named "neoteny" [38].

M. v. Chauvin expresses herself concerning that problem as follows [9, p. 338]:

"Whether it will ever be at all possible to rear, out of a salamander whose species has inhabited the land for thousands of years, a water-dwelling animal, only manifold experiments can show. I doubt it!"

It is by no means yet to be regarded as an example of neoteny when atra-larvae (as, for instance, some larvae of the above table) require, in the water, much longer for the completion of their larval life than would have been the case within the maternal uterus. Neoteny is only then to be established when the animals, despite their having been larvae for that abnormally long time, at the same time did not remain standing still in development, but rather made regular progress in growth. Neotenic larvae are not animals that have remained stuck in development at all, or have lagged behind in growth, but rather normally thriving animals, in which solely and alone the point in time of metamorphosis into the lung-breathing land form is pushed out, or the metamorphosis even fails to occur altogether. — If the metamorphosis is merely postponed, then there is partial, and if it is abolished, then there is total neoteny. — This much for the precise definition of the often incorrectly conceived concept "neoteny." (Compare on this also the works of v. Bedriaga [3] and Wolterstorff [74].)

In order now to be able to speak, after Kollmann [38], of total neoteny, there exists, however, not merely the requirement of lifelong persistence in the larval form, but in addition that of the attainment of sexual maturity. Since this, in all the cases of neoteny I have obtained, did not hold true, but rather all the larvae did ultimately transform after all into lung-breathing earth-salamanders, the result of my experiments remains so far only partial neoteny. The v. Siebold and v. Chauvin problem is thus not to be regarded as solved, and in consideration of the exceedingly strong drive-to-transform of the larvae, I can only join in the doubts of Fräulein v. Chauvin as to whether it will ever be solved.

Since the length-measure of 49–50, at most 51 mm, is to be assumed as the rule for an atra-larva standing immediately before metamorphosis, then already larvae 4, 8, 10, 18, and 20 of the above table, and likewise "Larva I" of the v. Chauvin experiments, fall under the concept of partial neoteny. Besides these I had three more larvae which attained the total length of 55, 56, and 59 mm immediately before metamorphosis. None of these, however, reached the size and high developmental stage of the larva listed last in the table, the 21st.

The arrangement of the experiment is the following: the upper rim of a roomy glass tank, tall in proportion to its breadth and length, is ground down so evenly that a glass cover laid upon it closes tightly everywhere. In the glass cover two holes are bored, barely sufficient to let pass through, on the one side, an inflow-pipe with injection-aerator, on the other side a self-acting outflow-siphon. The bottom of the glass tank is made more habitable by some substrate, clean gravel or the like intermixed with several larger stones; the tank is then filled with water up to the rim, so that on laying on the glass cover no air-space worth mentioning remains between it and the water-surface. Since not only the air-richness, but also the flawless purity and low temperature of the water (8–12° C.) is decisive for the success of the experiment, both constant aeration and uninterrupted flow-through of fresh tap-water is necessary. Therefore one does well to set aside here the Simon aerating-apparatus, otherwise repeatedly used and described in the technical section, and to employ in its place an injection-aerator, which combines inflow and aeration in that, connected to the inflow-pipe, it sweeps countless air-bubbles along into the water. This must not, however, happen with such vehemence that the water in the glass tank is torn into a violent motion, into a vortex. Rather, it should resemble in its constitution a calm, deep, and clear mountain lake.

The feeding of the larvae so kept succeeds best with *Tubifex rivulorum*, and indeed fairly abundantly; for it is, as said, not a matter of inhibiting the animals in their development, but on the contrary of granting them, already within their present medium, in the water, such favorable living-conditions that a leaving of the same does not appear desirable in the interest of the individual. Scantily fed larvae tend, once they have reached a certain developmental stage, far more toward metamorphosis than well-nourished ones, precisely because hunger (and likewise lack of air or some other unfavorable factor) then drives them to seek out other, perhaps better, existence-possibilities. (Compare Barfurth's investigations on frog-larvae [1].)

But how strong the drive to transform is, even under such conditions exceedingly favorable to permanent water-life, expresses itself among other things in this, that the larval character is in the end maintained only by the presence of the gills and of the fin-seam on the tail, while the rest of the habitus and the development of individual body-parts already entirely match the perfect salamander: the smooth larval skin gives way to the wrinkled, gland-rich skin of the metamorphosed animal, and the head-shape too already resembles that of the latter, which emerges especially from the wholly normal, but on a larval form most striking, development of the parotids.

Was it a mere chance that precisely that larva in which I was able to obtain the phenomenon of partial neoteny most beautifully came to the light of day not through operation, but (cf. table) as a natural premature birth? Naturally no further conclusions may be drawn from this single case, but the supposition lies near that this larva was from the very outset more suited for a longer endurance in the water, because it had been deposited into the water by the maternal animal in a natural way, only at a much earlier developmental stage than otherwise takes place in *Salamandra atra*. The same female has, be it remarked in passing, brought premature births into the world several more times! Later experiments, however, can alone consolidate the probability that such prematurely-born larvae lend themselves better to experiments on neoteny than those freed from the uterus by artificial means.

## F. Experiments on Adult Alpine Salamanders.

### 1. On Reproduction.

Larva 21 of the above table was already there, as well as in the experiments described above, designated as a "natural premature birth"; for it was not, as is wont to happen, born first as a lung-breathing animal without gills or with slight, dead gill-remnants, nor was it brought to the light of day in the larval stage by artificial means, through operation, but rather it found itself, at its normally proceeding birth, still at a comparatively early developmental stage. V. Bedriaga [4, p. 375] too knows how to report of this: "Premature births are rare and probably occur only in captivity. In this case larvae are born in the water with strikingly large gills, reaching almost half of the whole animal." I have observed such a thing in all on 11 captive females of *Salamandra atra*, and indeed 37 times. In this the following accompanying phenomena are noteworthy:

1) Those females which had once given birth too early seemed to incline to this in general, so that at the following gestation-periods the young were as a rule again thrown in the larval stage.

2) Among the cases registered by me there are strikingly many in which the number of young thrown amounted to more than two, namely three to four.

3) All females in which habitual premature births occurred originated from relatively low-lying collection-sites, from a sea-elevation of 750 to at most 1000 m.

These three points suggest the supposition that premature births perhaps by no means come about merely under the influence of captive life, but occasionally also occur in freedom at low-lying collection-sites where favorable hydrographic conditions prevail. Any observation from the free life of the salamanders that might support that supposition I have, to be sure, not made. True, I once encountered, among a quite freshly caught series (X), a little animal that had come into the world as a larva; yet here just as well an abortus in consequence of the exertions to be endured by the female in question during capture and transport might be present.

Brehm [7, p. 750] remarks that captive female alpine salamanders always cast their young on the dry ground, even when abundant water is offered to them. Against this I experienced that even freshly caught females very often, and with an unmistakable preference, deposit their young into the water-basin. Since the young normally no longer have gills at birth, they strive with all their might to get out of the water; but they are able to linger in it for many hours, provided only the gill-clefts are not yet entirely grown shut; yet they do suffer respiratory distress, for they have the mouth wide open and become wholly motionless; brought onto land, they recover after some time. If the gill-clefts were already grown shut at birth, then the young perish very quickly unless they succeed in crawling out of the water. — That the females of the alpine salamander occasionally give birth in the water, I observed, by the way, once also in the free life, and indeed in the grey of dawn of August 10, 1896, on the plateau of the Erfurterhütte in the Achental (ca. 1800 m sea-elevation), where several females had selected a shallow rain-puddle for this act. The young, thrown as fully developed land animals, crawled, after they had merely lain still for a few minutes, at once without difficulty out of the puddle. I conclude from these observations that the water makes giving birth easier for the females, perhaps in that, on the opening of the cloaca, it penetrates into it and into the womb and thus lets the young glide out more quickly.

In contrast to the cases where even the already gill-less young were born in the water, it also happened in captivity — though it was observed by me only twice — that premature births which still possessed functional gills were deposited on the dry ground: the one time with the freshly caught Series X in the transport-crate, where no water at all was available; I set the larva into the water, and it seemed to feel well in it, although it stood shortly before metamorphosis, which then also took place 12 days afterward. The second time in the terrarium; this time I left the larva in the moss, and it showed no discomfort, although it was somewhat younger than the one just mentioned. The gills died off within a few days; fairly extensive remnants of them, which for weeks still were carried about along, as well as the long persistence of the open gill-clefts, pointed however to the stage of this specimen being actually still too early for land life. That 45–50 mm long atra-larvae, which are still in full possession of their gills, can exist equally well on land (in moist moss) as in water, I had already tested before several times: the lungs are namely already at the same time sufficiently developed for air-breathing, and according to whether the larvae get into the water or onto land, the one or the other respiratory organ takes the lead. This wonderful power of adaptation finds mention in the passage cited just before in Brehm [7, p. 750], where it is said that embryos cut out of the maternal body remain alive on land for weeks (with appropriate care, permanently!). But this only in passing here.

If the atra-females in captivity, and occasionally even in free life, show on the whole a certain inclination to deposit their young in the water, contrary to the generally accepted rule, then this happens — apart from the very rare exceptions just mentioned — all the more readily when premature births occur. The captive females then use, one might say purposefully, the water-basin of the terrarium in order to give birth in it. In this, out of the total number of 34 premature births, 24 times the normal set consisting of two young was thrown, but 10 times three, indeed 3 times even four young were thrown.

With the female from Achenkirchen the young of the first three, with the female from Nasswald the young of the first two gestation-periods were normal, thus two in number and already finished land-salamanders, and only from the fourth, respectively the third, gestation-period since their capture had both females begun to deposit their young in the larval stage, whereby their number had at the same time increased. The remaining females listed above, however, had brought premature births into the world already at the first gestation-period of captive life, and indeed in a number exceeding the norm. With the six females here not specially enumerated, which to be sure likewise (and indeed together 24 times) bore larvae instead of finished earth-salamanders, but no more than the normal pair of two, the rule held that this premature birth-giving began only with the second or third gestation — reckoned from the beginning of captivity.

Unfortunately the foregoing observational material is not yet large enough to permit definite assertions in any direction. My experiences with *Salamandra maculosa*, still to be discussed, however, leave me in no doubt that, with a continuation of the experiments, a large part of the atra-females would let themselves be permanently accustomed to depositing their young as gill-bearing larvae into the water, and indeed with a simultaneous increase of the offspring progressing with each gestation-period — in a word, that it would have to succeed in bringing about the complete return, made possible under the offering of favorable water-conditions, to the mode of reproduction of *Salamandra maculosa*, the hypothetical ancestral species!

Since the young are born so much earlier, they neither lay claim to the uterus so much spatially, nor do they require — since they indeed pass through only a part of larval life in the maternal body — so much yolk-pulp as nourishment. Therefore opportunity is given to a few more sibling-eggs to develop themselves into germs, and the number of these will surely rise with each gestation-period, at the same time the stage at which the larvae come into the world becoming an ever earlier one. — Not all the eggs of an ovulation that do not grow into normal embryos flow into the nutrient-pulp; rather, several still show the tendency to develop further, but waste away already at a low stage, because they received no sufficient nourishment or were crowded out by their favored siblings. The frequent occurrence of such non-viable, mostly teratological abortive-eggs and abortive-embryos can be inferred, apart from the analogy with the conditions in *Salamandra maculosa* still to be discussed, also from the fact that such abortive formations were several times cast out along with them in the aforementioned cases where three normally developed larvae came into the world, and further, that in the cases where four larvae were born, individual ones among them exhibited slight cripplings. Not all too large abortive formations are probably also, although they do not belong to the actual nutrient-pulp, consumed by the favored embryos in the uterus; larvae of both salamander species kept in water fell greedily upon abortive eggs that were offered to them, and devoured them, although in some the embryo already showed itself fairly far advanced.

The yet-to-be-conducted experiments will, I hope, provide me over the course of several years with further clarification regarding these interesting relationships.

## 2. On Coloration and Marking.

In all descriptions of *Salamandra atra* found in the literature, one reads that the color of this species is a uniform, glossy black, at most with a brownish shimmer.

The latter arises in the material before me from the fact that occasionally on certain parts of the body (in particular on the throat, on the inner side of the legs and of the tail, on the underside of the shoulder and pelvic region, as well as in the lateral grooves) the dark pigment is not so densely massed as at the other sites; according to v. Bedriaga [4, pag. 369] also from the fact "that those lighter body parts are densely covered with tiny little, brownish, black-rimmed dots." Among the sometimes brownish or grayish-black body parts, v. Bedriaga, besides those already named, also counts the transversely striped toes and the vertebral line.

In addition, mention is made in the literature of two **partially albinotic** specimens. First, namely, Wiedersheim [73] found in the uterus of a female dissected by him, originating from Gaschurn in Vorarlberg, a colorless embryo, which displayed an accumulation of black pigment only at the epiphyseal end of the brain; second, Schwalbe [57] found an adult alpine salamander that was entirely light brown owing to poverty of pigment (the locality I do not recall), and surmises that to the occasional, albeit rare, occurrence of such partially albinotic specimens is to be ascribed the establishment of the "Salamandra fusca" discovered by Gessner [29] and described by Laurenti [41]. To these two finds I can add a third: I possess a stately specimen of light brown color, caught by me near Lofer (Series I), which on the forehead, between the eyes (cf. the case of Wiedersheim!), bears a large, triangular, dark chestnut-brown spot. It has, I may note in passing, struck me that even in bleached alcohol preparations a dark spot usually remains at the same place. — It does not seem improbable to me that brown specimens of *Salamandra atra* may be traceable to albinotic embryos, such as Wiedersheim found one.

Nowhere, however, in the descriptions is any pronounced spot-marking mentioned. All the more did it surprise me to find repeatedly in my material specimens which on their black ground-color bear numerous quite small, whitish-yellow little spots, especially on the throat and breast. A specimen before me, whose locality I unfortunately do not know, possesses, besides a moderate number of whitish-yellow dots on the throat and fore-breast, on the left half of the head and behind the eyes, also a quantity of fairly large, sharply delimited (somewhat faded in formol) spots of brownish color, which — beginning between the eyes and continuing as far as the tail — despite their generally irregular distribution nonetheless distinctly show the tendency to arrange themselves in two longitudinal rows; these spots are not caused, say, by poverty of pigment, but by the presence of a special, brownish pigment.

All dotted or spotted specimens whose locality is known to me originate from comparatively low elevations (750 up to at most 1050 m) and are found among Series VI and VII (Taufers), VIII (Partenkirchen), X (Vorderinnthal), and XI (Lienz Dolomites).

The small whitish-yellow dots and little spots on a black ground, which also frequently occur in *Salamandra maculosa* (but here are not to be confounded with the large yellow spots peculiar to that species, which are formed by an entirely different pigment!), apparently represent a very ancient urodele character. Exactly the same coloration and marking is found in several urodele forms: e.g. in the genus Amblystoma, of which the species Amblystoma punctatum Baird looks, at first glance, deceptively similar to a *Salamandra atra* marked as described. In general, the combination of yellow and black pigment is very widespread throughout the entire order of the Urodela.

It has now struck me that many individuals of *Salamandra atra*, which had originally been uniformly black, acquired those whitish-yellow dots once they had lived two to three years in the terrarium. The dots increase in number and size over the course of the years and pass from the throat, where they always appeared first, onto the breast and onto the upper side. In a specimen before me they have merged on the throat into a marbled marking. — Without having yet conducted systematic experiments on this, I nonetheless observed the appearance of the dots only when the animals were kept in a terrarium fitted out with loam-soil, and not, on the other hand, when black garden- or forest-soil was used. To forestall a misunderstanding, I mention that I do not, say, bring the yellow dots into connection with the yellow color of the loam-soil and regard them as a phenomenon of adaptation to the substrate; rather, the mentioned observation signifies for me merely a new confirmation of the ancient but apparently forgotten experience that loam-soil, for unknown reasons, forms by far the most favorable substrate for the thriving of both salamander species: not only do they attain on loam-soil an astonishing size (cf. the experiments of v. Schreibers communicated in Schweigger [58]), but hand in hand with this also goes a more luxuriant, more energetic unfolding of the colors (cf. my analogous experiments on *Salamandra maculosa*). — Incidentally, these experiments, in order to lead to firmly established results, all still require continuation and expansion over years.

Likewise I must for the time being leave it an open question whether the finds of yellow-dotted alpine salamanders in the wild are to be traced back to an atavism as a consequence of secondary immigration into lower-lying regions, or whether the alpine salamanders had always occurred at the relevant low-lying spots and in consequence had not entirely lost the primary spot-marking at all, or finally whether they have nothing to do with the elevation above sea level of the locality, and the hitherto available, admittedly already fairly numerous finds all stem only *by chance* from the lowest boundary of the vertical range of distribution of that species.

## V. Experiments on Salamandra maculosa.

If it had succeeded, with *Salamandra atra*, in forcing upon it the developmental course of the presumably older form, *Salamandra maculosa*, then with the latter species it was a matter of achieving the reverse: of bringing about also in the fire salamander the mode of reproduction and development of the alpine salamander, which arose through adaptation to the high mountains.

Although the question of how *Salamandra maculosa* would behave with respect to its reproduction in regions where no waters suitable for depositing the larvae are present surfaces in the literature a few times (e.g. in one of Marshall's popular writings, if I am not mistaken, in the "Spaziergänge eines Naturforschers" [Walks of a Naturalist]; v. Schreibers too speaks of "arbitrary procrastination of the birthing," [56] and Czermak, somewhat obscurely, of "delayed births as a consequence of lack of water" [13]), it nevertheless seems that the attempt to solve that question by the very simple experimental route has only very rarely been undertaken and never pursued further to the final goal.

There are found, however, in the literature very early statements that *Salamandra maculosa* sometimes gives birth on land, and it is not always easy to decide how those statements are to be construed: whether the salamanders in the cases mentioned in this connection had simply borne the larvae on land because they were unable to find water in time and had been surprised by the birth-throes, or whether a further-reaching adaptation to unfavorable hydrographic conditions was already present and the young consequently came into the world at an advanced stage.

The oldest statement that the fire salamander gives birth on dry land is found in Sturm [64] and has passed from here into Reider-Hahn's "Fauna boica" [51]. A second, very remarkable statement one meets in Leydig [43, pag. 91]: "If water is lacking, then, according to the report of several observers, the female salamander is said to give birth at places that are at least of moist nature. Probably, in such newborn larvae the gills too are then already in the process of regressing during the longer retention in the uterus." Unfortunately, the relevant observers are not named. — Similar communications, which are probably to be traced back to the cited passage from Leydig, are made by Brehm [7] and Dürigen [16, pag. 591].

More definite are a few statements which relate to observations on captive salamander females and demonstrate that the young thrown on land were born in the ordinary larval stage, and that consequently no further-reaching adaptation was yet present. The larvae then either reached the water on their own, in that they keep wriggling along the ground until by chance they get into the water basin — Fischer-Sigwart [21] — or they were placed into it in good time by the attentive keeper — Sachse [52]. — If neither was the case, then the larvae born in the dry perished miserably — Steindachner's report to Frauenfeld [27]. — O. vom Rath [50] too observed that the larvae were deposited into the moss. Jakob [33] relates that a salamander female on the way from the mountain forest to the water had been trampled or run over on the highway; the embryos squeezed out of it lay beside it and were still partly alive, although they had been exposed for the whole day (?) to the blaze of the sun. To occurrences of this kind Jakob ascribes the origin of the belief that the salamander sometimes deposits its young in moist moss or grass. That this explanation, however, does not suffice is shown well enough already by the observations cited hitherto, and still more by the following passage in v. Bedriaga [4, pag. 316], where, by a quite brief remark thrown out, as it were, merely in passing, a most noteworthy fact is reported:

"On the other hand, it has succeeded, by complete deprivation of water, in compelling the gravid female to retain her brood in the uterus for a longer time. Only in rare cases do the females decide to throw their young on land, and when it happens, these are mostly monstrous births, larvae with crippled limbs or without extremities. In other cases, *when the mother has been forced to live for a longer time exclusively on land, the larvae come into the world strikingly large and with short gills and transform very rapidly into lung-breathing animals*."¹

My own observations relate, as may be expressly noted, to *Salamandra maculosa* kept in captivity and are the result of experiments arranged in an extremely simple manner. Namely, by, after the definitive removal of the water basin, having merely the atomizer provide for the most indispensable soil- and air-moisture, I deprived the animals of the opportunity to deposit their young into the water. For the rest, it was merely a matter — and this was the actually difficult part of my task — of keeping the salamanders alive not only for years, but also of offering them living conditions so favorable that they regularly proceeded to reproduce. The construction and arrangement of a terrarium in which this can be achieved I have described in the technical part.

The most immediate consequences of water-deprivation are now different, according to whether the females freshly placed into the container are at the beginning or at the end of a gestation period.

If females which stand immediately before giving birth are brought into the terrarium, a great restlessness takes hold of them: evidently in search of water, they run, quite contrary to their otherwise sluggish habits, incessantly to and fro, indeed they raise themselves up against the glass walls and absolutely want to find a way out of the waterless container. Finally, however, they are seized by the birth-throes: through the cloaca, opened wide as if in a cramp, the larvae emerge, which naturally must perish if they are not taken out in good time and transferred into water. Many females succumb to the excessive exertions of such births, in which the soothing effect of the water was lacking; for which reason it does not appear advantageous to begin the experiments with animals that are in the state of advanced gestation.

If, however, the birth was not yet immediately impending, or if the females at the beginning of the experiment were not yet gravid at all, so that the whole following gestation period, beginning from ovulation, already runs its course within the terrarium, then already at the next litter the beginnings of an adaptation are to be noticed: one part of the larvae is somewhat larger, more advanced; another part, however, in contrast to this, has lagged behind, become stunted, indeed even crippled, incapable of life, or already dead. The favored larvae simply required, since they spent a longer time than usual in the uterus, a greater space and more ample nourishment, so that a part of their siblings necessarily came up short and degenerated into abortive formations, whose significance will have to be appreciated more closely in the statistical part.

From now on, there appear, at every gestation period — of which *Salamandra maculosa*, upon the lapse of hibernation, completes two regularly each year — decided advances. The viable larvae always come into the world larger and nearer to metamorphosis, but also in ever smaller number, while the remainder of the litter has lagged behind and is almost throughout crippled.

If the moss in the terrarium is kept very wet, if the atomizer occasions a greater moisture than the indispensably necessary one, then the adaptation does not come to completion at all: the young are then retained in the uterus only up to an intermediate stage, roughly as Fig. 6, Pl. XIII, represents it. The wetness of the moss prevents the drying-out of the gills, on whose function, however, the larvae, since they at the same time already possess sufficiently developed lungs, are no longer dependent. Even such nearly grown larvae, which were reared in the normal way in water, but whose gills are still in full activity, tolerate it — as experiments by Schenkling [53], Jakob [33], and myself have shown — without harm, when one transfers them directly out of the water into moist moss. For equally developed larvae of *Salamandra atra* this was already mentioned in the previous part.

Supposing, however, that the moisture of the terrarium does not suffice for the survival of such still gill-bearing animals, then two to three years, that is, four to six gestation periods, are required before the adaptation comes to completion and the young come to the light of day either only with tiny, functionless remnants of the gills, or without such but still with open gill-slits, or finally with already grown-together gill-slits as quite finished land-salamanders. These last, slight differences in the developmental state of the newborn young are no longer of importance, since the goal of making aquatic life dispensable for the young is already attained, even when small stumps of gills have still remained standing; all the more so, as this likewise frequently occurs in the young of *Salamandra atra*, and indeed of the young of one and the same set the one can come into the world with closed gill-slits, the other with open slits and dead gill-pieces.

The number of newborn young fire salamanders is not so constant as in the alpine salamander and fluctuates between two and seven.

It is noteworthy that neither the number of gestation periods during a year, nor their duration, depends on whether the young come into the world in the normal way as 25–30 mm long larvae, or as 39–42 mm long earth-newts.

The abortive embryos, which are expelled in a quantity increasing with each pregnancy, now, after the completion of the adaptation, decrease again. I conjectured that conditions in this respect too had now come to agree with those of *Salamandra atra*, namely, that the eggs which do not ripen into viable embryos must serve those [embryos] as nourishment, and that after the first cleavage stages they already merge into the yolk-mush, thus no longer having any opportunity to develop further. It turned out that this conjecture was correct.

Once about 80% of the experimental animals had become accustomed to reproduction without water, and only the remaining 20% became infertile — that is, no longer proceeded to reproduce — I was put in a position to lay out whole series of late-bearing fire salamanders and to examine several of them at various stages of gestation as to the condition of their gestational receptacle.

There then showed itself the presence of a viscous nutritive mush, which in every respect was equal to that of a *Salamandra atra*. If only a single egg in a uterus developed into an embryo, then it was the lowest one, lying nearest to the exit of the uterus (as in *Salamandra atra*); if two eggs developed, then the one again lay at the caudal, the other however at the cranial end of the uterus; with three to four "embryonal eggs" (terminology after Czermak [13]) in one gestational receptacle, these lay at fairly even distances apart from one another, surrounded all around and tightly enclosed by the creamy mass of the fused "embryotroph eggs." These positional conditions remain in place as long as the embryo persists within its egg-membrane; together with the leaving of the same (which occurs much earlier than normal, namely, as in *Salamandra atra*, already before the growth of the hind legs) the embryo attains a greater freedom of movement and frequently comes into collision with its siblings, which manifests itself in bitten-off toes and tail-tips. This is the case still more when the yolk is already entirely consumed: the meanwhile considerably grown embryos then lie, completely filling the uterus, closely one behind another and in stretches even side by side. Besides the embryos and the embryotroph eggs there were also found, a few more times, abortive formations embedded in the latter.

That the embryos of *Salamandra maculosa* nourish themselves from the yolk of the abortive eggs even under normal circumstances, and thus that these become embryotroph eggs, I have been able to establish several times: on the one hand, in that the embryos, upon the opening of the uterus, at times gave off from themselves yolk-threads which had hung out toward their mouth; on the other hand, in that I saw how the larvae, brought together into a dish with the abortive eggs, at once fell greedily upon the former, tore them to shreds and gulped them down. Sachse [52] also observed that the abortive eggs (called by him "larval germs") were gnawed up by the newborn larvae. — The nourishment of the embryos through abortive eggs presumably plays a fairly large role, namely in the mountains, at the upper limit of the distribution range of *Salamandra maculosa*, where the gestational receptacles, as will be set forth more closely in the statistical part, contain relatively few embryos — which before birth reach a considerable size — and very many abortive eggs. Here these are also often already in the process of entering upon the fusion process.

An intra-uterinely grown fire-salamander larva of the third stage (after the disappearance of the yolk mass) differs (cf. Fig. 6, Pl. XIII) from one normally raised in water (Fig. 5) in the following points:

1) The primary dark, almost black coloration has been retained.

2) The tail bears a much narrower fin-seam, often recognizable only with difficulty.

3) The gills have not undergone the change of form described in detail by Clemens [12], which in *Salamandra maculosa* also normally occurs upon the exchange of the sojourn in the uterus with that in the water; they are more delicate and 2–4 mm longer than the water-gills. Precisely with regard to the gills I had expected a much further-reaching differentiation for the embryonal functions, in particular, in analogy with *Salamandra atra*, a more considerable elongation; the more so, as Fischer-Sigwart [21] in salamander larvae, which he [reared] in air-poor water, observed a considerable elongation and an increase of the branching of the gills: in a 40 mm long larva they measured 10 mm, whereas the normal length in air-rich spring water amounts to only 5 mm. — It is possible that in the experimental material lying before me the adaptation to the intra-uterine larval life had not yet persisted long enough to call forth all the consequences of the same.

A fully developed late-birth (Fig. 7 and 8) gives approximately the picture of a young fire salamander whose metamorphosis had been forcibly accelerated through premature withdrawal of water. It differs in the following points from a normally developed young one:

1) The size is smaller (total length 39–42 mm, instead of 45–56 mm).

2) The nutritional condition is, as a rule, not the best — a sign that the yolk-mush was quite scant for two to three embryos in one uterus.

3) The spots are still little developed, dull olive-green and blurred; only after weeks do they take on the intensely yellow color. In individual cases (Fig. 8), however, the marking recedes altogether into the background against the black ground-color; the usually large spots are then dissolved into small spots and points; even the parotids, which otherwise are accustomed to be wholly or almost wholly yellow, are here strewn only with yellow points.

I do not wish to omit pointing out that the whole adaptation process proceeded much more rapidly and easily (with fewer deaths after the first dry-births) in those females which derive from high-lying localities (Series III and XII) than in those from the flat- and hill-country (Series II, XI and XIV, as well as Nos. 2, 4–8 of the individual specimens, cf. the inventory of material). The beginnings of the adaptation are in the mountains, namely, already given by the fact that comparatively few embryos are present which before birth have already left the egg-membrane and have nourished themselves from the numerous abortive eggs, whereas the lowland females bear small larvae, almost always still located within the egg-membrane, whose intra-uterine nourishment through sibling eggs is dispensed with as a consequence of their early birth.

It would now be especially interesting to draw upon a second generation and to test it with respect to its reproduction and its other behavior. The rearing of the young salamanders is indeed child's play: as indicated in the technical part, they can be accustomed to a substitute food in the form of raw meat-fibers. With abundant nourishment the young grow up quickly and already have, in two years, the size of a sexually mature animal, which according to my experiences is always at least four years old. Only the genitalia do not keep pace with this rapid growth, and also do not become functional later. They are wholly stunted and accompanied by an enormously large fat-body. If one sets the substitute food aside and feeds with little earthworms, snails and insect larvae, [and] keeps the animals on the whole short [of food], then nothing is achieved thereby except that the growth slows down and now approximately equals the growth prevailing in freedom; further, that the sex-organs do not become so fatty. But since they are nevertheless collapsed, they can never enter into action.

I intend to keep the late-born young fire salamanders in large, walled open-air terraria specially fitted out for the purpose, and here to leave them quite to themselves; perhaps then the second generation will become capable of procreation.

With these findings concerning reproduction there again run parallel some observations regarding coloration. There can namely be established, with respect to the saturation of the yellow and the distribution of the same upon the black ground-color, local variations which appear to be influenced by a host of diverse factors. Number, size and saturation of the yellow spots stand in direct proportionality to one another: one rarely finds individuals with few but large spots; the reverse — many but small spots — occurs only in the var. corsica Savi (cf. v. Bedriaga [4, pag. 295, 296]). Where there is much yellow, there it is usually also strongly saturated (dark straw- or orange-yellow), whereas sparse yellow, apart from rare exceptions, looks pale (light sulphur- or citron-yellow). With a moderate quantity of yellow the spots appear now in darker, now in lighter shadings, yet as a rule not in one and the same, but in different specimens. Besides this, there is found in *Salamandra maculosa* too, now and then, partial albinism and xanthorism, and indeed both in larvae (see Fischer-Sigwart [22], Landois [40], Spengel [62] and Jakob [32]), as well as in adult specimens (Jakob [34] and Kammerer [36], cf. also the inventory of material, No. 9 of the individual specimens).

Incidentally remarked, the garish coloration of the fire salamander is, in my view, not merely a frightening-color [warning color]; it is, however paradoxical it may sound, at the same time a protective color. Surely everyone who has himself gone into the forest in order to collect has already experienced for himself how difficult it often is, and what a practiced eye is required for it, to recognize a salamander in the midst of the yellow, fallen foliage, [or amid] the yellow egg-fungus [Fuligo] standing out against the black forest floor, and the clavarias, or not to confuse it with a fallen, crooked piece of tree-branch whose bark is partly peeled off and which thus — moreover glistening from the wet — feigns yellow spots. Only seen at immediate proximity does the garishness in the coloration of the fire salamander come into effect; only then does it become a frightening-color.

I will enumerate the findings of local deviations, by the hand of my material and of some literature data, first in geographical order:

1) In specimens from the hill-country (Series XIV, No. 2 of the individual specimens), from the North German lowland plain (Series XI and Nos. 4–8 of the individual specimens), further from South France and Spain (cf. v. Bedriaga [4, pag. 293]), the yellow is represented in very abundant measure; in that the spots on the upper side frequently fuse into broad longitudinal bands, it even becomes at times the predominant color: in South French and Spanish salamanders the dark ground-color recedes almost entirely. The underside too is, in the specimens deriving from there, strongly spotted or even wholly yellow.

2) In specimens from Portugal (var. Molleri, de Bedriaga) [4, pag. 294, 295] there appear, in addition to the mostly numerous yellow blotches — which here too can displace the ground-color — also red spots formed by a special pigment.

3) The same striking phenomenon is shown, on the other hand, also by many specimens from the surroundings of Vienna (Series II, IV, X, XXII and XXIII), in which the red is present in the most diverse shadings (brown-red, gray-red, wine-red, blood-red, cinnabar-red, brick-red), namely on the parotids, on the forehead and crown, in the encircling of the eyes, at the corners of the mouth and on the throat, but occasionally also on the whole rest of the body. Otherwise the Viennese specimens are mostly provided with moderately large and moderately numerous spots, and the saturation of the yellow varies, so that the spots in different specimens at one and the same locality can be light- or dark-yellow.

4) Specimens from the German Mittelgebirge, from the foothills and from the lower montane region of the Alps (Series I, V–IX, XIII, XV, XIX–XXI, XXIV, further Nos. 1 and 3 of the individual specimens) show a quite average stamp, that is, the mostly irregularly shaped and distributed blotches are on the upper side moderately large and moderately numerous, on the underside sparse and little intense. All shadings of the yellow occur extra-individually mixed.

5) Specimens from the alpine region, from the upper limits of the vertical distribution range (Series III, XII and XXVI) let the black ground-color dominate. The yellow spots are small, sparse, of roundish form and mostly very pale. The underside is usually not spotted (cf. also Werner [70, pag. 16]). To Mr. stud. phil. Walter Bendl of Graz I owe the communication that he, in a forest on the Schöckl-Plateau, at a height of about 1230 m, caught a *Salamandra maculosa* with only few, small and not pronouncedly yellow, but rather brownish spots. This is probably also one of the highest hitherto known localities of the fire salamander in Austria-Hungary.

6) Small number and size of the spots are shown also by specimens from the southeast of Europe, e.g. from Orsova (after v. Mojsisovics [46, pag. 48]), Montenegro (after Werner [72]) and Athens [71], generally the Turkish and Greek salamanders (after v. Bedriaga [4, pag. 304]), likewise the specimens from the extreme south of the distribution range (Series XVI of my material and var. algira de Bedriaga [4, pag. 296, 297]).

Not everywhere are the local variations quite constant; thus there occur at times in the alpine region — as Werner [70, pag. 16] too emphasizes for Alpine valleys up to 600 m — fairly strongly and largely spotted ones; further, in the surroundings of Vienna, in the very midst of the either averagely or strongly spotted individuals, also such with only quite few, small and very pale-yellow spots. Indications of red spots on the parotids, on the inner margin of the upper eyelid as well as between corner of the mouth and lower eye-margin are found also in specimens from Series XX and XXIV, further in Nos. 11 and 12 of the individual specimens. A complete constancy is also not at all to be expected, in view of the manifoldness of the factors influencing the spot-marking, of which naturally several mutually counteracting ones often coincide at one and the same place.

A precise examination of the geological, climatic and meteorological conditions of localities — as far as possible those that have become known to me from my own experience, and from museums through voucher specimens, as well as from the literature through descriptions of the specimens in question — has yielded that the color-variations can, in place of the geographical grouping, also be ordered in the following way:

1) Warm climate supports, cold climate suppresses the spot-marking and its saturation.

2) Regions with strong air- and ground-humidity, which possibly are moreover rich in small watercourses, favor number, size and saturation of the yellow spots, whereas dryness and poverty in [bodies of] water cause the same to recede.

3) In regions where slate, primary rock and sandstone form the subsoil, the salamanders are as a rule more numerous, more largely and more intensely yellow-spotted than in limestone-mountains, where they are altogether not so frequently to be found. Possibly this can be traced back to point 2, in that the limestone-mountains are always drier and poorer in [bodies of] water than the mountains composed of the other named kinds of rock.

4) A quite special predominance of the yellow color and its saturation, as well as the appearance of red spots, takes place on loam soil, whereas on black humus the reverse prevails. —

This compilation too is again subject to manifold irregularities, and I would set it down only with reservation if the latter did not, almost always, resolve themselves in a satisfactory way through the coincidence of opposed factors at one and the same locality (e.g. poverty in water, but loam soil; or warm climate, but limestone-mountains, etc.). For this, to be sure, a precise study of the physical conditions of each individual locality is necessary. Besides this, however, the influences to which the spot-marking is subject are also accessible to an experimental testing, in which the individual factors can as far as possible be isolated, or at least the positive ones be separated from the negative ones.

For a long time I was unwilling to lend any credence to the concordant statements of several terrarium-keepers known to me, that the captive fire salamanders become in age "as yellow as a canary-bird"; likewise the observation of v. Schreibers reported in Schweigger [58] and Dalla Torre [14]¹ , that salamanders tended for years in wet loam grow up into giant specimens, seemed to me highly doubtful. Finally I convinced myself of the correctness of both experiences: salamanders kept on moist loam-earth and at a temperature uniform throughout the whole year of 18–22° C. show within three or four years not only a considerable increase in length, but also an increase of the spot-marking. Hereby one distinguishes the following:

1) The ordinary yellow flecks increase: a) in intensity of color, in that pale sulphur-yellow flecks pass over into dark orange-yellow; b) in number, in that new flecks are added to those already present.

The newly arisen flecks, however, are not at once yellow and sharply delimited, but rather at first indistinct, blurred oily-green, and thus correspond exactly to the beginnings of the fleck-marking in the just-transformed young. In the wild too one likewise often finds salamanders which — notably on the underside — bear such dull oily-green flecks, which presumably develop further later on into yellow, or else, if no factors promoting the fleck-marking are present, remain standing at that primitive stage in the midst of the already-yellow blotches. In any case, however, the indistinct oily-green flecks are to be regarded as the immediate forerunners of the sharply standing-out yellow flecks. — c) in extent, in that the flecks already present enlarge, whereby the newly added part likewise at first takes on the dull oily-green coloration and only gradually, passing through a multitude of shadings, attains the intensely yellow color. In the wild too I found, especially half-grown specimens, whose flecks were in stretches already intensely yellow, but for the rest dull oily-green or dirty greenish-yellow.

2) The small whitish-yellow flecklets, which stand together especially on the flanks and on the underside, and indeed almost always in larger clusters, and of which it was already emphasized in the case of *Salamandra atra* that they are well to be kept apart from the large yellow blotches as something quite different, increase only in number, not in extent, for it is precisely characteristic of these flecklets that, at least in the genus *Salamandra*, they always occur quite small; nor [do they increase] in color-intensity, for this does not vary, as the large yellow flecks do, in various nuances; only at their first arising are they somewhat more matte and less pronounced than later.

3) The red flecks have, in my experiments hitherto, increased neither in number nor in extent, nor have they appeared in a specimen where they were originally not present; but they have indeed colored themselves more intensely, in such a way that they passed over from brown-, gray- and wine-red into blood-, cinnabar- and brick-red.

A particularly strong increase of the yellow-coloration I achieved when I kept small salamanders, beginning from the just-overcome metamorphosis, under the conditions indicated. If one gives black garden- or woodland soil in place of the loam, if one keeps the experimental animals at a temperature as low as possible and lets them fall into lethargy in winter, if one finally grants them only the moisture most indispensable for the dampening of the skin, then existing fleck-markings indeed change neither in a positive nor in a negative direction, but the coloration of young, just-transformed salamanders which one rears under such conditions turns out to the disadvantage of the yellow flecks, which present themselves as extremely small and sparse, in roundish form and slight intensity. Perhaps already-laid-down, dull oily-green flecks vanish even in this early stage and yield place to the black ground-color. — That the fleck-marking in late-births, which were brought about by water-scarcity, often greatly recedes, was already mentioned on the occasion of the experiments on reproduction. Such specimens, too, never attain the size of the others; to achieve true dwarf- or stunted forms, however — apart from the application of a hunger-cure, in which case simply plain lean forms come out — would not succeed for me. Probably several generations belong to this.

A few biological experiments on eggs and larvae of *Salamandra maculosa* may now find place here which, although they do not belong directly to the question of the relationship- and descent-affinities of the two salamander species, might nevertheless claim some interest.

That one can fetch larvae, which are either entirely or nearly carried to term, from the uterus by artificial means without this harming their development, is now already an old-known fact, and indeed it succeeds both by operation (SEMPER [59]) and also by causing the pregnant females to give off the larvae prematurely in cold water (HUNTINGTON [30], cited in SEMPER).

On the occasion of a repetition of the experiments of BORN [6] — to bring eggs of *Salamandra maculosa* with embryos, which are still at a very early stage of development (9—10 mm long), to development outside the maternal body — the technical arrangement of this experiment was somewhat modified, so that it yielded these more favorable results: the water in the vessel, in which the eggs lay in rows on a grating of thin glass rods, was not changed, and thereby a disturbance of the eggs and a premature destruction of their delicate envelope was avoided. The water-temperature amounted to 17—20° C. Instead of the GREINER apparatus, a SIMON aeration apparatus was brought into use, and the rings of the outflow-body were screwed together so closely that the finely atomized air-pearls emerging in between likewise caused no swirling-up of the water and, in connection with this, no perforation of the gelatinous envelope. It was the embryos themselves which burst the envelope through their movements earlier than is normally the case, namely already at a total length of only 17—19 mm (instead of at least 23 mm) and before the completed development of the hind legs. — To obtain viable larvae and to rear them up to the metamorphosis and beyond succeeded not only with eggs containing young embryos of 8—10 mm length, but also with such eggs in which scarcely a trace of the embryo was yet to be seen. For the latter, the breeding-trough was filled, in place of water, with physiological common-salt solution, and this was only later, when the embryos were already far advanced, gradually replaced by water.

The experiment of bringing forth larvae from eggs outside the mother-animal has also been undertaken with success by STOFFERT [63]. However, it seems to emerge from his not-quite-clear account that the "fertilized eggs," which he detached from the uterus and let ripen in a special small aquarium on a sieve-bottom of canvas, already contained nearly birth-ripe embryos. At least this conclusion [is to be drawn] from the time of year about which STOFFERT reports having found the fertilized eggs exclusively, namely in spring and in autumn, to raise [them]. It will therefore probably have to be called "fruit-ripe eggs" instead of "fertilized eggs."

Also such eggs, containing more or less advanced embryos, as are found among the birth-ripe embryos, and which I had held to be non-viable abortive formations, could be brought to further development up to the hatching of the larvae, provided that they had not been deformed by pressure on the part of the favored embryos. Insofar, however, as these eggs as a rule delivered larvae with small or larger cripplings, which could not be kept past the metamorphosis, and insofar as they had come to development only in the breeding-trough, but hardly under the harsher conditions of free life, they may nevertheless count as abortive formations.

Some larvae obtained from the abortive eggs with the help of the breeding-trough, however, turned out so well-formed that in this respect they in no way stood behind the other larvae. This unexpected experimental result on the one hand, and on the other hand the observation that many of the abortive eggs expelled at birth simultaneously with the larvae — alongside such as are already dead, or such as one can see at first glance to have a non-developmental, teratological character — possess a quite healthy exterior and actually do, when isolated in the breeding-trough, develop further, lead me to suppose that in *Salamandra maculosa*, alongside viviparity and ovoviviparity, sometimes also, as a throwback to the original form of urodele reproduction, true oviparity occurs; this, however, under the express emphasis that eggs deposited at the same time as the larvae, which are perhaps still capable of germination, could hardly come through in freedom, already for the reason that they are at once devoured by the larvae.

In accordance with the well-founded supposition of an oviparity occurring here and there, the reports present in the literature concerning salamanders which were said to have laid larvae and eggs at the same time are now likewise not entirely to be dismissed. Unfortunately it seldom emerges clearly from the relevant communications of what kind the egg-laying accompanying the live-bearing may have been — whether the embryo ripe for hatching was still surrounded only by the thin little skin, which it bursts at once or a few minutes after birth (ovoviviparity, which forms the rule in the lowlands), or whether the eggs that came into the world, whose flattened, deformed appearance let the incapacity to develop be at once recognized, [were such] — finally whether the eggs had a normal, spherical character similar to that of other amphibian eggs, and whether traces of further development were to be ascertained on them later. It is therefore at any rate not entirely excluded that the much-cited ERBER observation [17] — subjected by SCHWALBE and v. BEDRIAGA to a rectification in different senses — has true oviparity alongside viviparity at its basis; on the other hand, the statement in FRANKE [26] is, in consequence of the expression "fruit-ripe eggs," decidedly not to be traced back to oviparity, but rather to the very ordinary ovoviviparity. — Also in the "Zoologischer Garten," Volume XVI, Issue 5, page 194, mention is made of a salamander said to have laid eggs.

With regard to the regenerative capacity of gills, limbs, tail-fringe and lens, I have nothing new to add to the findings of LEGROS [42], FRAISSE [25], KOCHS [37] and PROWAZEK [49]. The tail-tip seems to be regenerated also by adult or at least half-grown specimens, for one finds not at all seldom specimens whose tail shows malformations permitting such a conclusion; especially I found broadly truncated tail-ends, from which a several-millimeters-long shaft set off abruptly. Pertinent experiments, however, have so far failed. JAKOB [33] mentions that mutilated specimens still, as land-animals in the first weeks after the transformation, replace "torn-off" (?) limbs again.

The behavior of the maculosa-larvae toward different temperatures is in principle the same as that of the atra-larvae; only the former are even more insensitive to cold, so much so that they — as emerges, among other things, from the written communications of RICHTER to FRAUENFELD [27] — can even freeze for a while without taking harm.

As regards the influence of different light-intensities and light-colors, a difference compared with the behavior of the atra-larvae makes itself felt insofar as the chromatophores of the maculosa-larvae show themselves much more capable of transformation. The coloration and marking of the maculosa-larvae turns out differently according to whether these are kept in ordinary or colored glass dishes, in white porcelain or brown clay dishes, and according to whether gravel, sand, loam or moor-earth forms the substrate. The principle, however, that the light-intensity stands in direct proportion to the brightness of the larval color, is here too retained and can also be observed in free life: larvae on loam-ground, in waters simultaneously strongly illuminated, are light and fairly uniform in color; larvae on gravel- or sand-ground possess a body-color that is lighter or darker according to the stronger or weaker illumination of the water and are variously flecked, clouded and marbled; larvae finally that live in dark cisterns and caves have kept their primary dark, almost uniformly black-brown embryonal color fairly unaltered. Accordingly, the larvae found by WACQUANT-GEOZELLES [67] in a subterranean well, whose extraordinarily light color he emphasizes in contradiction to what has just been communicated, were probably partial albinos.

It now remains to remember my experiments on neoteny. Cases of partial neoteny have been observed repeatedly in *Salamandra maculosa*, and indeed both in freedom and in captivity [3, 15, 54, 56, 62, 69, 74]; Nos. 13, 14 and 15 of the single specimens which were enumerated in the material-record are likewise to be reckoned in. Many larvae remain — without a definite cause for this being findable — far beyond the normal point in time of the metamorphosis in the water, while yet others, despite the precautions taken for the inhibition of the metamorphosis (see these in the analogous experiments on *Salamandra atra*) and without having come into contact with the air, lose their gills and then perish if one does not in good time set them on dry ground. Particularly suitable individuals for the calling-forth of neotenic phenomena are those on which regeneration-experiments are carried out at the same time, for these regularly retain — probably in consequence of the greater material-consumption (WOLTERSTORFF [74]) — for a long time, after the formerly amputated body-parts have replaced themselves again, the larval form, without their general body-growth thereby lagging especially behind: they thus become genuinely neotenic, not hunger-larvae! Furthermore, those larvae are best suited which are reared artificially from eggs in the breeding-trough. The partially neotenic animal represented by Fig. 9, Pl. XIII, which by a slight swelling of the cloacal region already lets the approach of sexual maturity be recognized, and which at a total length of 89 mm exceeds the largest larvae that have become known in the literature (v. BEDRIAGA mentions such of 75, v. SCHREIBERS such of 80 mm)

by yet a considerable amount, has passed through the following developmental course:

The egg with the approximately 7 mm long embryo had been operated out of the uterus of a female of the IXth series on 30 September 1897 and incorporated into the breeding-trough. On 16 November it left the egg-envelope with a length of 16.5 mm; the hind legs were only fully developed ten days later. On 4 January 1898 the larva had attained the average length of those born in the normal manner, namely 25 mm. In the course of January it increased by 6 mm with abundant feeding. The growth in the following months was a wonderfully regular one, only it became, during each 30 days, smaller by half a millimeter in arithmetic series; thus the larva in December 1898 had grown only by the half millimeter and now possessed a length of 84 mm. Up to the day of its metamorphosis, 20 December 1899, it had become larger by a further 5 mm. — Whereas that larva of *Salamandra atra* which had shown the phenomenon of partial neoteny most pronouncedly had spent not quite a year (19 days were lacking from it) in the water, the latter [period] was, in the case of the maculosa-larva, two years and (reckoned from the leaving of the egg-envelope) 34 days. In this, both larvae, if one takes into account the specific size-difference between *Salamandra maculosa* and *atra*, stood on the day of their metamorphosis at approximately the same growth-stage. Whether it will succeed to make, out of fire salamanders — which indeed, despite their terrestrial mode of life, depend on the water much more than the alpine species — permanently water-inhabiting animals, is only by a few degrees more probable than in the case of the latter; the most promising way to this goal seems to me to be to draw, from such animals as have remained larvae for a long time, one or more generations capable of reproduction, and to keep the larvae each time under conditions such as are most favorable to neoteny.

That partially neotenic larva mentioned just before had, already in the course of July 1898 — self-evidently with retention of the most important larval characters, the gills and the fin-fringe on the tail — taken on more and more the appearance of a perfect land-salamander: the outer integument let all the glands, not forgetting the bulging parotids, stand out distinctly; the coloration and marking, deep black with sharply delimited yellow flecks, likewise already resembled that of the finished land-animal. As in all neotenic urodele-forms, the gills here too were reduced to the most necessary [minimum]; here a predominance of the gill-body makes itself noticeable; of the gill-filaments nothing more is to be recognized than a row of quite small, wart-shaped formations, which, on close inspection, lend to the edge of the thick, fleshy gill-body a lobed exterior. A similar shape is possessed by the gills of neotenic Triton-larvae and of old neotenic larvae of *Amblystoma mexicanum*.

## VI. Statistics of the Uterine Contents of Both Salamander Species.

In place of introductory remarks, I should like to set at the head of these statistical investigations the following three sentences from SCHWALBE's treatise "Zur Biologie und Entwickelungsgeschichte von Salamandra atra und maculosa" [On the Biology and Developmental History of *Salamandra atra* and *maculosa*] [57]:

1) "For future accounts it is desirable always to state the size and number of the embryos found in the uterus, as well as the number of abortive eggs, since these factors can be of importance for assessing the developmental conditions" (pag. 388, footnote).

2) "It is to be hoped that, through further numerous protocollings which simultaneously take into account the condition of the ovary, eggs or sperm in the oviduct, embryos in the uterus or empty uterus, and the occurrence of spermatozoa in the SIEBOLD'sche [SIEBOLD's] sacs, a greater regularity in the development of *Salamandra maculosa* will gradually be found. Naturally one will have to proceed in the same way for *Salamandra atra*" (pag. 388).

3) "It is most highly desirable to compare, in a larger material of *Salamandra maculosa*, the number and size of the embryos and abortive eggs with one another and with the volume of the mother animal, whereby, however, the material would have to be carefully separated according to locality and vertical distribution. Here one will doubtless have the opportunity to observe constant local differences. Such investigations would prove especially interesting in the regions where the two salamander species come into contact. By this route it does not seem to me impossible to arrive at results which would render the differences in the development of *Salamandra maculosa* and *atra* less abrupt, and so perhaps to gain insights into the processes which here have given occasion to the transformation of species" (pag. 396, concluding sentence of the work).

On those salamander series which have passed through my hands since the SCHWALBE'sche [SCHWALBE's] treatise became known to me, I have conscientiously followed the cited instructions of the meritorious researcher, and I give in the following a summarizing overview of my findings, in so far as they concern the uterine contents, which alone come into consideration here. Perhaps an opportunity will arise to publish elsewhere, on some other occasion, my notes on the conditions of the remaining female genital tract at various seasons and at various localities — notes which in fact, as SCHWALBE foresaw, make much in the developmental conditions of both salamander species appear clearer and more regular than hitherto.

Regarding the locality and time of the series listed in the following two tables, one may compare the material-record [Materialnachweis]; only the sea-level elevation of the locality have I here once more appended, in order to make an immediate overview possible. The investigations of each series were always undertaken, as far as possible, immediately following the capture of the same, so that the conditions prevailing in the uterus at any given time can without further ado be referred to the time of capture, or at any rate to the time of acquisition.

With regard to the terminology of the tables, it must be recalled that by "abortive formations" [Abortivgebilde] are to be understood, in general, germs that have remained at a subordinate stage and are incapable of life; "abortive eggs" [Abortiveier] I call those that have not advanced beyond the first cleavage stages; "abortive embryos" [Abortivembryonen] those in which more or less advanced, mostly crippled embryos are already clearly perceptible. SCHWALBE calls the "main embryo" [Hauptembryo] the life-capable embryo which attains the completion of its development, and which in *Salamandra atra* normally occurs as the only one in a uterus; "secondary embryos" [Nebenembryonen] is SCHWALBE's name for the abortive embryos sometimes to be met with alongside the main embryo in the uterus of *Salamandra atra*.

SCHWALBE obtains a constant expression for the volume of a salamander by multiplying together the total length (from the tip of the snout to the tip of the tail), the breadth of the trunk midway between the two pairs of limbs, and the height — the dorso-ventral diameter of the trunk at the same place.

The comparison of the total volume of the embryos with that of the mother animal then yields, expressed in percent, the share of volume which the former claim within the maternal body. Since the embryos, especially in *Salamandra maculosa* from high-lying find-spots, are often very unequally developed — in such a way that some have already attained readiness for birth, while the remainder still remain in the uterus for days, indeed weeks, and come into the world at various intervals — it does not suffice to measure a single embryo from each gravid female with respect to its length, breadth and height, and then to find the total volume in the product of these dimensions multiplied by the number of embryos; rather, one must not let it vex one to carry out the mentioned measurements on each individual embryo: only the sum of all these volume-measurements then yields a tolerably accurate result usable for comparison with the volume of the mother animal.

It must further be noted that the number of abortive eggs found in the uterus can by no means always be quite correctly ascertained: for as soon as some embryos already find themselves outside the egg-membrane, the possibility is also already given that any abortive eggs that may have been present have been wholly or partly devoured. This is indeed proved by some embryos whose entire digestive tract is filled with the yolk of abortive eggs. The ascertainment of the latter becomes wholly impossible in those fire-salamander females which come from a height of 1000 m and above: the boundaries between the individual abortive eggs, which here are already on the point of entering upon the fusion-process [Schmelzungsprocess] that has become the norm in *Salamandra atra*, are indistinct or no longer distinguishable at all, even if one should by chance still encounter all the embryos within the egg-membrane, so that at least no diminution of the abortive eggs through being eaten is to be assumed. I remember these circumstances very well still from the treatment of Series III (caught above Lofer, at about 1000 m height), although the SCHWALBE'sche [SCHWALBE's] work had not then yet appeared and I had therefore as yet made no numerical records about it, and I have now lately found the same again confirmed in Series XXVI.

1) Not only contrary to the statements of KNAUER [39], that the number of males amounts to scarcely one percent of the total number of individuals, but also in contradiction to other authors who, like e.g. BENECKE [5], have found the number of males and that of the females to be equal, I found as a rule a considerable preponderance of the former.

2) However various the seasons in which the female salamanders were also caught (cf. the material-record), in part they nevertheless always contained embryos of various stages, from quite young ones on up to those ready for birth; and indeed these stages occur not only in the uterus of various specimens caught at the same time, but also in the uterus of one and the same specimen.

From the fact that the occurrence of birth-ready embryos in the uterus is bound to no definite season, one ought really already to draw the conclusion that for the native land-salamanders no definite reproductive period exists. In *Salamandra atra* this is also fully the case: this species is, in the time — already so short anyway — during which it is not compelled to keep its winter sleep (according to the altitude, beginning of May to beginning of October as the maximum, end of June to beginning of September as the minimum), uninterruptedly occupied with mating and giving birth. As alpine salamanders kept captive under natural conditions prove, the female completes two gestation periods [Trächtigkeitsperioden] in the year. It is scarcely conceivable that more of these come about (at least two, as CZERMAK [13] states; two to three, as M. v. CHAUVIN [9]; and several, as WIEDERSHEIM [73] states) in the still shorter time available in free life. Rather, I believe that *Salamandra atra* gives birth twice only in the montane and lower alpine region, but in the higher alpine and in the snow region (concerning the delimitation of these regions see TSCHUDI's Thierleben der Alpenwelt [Animal Life of the Alpine World]) unconditionally only once. The acceleration of the whole development, made possible by the uniform and protected conditions of the uterus — which we have come to know also in the late births of *Salamandra maculosa* — is in any case already an astonishingly great one.

In *Salamandra maculosa*, too, individual births occur in every season that does not belong to the winter sleep. I know individual small brooks in the environs of Vienna, in which from April to October freshly deposited larvae, 25 mm long, are to be found in every month, and I have likewise observed at the most varied other find-spots that, during the months of July, August, September, newborns were here and there added, after nocturnal rain-showers, to the larger larvae already deposited earlier. Yet these are after all exceptions. The actual reproductive period, during which by far the most births take place, and which coincides with the mating period, is nevertheless concentrated in *Salamandra maculosa* upon definite, mostly two consecutive months, earlier or later according to the climate; but the birth-ready embryos present in the uterus in every season are in all probability chiefly due to the fact that the supply of sperm preserved in the SIEBOLD'sche [SIEBOLD's] sacs is brought to its purpose — to fertilize the ripe ovarian eggs that have passed over into the oviduct — at any arbitrary time, perhaps one left to the discretion of the female. — In the Vienna region the reproductive and mating period comprises the months of April and May; in the North and in the mountains, May and June; in the South, February and March, as well as here a second time from the end of September to the middle of November. Cases of twofold giving-birth — in spring and autumn — also occur in our regions, in wind-sheltered places and in years with favorable, warm weather, apparently not at all so seldom, without however, as in the South, forming the rule. The larvae then naturally overwinter under the ice, which can happen all the more easily since even in the smallest brooks a channel remains over which does not freeze even in severe cold. But should nevertheless a freezing to the bottom occur once, this can only be temporary and, as experiments prove, can do the larvae no harm. Besides, many spring-pools possess, like FISCHER-SIGWART's "Brünnele" [little well] at the Born [21], a temperature that is indeed low but constant throughout the whole year. On the other hand, again, the number of annual births can be limited precisely in warmer climates to a single one, namely where the small waters serving for the deposition of the larvae periodically dry up. Here the summer- or dry-sleep of the salamander brings about the same thing as the winter sleep brings about in temperate and cold climates: the (connected with the on the whole reduced-to-a-minimum vital activity)

almost complete standstill of the intra-uterine embryonic development. Since even captive, well-acclimatized specimens of *Salamandra maculosa* complete one gestation period in every six months, and the so closely related *Salamandra atra* behaves the same way, since further, as I have observed, even in regions where, on account of the early-arriving frost, a renewed laying-time [Legezeit] in autumn cannot follow that in spring, a renewed mating period nevertheless takes place in early autumn — so I regard the annually twofold reproduction as the normal, original one for both salamander species. The hydrographic and climatic conditions of various observation-sites, as well as the individual fluctuations (dependent on the age and state of vigor of the animal), have given occasion to such a quantity of opposing, mostly generalizing views about the reproductive period in *Salamandra maculosa*, that the discussion of this literature alone would fill many pages. It suffices if I refer to the works of PARÄTRE [47] and v. BEDRIAGA [4], which summarize and set against one another those statements, and which are furnished with rich literature-lists.

It is obvious, in connection with the observations on the periodicity of the uterine contents, to give also a description of the mating, all the more since this nowhere happens in the literature: always only the long-drawn-out mating preliminaries, the courtship-play, were seen, but never the moment of the transfer of semen. Since this subject does not belong immediately to the theme of the present work, and since I do not wish to anticipate a large work, soon to appear, on the urodeles of the palaearctic region — in which work, as I learn by letter from its author W. WOLTERSTORFF, an exact description of the mating in the land-salamanders will finally also be contained — I restrict myself to the following brief remarks: the whole procedure, which I have often observed from beginning to end and which exhibits no kind of specific differences in the two salamander species, can take place both on land and also in the water. In the former case it is made possible by a violent turning, I should like to say wrenching, on the part of the male, that, despite the peculiar amplexus, seemingly most unfavorable for this purpose (ZELLER [75]), the genital openings approach one another so far that the spermatophore can be pressed over directly into the female cloaca.

3) However great the fluctuations may be in individual cases with respect to the number of embryos and abortive eggs, which stand in inverse proportionality to one another, the arithmetic mean nevertheless already reveals the influence of even slight differences in the elevation of the locality, and indeed in such a way **that the abortive formations increase with increasing elevation of the locality, while the embryos at the same time decrease and, up to the onset of readiness for birth, attain a more advanced development.** — The fluctuations of the individual findings arise not only through the convergence of contrary factors in one and the same find-district (e.g. a high but wind-sheltered situation; a harsh climate, but favorable hydrographic conditions, etc.); they therefore depend not only on differing physical conditions of the locality, but also on individual conditions of the female in question, her age and state of vigor, as well as on whether she has been freshly mated, or has had to subsist for several gestation periods, without renewed mating, on her store of sperm in Siebold's sacs (the "receptaculum seminis"). For a single mating suffices, namely, as I have learned, for the fertilization of three to four sets of ovulations, and hence for the completion of just as many gestation periods. The inversely proportional relationship between embryos and abortive eggs nevertheless remains in and of itself always, and even intra-individually, in force: if the one uterus of one and the same female contains more embryos than the other, then the expectation is justified of finding more abortive eggs here than there.

4) A smaller number of large embryos lays claim to the maternal uterus more than a large number of small embryos.

Nevertheless, the spatial claim upon the uterus appears to cause the female no distress, or at least these [discomforts] are at any rate beyond all comparison with those caused by the bearing of numerous young. To bear few, even if large, young means for the females a decidedly lesser exertion than the reverse; this is best proven by the numerous deaths of the female fire salamanders after and during birth (cf. Wacquant-Geozelles [66, 67]), which are caused either directly by the labor pains, or by the fact that the female drowns when, exhausted by giving birth, it can no longer leave the water.

This fact offers occasion once again for a purely biological digression. Fräulein v. Chauvin concerns herself, namely, on pp. 327 and 328 [9] with the question of how it comes about that Salamandra atra is just as common as Maculosa, even though the former each time brings only two young into the world at once. Her first explanatory ground (sheltered conditions in the uterus and the readiness of the weapon, the skin secretion, at birth) is to be agreed with without reservation; likewise the third (fewer hostile animals in the high mountains); less cogent is the second ground, concerning the predatory fish which are supposed to make away, in the waters where the Maculosa larvae are deposited, with these [larvae]. Knauer [39] has already rightly corrected this to the effect that in the small brooks which as a rule form the dwelling-waters of the larvae, no fish, and indeed in general no animals superior to the larvae, are to be found. In some such rivulets, to be sure, I did find large leeches (Aulastomum gulo Mq. Tand.), and it is conceivable that these inflict damage on the stock of salamander larvae.

Two quite different factors, however, are those which seem to me chiefly to bring about the equal frequency of the two salamander species, that is, the reduction of the Maculosa to the measure of the Atra: first, the fact just emphasized, that the females of the first species very frequently perish in consequence of birth; second, the circumstance that the small spring-waters in the mountains often either dry up or, through prolonged downpours of rain, are transformed into raging mountain torrents and carry the salamander larvae away with them, whereby these probably meet their death. This experience I have found confirmed in the mountains and at the foot of the mountains at the most diverse places: as soon as the high water had receded, nothing more of the larvae was to be seen; at most that, after some days, individual specimens which had probably crept into deep crevices in the rock and thereby been saved came once more into view. — A third thing, too, must not be overlooked: to Salamandra maculosa the possibility is given of spreading itself further within its dwelling-region. Although one cannot by any means describe the earth-salamanders [Erdsalamander = fire salamanders] as fond of wandering, and their occurrence within a find-district is for the most part restricted to a comparatively narrow circuit, there nevertheless occur, at the time of propagation, when the females make their way to the sometimes quite distant waters, migrations which can lead to a further spreading; and this can likewise take place when the freshly transformed young creep onto land and seek out a habitat agreeable to them. In Salamandra atra, by contrast, these two occasions to wander fall away [in Wegfall]; moreover, this species is much more bound to its respective locality, a narrowly delimited mountain plateau, an alpine meadow, by the fact that such a locality is frequently surrounded all around by terrain inhospitable to the newts, further by the fact that migration to a neighboring elevation could for the most part be accomplished only by passing through deep valleys — which, however, present to the Alpine salamander just such an obstacle as a stream or a sea does to other animals. It is thus comprehensible that a colony restricted to so small a region, despite weak propagation, gradually attains a considerable number of individuals. In those find-districts, finally, where the ranges of distribution of Salamandra maculosa and atra touch, yet another ground for the equal frequency of the two is added: the differences in their mode of propagation are here no longer so abrupt, inasmuch as Maculosa too brings forth only a comparatively small number of larvae, the Atra on the other hand sometimes somewhat more than the normal pair; moreover the latter here completes, in consequence of the extraordinary acceleration of the development of its embryos, two gestation periods in the year, but Maculosa only one — which, in view of the numbers of embryos brought close to one another, can at any rate contribute to an equalization. — — It has been said that embryos and abortive eggs stand in inverse proportionality to one another, and that the former, with increasing elevation of the locality, in the case of Salamandra maculosa enlarge themselves and at the same time diminish in number, whereas the latter increase. The increase of the abortive formations on the one hand, the diminution of the embryos and the simultaneous progress in development of the birth-ripe embryos on the other hand, now reach their culmination point in Salamandra atra, where as a rule only one embryo, which is born only at the end of its larval life, comes to maturity in each uterus, while all the remaining eggs become abortive eggs, that is, remain standing at a low developmental stage. But inasmuch as in Salamandra atra at low-lying find-sites there are comparatively often present (cf. also the findings of Czermak [13], v. Siebold [60], Wiedersheim [73], and Schwalbe [57]) so-called accessory embryos [Nebenembryonen] and such abortive eggs as have not become mingled with the pulp of the nutritive eggs, but have passed through a few more cleavages — indeed in rare cases even two principal embryos [Hauptembryonen] are present in one uterus — there takes place, in regions where Salamandra maculosa and atra touch one another, **a formal transition between the propagation-forms of the two species.** Finally, the circumstance that in Salamandra atra all, or at least most, of the abortive eggs, through flowing together, constitute a yolk-pulp also constitutes no specific peculiarity of this species; for in the fire salamander too, at the upper limits of its vertical range of distribution, quite the same melting-process [Schmelzungsprocess] sets in, even if less completely and regularly: the surplus abortive eggs become here, too, embryotroph eggs [Embryotroph-Eier].

For the rest, the contact of the two ranges of distribution, as far as the existing experiences reach, never goes so far as to a mixing of the two species at one and the same locality. They are, precisely, as was already remarked in the introduction, vicariating species. Between the uppermost limit of the Maculosa-region and the lowermost limit of the Atra-region there is always found a completely salamander-free altitudinal belt, which is, to be sure, subject to local fluctuations, but on the average may be taken at from 900 to 1000 m above sea level (e.g. Loferer Kalkalpen, Hoher Göll, Achenthal, Glärnisch). Only in regions where Maculosa is lacking does Atra descend lower into the valley — in Switzerland, according to Tschudi [65], down to 600 m — while conversely Maculosa ascends up to 1000, indeed 1200 m, where Atra does not occur (once again in some regions of Switzerland, according to Fatio [18]; further on the Schneeberg in Lower Austria [68, 69], on the Schöckl plateau in Styria, at various points around Bozen and in the high Tátra).

**Statistical investigations into coloration and marking** yield, as has already been set out in the experimental part, transitional phenomena between the two salamander species, which run parallel with those of propagation: the yellow blotches of Salamandra maculosa diminish and become smaller in the alpine region, while in Salamandra atra in the montane region small yellow spots appear. The transition is by no means a complete or gradual one, but a certain tendency to form transitional forms is decidedly unmistakable.

Unbridged, however, there remains the specific **difference in size** of the two species: the adult Salamandra maculosa attains, according to v. Bedriaga [4], 150–285 mm, Salamandra atra only 119–158 mm in length. Neither the experiment nor the statistical investigations have hitherto been able to establish dwarf forms of the Maculosa or giant forms of the Atra.

## VII. Results¹⁾ and Conclusions.

### A. Results obtained by the experimental path.

1) Embryos of Salamandra atra which are freed by operation from the uterus of the maternal animal can be reared in water already from the "second stage" on (after leaving the egg-membrane).

2) The gills, the fin-seam on the tail, the entire external integument, and the color of the upper side undergo, in those artificially obtained larvae, changes which are conditioned by aquatic life.

3) The most important of these changes concern the gills, which adapt to the following modes of aquatic respiration:

a) Through partial resorption and molting (formation of a coarser, pigment-rich epithelium, casting off of the old epithelium), with simultaneous restriction of the capillary vessels.

> ¹⁾ Only those results are enumerated in this overview which bear immediately upon the actual task of the work, namely to furnish contributions to the knowledge of the relationships of affinity.

b) Through casting off of the intra-uterine gills and regeneration of new, normal-looking gills, which already possess the properties necessary for respiration in water.

c) Through casting off of the intra-uterine gills and regeneration of monstrous respiratory organs.

4) Females of Salamandra atra which come from the lowermost limits of the vertical range of distribution of this species sometimes voluntarily deposit their young in the larval state into the water (habitual premature births), whereby the number of the young is at the same time increased.

5) Females of Salamandra maculosa which are deprived of the opportunity to deposit their young into the water retain these in the uterus until their completed metamorphosis (habitual late births), whereby the number of the young is at the same time diminished.

6) The intra-uterine conditions of such late-bearing females of Salamandra maculosa resemble those of Salamandra atra: the portion of the eggs gained through the diminution of the embryos flows apart, already after the first cleavage stages, into a yolk-pulp, which serves the former [the embryos] as nourishment.

7) Intra-uterine Maculosa larvae of the "third stage" (after consumption of the yolk-pulp) are distinguished from those of equal size which live in the water by the following features:

a) The gills retain the form necessary for the embryonal function.

b) The fin-seam on the tail is very narrow.

c) The primary dark coloration remains in existence.

8) Newborn metamorphosed young of Salamandra maculosa are distinguished from those which have spent their larval life in the water by smaller size (hunger-forms) and by the receding of the yellow marking in comparison with the black ground-color.

9) The larval form of both salamander species can be retained, without inhibition of natural growth, far beyond the usual time of metamorphosis (partial neoteny).

10) On loam-earth, at relatively high temperature and strong moisture-content of the air and of the soil, in Salamandra atra whitish-yellow points and likewise colored small spots appear.

11) The spots of Salamandra maculosa increase in number, size, and color-saturation when the animals are kept on loam-earth, as well as at relatively high temperature and high degree of moisture of the air and of the soil.

12) In young, just-transformed Salamandra maculosa which are kept on black earth, at low temperatures, and at the minimum of the moisture necessary for them, there shows itself a predominance of the black ground-color to the disadvantage of the yellow spots.

### B. Results obtained by the statistical path.

1) Embryos and abortive formations stand in inverse proportionality to one another.

2) The abortive formations increase in Salamandra maculosa with increasing elevation of the locality, while the embryos at the same time diminish and, up to birth, attain a riper developmental stage. — The same process can also take place elsewhere than in the mountains, when the physical conditions are similar to those there (harsh climate, unfavorable hydrographic conditions).

3) The abortive eggs of Salamandra maculosa undergo, at high points of the vertical range of distribution, a partial melting-process and serve the embryos as nourishment.

4) At the lower limit of the vertical ranges of distribution of Salamandra atra there sometimes sets in an increase of the principal embryos (up to two in each uterus). The occurrence, too, of accessory embryos and of such abortive eggs as have not become mixed with the nutritive pulp, but have passed through a few more cleavages, is comparatively frequent at low-lying find-sites of this species.

5) At low-lying localities of Salamandra atra there are not infrequently specimens with small, whitish-yellow spots.

6) The yellow spots of Salamandra maculosa dominate, as regards number, size, and color-saturation, in regions with a warm climate, high degrees of moisture, and on loam-soil.

7) The yellow spots of Salamandra maculosa decrease in number, size, and color-saturation with increasing elevation of the locality. The same process can also take place in lower situations, when the physical conditions occasion it. (Harsh climate, dryness of the air and of the soil, on black humus.)

To what extent, then, can the results obtained be turned to account for the answering of the principal question of the present work, the question concerning the relationships of affinity of the two European salamander species?

The results of the experimental path are, as even a fleeting overview teaches, in many respects analogous to those of the statistical path, and may be summarized in a single sentence as follows:

Both salamander species have in common the endeavor to form transitions to one another, which speaks for a very intimate affinity between them.

As regards the transitional phenomena of coloration and markings, the matter stands as follows: the disappearance of the spots in the rough high mountains, the increase of the same in the valley and on the plain, is nothing other than a special case of the general property of organisms to unfold the energy of colors and forms more luxuriantly under favorable living conditions, but to restrict it under scanty conditions. In particular, the becoming-darker and simultaneous becoming-smaller in the mountains (or under physical conditions corresponding to these) is common to many animals which have not the slightest morphological affinity to one another; Leydig [43, pag. 112 and 113, footnote] cites Heer, in that he points to an analogy among the beetles, and reports from his own experience on the becoming-smaller and becoming-black of Chrysomela cerealis L. and related species in the high mountains. A whole long series of analogous cases could be cited among the butterflies: in a list pertaining to this matter, which Herr E. Galvagni kindly placed before me, eleven native species are enumerated, of which I would mention as especially striking the colline and montane form of Parnassius Apollo L. (increase of the gray dusting), Pieris Napi L. with the alpine-nordic var. Bryoniae O., Lycaena Arion L. with var. obscura Frey, Hesperia Serratulae Esp. with the var. Caecus Frr., and Mamestra Serena Fabr. with the var. obscura Stgr. Among the snails it is especially Helix arbustorum L. var. alpicola Tsch. (smaller than the parent form and with an almost uniformly dark-brown shell) that is to be named, furthermore also Helix pomatia L., which indeed exhibits the phenomenon in question only irregularly (thus perhaps still stands at the initial stage of it!), Arion empiricorum Fer., and Limax cinereo-niger L. Among the amphibians, Molge cristata Laur., Bombinator pachypus Bp., Bufo vulgaris Laur., and Rana temporaria L. show distinct beginnings of the mentioned phenomenon; among the reptiles it is to be found in Vipera berus L. (with the alpine-nordic var. prester L.), Tropidonotus natrix L. (with the predominantly alpine var. nigra Nord.), Lacerta vivipara Jacq.

(with var. nigra Sturm) and Lacerta oxycephala D. B. (with var. Tommasinii Schreiber), and in their beginnings also in Lacerta agilis L., muralis Laur., as well as in Anguis fragilis L. Among the mammals, the melanic forms of the Sunda panther (Felis variegata Wagn. var. melas) and of the jaguar (Felis onca L. var. nigra Erxl.) are mountain-dwellers; furthermore I found, which may not yet be known, at Sulden (1845 m), at the Erfurt Hut in the Achenthal (1800 m), and at the Wildkirchli on the Säntis (1477 m), that our house mouse (Mus musculus L.), alongside the ordinary gray form, occurs in numerous, conspicuously smaller and more delicately built nigrinos.

The causes of the becoming-smaller are without difficulty to be recognized in the less favorable, at times downright wretched living conditions of the higher mountains, perhaps also in the isolation of many high-alpine animals to narrowly bounded places — as is precisely the case with Salamandra atra at most of its sites of discovery — and in the consequent unavoidable inbreeding.

The causes of the becoming-darker Werner [70, pag. 11 and 12] subjects to a brief discussion. As the principal cause I regard, in agreement with Werner, the climatic conditions: since the darkest color absorbs the relatively greatest number of warmth-rays, dark- and black-colored animals can still live in the mountains at temperatures at which their many-colored congeners and species-mates of the valley and the plain would no longer find their subsistence. As a second cause I would, like Werner, allow the adaptation to the soil-type to hold good: although the statistically and experimentally found facts prove that Salamandra maculosa, on black humus, inclines toward reduction, but on ochre-yellow loamy earth toward a preponderance of the yellow spots, and suggest the assumption of a mimicry-phenomenon, I would nevertheless not so much think here of such a thing as of chemical and light-effects not yet more closely known. With the third explanation which Werner gives for the arising of melanic forms I cannot declare myself in agreement, since my investigations, by the experimental as well as by the statistical path, prove the very opposite. Werner makes a strong soil-moisture responsible for the becoming-darker, and adduces as evidence an experiment easily to be carried out on land-frogs: the frogs, namely, become dark if one keeps them wet, but light if one keeps them dry. In my opinion this experiment offers no analogue for the arising of melanic forms; the becoming-dark of wet-kept frogs, which sets in already within a few hours and just as quickly lifts again after the frogs' return to dry ground — even when the sojourn on wet soil had lasted for months — is evidently to be traced back to quite differently constituted pigment-reactions than those which prevail in melanism. Precisely one of those land-frogs, Rana temporaria L., proves this, in that in dry locations it assumes a simple brown, in the high mountains a dark-red to chocolate-brown color, whereas in moist forests of the middle mountains and hill country, in gorges and ravines, and there where it is called to replace Rana esculenta L. and, like it, lives at the margin of the ponds and brooks (many regions of Galicia, northern Hungary, and Bohemia), it acquires an upper side richly besprinkled with light and dark, vividly colored spots and very variegated in appearance [35]. I am inclined, on the contrary, to ascribe to a relative dryness of the soil and the air a share in the formation of the melanisms. In fact the air and the meadow-soil of the high Alps — I am now thinking again specially of the abodes of the earth salamander — are, despite countless mists and downpours, far poorer in moisture than the air and soil in the forests, where the water content, in consequence of the seclusion by the trees, in consequence of the rich vegetation and the many decaying substances, can maintain itself longer. Now, however, Salamandra maculosa is predominantly a forest-dweller, whereas I have at least found Salamandra atra, even where it occurs down to the tree region, never in forests, but at most in dwarf-pine stands, mostly however on open meadows and pastures. In the southeast of Europe, where Salamandra maculosa is compelled to live not only in water-poor regions, but also frequently outside the forests, in places which, though moist, yet bear no comparison with the dull, stagnant moisture of the forests (in small bosquets and park grounds, in vineyards, in caves and cellars, in the masonry which encloses the cisterns), the influence of the dryness even surpasses that of the warm climate, and there is shown there, quite as in the mountains, a recession of the yellow spots.

When, therefore, Salamandra maculosa, through partial loss of the spotted marking, in some places exhibits an approximation to Salamandra atra, this is only a convergent adaptive phenomenon, which belongs to many, even non-related animals; and the small whitish-yellow spots which are now and then to be found in Salamandra atra at low sites of discovery represent a primordial urodele character, and are therefore also not to be brought precisely into relation with the similar spots of the maculosa. The transitional phenomena of coloration and markings can therefore not, or only conditionally — with regard to the remaining findings and to the so concordant anatomical structure of both salamander forms — be brought forward as arguments for their close affinity.

Otherwise it stands with the transitional phenomena of reproduction. The remarkable form of the same in Salamandra atra is not further widespread in the animal kingdom, but stands, in the whole class of the amphibians, so far as is hitherto known, unique. When now Salamandra atra, as an atavism, returns to the mode of multiplication of the maculosa, and the development of the latter can be forced upon it by experiment; when furthermore Salamandra maculosa, under artificial compulsion, takes up the reproduction of the atra, and beginnings toward this can be demonstrated also in free life, in the border districts of both ranges of distribution, — then the mere assumption of a very close affinity no longer suffices, but in its place must step the well-founded supposition of a dichotomous descent from a common ancestral species. That Salamandra atra is the more highly differentiated, phylogenetically younger species, of this there can be no doubt; Salamandra maculosa would thus stand nearer to the ancestral species, if it does not itself represent the ancestral species, from which the atra would be directly derivable.

What, finally, the statistical findings had already made probable, the experiment has proved through its technical foundation: the cause of the peculiar reproductive form, as it occurs in Salamandra atra, rests in the lack, in the higher mountains, of waters suitable for the depositing of the larvae, which already v. Schreibers [56] and, following him, all later authors correctly grasped.

We are thus given essential points of support for allowing the processes which, in the special case of Salamandra maculosa and atra, have accomplished the species-transformation to be, if not recognized with unconditional certainty, yet supposed with great probability.

## IX. Explanation of the Figures.

### Plate XIII.

The figures were drawn (with the exception of Fig. 4 and 9) by Herr Adolf Kasper in the II. zoological Institute of the University of Vienna; Fig. 2a, 3a, 5a and 6a enlarged, the rest at natural size. — The originals are located: 1–3 and 5–8 in the developmental-mechanics collection of the Biological Experimental Institute in Vienna; 4 and 9 have metamorphosed into lung-breathing land forms.

**Fig. 1.** Embryo of Salamandra atra at the end of Schwalbe's "second stage" (the yolk-pulp in the uterus consumed down to a few remnants). The gills, which lie against the body of the animal and whose filaments are in many cases stuck together with one another, have not yet attained their full length.  *(figure not reproduced)*

**Fig. 2.** Larva of Salamandra atra, somewhat younger than the embryo depicted in Fig. 1, after fourteen days of water-life. The primary body color is somewhat lightened and strewn with dark points.  *(figure not reproduced)* **Fig. 2a.** Forebody of the same larva enlarged, in order to let the gills be seen more exactly, which have adapted themselves to water-breathing through molting (formation of a thicker, more pigment-rich epithelium, casting off of the old one) and partial resorption, as well as through altered posture.  *(figure not reproduced)*

**Fig. 3.** Larva of Salamandra atra after seven weeks of water-life; color and glandular condition of the skin already as in the metamorphosed animal.  *(figure not reproduced)*

**Fig. 3a.** Forebody of the same larva enlarged: the gills, which here had adapted themselves to the sojourn in the water through complete casting-off and normal regeneration, are, on account of the nearly impending metamorphosis, already rather strongly reduced.  *(figure not reproduced)*

**Fig. 4.** Partially neotenic larva of Salamandra atra after eleven months of water-life, eleven days before metamorphosis. The gill-filaments are reduced down to small warts (which, on account of their smallness, are not brought to expression in the illustration) at the margin of the gill-body in favor of the latter.  *(figure not reproduced)*

**Fig. 5.** Normal larva of Salamandra maculosa, presumably two to three weeks before metamorphosis.  *(figure not reproduced)*

**Fig. 5a.** Hindbody of the same larva enlarged, in order to let the breadth of the rudder-tail clearly stand out.  *(figure not reproduced)*

**Fig. 6.** Embryo of Salamandra maculosa retained in the uterus, at approximately the same developmental stage as the larva depicted in Fig. 5. The gills are somewhat longer and have retained the form characteristic of the embryonal function. Dark body-pigment strongly accumulated.  *(figure not reproduced)*

**Fig. 6a.** Hindbody of the same embryo enlarged: fin-seam on the tail extremely narrow; on its ventral part there is a small notch, originating from the bite of another embryo.  *(figure not reproduced)*

**Fig. 7.** Newborn Salamandra maculosa: the yellow spots still little pronounced.  *(figure not reproduced)*

**Fig. 8.** Newborn Salamandra maculosa: the yellow spots for the most part dissolved into points, predominance of the black ground-color.  *(figure not reproduced)*

**Fig. 8a.** The same animal from the underside.  *(figure not reproduced)*

**Fig. 9.** Partially neotenic larva of Salamandra maculosa, after two years of sojourn in the water, 34 days before metamorphosis. Color and habitus of the adult animal. Gill-filaments reduced down to small warts at the margin of the gill-body in favor of the latter.  *(figure not reproduced)*
