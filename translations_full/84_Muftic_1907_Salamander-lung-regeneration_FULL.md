## I. Purpose of the Work.

August Weismann has repeatedly expressed himself in his writings to the effect that internal organs do not regenerate, because, being protected by the body-parts surrounding them, they are not so easily and frequently exposed to injuries as the external, peripherally situated organs, and therefore also could not, by the path of natural selection — by which, according to Weismann, the phenomena of regrowth come about — have acquired the capacity for regrowth or regeneration.

Thus it reads in one of Weismann's more recent works [13, p. 14]¹): "Parts which in the natural life of the species are never injured also often possess no regenerative power. So it is with the inner parts of the otherwise so regeneration-capable water-salamanders. I cut off, from animals under ether narcosis, the one lung half-way, or even almost entirely away; the wound closed, but a restoration of the organ did not take place. It went likewise when a piece of the spermatic duct or of the oviduct was removed."

The truth-content of this conception I do not here intend to subject to doubt by means of any particular adaptation theory of regeneration — disputes about which, given the abundance of opinions already put forward, would require above all great weight of evidence, and secondly experimental material to support them, which Weismann, as cited, has himself not furnished.

With regard, however, to the many facts which nonetheless speak against the conception of regeneration as a special capacity acquired through selection of the organism — as, for example, the capacity for substitution of rudimentary organs, the incapacity for substitution of many autotomizing limbs (adult spiders, grasshoppers), then again the renewed formation of autotomizing extremities from places other than the autotomy sites (Decapods, woodlouse [Kellerassel], Orthopteran larvae), the refutation of a number of so-called exceptional cases in which the regenerative potency was supposed to be lacking owing to want of opportunity for adaptation (Olm [Proteus], Marble-newt [Marmormolch], Water-spider [Wasserspinne], female "Hen" [weibliches "Huhn"], etc.) — with regard, then, to all these facts which speak for a primary, general regenerative power of the animal body, I held it desirable to subject the results of Weismann, concerning the non-regeneration of the newt-lung, to a re-examination.

Proceeding from the object which Weismann too used for his experiments, namely the lung of the Triton or water-salamander (*Triton cristatus* Laur.), I extended my experiments especially thoroughly also to the earth-salamander (*Salamandra maculosa* Laur.), as well as to the frog-amphibians [Anura], of which I have so far treated two forms, the water-frog (*Rana esculenta* L.) and the common toad (*Bufo vulgaris* Laur.).

The principal question of the present work accordingly runs: Is the lung of the Amphibians capable of a truly regenerative replacement? Does there take place in it a new formation corresponding to Weismann's requirements, and not merely a hypertrophic proliferation?

The answering of this principal question appeared to me, in the case of the named nearer and more distant relatives of the water-newt, still to contain an important secondary question — with regard to the here-and-there differing functional significance of the organ in question — and therefore appeared as especially desirable.

To what extent, in my experiments, I have, by keeping in view the functional importance of the *Triton-*, *Salamandra-*, and Anuran-lung, come nearer to the solution of some accompanying problems of the doctrine of regeneration — at least as concerns the cases worked on by me — about this the experiments themselves, as well as the section following them, treating of their theoretical significance, will provide information.

## II. Material, its Maintenance, and the Operative Technique.

My experiments referred chiefly to *Salamandra maculosa* Laur., but I set up the same ones also with *Triton cristatus* Laur., *Rana esculenta* L., and *Bufo vulgaris* Laur. All the animals had already been housed beforehand in naturalistically arranged containers. Thus the salamanders, of which I needed about 66 to 70, were kept in a large stone trough lined with earth and covered over with moss. As food, earthworms (*Lumbricus terrestris*) were given to them. The animals were sprayed daily, and in addition one gave them a large vessel of water.

*Triton cristatus* was kept before the operation in large aquaria and fed with horse-flesh or *Lumbricus*.

*Rana esculenta* and *Bufo vulgaris* were kept in glass terraria with a naturalistic installation and an abundant water supply, and were fed with mealworms or flies.

The maintenance of the animals after the operation is a different one from that just described. As a container for an animal that has become sensitive to infection through the operation, a glass vessel is to be prepared which is covered on the bottom with blotting-paper and, sprayed often, retains the moisture. The operated animals can either be left permanently in these containers, or, after the wound-healing is complete, be placed into suitably arranged containers, as described above.

The moisture is of great importance for the salamander operated on the lung. The respiration through the skin, which then takes over the respiratory activity alone in place of the lung, is easier in a moist air; and I believe that the keeping-dry is also to be regarded as the principal cause of the accelerated death of some experimental animals.

All salamanders were freed before the operation from the dirt adhering to the skin, then anesthetized. As anesthetic, only the sulfuric-ether [diethyl-ether] narcosis is usable, because through chloroform-narcosis the animals are very easily poisoned and are then no longer capable of life. The anesthesia I carried out in the customary manner, in a well-covered glass vessel in which there was a piece of cotton-wool moistened with ether.

Before each operation I sterilized both the dissecting instruments needed for it and the needle required for sewing up the wound.

The first experiments I set up on about 20 specimens, which however all perished on me as a consequence of an operative error, which I will point out at once.

The animals were washed and anesthetized in a not-too-dense ether-atmosphere. After about 3 minutes they were taken out and laid on their backs. For it is a sure sign that the animal is anesthetized when it is no longer able to free itself from this position. An animal so anesthetized is now subjected to an operation, in that one opens it with a small lateral incision in the long axis of the heart-region, below the front extremities, at the boundary between the upper and lower side. The skin is lightly raised in the transverse axis with the thumb and forefinger, so that with a single closing-snap of the scissors one cuts through the skin together with the peritoneum. The incision may not be longer than 5 to 6 mm. In case the incision was made correctly, one sees the lung lying lengthwise in the chest-cavity like a red grape-cluster. The taking-out of the lung requires great caution; it is, with its medial side, from the beginning (from the bronchi) up to almost the tip, grown to the intestine by means of mesentery, and stands in firm connection with it. I name in the following the grown-together part the proximal, and the free tip the distal part of the lung. The lungs were cut off and the edges of the opened skin simply drawn together with darning-thread and poured over with collodium.

It is not superfluous to mention that, in operations carried out in this way, the bleeding is a very great one and becomes the cause of the early death of the animals. The inner bleeding reaches, in some specimens, up to the pelvic girdle, adheres to the walls of the most diverse organs, and often causes their inflammation, as I was able to observe particularly on the liver.

In some cases the wounds were poured over with collodium alone, but already at the first movement of the animals the wounds sprang open again in consequence of the mechanical action of the air. Here it is further to be observed that the veins of the skin swell up, which is to be traced back to a greater internal pressure. The yellow skin-blotches become blood-red, which in turn allows one to conclude an abundance of blood-capillaries in them. Animals operated on in this way make no progress at all and also perish from hunger. Only later did I realize that the error lies in cutting off the lung without special precautions, and I undertook my later experiments in the following manner.

The animals were anesthetized and opened in the same way (as previously described). The lung-tip — the distal part — is taken out with a bluntly-pointed forceps. But if one wants to amputate the whole lung, it must be carefully separated from the intestine. At the place determined for cutting-off, the lung is in this case first tied off doubly with a thread of darning-cotton, and only then cut off in the middle between the two ligatures. Thereby not a droplet of blood can be lost. The part of the lung left behind is pushed back into the chest-cavity, the skin-wound is sewn up, but without squeezing the animal. A pouring-over with collodium cannot do harm, yet collodium alone is here less to be recommended, since the incision opens already at a slight movement of the animal. In no case, however, is the gluing-on of a plaster at the wound-site advisable.

In some cases suppurations appeared on the wounds, which I then treated with Lignosulfit, and I observed that this proved itself a very good healing-remedy for the wounds. In this way I also healed several Tritons. In case of a lack of Lignosulfit, it is better only to sew up the wound, but to keep it always clean without further closing-devices. The wound then closes soon by itself.

The cut-off parts of the lungs were at once conserved, in order to be able later to serve a histological examination.

It may yet be mentioned that the females endure the operation, and the diseases caused by it, more easily than the males. The reason for this I believe to have found in the difficulty of operating on the males. There are situated in these, in the vicinity of the lungs, the testes, which are very hard to avoid, and the injury of which calls forth the certain death of the animal.

The general phenomenon, that younger animals regenerate more easily than older ones, has shown itself clearly also in my experiments.

## III. Account of the Experiments.

### A. Experiments on *Salamandra maculosa* Laur.

In my experiments on the regeneration of the lungs I used 66 to 70 salamanders. In the preceding part I have already described two kinds of my procedure; in this part I wish to set forth the course of the two methods somewhat more precisely, and especially to point out the individual section-positions in the second kind of my operative method (ligation before amputation).

There occur, however, alterations of some inner organs, to which I will return more precisely in the V. section.

Not all 20 animals were operated on at the same lung-site. I amputated the distal lung-part on one or on both sides. Of the latter, only three animals remained alive for 25 to 32 days; with the others, however, as soon as I opened them, I found only the flatly compressed lung-remnants, that is, functionless proximal lung-parts.

With those specimens, however, in which one lung-wing remained untouched, while the other was cut off wholly or only partially, the former expanded strongly in length. Here, therefore, a hypertrophy has arisen in consequence of the function having to be performed double.

In this case there was also to be noticed, from outside, a swelling of the body at the non-injured site. The amputated lung had to lose its function, since its lumen gave a completely free passage for the inhaled air, and this [air] found itself in immediate contact with the outer air, first through the still-open wound on the skin, or later through the porous skin itself. In the animals which had lived nearly or a whole month, the wound closed in about 14 days, and during this period the function of the uninjured lung developed very suitably, in that it pushed the intestine, and in part also the liver, toward the operation-site, and on the one hand caused the flat form of the amputated lung-wing, and on the other hand gained room for its own larger expansion.

To the origin of the flat form of the amputated lung-wing, however, the over-pressure arising in the body-cavity through the inhalation and the not-again-exhaling may also have contributed; in any case the causes together afford for the flattening a purely mechanical explanation.

#### b. Experiments with ligated lungs and subsequent amputation.

These experiments I set up on 26 salamanders. The animals were operated on in the manner already described in the section on operative technique. Some perished during the operation, the others in consequence of infections, since they were brought, right after the operation, into naturalistically arranged terraria, where they could easily contract disease-germs on the rotting plant-parts. There remained to me, however, 16 head of salamander alive, which indeed delivered to my experiments certain results. The operated animals lived 12 to 58 days after the operation. A single one, which was operated on the 14th III. 07, still lives today, without bringing any defect to view.

 Before I go into the classification of these experiments of mine, I should like, in order to make my own discussion of them comprehensible, to add to the accompanying schematic drawing (Fig. 1) of the lung an explanation, so that the changes of the lungs during regeneration may be more easily understood.

Let us assume that the axes run through the salamander, so that I designate *a* as the longitudinal, *b* as the transverse, and *c* as the sagittal axis.

I divided these experiments of mine into the following six groups:

1) Lung halved on one side.
2) Lungs halved on both sides.
3) Lung almost entirely removed on one side.
4) Lungs almost entirely removed on both sides.

6) Lungs halved on both sides, however bound off on one side and left open on the other.

In the first five groups it was generally possible to observe clear regeneration; in the sixth case, however, this failed me, since the single animal used for it died after 7 days, without showing even a trace of regeneration.

In what follows I shall report in detail on each of the points mentioned above.

### 1. Lung halved on one side.

In the two operated animals, half of the right or left lung wing was bound off and amputated. In the case of the first animal I performed the operation on 31. VII. 06 [so printed; the table below gives 31. XII. 06, consistent with the 14-day lifespan]; this animal lived until 14. I. 07. The second was operated on 7. II. 07 and lived until 2. IV. 07. Both animals were killed by me and each subjected to a careful examination. It turned out that the axes *b* and *c* had partly lengthened, namely the axis *b* laterally, the axis *c* ventrally. The axis *a* had lengthened itself somewhat at the tip, but no more than would also have happened in normal growth.

Here one might assume that an inflation took place in the axes *b* and *c*, but not such an inflation in the axis *a*. To all appearances there arose, at the beginning of the re-growth, under the influence of the air pressure from within, an expansion in the latero-ventral direction, which, however, proceeded only up to a definite limit necessary for functional compensation, and was later superseded by the actual regeneration of the amputated lung wing.

The uninjured lung wing had, in both specimens, expanded a little, which may count as proof of a hypertrophic process, one that may have been called forth by the internal pressure and by a necessarily more intensive function (Fig. 2).

### 2. Lungs halved on both sides.

For this experiment I operated on three animals. The first, which was operated on 31. XII. 06, I examined on 5. II. 07; it showed clear regeneration on both sides. The two other salamanders were operated on 5. III. 07 [so printed; the table below gives 15. III. 07] and were killed by me on 12. and 15. V. 07. Here too I found that the regeneration had proceeded uniformly in all axes on both sides (Fig. 3). Here there can be no talk of a hypertrophy. The formation of the new tip came clearly to the fore in all six cases (lung wings).

### 3. Lung almost entirely removed on one side.

The only animal that was operated on in this manner showed the regenerate fairly clearly in all three axes. The lung vesicles, however, were built somewhat deviating from the normal form, and the lung was entirely clothed with a thin skin (Fig. 4). The little skin is, to all appearances, to be derived from the bronchi, whose frequent enwrapping passed over onto the lung simultaneously with the regenerative growth of the lung.

**Fig. 4.** *(figure not reproduced)*

**Fig. 5.** *(figure not reproduced)*

The uninjured lung wing that was left in place is strongly expanded; in freshly opened animals one observes very beautifully the appearance of the hypertrophy (Taf. IX Fig. E).

The tip of this lung wing had expanded almost as far as the pelvic region, and only after I had cut through the peritoneum did it, as a result of the entry of air, retreat back into the abdominal cavity.

Since the regenerated lung still functioned only little, its vesicles too developed only weakly, and the physiological task of breathing was performed by the left lung wing alone.

### 4. Lungs almost entirely removed on both sides.

In this manner four animals were operated on. The first on 31. XII. 06; it showed, after a duration of 57 days, clear regeneration. The three other animals were operated on 7. II. 07 and showed, after a similar course, after 40, 41, 48 days small indications of regeneration.

From that animal operated on in December I obtained the surest proof of the regenerative capacity of the lung in the salamander.

Both lungs grew in afterward uniformly in the directions of the axes *b* and *c* (Fig. 5). The axis *a*, however, still did not after all reach its natural length. The vesicles possessed their normal form and were, on opening the animal, quite filled with air. The little skin, of which I spoke in the preceding point (3.), was considerably thinner. Of a hypertrophy there can here be no talk at all, since the lungs possessed a natural form and both were in function (Taf. IX Fig. F). Of this regenerated lung I made histological preparations, to which I shall return in the section devoted to histology.

### 5. Lungs completely removed on both sides (extirpated).

For this experiment both lung wings were completely removed (extirpated) from five salamanders on 14. III. 07, of which the first died on 19. III., the second on 9. IV., and the third on 18. IV. 07. Already in the last one I found an indication of regeneration, while the fourth, which was opened on 7. V., showed clear regeneration of both lung wings (Taf. IX Fig. G). The left lung wing, however, showed two abnormally large lung vesicles, of which the one was twice as large as the other, both together the size of a grain of wheat. The color of these vesicles was greyish-white, thus differing from the usual, grey-black lung color; both vesicles were filled with air and tense. These vesicles are nothing other than the beginnings of the new development of the breathing apparatus, which, on amputation of this (left) lung wing, I had removed entirely, even together with a part of the bronchi.

Different was the appearance on the right side. The right lung wing grew twice as large as the left, although it had been extirpated under the same conditions; it also had the normal lung color and a multiplicity of lung vesicles, which, however, exceeded the size of the normal lung vesicles twofold. The regenerated lung wing developed itself normally in all three axes.

The present case is to be regarded both as a regeneration and as a hypertrophy. As regeneration because, firstly, the complete right lung wing together with a piece of the bronchi had been removed, and secondly, new lung vesicles actually formed. As hypertrophy, however, because the breathing function was too great for the newly formed vesicles and consequently called forth an excessive expansion of the same.

This case one can therefore designate either as »hypertrophic regeneration«, or we call it, with Morgan, simply »hyperplasia«.

### 6. Lungs halved on both sides, yet bound off on one side, left open on the other.

Here one half was cut off from each of the two lung wings, only with the difference that I pushed the left half back into the thoracic cavity without binding it off, and amputated the right half as usual only after binding it off. I set up this experiment in order to see how the process goes when one lung is open, the other bound off, but both are injured.

**Fig. 6.** *(figure not reproduced)*

The animal operated on in this manner on 7. VII. 07 died after 21 days, without showing even a trace of regeneration. On the side where the lung was bound off, I was able during life to observe an inflation, which was likewise to be seen on examination of the dead animal. The unbound lung was flattened and functionless (Fig. 6). I operated on four further animals in the same way (these animals are not entered in the following table), but they were able to live only a few days.

In none of these specimens was I able to observe regeneration. The absence of regeneration I explain to myself 1) by the too short lifespan, 2) by the too great loss of blood during the operation, which in turn 3) brought about various inflammations of the inner organs.

The absence of regeneration too, as Daibler occasionally describes from experiments on the spleen, is doubtless to be attributed solely to the analogously faulty operative procedure.

## B. Experiments on *Triton cristatus* Laur.

The operations here turned out far more difficult than in the case of the salamander. The lung of the tritons differs essentially from the lung of the previously described genus, and at first glance resembles a blood vessel rather than a lung. I undertook the operations, with preceding binding-off, in the case of 14 specimens. The animals, which before the experiment had been housed in a large aquarium, were after the completed operation reared partly in water, partly on wet gravel sand. The degree of operation was again a varying one.

I operated: 1) Only one smaller part each on the one side.
2) One half each of one lung.
3) One lung each almost entirely.
4) Both lungs almost entirely.
5) One lung each completely.
6) Both lungs completely.

Except for three females, which I still keep, all eleven animals died on me. Their examination, however, gave me no clear positive results.

I can report just as little of a regeneration as of an absence of the same, because on opening up two specimens I observed an indistinct compensation at the cut-off lung wings, and indeed one of these animals was delunged on only one side, the other on both sides.

I shall therefore continue to rear the three living females together with the one still-living salamander, which all at present express the best well-being, in order possibly, after a longer lifetime, to be able to observe an appearance of regeneration.

## C. Experiments on *Rana esculenta* Laur.

Since I was chiefly concerned with regeneration in *Salamandra* and conducted the experiments on the other amphibians only incidentally, I operated on only two specimens of this species, a male and a female, on 27 XI 06, and indeed I removed both lungs completely after prior ligation. Death occurred in the female as early as 28 XII 06 as a result of a poorly executed operation. The male I killed on 30 I 07.

Despite the short duration of life, I observed an increase in volume of the remaining portions of the lung, which took an especially striking form in the male. Since I refrained from the histological examination of this species, I can designate this phenomenon with certainty neither as regeneration nor as hypertrophy.

It is most remarkable that the frogs were able to live without a lung and to perform their respiratory function solely through the mediation of the skin.

## IV. Histological Findings.

To the presentation of the operation results hitherto described I now append a few words about the morphological appearance of the lungs, and then pass over to the description of their histological structure.

The lungs of *Salamandra* hang in the chest cavity, in the anterior angle of the peritoneal sac, on the short bronchi. Through each lung-lobe there runs a lumen in the direction of the vertical axis, which on the preparation is already easily seen with the naked eye. Into this lumen the alveolar ducts open from all sides. The alveoli are, as Hatschek and Cori say in their "Elementary Course of Zootomy" (p. 13): ¹) "marked off on the inner surface by honeycomb-like arranged little mucous-membrane folds, a contrivance which serves to enlarge the respiratory surface." — "A rich capillary blood-vessel network spans the individual lung-combs."

Smooth muscle fibers run, in company with the thick *Vena pulmonalis*, along the margin and through the septa of the lungs. The connective-tissue fibers, which are distinguished by their elasticity, are to be met with everywhere in the lung.

Large arteries take their course more at the surface of the lungs, whereas the veins, as mentioned, run in the longitudinal direction through the middle of the lungs, or, as in the frog, in the caps of the septa beneath the marginal muscle-bars (Ecker–Wiedersheim). The large blood-vessel of the salamander lung is the *Vena pulmonalis*.

Nerves too are abundantly present in the lung. All the lung-nerves branch off from a branch of the *Nervus glossopharyngeus-Vagus* and traverse the lung, strongly ramified, up to its tip. Even medullated nerves and ganglia are not seldom¹) to be found in the preparations.

Connective tissue is, as already mentioned, abundantly contained in the lung. Besides the muscles, vessels, and nerves, there are also embedded in the connective tissue the inner and outer epithelial cells. The epithelium is on both sides composed of rounded, flat epithelial cells. These cells possess a giant nucleus surrounded by protoplasm, which usually lies in the middle of the cell and exhibits a transversely-lying egg-shape. The marginal epithelium is accompanied by a cutis, which is the actual serosa of the lung. The cells of the marginal epithelium follow one another closely and form a stratified epithelium. The dense succession of these epithelial cells gradually decreases toward the tip of the lung (Pl. IX Fig. *A*).

According to Bethge's "Observations on the blood-vessel system in *Salamandra maculata*, *Triton taeniatus* and *Spelerpes fuscus*," it follows, on the basis of the distribution and development of the capillaries, that in *Salamandra* respiration is possible through the lung, the oesophagus, the mouth cavity, and through the skin. In *Triton vulgaris* = *taeniatus*, respiration through the oesophagus is lacking entirely or to a considerable degree. The diameter of the skin capillaries in *Triton* amounts to 12 to 16 µ and is on that account larger than in the salamander (7 to 12 µ), whereas the diameter of the skin capillaries in the lungless *Spelerpes fuscus* measures 24 to 30 µ.

These would be a few words about the constituent parts of the normal lung; now, however, I will speak briefly about the behavior of the epithelial cells in the regenerated lungs.

In my view, which I shall endeavor to explain more fully in the section on the theoretical significance of the findings, the large share that the epithelial cells take in the construction of the lung is a decisive factor for the regenerative potency of the lung. The epithelial cells are to be met with everywhere both in the normal and in the regenerated lung; yet with the difference that in the regenerated lung they appear, at the former site of injury, accumulated to a large degree and concentrated toward the middle. They detach themselves from the common inner epithelium, multiply in large quantity by division, and in this way form, at the site of injury together with the connective tissue already present, a scaffold (regeneration bud), in which then all the other tissue types are able to differentiate (Pl. IX Fig. *C*).

Besides the epithelial cells, I have not seldom come to see peg-shaped cells with a tiny nucleus, but never a formation of giant cells in the regenerated lung-portions, which points to a definite new formation, but not to a mere expansion of the cells.

The size, form, and structure of these cells agree completely in the regenerates with those of the normal epithelial cells, which likewise is to be regarded as a proof of a regeneration and not, say, of a mere hyperplasia.

In order to be able to bring out clearly the expressed difference between regeneration and hypertrophy, I have also prepared specimens from a hypertrophied lung and lay these (Pl. IX Fig. *D*) before [the reader]. The preparation was made from a salamander whose lung had been removed on one side. In such a case it is not the vesicles alone that enlarge, but also the cells out of which these are formed. The epithelial cells here take on a much larger shape, in that they give up their round form and stretch out lengthwise; their nuclei too enlarge and change their form accordingly. In many places the individual epithelial cells fuse into a colony, which is closed off for itself by a common membrane. Of a cell-multiplication there can here be no question, since the cells, instead of remaining tightly against one another, form intercellular spaces, which under the microscope appear as hollow spaces.

On the basis of this description, which was possible to me only as a result of the outcomes of my experiments and of the microscopic preparations prepared, I believe I have furnished with certainty the proof concerning the regenerative power of the lung, that is, of an important organ. The macroscopic preparations of the experimental animals are kept in the Biological Experimental Station in Vienna; the microscopic documentary material is located in my private possession.

## V. Accompanying phenomena on the non-operated organs.

The operations that I carried out in my experiments evoked changes not only in the operated organs, which in many cases, as for example in the newts, were far-reaching, and as a consequence of which the operated animals even forfeited various body-parts.

The present section is intended to enable the changes just mentioned, which are not connected with the regeneration, to be treated together.

The changes on the external organs and their functions in the salamander extend to the movementorgans and to the reddening of the previously yellow-colored markings of the skin.

As regards first the movement, this is in animals just operated upon a minimal one. Although the salamanders are also calm animals in free nature, their especially calm behavior nevertheless comes out distinctly immediately after the operation, in that they remain for days on one and the same spot, without carrying out even the slightest movement. The motionlessness, which is not to be confused with the stupor resulting from the narcosis, is especially noticeable in completely de-lunged specimens. One could regard that phenomenon either as a consequence of the decrease in strength, or as a useful arrangement to call forth a healing earlier, or as both together.

On the reawakening of the animals from the narcosis there is to be observed, besides the swelling of the body already mentioned in the second section, the reddish discoloration of the previously yellow gland-spots of the body, which comes out far more sharply in the animals operated upon without prior ligation.

This red coloration comes to expression especially on the yellow gland-spots of the head, but mostly around the eyes. After complete recovery of the animals this red coloration too gradually disappears. It is called forth by the inner over-pressure of the blood that has set in after removal of the lungs, more precisely, by the lack of space for blood-distribution, which is now concentrated, instead of on the lung-network, on the skin-capillary network. At the same time a more intensive skin-respiration is thereby achieved.

In no case is this color-change to be confused with the appearance of that violet or brick-red spot-marking which is not seldom to be observed in normal animals (cf. on this pigmentation of *Salamandra*, little mentioned in the literature, Kammerer and v. Schweizerbarth).

The operated animals molt several times in the first days, that is, a strong acceleration of the natural molting process, the ground for which is unconditionally to be sought in the artificial intervention. Once the first moltings are completed without any harm to the animal, it then proceeds calmly toward its recovery, and one can, after a lapse of 4 weeks, distinctly observe a regeneration.

If we cast a glance at the behavior of the inner organs in the chest and abdominal cavity, we see that here, just as [in the case] of the external changes, changes have taken place, which extend especially to the heart, the liver, and the spleen.

The heart has become much larger, and seems in many cases to have taken on double the magnitude. The cause of this hypertrophy lies in the residual circulation, in the strenuous labor of having to restore the equilibrium of the body again. Here it is to be remarked that this heart-hypertrophy in many cases was the cause of the early death of the animals. Also the heart-coloration agrees in many cases with the lung-color described above.

The liver retains, indeed, almost always its form unchanged. Yet some change of its color is to be observed.

The spleen swells, indeed, and takes on now a darker color in several nuances. It is moreover entirely superfluous to explain that the appearance of the hyperaemia, that is, of the tissue-enlargement, can be ascertained macroscopically only in the case of the liver. This case I have here, insofar as a macroscopic judgment is possible, more or less only touched upon, since I have indeed compared the quantity of blood obtained with the normal one, in the first line for blood-spilling, and, on the other hand, have compared the diseased spleen with the healthy one, all of which I have observed in several examples in larger blood-quanta.

These physiological phenomena on the heart, liver, and spleen will now go away of their own accord after the disturbed blood-circulation, as a result of the carrying-off of the lungs or of parts thereof, has been restored. The injured organs remain behind, untouched, that is, take on their natural form and their original appearance again. With a certain qualification, this is the case with the relations in the newts. The animals were, in the orange-yellow colored condition normal at rest, more strongly reddened after the operation, hence likewise imbibed with blood, and this coloration held on in the same fading until a uniform blood-circulation had set in in the animal. While *Triton*, in the first days of the operation, was engaged in very strong molting. Besides, those in particular which were de-lunged on one or both sides lost the toes or even the whole extremity. These body-parts regenerated after a lapse of 2 months. The toes fell off in an operated newt, which falling-off must stem from a special inner sickness, similar to which I have also observed in the operated animals which [were kept] under poorer conditions. It would be still to be mentioned that with two specimens, which were completely de-lunged, no arbitrary movements were any longer to be observed for a longer time; besides, under intensive molting, more than the half of their tails and all the extremities fell off them.

On *Rana esculenta* and *Bufo vulgaris* I have followed no accompanying phenomena of any kind, neither on the external nor on the inner organs and their function.

## VI. Theoretical significance of the findings.

Already in the introductory chapter ("Aim of the work") a fundamental antithesis was emphasized, which prevails between the present-day views in regard to the regeneration facts. The one party, at its head Weismann, denies the power of selective adaptation; the other direction, with Morgan and Przibram as the tone-setting representatives, comes out for the existence of a primary, general regulative force as origin of the organic restitutions.

Yet others, in the first line Korschelt, are of the opinion that both main factors are effective, that thus a general regenerative potency does indeed exist, but is strengthened at certain points of the animal series through special adaptation.

In our theoretical position-taking we wish, for the moment, to leave that antithesis out of account and to subject the individual factors coming into consideration for the present case, one after another, to a possibly precise examination.

As regards first of all the probability of loss, this can, for the lungs as inner organs, be no great one. In the natural life of the animals investigated, it scarcely occurs that the lungs are injured from outside in a way that does not at the same time destroy the life of the specimen concerned. It is accordingly not seldom that injuries from the side of inner enemies (entoparasites) befall the lungs in the most varied way, and seldom; one would otherwise more frequently encounter such cases in the dissections. The danger of injury can therefore have no share in the regenerative capacity of the amphibian lungs.

As further regards the importance for the life of the animal, to that organ whose absence is so indifferent for the well-being of the individual concerned, as it is to my experimental animals the lack of their two lung-lobes, no […] occupy a high rank. To be sure — one will object — all regenerating organs must, from time to time, do without their re-growth; but here there is added, moreover, the weighty fact that, in several groups of the amphibian species mentioned, there are species which are normally lungless, or possess such small amounts of lung that some individuals have lungs and others do not. In any case, the lungs are no indispensable component in the life of my experimental animals, and therefore by virtue of their importance can likewise not possess regeneration capacity up to the high degree present.

From this "importance" one must indeed distinguish the functional demand placed upon an organ [die funktionelle Inanspruchnahme eines Organs]. An organ may normally find itself in lively activity and yet be dispensable to the life of its bearer. A well-functioning organ is, however, subject to stronger wear and tear, and therefore demands also to be more intensively nourished than a weakly functioning one; it is this intensive metabolism which, in further consequence, must secure for it a better capacity for cell multiplication and growth. The lungs of the frog-larvae [Froschlurche] and of the terrestrial salamander [Erdsalamander] are such dispensable, yet strongly functioning, organs; the lung of the aquatic salamander [Wassersalamander], by contrast, plays — as is already indicated morphologically by its less rich architecture — a far smaller role as a respiratory organ, in that it leaves the greatest part of the respiratory function to the outer body-integument [Körperintegument]. It is accordingly not further to be wondered at if, between the growth and regeneration capacity of the Anuran and *Salamandra* lung on the one hand, and of the *Triton* on the other, a greater disparity prevails in favor of the former.

In the same respect yet another circumstance appears of significance: in the course of the experimental presentation it was repeatedly emphasized that the simply cut-off lungs do not regenerate, whereas the previously ligated ones [die vorher abgebundenen] do. In part this may certainly be ascribed to the smaller blood loss in the latter case; but probably the following also comes into consideration: as reported, a double ligature was applied to the lung, and the cut was made in the middle between the two, of which the proximal one therefore remained in place also after the operation. Upon simple amputation there resulted, then, a functionless shred of tissue, whereas upon amputation after ligation there resulted a sac closed by the binding, which was quite well able to go on functioning and thereby probably substantially accelerated the process of restoration.

One would further have the regeneration capacity stand in inverse proportional dependence on the degree of differentiation [Differenzierungshöhe], and indeed in a threefold direction: 1) the more phylogenetically ancient an animal, the better its regeneration capacity; 2) the more ontogenetically aged an animal, the poorer its regeneration capacity; 3) the more specialized an organ or tissue, the more difficult its rebuilding. From this point of view it is readily understandable why precisely the lung tissue, with its simple epithelial composition, regenerates easily, and in my opinion much weight is even to be laid upon this point. A subsidiary experiment [Nebenversuch], which I did not mention at all in the course of the actual experimental presentation, may here be supplied in addition. It will furnish an instructive example for the dependence-relation of regeneration just mentioned, as well as for the interlocking of the various regeneration factors.

From terrestrial and aquatic salamanders [Erd- und Wassersalamandern], a limb was cut off at the same time as the lungs. After one month, in the former the lungs were engaged in regeneration, the limb not yet; in the latter the limb was regenerated, whereas on the lungs no trace of it showed itself.

The example shows in any case also that the phyletic stage is not, as some authors would have it, the only thing on which regeneration depends; for otherwise the lungs of the *Triton*-forms [Tritonen] would have to regenerate better than those of the Anura, which is certainly not the case — although, in spite of the hitherto existing lack of distinct positive results, I have received indications that the regeneration capacity of the lung is not entirely wanting in the *Triton*.

The theoretical overall conclusion from my results, in comparison with those of other experimenters, can therefore run as follows:

A dependence on the probability of loss [Verlustwahrscheinlichkeit] is to be denied to the regeneration capacity. Such a dependence on the importance of the organ exists only insofar as its more lively function conditions greater growth energy; selection processes, reserve determinants [Reservedeterminanten] therefore do not come into consideration here. On the other hand, the following proportional dependence-relation can be asserted with great definiteness: the simpler the histological constitution, the greater the regeneration capacity.

The answering of the question in the present work thus speaks in the sense of the existence of a primary, general regeneration force [Regenerationskraft], which decreases with the ascent of the series of organisms, but which at definite points of that series is especially heightened or inhibited through secondary (yet not indirectly-selective, but always directly acting) factors.

## VII. Summary.

1) The lung of *Salamandra maculosa*, *Rana esculenta* and *Bufo vulgaris* is capable of regeneration after partial or complete loss.

2) In this, it plays a large role in a positive direction if remaining parts are kept functional by means of a ligature.

3) Upon one-sided operation, compensatory hypertrophy appears in the uninjured lung-lobe [Lungenflügel] of the opposite side, which during the regeneration process has to perform the greatest part of the respiratory function.

4) Likewise, after extirpation of both lung-lobes, hyperplasia is to be ascertained, caused by the functional overburdening of the first-regenerated lung-vesicles [Lungenbläschen], still few in number.

5) The finer structure of the regenerating lung exhibits at the former site of injury a large quantity of highly stratified epithelial cells, concentrated toward the middle, which form there a regeneration bud [Regenerationsknospe] and in the further course of the process let all other tissue types proceed out of themselves. In this, lively cell multiplication is to be observed, and the smallness of the nuclei especially strikes the eye.

6) The finer structure of the hypertrophied lung, by contrast, exhibits strikingly large, lengthwise-elongated, large-nucleated cells and no cell multiplication.

7) With regard to *Triton cristatus* the question whether the lungs here too regenerate and hypertrophy cannot yet be definitively decided; yet the observations speak in favor of both capacities belonging to the *Triton* lung, even if in weaker measure as compared with the well-functioning *Salamandra*, frog, and toad lung.
