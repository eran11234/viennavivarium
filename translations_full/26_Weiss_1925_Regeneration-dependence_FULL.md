## I. Introduction.

There is still lacking an uncontradicted and unambiguous answer to the question of whether the regeneration of the urodele limb is dependent on the nervous system or not. The last two works to be published on the problem in short succession one after the other, the one by *Goldfarb* (8), the other by *Walter* (24), contradict each other, however.

It was impossible to carry the investigations into the laws of regeneration further without first having removed the obscurity and the discomfort that had been called forth by the unsettled state of the question of nerve-dependence. Thus, while occupied with the regeneration of the urodele limb, I was quite of my own accord driven to a renewed re-examination of the state of affairs, upon which I would be able to rely in my further work.

To present here the whole course of the dispute, which has now lasted for more than 20 years, would only be a repetition of what has already often been said; one finds the bibliographical references on the theme in the two works named, and further in *Przibram* (18) and in *Barfurth* (1).

It was precisely with this problem that the traditional manner of posing the question always created much difficulty and confusion. One chases after the impossible if one seeks a uniform answer to the question: Is regeneration dependent on the nervous system or not? One expected a Yes or a No, without first really letting nature have its say; otherwise it would have shown that it was the expectation and not the result of this or that experiment that was false—indeed, that the various findings did not contradict each other nearly so sharply as it had to appear, for after all the one said Yes and the other No.

Above all, the quite indiscriminate use of the concept "nervous system" alone, with its incessant misunderstandings, was bound to bring with it a derailment of the question onto the dead track of a fruitless polemic for and against. By "dependence of regeneration on the nervous system" the one understood some centres or other, perhaps the brain; the second, the necessity of the integrity of the whole central system; the third, the intactness of particular segments; the fourth, dependence on the presence of the connections between periphery and central organ; the fifth, the mutual influence of various bodily regions upon one another via the nerve-tracts; the sixth, functional effects; and yet another, perhaps, the presence of nerve-substance; but all of them spoke simply of "the influence of the nervous system." Thus the first could deny the question, the fourth could affirm it, and both were in the end right.

### State of the Problem.

For the object chosen for the investigation (the limb of the full-grown newt of *Triton cristatus* [modern *Triturus cristatus*]), several findings already lie before us which have at least to some degree narrowed down the problem of the relations of the nervous system to limb regeneration. Above all, it appears established that for the formally correct regeneration of the *Triton* limb the intactness, or indeed even merely the presence, of those spinal-cord segments from which the limb is innervated is not required. This has most recently again been confirmed by *Walter*. The analogous findings of *Goldfarb* (7), who had also controlled microscopically the extent of the central destructions in the experimental animals, hold for the urodele *Diemyctylus viridescens*.

Through the experimental destruction of the contents of the vertebral canal, all the spinal motor elements of the limb segment are eliminated, and nevertheless the *quality* of the limb regeneration is not impaired by the intervention. In this single finding there are now further contained, that is clear, also all the earlier results concerning the qualitative independence of limb regeneration from the presence of the brain or of an intact whole spinal cord; for with the removal or destruction of the spinal-cord segments belonging to the limb, every spinal connection of the limb with the rest of the spinal cord and brain has likewise been lost.

The insight that integrity of the central apparatus is no precondition for orderly regeneration was bound to take from the assumption of a nerve-dependence of regenerative form-building much of its appeal. For it is so readily understandable that one would have liked to ascribe to the central nervous system—through which one saw the uniformity of the organism's mode of functioning guaranteed—also the mediation of the uniformity in form-building during the restoration of the total form of the organism, as which regeneration, after all, by and large presents itself. This assumption had now been overturned by the experimental results, and at the same time also another, which could equally well have served as an explanation: namely, that perhaps the *function* of the developing form determines its quality. The limb can regenerate in full form without being reached by motor impulses, hence also without any active, nervously mediated function of movement.

On the other hand, it had been established from several sides that the severance of the peripheral nerve trunks leading to the limb is capable of inhibiting regeneration (e.g. *Rubin* 20). The interruption of the nerve connection of periphery with centre had therefore eliminated some factor or other which was of influence on the regeneration; this factor had, in accordance with the foregoing discussions, to lie outside the vertebral canal, but central to the point of nerve section made in the peripheral course. Experiments by *Wolff* (32) already seemed to permit a still closer localization; for he found that if not the spinal cord alone, but the whole vertebral-column segment of the limb section was thoroughly removed, regeneration failed to appear, and 21* it was natural to suspect the determining influence in the spinal ganglia. For these too had, in the radical operation after which the suppression of regeneration was observed, been removed, in contrast to the experiments with mere boring-out of the vertebral canal. *Walter* in particular then argued for the probability that it might be the spinal ganglia whose presence would be necessary for the formally correct regeneration of a limb.

The results after nerve section, however, were mostly not entirely clean; the impediment was, namely, neither a total nor a lasting one, and one could not speak of a *suppression* of regeneration, but at best of a temporary *inhibition*. This came about because the severed nerve fibres were themselves able to regenerate vigorously, and as a consequence of this the interrupted continuity of the nerve trunks was soon restored. But as soon as the nerve connection with the amputation stump is reorganized in this way, every ground for a further incapacity for regeneration of the stump falls away, and regeneration can now set in, or proceed, provided that meanwhile no peripheral changes of a non-nervous kind have created insurmountable obstacles; but of these latter we shall treat only further below.

Since the accomplished restoration of the nervous connection of periphery with centre makes itself known through the reappearance of motility and sensibility in the previously paralyzed and insensitive limb, and since the time-point of this reappearance coincides with the onset of regeneration, it is doubtless beyond question that one was justified in representing the earlier absence of regeneration as a consequence of the interruption of the nerve connection with that (otherwise, for the time being, not yet more precisely localizable) influence-centre. Nevertheless, some cases had come to observation in which, after section and excision of a piece of the peripheral nerves, regeneration was at first not observed in the denervated stump, but after some period of rest did then set in, without the examination thereupon undertaken having shown a re-union of the severed nerve stumps. *Rubin* described such a case and assumed that here a *collateral* nerve regeneration into the stump must have been involved. In the 20 years that have since elapsed, our knowledge of the regeneration of peripheral nerves has greatly expanded, and yet nothing has become known that would contradict the assumption of a collateral regeneration into the stump.

Given the indiscriminateness with which the nerve fibres sprouting out from the central stump take their way (*Boeke* 4), it is no wonder that even formerly non-belonging parts of nerves become united, so that the amputation stump comes to be supplied, not from its own nerves, but from foreign, co-injured nerves, and this is then the collateral innervation of which *Rubin* speaks.

Given the vigorous regenerative capacity of injured nerve fibres, it seems almost impossible to keep a peripheral section permanently nerve-free; one can render the nerve regeneration more difficult by enlarging the scar-stretch to be grown through and by dislocation of the stumps, but one cannot eliminate it entirely. It therefore likewise appears excluded that a lasting suppression of regeneration could be achieved as a consequence of nerve section. But for this reason the fact that in many cases regeneration did finally occur despite nerve section may not be adduced as an argument against the assumption of an influence exerted by a nervous factor upon limb regeneration.

The objection that the operation itself might be to blame for the alteration in the course of regeneration is, as *Walter* has already emphasized, not valid; for, firstly, after the much more drastic operation of spinal-cord destruction the limb can nevertheless regenerate, and, secondly, the inhibition of regeneration after merely one-sided interruption of the peripheral nerves also comes to observation only on the one side.

It could thus be regarded as established, on the basis of *Walter*'s experiments, that a nervous influence centralized outside, but yet in the immediate vicinity of, the spinal cord is necessary for the onset of limb regeneration in *Triton*. That this influence proceeds from the spinal ganglia was, to be sure, inferred only *per exclusionem*. And if uncertainty already prevailed as to the location of the influence-centres, then all the more so as to the manner in which one had to conceive of their form-function. An influencing by way of function it could not be, as we have already seen. There remained, however, still a whole series of *possibilities* for consideration:

I. The nervous centre in question exerts upon the form-building processes a *specifically determining* influence: the nervous centre would then have to be capable of prescribing to the regeneration blastema a particular course of development; but this in turn would be possible in two ways:

a) The form-function would be broken up into parts, and each of these parts bound to anatomically separate parts of the nerve-mass that comes into consideration, so that at least each of the larger nerve trunks in the limb would have a separate task. — To a partial loss of nerve connections there would then have to correspond also a partial loss in the regenerated form. The mention of this possibility is important in connection with certain results of *Wolff* (31, 32): when he had established the absence of regeneration after nerve section, he too finally noticed that after some time the arrested regeneration came into progress again, but: the results of this regeneration were mostly malformations, hypotypies. We shall have to come back to these findings later; here they are mentioned only because *Wolff* believed himself entitled to trace the formal defects back to the fact that the restoration of the nerve connection, by virtue of which regeneration could come into progress again, had also taken place in a defective manner; and he did not merely think that, quite in general, the poorer nerve supply had a diffuse disturbance of regeneration as its consequence, but rather that a definite lack of nerve connections was correlated with a quite definite formal loss; in proof of this he adduces that, after amputation of a malformation caused, as he supposes, by nerve defects, there always regenerated again a limb mistypical in just the same way.

b) The form-quality of the terminal section would be represented somehow, not in parts, but *as a whole*, in the much-mentioned (and, in particular, still to be sought) centre of action, and would then flow as some formed excitation-process or other over all the conducting nerve fibres in the same way out to the periphery, to the regeneration region.

This is, according to my latest investigations (*Weiss* 28), also the nature of the operative-functional nerve-process; in that case a loss of a part of the nerve connections with the terminal region would entail no formal losses in the regeneration product, for the remaining fibres would still bring along the whole form-excitation; indeed, even a partial damaging of the centre itself would just as little necessarily be followed by a lasting formal loss as, after a partial disturbance in the central nervous system, a lasting functional loss need appear. The assumption of such a mode of action would, however, demand that the form-quality of the regenerate should correspond to the segmental region in which lies the centre with which the developing form is nervously connected; innervation from a foreign centre ought to be able to occasion a regenerate foreign to the stump. This possibility I have tested by letting arms with innervation from the leg-centres, and conversely legs with innervation from the arm-centres, come to regeneration (*P. Weiss* 29); thereby it appeared that the foreign innervation was incapable of exerting any modifying influence.

After all this one is surely entitled, if the possibility I a should now also be experimentally excludable, to dismiss finally the assumption of a specifically determining influence of the nervous system upon the regeneration of the limb in *Triton*. I may anticipate here that I a did indeed also prove untenable in the experiment. The nerve-influence was therefore not determining, functional it was likewise not, and yet that it was present is beyond doubt.

II. Perhaps one would like to see the essential thing in the presence of nerve-substance in the cut surface; but here one should reflect that after nerve section, too, the nerve-substance is present. To be sure, it undergoes certain changes, yet it remains living, plasmatic. It is therefore not *living* nerve-substance that the regeneration process requires, but, if one may so put it, "*active*" nerve-substance.

III. And finally let one more conjecture be considered, which has indeed actually been voiced from another side; in it the nerve meets us merely as a morphological structure, as "tissue," and without reference to its special function: If, namely, for the coming-about of the regenerate the presence of all the typical tissues in the cut surface in a growth-capable state were necessary—because otherwise the tissue-harmony of the developing form would be disturbed and therefore perhaps the form-building would rather be omitted from the very outset—and if, further, this conception held good for all tissues, and if one then regarded the nerve as a tissue, then one might suppose that one had found an explanation of the nerve-dependence of regeneration on the basis of a more general law of regeneration; the influence of the damaging of the nerve would then be analogous to that of the damaging of any other tissue; in such a manner *Goldfarb* explained the defective course of regeneration in those cases where he cannot avoid conceding a certain disturbance of regeneration after interventions in the nervous system. Unfortunately, however, the premises on which this view is built are untrue. Firstly, the growth-capacity of the individual tissues is in this way not even disturbed by the nerve-elimination; we shall rather, precisely on this point, come to know further below a characteristic difference between the processes after simple amputation and after amputation combined with nerve section. Secondly, as I have recently been able to show, and indeed precisely with reference to the bone tissue specially adduced by *Goldfarb* (*P. Weiss* 25), the limb regenerate does not come about at all through a continued growth of the tissues out beyond the cut surface, but is laid down as a unit and is not affected in the least by the absence of one or another tissue (in particular of bone) in the cut surface or anywhere in the stump; it contains in any case the whole typical tissue-stock belonging to it, and indeed creates this out of itself. To be sure, this holds only for the *form-building processes* in the regenerate; the connection of the newly formed structure to the humoral and nervous integration system takes place, so far as we today know, through the in-growth of the blood vessels and nerves from the substratum; but here the nerve-tracts are precisely not involved as tissues that would have to make a contribution to the morphological development, but simply as connecting-tracts to the central system. With this, however, the possibility is in any case taken from our hands of being able to subsume the disturbance of regeneration after nerve-elimination under a general law according to which the disturbance of a tissue would seriously impair the regeneration process; such a law simply does not exist.

IV. So in the end there remain only the possibilities still to be examined, namely that the undeniably present nervous contribution to limb regeneration is a *trophic* one, a *tonic* one, or finally one of a kind not to be found again at all in other processes.

### General remarks on the methodology of the operation and the care of the *Tritons*.

Since further publications on already completed regeneration studies on *Tritons* will follow in connection with this work, I wish here to compile the methodological data once and for all, and to refer to them as needed in the later communications.

My experience has been gathered from many hundreds of *Tritons*. The most suitable for the regeneration experiments and the most resistant proved to be *Triton cristatus* [modern *Triturus cristatus*]. Since the regenerative power becomes weaker with increasing age, it is not advisable to use animals older than three years; otherwise one easily obtains impure results. Animals that are too young, on the other hand, one will likewise be unable to use, unless the matter concerns simple, easily surveyable operations, for the smaller the animal is, the more difficult it is to achieve a clear operative picture. Regeneration experiments set up in winter often suffer from the considerable delay of the process in the cold season, and whoever has not yet learned through numerous experiences to recognize these influences on the regenerate had better begin the experiments only in spring. Above all, animals in poor nutritional condition should be excluded from the experiments, in particular those in which the skin, instead of the healthy wrinkled appearance, displays a smooth, pale, sticky appearance. On the day of the operation and the day before, the animals must not be fed, because as a consequence of the narcosis they easily vomit.

The narcosis is best carried out in sulfuric ether [diethyl ether]; however, one must take care that the animal itself comes into no contact whatever with the liquid. One proceeds advantageously in such a way that one places the animal into a small dry glass vessel and only then introduces this into the actual narcosis vessel, which contains the ether-soaked cotton wool; one can also separate off the ether space of the vessel by a wire screen and then lay the animals directly upon it. Details are indeed of secondary importance; the only thing that remains essential is that the skin of the animal must come into no kind of contact with the liquid ether, otherwise, given the intensive cutaneous respiration of the animals, losses as a consequence of the narcosis are unavoidable.

In the narcosis vessel the animals at first crawl about briskly, then they suddenly stretch out and make vigorous swimming movements; one sees how the movements become ever more laborious, finally they cease and there ensues for the most part a strong one-sided contraction of the trunk musculature; the animal is rolled up sideways into a spiral. Now one should bring the animal into the dorsal position by shaking the vessel, and, as soon as it no longer shows a righting reflex, also take it out into the air. A longer stay in the narcosis has death as a consequence. In the narcosis the animals "sweat," they secrete their whitish skin secretion, probably homologous to the parotin of the salamanders, and are then best cleaned of it after removal from the narcosis vessel with a dry cloth; water is ineffective, since the secretion is insoluble in water. Further preparations for the operation are actually not needed; asepsis is quite superfluous, any tying-down or other fixation of the small animals is rather a hindrance than an advantage. The mode of operation itself is, of course, to be adapted entirely to the problem under investigation; among the various possibilities, however, that one is always to be chosen which yields the smallest wound surface. For the larger the wound surface is, the lower also is the resistance of the organism against *Saprolegnia* infection, but this latter has necrosis of the affected operated parts as a consequence. Sutures, where they are absolutely necessary, are made with the finest surgical silk and the smallest vessel needles.

*Keeping of the animals:* In regeneration experiments individual keeping is to be recommended, because the animals, when brought together several at a time, bite off one another's limbs. If individual keeping is not feasible with too large a number, then the marking of the animals is advantageously done by means of colored glass beads, which in ten colors signify the ten digits and are strung in a corresponding arrangement, representing the protocol numbers of the animal, upon a silver wire drawn through the root of the tail.

After the operation the animals are at first brought into vessels with wet gravel, and the vessels are closed off to avoid drying out. Two days later, water is then filled in up to a level of a few centimeters in height, so that the animals can resume their swimming activity; sojourn upon gravel without water brings with it strong friction of the wound surface during locomotion and should therefore not be extended longer than necessary. As a consequence of the operation, a complete molt sets in right away in the first days; the old skin is stripped off on the gravel, from paralyzed body parts it is expediently removed with forceps. A change of water in the vessels is no longer necessary in the subsequent period; the gravel on the bottom adsorbs all filth and aquatic plants provide for richness in oxygen. It suffices to feed the animals once a week; for this one should use living insect larvae and the like in summer, *Tubifex* in winter. With feeding with the latter, caution is advisable: the food portion should be held out to each animal with the forceps, from which it is snapped away without further ado; the falling-to-the-bottom and independent crawling-about of the worms in the glass must be absolutely prevented, for they creep away under the gravel, are not found there by the *Tritons*, and, since they require flowing water for life, soon perish, but thereby foul the water for the experimental animals.

Occasionally (opening of a suture, ligature, etc.) small subsequent operations become necessary later on; since it is not favorable to subject the animal repeatedly to the narcosis, one can in such cases advantageously bring it to rest by setting it — similarly as is known with frogs — into "hypnosis" (rigidity of the postural-correction muscles) by means of a not too strong striking of the upper side of the head against a hard object.

## II. Experimental Reports.

For the experiments which are now to be described in the following, it is to be remarked that the operation on the peripheral nerves was always undertaken one-sidedly, so that the regeneration on the non-operated side could serve as a control. That is to say, in this case the amputation surfaces were brought to the same height of the limb axis on both sides.

The most important point about the innervation relations of the limb in *Triton* may here be summarized: The free arm is in the main supplied by the ventral branches of the 3rd–5th spinal nerves, yet the 2nd and 5th can be almost entirely neglected in comparison with the strength of the 3rd and 4th. The 3rd and 4th spinal nerve are designated in the following as I and II respectively; they nestle up against each other roughly halfway along the path from the spinal cord to the shoulder and from there run on together, the *Arteria subclavia* between them. In the shoulder region they enter into the formation of the *Plexus brachialis*, in which the weak spinal nerves of the limb segment also participate with branches. From the *Plexus brachialis* there emerge, for the free limb, chiefly two large nerve trunks, the *Nervus brachialis longus inferior* and the *Nervus brachialis longus superior*, each of which divides again into a *Ramus profundus* and a *Ramus superficialis*. I and II are most easily accessible during their course beneath the *Scapula*; the nerve trunks distal to the plexus, on the other hand, one reaches best by everting the skin of the upper arm out of its circular direction and pushing forward from the ventral side of the upper arm between the muscles toward the upper third of the *Humerus*. At the places named one can, without any essential damage to the cut surface, lay free a stretch of nerve about 5 mm long. If one then does not cut this out, but only grips it firmly with the aid of a sharp forceps and extracts it, one can remove up to 10 mm of nerve — in the one case the whole stretch from the vertebral column to the plexus, in the other case the whole length of the upper arm. In order to isolate the nerve to be removed from the accompanying vessels and also from the neighboring nerves, it is undergirded with a blunt spatula and dissected off upon it.

At the hind limb only few exact control experiments were undertaken, so there was no need to go into its innervation relations in more detail.

### Elimination of the greater part of the limb nerves.

Besides a generally orienting re-examination of the fact of the failure of regeneration after severance of the limb nerves, in this experimental series the question was to be investigated whether the intactness of the nerve pathways is necessary only for the onset of regeneration or also for its further progress. At the same time, the opportunity arose to observe the differences between the processes at normal and at denervated amputation surfaces.

In this group of experiments, the largest possible stretch from the course of nn. I and II proximal to the formation of the plexus was always extracted; the *Arteria subclavia* running between the two was thereby most often severed. This operation will be designated throughout in the following as "E I, II". If, however, as happened in some control experiments, the artery was spared, and only the two nerves were extracted, then this is designated as "F I, II".

### Discussion of the findings so far:

#### a) Exclusion of the nerves before, or simultaneously with, the amputation (Series M I, M II and Animals A 22 and 23).

Whereas normally, in the sequel to an amputation within the upper arm, processes set in which, by way of bud and anlage, lead in typical form to the re-establishment of the removed parts, the denervated stump shows an entirely different behavior. In it no shaped regeneration of any kind comes about; no bud, no cone, no anlage arises. It is not, however, that the *proliferative capacity* of the stump is disturbed by the nerve exclusion; for at the cut surface, despite the lacking innervation, proliferative processes set in after the amputation also at the denervated stump, only they lead to an essentially different result than at innervated stumps. Through the *further growth of the tissue* at the denervated stump there form, namely, no typically shaped *regenerates* over the wound surface, but rather there arises *an unorganized, calotte-shaped tissue cap which seals the stump off.* *Rubin* has been able to observe these conditions under the same conditions and depicts in the microscopic picture how epidermis, cutis, indeed even skeletal (bone, cartilage) tissue begin to proliferate at the wound site even despite the nerve transection. The skin (epidermis and cutis) grows over the wound surface, partly through pushing-over, but in large part through genuine proliferative activity of the individual tissues, and in this way there forms the ever-observable, tough cutaneous covering of the stump at the wound site. The skeletal tissue, since after the amputation stronger destructive processes make themselves broadly felt in it, sets in with proliferative activity only somewhat later, and forms beneath the soft-part calotte — by then already closed — a callous proliferation; but a limit is soon set to the further growth of this by the tough overgrowth of the wound that has meanwhile taken place. Of any proliferative activity of the muscle tissue *Rubin* could observe nothing.

The processes just described at the denervated stump proceed quite promptly in the first days after the amputation, that is, at a point in time at which a re-innervation cannot yet be spoken of at all. With the production of the described tissue cap, however, the activity of the de-nerved stump is by no means ended; rather, work continues to be done firmly and steadily upon the new closing-piece thereafter: the thickness of the skin and its toughness increase, pigmentation processes set in within the at-first delicately translucent covering tissue — all proofs that, first, the growth- or proliferative activity has not been extinguished, but, given the *permanently* undisturbed continuation of these processes, above all a proof that here there is not, say, a mere *residuum* of capacities that had existed only at the time of intact nerve function.

The capacity to form anything new at all thus appears to take effect at wound surfaces *with and without nerves*; the essential difference lies rather in the manner in which this capacity is utilized: in the one case it stands in the service of the build-up of a well-organized, typically shaped formation; in the other case it leads to the production of an unorganized tissue lump. We shall have occasion to return to this point below.

Extraction of the two main nerves of the plexus thus has as its consequence that at an amputation surface the anlage of the missing organ part fails to appear; instead, by means of proliferative processes in which the skin is chiefly involved, a tissue-like closure of the stump comes about. These conditions are unconditionally a consequence of the falling-away of the two peripheral main nerves; that in many cases the blood vessel running between them has been severed along with them does not come into play, for, first, it has been found that transection of the vessel alone does not impair the course of regeneration (*Hines* 14) — nor would that even be expected, given the rapid capacity for restoration in the vascular system — and, second, my own control experiments, in which particular attention was directed to the preservation and sparing of the vessel (Animals N 2, A 22, A 23), have shown that the consequences of the nerve transection then set in in the same way as otherwise.

For the achievement of the described consequences a really *complete* denervation of the extremity is not necessary; it would moreover scarcely be technically feasible by peripheral interventions. At any rate, with the 3rd and 4th spinal nerves (I and II) the overwhelming majority of the nerve fibers was indeed excluded, as was already recognizable from the complete failure of motility and sensibility in the free extremity. The weak remainder of intact nerve fibers that then still remained in the extremity was not able to bring forth the characteristic regeneration effects, and that is precisely what matters here. One might think it is to be ascribed to this nerve remainder that, nevertheless, proliferative processes set in at the wound surface, as was described, and that with an increasing quantity of nerves, in steady transition, more and more could be regenerated, until finally intactness of all the nerves, as the end member of this series, would permit the full development of a well-shaped extremity. We shall see further below that this view is incorrect. The sparse number of nerve fibers that remains after the exclusion of the main arm nerves is insufficient for any influence whatever upon the events at the amputation surface.

While at the operated side the described phenomena occur, the extremity regeneration on the control side — where the amputation was carried out at the same section height as on the operated side — runs to its end in one piece, without appreciable impairment, formally faultless.

Just as with the earlier investigators, there were also in my case instances where, after several months of regenerative inactivity at the de-nerved stump — after a time during which a finished regenerate had already long been produced on the control side — regeneration processes set in all at once and now led to the production of a genuine, shaped regenerate. The simultaneously reappearing function of the previously paralyzed stump is proof enough that the restoration of the nerve pathways gave the occasion for such new activity.¹

Thus, roughly, one would have to assume according to the lines of thought of *Wolff*, who had brought faulty regeneration into connection with faulty restoration of the nerve pathways. But the view of *Wolff* does not hold good. In reality the reason that, in many cases, despite the re-growing-in of the nerve fibers, regeneration permanently fails to appear lies in the fact that in the meantime peripheral changes have taken place which have created insurmountable resistances to the regeneration process: we have seen that the over-skinning of the wound at the denervated stump becomes progressively tougher and tougher; if this process has now exceeded a certain degree, then a regenerate coming to anlage later beneath the cutaneous cap will often no longer be able to break through.

The findings of *Wolff* (32) on his malformations likewise explain themselves in the same way. For if a belated regeneration anlage is nevertheless able at some spot to fight its way through the scar-like covering, then it is on this occasion often severely altered; now, defects set later than at the bud stage — thus also already such as are set at the anlage stage — are irreparable, and the anlage develops into a defect-formation (*Schaxel*). Such a malformation thus again has nothing whatever to do with poor nerve supply, but is at bottom conditioned by mechanical impairment of the formative material. That, in the alteration to which the malformation is owed, districts of definite potency have dropped out irretrievably may perhaps explain why, after a renewed amputation within the malformation, a malformation of the same kind always regenerates again, as *Wolff* found (this observation, however, likewise still requires a thorough examination). At any rate, the second-time regenerating of a malformation is, in its formal faultiness, just as little directly dependent on nervous defects as was the first-time production of the malformation. I should like to add that in my experimental animals the regeneration at the formerly denervated stump, when it came into operation again after months, proceeded normally from then on in all cases and yielded well-shaped formations. That *Wolff* so frequently obtained malformations may well come from the fact that in his animals he always placed the amputation cut far distally (in the region of the hand), that is, in a region in which the intensity of regeneration is at its lowest.

In a few of my experimental animals the nerve transection had as its consequence such a weakening of the vital activity of the stump that the latter could not offer resistance to the ever-present *Saprolegnia* infection, became necrotic and was cast off. Such animals were thereafter excluded from the experiments.

But that the failure of regeneration may not simply be traced back, too, to a general weakening of the vital activity in the limb concerned is already evident from the fact that the healthy persistence of the stump, the lively proliferative activity at the wound surface, and the working-out, strengthening and pigmentation of the described tissue cap thoroughly testify to a well-preserved vital activity.

#### b) Exclusion of the nerves after the onset of the regeneration process.

It would have been conceivable that the intactness of the nerve connections between extremity and center was indeed necessary for the *onset* of the regeneration process, but that, by contrast, the process, once set in operation, could from then on run to its normal end even without being directly dependent further on the presence of the nerves. To test this possibility, amputation was carried out on both forelimbs at the same height; the regeneration set in unimpaired, and only after several days or weeks was the exclusion of nn. I and II carried out on the one side (Series III, IV, V, VI and N). In general it turned out there that the process discontinued its progress on the operated side on the day of the nerve exclusion; the nervous influences were thus in any case necessary for the *whole duration* of the regeneration. But even more than this shows itself: on the young regenerates, which already distinctly displayed a shape of their own, involution phenomena began to become noticeable. This involution is the more considerable the less rigid the regenerate still is. In detail we best consider the phenomena separately according to the stages at which the regeneration process had been interrupted (cf. Fig. 2).

A few days after the amputation the wound is covered by a tautly stretched, slightly bulged-out, translucent blastema. If the nerve exclusion is now carried out, the blastema remains arrested in its development and only its pigmentation continues further. For the most part the blastema then persists in this state, without taking on the tough consistency such as we have come to know in the tissue caps which come to development after a nerve exclusion carried out simultaneously with the amputation. The regeneration blastema held back in its further development is thus something other than that tissue cap, although in its external form it may perhaps look more or less similar to it. The finer structure, however, is here and there a different one, and with this observation there also falls away the possibility of regarding the tissue caps described above — which also develop without nerve influence — simply as starts toward extremity regeneration that have come to a standstill at early stages.

If one lets the blastema develop further undisturbed, then it passes, by way of the conical bud stage, into the stage of the dorsoventrally flattened anlage, trapezoidal in outline. If the nerves are excluded at this stage, one can in the sequel observe regressions of the regenerate down to the blastema. Only the pigmentation continues unrestrainably further, so that the stump is finally closed off again, as in the series just discussed before, by a pigmented blastema.

The flattened anlage develops, under normal circumstances, further into a typically *two-pronged* stage, whose radial prong contains the anlage of the first finger, [and] whose ulnar [prong contains] the anlage of the three remaining fingers. By this point in time the formation has already become more feeble and for the most part already contains the anlage of the forearm and of the elbow joint. The consequences of the nerve transection at this stage are the following: First the externally visible differentiations regress, [and] the contour, made jagged by the projecting finger anlagen, becomes smooth-edged again; at the same time the anlage as a whole becomes smaller and more slender, and finally comes to a standstill in the state of a pigmented peg (N 2, N 5, N 8). In principle the involution proceeded in the same way in N 7; only here there was no regression of the already-executed two-pronged differentiation, but rather, although the anlage as a whole shrank, it remained permanently two-pronged.

In the series of the last-discussed animals there now also appeared — as was already pointed out above — the only animal which deviated in its behavior from what is otherwise generally observable (N 6). For in it the exclusion of nn. I and II brought with it no, or at least no noteworthy, impairment of the course of regeneration. A re-innervation through I or II had not taken place, as one could already see from the functionlessness of the upper arm and as could be confirmed by the anatomical finding. There may therefore in this case have been present an extensive collateral neurotization of the stump. I have unfortunately neglected, by means of suitable preservation, to make the stump accessible to microscopic examination of its nerve supply.

In contrast to the strong involution of the distal parts, such as comes to observation after the nerve exclusion, it is striking that the elbow joint, once it has been laid down, is, even through the nerve interruption, no longer able to be brought to disappearance. Although here, of course, there can naturally be no question of a functional development, nevertheless the peg-shaped remainder of the forearm regenerate remains permanently set off at a sharp angle against the upper arm.

As the last [point] we now have to discuss the nerve exclusion at the stage of the regenerate already *differentiated* into elbow, forearm and four-fingered hand, *but not yet finished in development*. In Series M V we see, at the examination carried out 11 days after the nerve exclusion, the forearm present only any more as a feeble pigmented peg, [and] the hand missing. With these animals, to be sure, I cannot exclude with certainty that the hand has not, say, fallen off at the wrist — that is, that a genuine involution is not present at all. Against this, the cases d and e from Series M VI are thoroughly conclusive; in them the nerve exclusion was quite distinctly followed by regression phenomena. These did not go so far as we saw in the preceding series, where the nerves had been interrupted at an earlier stage; the four-rayed development of the autopodium remained clearly recognizable thereafter as well, only the formation as a whole became weaker and took on the appearance of a younger, less far-advanced stage.

The results of the nerve transections carried out at various stages of regeneration may thus be summarized as follows: shape-regressions take place which are the more extensive the more plastic the new formation still is; in the process there arise, out of the more finely differentiated structures, through massive amalgamation, more simply shaped involution products — spherical calottes, discs, pegs, in general at any rate formations with a smooth, unarticulated contour. It looks as though surface tension, tissue elasticity and the like — those physical factors, then, which strive to produce the smallest possible surface, and against which formerly the inner differentiation processes had begun to work out the proper shape of the formation — had now gained the upper hand and could now for their part alone determine the shape of the formation, in so far as the inner processes had not already previously created a structure. I should therefore like to set the phenomenon in analogy to the rounding-up into a sphere of a previously lappet-bearing protist-piece drawn out into pseudopodia, after the severing-off from the nucleus-containing body.

### Discussion of the findings under partial exclusion of the nerves.

#### a) Exclusion before the plexus formation.

If one of the two main nerve trunks leading to the limb was excluded centralward from the plexus formation, then the regeneration process ran off, in formal respect, just as faultlessly as it would have if both trunks had remained intact. From this it follows that, in order to bring about the characteristic nerve action upon the regeneration process, *half* of the normal nerve supply is entirely sufficient.

The functional deficiency phenomena after the operation are, moreover, only of very slight degree.

#### b) Exclusion after the plexus formation.

In the plexus the nerve fibers are combined into new associations, in which they now lead to anatomically delimited end-regions. The functionlessness of particular muscle groups after the interruption of one or the other nerve peripherally from the plexus formation gives evidence that, through the operation, quite definite end-regions are switched off from the central excitation supply. Moreover, the individual nerves also form, even in the free limb, various smaller anastomoses, and this again with strong inter-individual variations, so that one cannot speak of a stereotypy in the anatomical nerve supply; correspondingly, the deficiency phenomena in two different animals, after the operation that is exactly the same in both, are by no means also the same.

While after the severing of one of the main nerves centralward from the plexus formation *all* parts of the free limb are still innervated (only just to a weaker degree), the interruption of a nerve emerging from the plexus produces almost complete denervation of a circumscribed district in the midst of regions with intact nerve supply. Now were a partitioning of the nervous function in limb regeneration of the kind that *Wolff* has imagined it to be a fact, then after partial nerve exclusion distalward from the plexus the morphogenetic component belonging to the excluded nerve part would have to drop out of the regeneration process, and then in the final structure the peripheral section concerned would have to be missing. Such a behavior was now shown in none of the investigated cases. To be sure, after the exclusion of one or the other nerve there resulted a quite considerable circumscribed deficiency of movement-function, but one never noticed, when afterwards amputation was carried out and when a regenerate then arose, any formal defect whatever in it: it always had *typical form*, just like any other regenerate on a well-innervated stump. With this, however, the possibility — discussed above under point I a — of a determinative influence of the nerves upon the regeneration process falls away. It is thus not only a part of the normal innervation alone that is sufficient for typically shaped regenerates to be able to arise, but it is also *for the quality of the regenerate entirely a matter of indifference which part of the nerves it is*.

Up to now we had pushed the form-quality of the regenerates into the foreground of consideration, for it was precisely this that had been held by many quarters to be a product of nervous influences, and therefore its relation to the now firmly established nerve influences — whose mode of action, however, was still not seen through — had to be clarified in the first place. The far-reaching independence of the form-quality of the regenerate from the amount, kind and distribution of the nerve supply, as it was taught by the experiments, now makes the assumption of a closer connection between form-quality and nervous influences impossible.

If, however, we now consider no longer the quality-relations but rather the *intensity*-relations of the regeneration process after nervous disturbances, then the experimental results do nevertheless yield us a positive finding which may in further sequence perhaps put us on the track of a more precise knowledge of what kind of efficacy the nerves exert upon regeneration: it shows itself, namely, in the experiments with partial nerve exclusion, that the intensity of the regeneration process is lowered on the operated side compared with the opposite side; the regenerates lag somewhat behind, during the differentiation, in comparison with the control side. In the protocols this phenomenon has in each case been given expression by the sign: „l < r". The lag, even if it is as a rule distinctly noticeable, is yet mostly only slight, in any case much too slight for one to be permitted to assume that it is conditioned by loss of time up to an eventual re-ingrowth of the removed nerves; this latter assumption is moreover already rendered untenable by the fact that the process does not, say, set in belatedly, but rather begins at the same time as on the control side, only at a somewhat slowed tempo. When both regenerates, the one on the intact stump on the control side and the one on the partly denervated stump on the operated side, are already fully differentiated out, have attained normal form and also already stand in full function, the regenerate on the operated side is yet of lesser size than that on the opposite side, and only after several weeks are the two also equal to each other in size.

We can thus establish that *partial exclusion of the limb nerves* (up to a certain extent, at least however still up to the absence of ½ of all normal nerves) does not indeed impair the *quality* of the regeneration process, but does clearly *weaken* its *intensity*. From this form of efficacy I now believe I may draw an indication as to the nature of the nerve influences involved; in doing so I start from the following considerations, which on the whole are probably apposite:

The nervous system of the vertebrates falls into two fairly sharply demarcable parts, different both in their behavior and in their functions: the „somatic" and the „autonomic" (terminology after *Langley*, 16). The opposition between an „animal" and a „vegetative" system, or between a system serving the „voluntary" and one serving the „involuntary" functions, has indeed long been known, and each of these has not only its functional but also pronounced anatomical and pharmacological characteristics. While the somatic system serves in the first place the operation of the skeletal musculature, the autonomic system acts upon the course of the maintenance functions of the organism, hence in particular upon the „involuntary" activity of the circulatory, digestive, reproductive, excretory and secretory apparatus. The essential thing now is that the latter functions run their course to a certain degree also independently of central influences, and that the nervous actions of the autonomic system express themselves only in the regulation of a process going on anyway, „aneurogenically"; they promote or inhibit, but they do not determine. As against the localized and purely differentiating activity of the somatic system, the efficacy of the autonomic apparatus seems to extend rather to the intensity-relations of processes that are qualitatively already fixed (independently of any influences whatever).

One already notices now how I was led to the conjecture that in the nerve action upon the regeneration processes too there is probably involved an activity of the autonomic system: That nervous factors had anything at all to do with the regeneration process was proved; an influence of specifically determinative kind had been excluded by the experiments, so that the quality of the process was nerve-independent; on the other hand the „stronger" or „weaker" of the process showed itself in distinct dependence on the „more" or „less" of the nerve supply. We thus observe a similar intensity-impairment of a process such as we are accustomed to see exercised by parts of the autonomic system.

This consideration now leads to the hypothesis that the behavior of a denervated amputation stump, deviating from the normal regeneration processes, is to be traced back to the absence of the autonomic (probably *sympathetic* sensu str.) innervation, and that accordingly *the regeneration of the limb is bound to the presence of sympathetic innervation*.

This conjecture I have already briefly expressed in an earlier compilation of regeneration findings (*P. Weiss*, 26); it agrees excellently with the experimental results of the other authors:

When the regenerative capacity of animals with a bored-out spinal cord made the assumption of motor, as of medullary influences in general, impossible, while one nevertheless, in accordance with the experimental results, had to seek the influence-center in the region of the vertebral column, then it was indeed most natural to think of the spinal ganglia. That one could form no more precise conception of how their mode of action might be constituted could not yet be regarded as a ground for exclusion. In the first place *Wolff*, and then later *Walter*, took the spinal ganglia to be the sought-for center of action. This fitted well with the view of *Herbst* (11), who strove to find again, in more general distribution as well, the influence of sensory elements upon morphological processes that he had inferred from his well-known heteromorphosis findings on Crustacea, and who in consequence had already earlier discussed and advocated the possibility of a connection between the sensory nervous system and form-formation. Let it now be mentioned right here, in order that no misunderstandings may arise, that an experimentum crucis for the proposition that not sensory but sympathetic efficacy is at play in the regeneration process has up to today still not been furnished¹). *Goldfarb's* cases too, in which regeneration set in although only remnants of the spinal ganglia were present, cannot be counted as a strict counter-proof against the efficacy of the spinal ganglia in the regeneration process, for I have myself above, under point I b (p. 322), described a possibility of how a center might still be capable of unfolding full efficacy even after partial damage.

No compelling experimental results, then, but solely and alone the consideration of what we know of the other activity of the various parts of the nervous system, makes it up to now probable that not spinal but sympathetic actions are present in limb regeneration. The associated centers would accordingly be sought in the ganglia of the border-strand; with

The post-ganglionic fibres destined for the limb run through the *Rami communicantes grisei* from the vertebral ganglia and join the spinal nerves still within the region of the vertebral column. Severance of the continuity of the spinal nerves peripherally from this junction at the same time entails the elimination of the sympathetic innervation of the corresponding terminal region. In the experiments involving resection of the whole section of the vertebral column (*Wolff*, *Walter*), it was naturally not only the spinal ganglia that were removed together with the spinal-cord segments; hence the failure of regeneration was not necessarily to be ascribed to their absence, for the *Rami communicantes* — if indeed not even the sympathetic trunk itself — had also been excised, and it is precisely the loss of these that I should be inclined to blame for the regenerative incapacity of the stumps in these experiments. That, on the other hand, the mere boring-out of the spinal cord within the vertebral canal can have no influence on regeneration — since thereby neither the sympathetic ganglia nor their connections to the spinal nerves and thus to the periphery are damaged — follows, as one sees, likewise from our conception, and stands in agreement with the actual experimental results. Thus no contradiction with the facts arises if in what follows we now localize the nervous influence-centre designated above as "for the present still unknown," precisely that one which by others was suspected in the spinal ganglia, in the *sympathetic ganglia of the sympathetic trunk*. For the present, in the absence of direct experimental proofs, the probability of this hypothesis may be supported by its capacity to explain the phenomena.

## III. Results.

### The differences in the regenerative behaviour of intact and denervated stumps.

In the introduction we were indeed able to restrict the number of possibilities as to how regeneration might be conceived in a manner faithful to the facts, but nevertheless could not arrive at a clear decision. It is foreign to the economical nature of investigation to assume prematurely novel modes of action, so long as any probability still exists that the old, familiar ones suffice for the explanation. We shall therefore have to endeavour to make do, as far as possible, with the assumption that even in the regeneration-nerve-action only a variety of a nervous process already known from other actions is at play, and not an essentially new function of the nervous apparatus. This principle is made considerably easier for us by the fact that many a nervous influence has already been inferred from its effects and the phenomena belonging together have already been given a name, without, however, the extent and the peculiar character of the producing nervous process being thereby more precisely known and delimited. To this category of nerve-actions, of which for the present we still have rather vague concepts, belong also those two kinds whose participation in the regeneration-process turned out above to be possible, or could at least not be excluded a priori: "tonic" and "trophic" nerve-action.

As regards the former first of all, its methodical separation from the differential excitatory functions of the nerves seems thoroughly justified. The concept of "tonus" was set up with reference to the muscle and connotes a kind of persistent excitatory state, upon which the differential activity-excitation is superimposed. The transference of the concept to other "persistent excitatory states" gives it a more general significance; thus one speaks, for instance, also of a "tonus" of nervous centres. Indeed, an attempt is even made to hold an autonomous innervation — that is, an essentially different one from that for the movement-function — responsible for the "tonus" of the skeletal musculature; this point, however, is still too much disputed among the physiologists themselves (compare, for example, *E. A. Spiegel*, 23) for any conclusions to be already permitted from it; nevertheless, in the endeavour to carry through such a separation we may perhaps see a proof that the phenomenal difference between tonic persistent excitation and differential individual excitation is a quite striking one and precisely permits the inference of a fundamentally separate position of "tonic" processes.

Much more vaguely circumscribed than the domain of "tonic" innervation-actions is that of the "trophic" ones (compare *Jensen*, 15). The impetus toward assuming a nervous trophic activity at all was given by the observation of the so-called "atrophies" following nerve-section. The regressive changes that showed themselves in denervated tissues were sometimes traced directly to the absence of function, sometimes again to the cessation of a "nutritive" stimulus which the nerves, alongside their specific transmission of function, had exerted on the tissue. In favour of the latter conception one could adduce the fact that already the mere presence of the intact nerve-supply, even in organs incapable of functioning (for example, in muscles set in plaster), prevents the atrophy; one would then have to assume otherwise that already the impulse to function alone, even when it cannot be acted upon, exerts the same effect as the function itself. This latter one might, particularly after the findings of *E. Cords* (5), again be inclined to regard as quite probable: she found that anatomically "functionless" muscles which insert on the same skeletal element, that is, muscles which would have to degenerate equally, do not degenerate; only those parts of functionless muscles that could function were degenerated. It will, however, always remain difficult to decide directly in any individual case whether the functional dependence of a tissue on the nervous system is of a tonic or really of a trophic kind. From this one may already foresee that the boundary between tonic and trophic nerve-action will be especially hard to draw. The thought arises that the trophic activity might be only something belonging to the proper province of tonus, only a variety of the latter. The trophic effect would then, if one thinks the matter through, in fact be the actually maintaining, persistent effect of the afferent nerve-fibres directed indeed toward sustenance, but addressed to the life-processes of the tissue itself, while one had hitherto had a tonus only of the activity-processes before one. The thought that the functional activity has something trophic about it, if one thinks of the functional excitatory conduction of the afferent nerve-fibres as a centrally directed one, this then seems thoroughly conceivable; for the life-processes of the denervated nerve too proceed centripetally, that is, from their own trophic centre, the spinal ganglion, away, also in a centrifugal direction.

We shall now have to examine which of the two designated, sharply contrasted kinds of nerve-action — the one again found in the regeneration-processes or the other one nearer at hand — it is that comes to expression in the failure of the typical configurative capacity of denervated stumps, and to clarify summarily the differences between the processes in the intact and in the denervated stump, so that the overview becomes easier as to which processes are influenced by the nervous system.

The experiments have yielded the following antithesis: In the case of an intact stump, typically configurative processes (formation of limb, configuration, and differentiation of the newly built-up material). In the case of a denervated stump, mere proliferation, typically formless. Proliferation occurs in both cases, that is, in both an arising takes place, a continuation by way of cell-division of the process already begun at an earlier stage with the cessation of the stump, indeed not only, as it seems, in the same way, but rather still more intensely; for the proof, that already the mere existence of the formless structures testifies, this denervated stump is no longer at all incapable of growth. We thus find in the course of the configured regeneration-process from the unorganized tissue-cap of b a corresponding [formation] would be — but rather what we know about the differences in the genesis of the two may be drawn upon.

On the basis of experiments I have been able, as already mentioned above, to render probable that the anlage of an extremity-regenerate in *Triton* proceeds from uniform material, and that the individual tissues differentiate themselves out only later within the anlage, so that accordingly no *genetic* continuity exists between each tissue of the regenerate and the tissue of like kind in the stump (*P. Weiss*, 25). On this insight we have grounded a distinction between a process of "organ-regeneration" (as an autonomous process) and a mere sum of "tissue-regenerations" (*P. Weiss*, 26). The experiments have shown, specifically for the bone, that the bone of the regenerate does not proceed from the residual piece of the bone in the stump.

If we now apply this experience to the case here under discussion, then in process a we have before us those processes, designated as "organ-regeneration," which lead to typically configured terminal structures; on the other hand, in process b we have before us formless proliferations, which are set in train by the individual tissues, each for itself, and of which we now know that they do not, say, represent building-blocks in the restoration-process of the extremity. We see in b the bone proliferating, and precisely of the bone we have heard that the skeleton in the regenerate is not built up from its proliferative activity.

We can establish yet a further point: what the proliferative activity in process b accomplishes is not throughout tissue-regenerates, although each tissue for itself creates something new; for, in order to count as a "regenerate," the products of this neoformation-activity lack the typical configuration. Not every neoformation is therefore already a regenerate — this cannot be emphasized sharply enough; the bone-callus, the connective-tissue scar, these are no regenerates (*Bier*, 2), these are formless tissue-masses which can only cement, without configuring themselves. For that reason I too came out already earlier against the use of the word "regenerative" in cases in which it is merely a matter of cementing-processes (*Weiss*, 27). In the tissues too, then, we have to distinguish a little-structured re-formed state, and only where the latter is restored may one speak of "tissue-regeneration." It may be that in process b the epidermis and cutis "regenerate," but bone and connective tissue, according to the findings of *Rubin*, in any case do not do so; they proliferate *formlessly*.

From all this it follows that a and b are not processes differing simply in degree alone. What now appears important in principle is the decision whether, of the processes a and b, the one is, through its influence, in each case excluded by the other from the outset, or whether both processes always coexist alongside one another, in such a manner, however, that the one time a gains the upper hand over b, the other time b over a. If a and b were, to begin with, mutually incompatible processes, then in the case of normal regeneration there would be only "organ-regeneration" without any unorganized tissue-proliferation, whereas after nerve-section there would again be only formless tissue-proliferation without even the slightest approach to organ-regeneration. On the other hand, one would have to consider also the second of the mentioned possibilities, namely that in every regeneration, with and without nerves, a *and* b set in, but with one of these processes of overpowering strength. According to this view, then, even in the case of normal regeneration at the innervated stump (a > b) a certain, though only weak, special-proliferation-activity of the individual tissues would have to run alongside the "organ-regeneration-process" and into it; and analogously one would then have to assume, for the processes at the denervated stump (b > a), that the capacity for the execution of an a would indeed be present, but that the by far more powerful process b does not let the weak a come to unfolding. For the view that a and b coexist alongside one another, I could adduce the fact that in normal regeneration, at the place at which, in the "organ-regenerate," autonomously arisen bone meets the old bone-stump, I have also found unformed callus, which probably goes back to production on the part of the old bone. Against the assumption of the capacity of a and b to coexist, however, there again seems to speak the fact that, after elimination of the nerves, we have on later regeneration-stages not simply observed an outstripping of process b, but rather an active regression of the phenomena of a.

Even though the alternative posed above cannot for the present be decided definitively yet, the experimental results nevertheless point in the direction of the following solution: When the nerves are present, a *and* b set in, but a > b; without nerves only b runs its course and a fails to appear, or rather regresses, if it had already got under way beforehand. b thus seems in all cases to set in *after wound-infliction*. It is obvious to hold responsible for its release a direct effect of the wound, whether it be now of humoral nature (*Haberlandt*, 10) or whether it be of the kind of the wound-far-actions discovered by *Gurwitsch* (9). This wound-action is accordingly also nerve-independent. There also results quite easily an explanation for why these b-processes can proceed with considerable intensity after nerve-section: namely, as soon as the stimulus to proliferation from the wound is present, there is needed only the requisite quantity of nutritive substances for the neoformation really to be set in train; [Page heading: P. Weiss: Dependence of the regeneration]

now however lacks, in consequence of the nerve-section, the whole sympathetic vasomotor innervation; the vessels are therefore maximally dilated, the whole region becomes hyperaemic and thus is supplied with nutritive-substances in superabundant measure. Thus the vigorous growth-processes after the denervation are not at all astonishing. In the same way, for example, *Zechanowitsch* (33) found, after section of the cervical sympathetic in the mammal, hypertrophy of cartilage and cutis.

At the innervated amputation-stump there then comes, in addition to b, the process a, and this is the nerve-dependent one. If we have now just heard that the nutritive-conditions of the stump after the denervation are rather even better than before, we shall scarcely be permitted to trace back the failure of process a after denervation to deficient nutritive-conditions. Accordingly, the nerve-action too, which normally has process a in its train, cannot simply consist in this, that for the a-process especially favourable nutritive-conditions are created; one will thus hardly be able to speak of a "trophic" effect.

### The "configuration-tonus."

We are finally led to test whether the nerve-action upon a might not be of the kind of the "tonic" one. And this assumption, as it seems to me, hits upon the right thing. In doing so I picture the state of affairs to myself as follows:

In order that typical form-formation should really occur in a material otherwise capable of form-formation and also potentially already containing the form-quality, a definite persistent (*unspecific*) excitatory state of this material is required. So long as this excitatory state persists, the form-formation continues, and from it it derives not only the force to carry through the proper form of the structure itself against the other physical factors governing the material (surface-tension, tissue-elasticity, etc.) in the course of the making, but rather this persistent excitatory state is also required further for the *maintenance* of the young form, so long as the structures created are not yet consolidated, so long as the form is not yet rigidified and supported by firm structures. These shall be the only assumptions that we wish to make concerning the "configuration-tonus"; they alone already explain to us the whole of the experimentally found facts.

Thus we now comprehend why the autonomous nervous system, in which we must perceive the transmitter of this tonic state, is required not only for the *onset* of the a-process, but rather throughout its whole continuance. If the nerves are interrupted and the tonus falls away, then thereby at the same time not only does every further form-formation cease, but rather there clearly shows itself the incapacity of the structure to maintain henceforth even merely its proper form, already created up to then, against the resisting (surface-diminishing) factors. If up to then the tonus had furnished a kind of force-source, from which the material maintained its capacity for proper configuration, then this material was now, after the falling-away of the tonus-source, given over solely to the alien-configuring constraint of the previously overpowered physical factors (surface-tension, etc.); these latter factors now hold sway unrestrictedly and lead, in so far as rigidified structures do not put a stop to their action, to the involutional form-simplifications described above, such as roundings-off into spheres, rounding and smoothing of the contours, etc. — in short, there comes about an unmistakable transition from typically differentiated organic forms into forms such as would have to be formed by the material involved even in the unorganized, "dead" state according to the law of the least surface; only simple growth in already-fixed directions might perhaps continue. —

Therefore I have already, in the discussion of the relevant experiments, pointed to the analogy of the configurative incapacity in denervated stumps and in enucleated Protistan plasm; here too there comes about that transition from typical proper Gestalt into the spherical form demanded by the surface-tension alone. We can now go a step further: we can regard the nerve-dependence of regeneration in *Triton* and the nucleus-dependence of form-formation in the Protist under a unitary point of view. It is then to be assumed that, in the configuration-formation and -maintenance of the single-celled organism, the nucleus is the agent which furnishes to the plasm — qualitatively *per se* capable of configuration-formation — the realizing persistent excitatory state, the "configuration-tonus." With the enucleation this configuration-tonus is lost, and under the now solely operating influence of the surface-tension a rounding-off into a sphere can ensue.¹ In remarkable agreement with this view stands the observation that, with the entry of certain resting-states in the life of the single-celled organism, with a reduction of the life-activity to a minimum,

Most clearly does the kind of role that falls to the nucleus in the formative processes show itself in regeneration. Exactly as in our case, it shows itself there that the form-quality is contained in the building-material and can as little be thought of here as represented in the nucleus as there in the nervous system; for the production of the regenerate, however, a certain — and again unspecific — influence on the part of the nucleus is required. To some degree even nucleus-fragments or substances secreted by the nucleus seem to suffice to unfold the peculiar activity (compare *B. Sokoloff*, Arch. f. Protistenkunde 47, 143. 1924).

It is clear that — *natura non facit saltum* — we may now expect to find every typical formative process accompanied by a realizing formative tonus, even if perhaps we cannot always, as exactly as in the preceding cases (nerves, nucleus), be able to identify a definite excitation centre. Thus, while it is indeed straightforward to regard it as probable that the triggering of the formative tonus required for normal ontogeny is effected by the developmental excitation, here the question of a delimitable region of origin of the tonic effects in the germ seems — if it is justified at all — for the present not decidable. In the course of ontogeny, with the increasing rigidification of form, with the partitioning of the organism into "partial spheres of action" (*Weiss*, 30), and with the creation of fixed scaffolding structures, the formative tonus becomes superfluous in its original extent. With the transition from the first into the third developmental period (*Roux*, 19) the tonic activity originally set in motion in the formative material at the developmental excitation, as well as many another originally autonomous function, is then apparently taken over by the nervous system, and in consequence we have before us, in the developed stages, the state of affairs found in regeneration: that the formative material, in order to be able to arrive at form-building, requires a tonic excitation by the nervous system.

Now these hypothetical considerations would perhaps not carry much value if it were a matter of a fact to be drawn solely from the regeneration phenomena of the Tritons. But if one looks more closely, one is led to the insight that in the "formative tonus" we have found a quite generally usable principle of explanation. First of all, I believe that from it most of the form-changes designated as "atrophy" and dependent on nerve loss can be interpreted. In many an "atrophy" we are dealing not with simple shrinkages, proportional changes of size, but for the most part with active changes of form. Let me, taking the bone as the organ which surely exhibits the most sharply pronounced characteristic form, adduce one example for many:

*H. Nasse* (17) studied the changes of the bones after nerve section in the dog, and on bones which were not exposed to any secondary inflammatory process he generally found "atrophy". Now when we see how, under this concept, what is understood is not, say, a form-preserving diminution of the bone, but a quite definitely directed change of form, then we are led to the recognition that it is not a matter of the dropping-out of a diffuse "trophic", i.e. nutritional, influence, but rather of the consequence of the dropping-out of a nerve action which had something to do with the form itself and which is quite analogous to the one observed by us. *Nasse* writes, namely (loc. cit. p. 381): "With the change which the thickness of the bone undergoes in paralysed limbs, there is connected one concerning the form, to which *Schiff* has already drawn attention. This is the rounding-off conditioned by the flattening of the edges and projections … With the regeneration of the nerves … it must disappear; at least I then never found it again."

What, now, do we find in this report? I think we meet here with exactly the same phenomenon that we observe, say, after nerve section at an advanced stage of regeneration: a regression of the most advanced, i.e. youngest, differentiations and an approach toward a shape conditioned by the physical material-factors, above all once again "flattening of edges and projections", "rounding-off", which we have to regard as the effect of the purely physical tendency toward the production of minimal surface extension. How little real nutritional and growth-impediments are here primarily in play, how little it is therefore a matter of "trophic" disturbances, one may gather from the observation of *Schiff*, cited by *Nasse*, that in young dogs after nerve section callous proliferations, hence lively growth processes, appear at the bone. With this statement of *Schiff's* the analogy to our results communicated above becomes still more impressive: for here too we have before us not only the regressive changes of the form-process *a*, but alongside them the progressive unorganized proliferations of the process *b* (presumably in consequence of the hyperaemia appearing after vascular denervation).

In order now to adduce yet one more confirming finding from another quarter, again for the bone, let reference be made to experiences of *Bier* (3): he has succeeded, in man under suitable conditions, in obtaining in bone-gaps "true", i.e. typically shaped, bone regenerates, which by their well-formedness differ quite sharply from the unorganized callus masses such as are usually formed as a bridge between two ends of a bone-fracture. It did not escape him, now, that in patients afflicted with definite nervous disorders the callus formation appeared enormously increased; and there is no doubt that the failure to appear of formed regeneration, despite excessive proliferative capacity, must here again be brought into connexion with the lack of the nervous formative tonus — it is evidently absent in consequence of the nervous disorder.

There shall now be no further entering here upon the general distribution of the formative tonus; what was to be shown — that the assumption of such a thing is not simply plucked out of thin air, but is inferable on the basis of facts, and that its applicability is by no means restricted to the particular case of Triton regeneration — has been shown; everything further will have to be decided experimentally. Many an insight I promise myself from experiments in which, instead of the crude method of nerve section, that of the differential action of pharmacological agents will be employed. Since in the case of the formative tonus — which cannot be emphasized sharply enough — we are dealing with a thoroughly *unspecific* state of excitation, to which no qualitative, but only a triggering and intensive capacity of action belongs, perhaps even agents may be found which would be in a position to replace the nerve action in this point.

For those who are eager already now to connect more precise material conceptions with the concept of the tonus, let it be added that to them, in *Hering's* metabolic scheme (12), for instance, the bridge to a material conception is shown. Thus let it be mentioned, for example, that to the intrinsic light of the retina — which was represented by *Hering* (13) as an equilibrium-state of the assimilation- and dissimilation-processes in the visual substance — a "tonus" of the visual centre was assigned by *Fr. W. Fröhlich* (6); here, then, an affinity-relation between tonus and some metabolic processes is directly alluded to. In our case, however, it does not seem appropriate to lay down for oneself, already now and without experimental support, special conceptions of the "formative tonus".

## IV. Summary.

1. After excision of the principal arm nerves, before or simultaneously with an amputation in the upper arm of *Triton cristatus* [modern *Triturus cristatus*], the "organ regeneration" fails to appear. The process (*a*), which otherwise at the innervated amputation stump leads, by way of blastema, bud, and rudiment, to the production of a differentiated, typically shaped limb, does not set in without nerves.

2. Instead of this, one observes at the wound surface on the denervated stump *unorganized proliferative activity* (*b*) of the individual tissues; through this there comes about a scar-like, cap-shaped covering-over of the stump.

3. Switching off the nerves on amputation stumps on which the "organ regeneration" is already under way has as its consequence not only an immediate standstill of the form-building process, but in addition there set in, on the young formations, involution processes which express themselves in the regression of differentiations already formed; the directional sense of these changes corresponds to a transition from the typical characteristic shape of the formations to a form demanded by the purely physical striving toward the production of minimal surface (surface tension, tissue elasticity, etc.).

4. The nerve influence is necessary not only for the setting-in, but *during the whole duration* of the regeneration process.

5. After switching off only a *part* of the nerves (one of the two main trunks proximal to the plexus formation; of the nervus brachialis longus inferior, wholly or by half, or of the nervus brachialis longus superior, wholly or by half, distal from the plexus formation) there arises in all cases a form-correct typical regenerate, no matter which part of the innervation is missing. The assumption of a determinative, i.e. quality-determining, influence of the nervous system upon the regeneration process becomes untenable; the influence is rather an *unspecific* one.

6. Partial switching-off of nerves has as its consequence a *retardation* in the tempo of the regeneration process.

7. In the described influence of the nervous system we are probably dealing with an action of the *sympathetic* nervous system.

8. The proliferative capacity at the wound surface is nerve-independent; only in order that the material may arrive at *form-building* does it require the nervous (unspecific) influence. The nerve action here seems to be of the kind of the "tonic" actions, i.e. it supplies the material with a realizing, *unspecific* state of permanent excitation, which appears to be generally required for the coming-about of form-development ("formative tonus").

9. The "formative tonus", which for the Protists is evidently supplied by the nucleus, seems for the first stages of ontogeny in the Metazoa to be triggered by the developmental excitation, and its maintenance seems (in the higher forms) only later to pass over to the autonomic nervous system. As soon as rigid scaffolding structures are once formed, it becomes dispensable for these. Nerve section has as its consequence the dropping-away of the formative tonus, and hence there then comes about a back-differentiation of forms not yet rigidified toward the state of smallest possible surface, phenomena which hitherto were in part classed among the "atrophies".

## Addendum.

After completion of the work I became acquainted with several publications concerning the theme treated here, of the first of which I had already previously gained a fleeting knowledge through an abstract in the *Année Biologique*. The author of these works, Herr *Oscar Schotté* in Geneva, had the kindness to send me his communications, which are all published in the *Comptes rendus des séances de la société de physique et d'histoire naturelle* (Vol. 39, p. 67 and 134, 1922, and Vol. 40, p. 86, 100 and 160, 1923). The factual results of his experiments (on Tritons) coincide, in general and gratifyingly, completely with those which I have brought forward in the foregoing. In one essential point, however, his findings go far beyond mine: he succeeded, namely, in showing experimentally that that part of the nervous system which is necessary for the regeneration of the limb is in actual fact the *sympathetic* nervous system. He was able to show that after extirpation of the sympathetic chain [Grenzstrang], with completely intact motor and sensory innervation of the limb, regeneration after amputation failed to appear. Thus what I above could only infer *per exclusionem* is now, through the experiment, irreproachably established. The involution phenomena of regeneration stages after nerve section during the regeneration process *Schotté* was also able to observe. He also enters upon my findings after partial nerve section, as they had become known to him from my preliminary communication of 1922, and adduces analogous experiments in which he believes himself to have come into contradiction with me, when he establishes that partial nerve severance can entail not only retardation but also total inhibition of regeneration. The state of affairs, however, now clears itself up without further ado: *Schotté* always conducted his amputation cuts in the forearm, in the vicinity of the carpus [Handwurzel], hence in a region of much weaker regenerative power than in my experiments, and therefore a resistance against which an upperarm regenerate would still be capable of properly prevailing could often no longer be overcome; but if *Schotté* takes exception to my statement that, for the orderly regeneration of the limb after partial nerve severance, the prior restoration of the interrupted connexion is not necessary, then I can, in reply and as an argument against all indirect inferences, refer only to the anatomical finding, as it is appended above to the experimental descriptions and as it, again and again after the running-off of the form-correct regeneration, still permitted, after months, the establishment of the *complete absence* of the nerve excised at the time. —
