## A. Introduction.

### Aim and history of the experiments.

In contrast to the view of Weismann [16, 17]¹⁾ and Bordage [5], that the regenerative capacity of an organ depends on its importance and on the danger of its loss, many experiments have shown that these two factors play no role with respect to the renewal capacity; according to the experimental results this stands, rather, on the one hand in relation to the degree of differentiation

> ¹⁾ The figures standing in square brackets refer to the list of literature.

of the organ, and on the other hand to the stage in the phylogeny of the animal and the particular specialization within its phyletic group, and finally to the stage of ontogeny in inverse proportionality.

On the basis of this knowledge, that the regenerative potency depends in general on the developmental height upon which one has stood up till now, well-collected compilations of the factual material are found in Przibram's "Regeneration" [14]; here, therefore, it does not need to be examined in further detail whether still other general factors, for example specific body size, exert an influence on the regenerative capacity.

That age, namely, is by no means of equal significance with the ontogenetic developmental stage — this is taught us in the animal kingdom by the widely distributed phenomenon of neoteny [4], that is, the retention of morphological characters of a lower phylogenetic stage even in older specimens. The ontogeny of various specimens of one and the same species differs in that the genuine morphosis is held back, so that the animals remain longer in the larval morphosis; namely, this depends already on the external factors, which are more or less hereditary, but also on the onset of sexual maturity.

Nowhere, however, does neoteny come more clearly to the fore, and nowhere can the regenerative capacity standing in relation to it be better studied and even now in some places be evoked by simple experimental means, than is the case in the larvae of the amphibians. For that reason the regeneration experiments described here had as their material my regeneration experiments undertaken in the months of June, July, August and September 1904, with which I occupied myself in like manner with the two questions, both of the dependence relation of the regenerative capacity on age, developmental stage, and specific size. Apart from this solution, however, there hovered before me at the beginning of the experiments the question of what next-following results are yielded by the influence which age exerts, by the influence of amputation and its consequences (wound-healing, regeneration) on the respective body parts. — For the completion and roundness of the results, some tableaux also attach themselves which were obtained on the occasion of the earlier work as material lying before me for observation, but which stand in inner relation thereto.

The experiments were for the greatest part (and indeed experiment Nr. I–IX inclusive and Nr. XI, 1st series) carried out in the Biological Experimental Institute at Vienna, only the experiment Nr. VIII in Johannisbad (Bohemia) and the experiments Nr. X and XII in Wolfsegg (Upper Austria).

## C. Technique.

### Keeping and care of the experimental animals, together with mention of some movement-tendencies peculiar to them. — Method of the operations. — Conservation of the material.

The amphibian larvae used in the experiments were kept in **aquaria** and **glass tubs**, whose size was governed by the number of larvae inhabiting them. The height of the water level was 10–15 cm for the larvae destined for a normal developmental course; 1–2 cm where an accelerated, about 50 cm where a delayed onset of the metamorphosis was at issue. The water temperature was 19–20° C. and, since the vessels were set up inaccessible to the direct rays of the sun, remained constantly even at this height. Only where an accelerated metamorphosis was intended was the temperature raised by intermittent insolation up to 28° C., whereas for the retardation of the metamorphosis (in flowing water) it was kept pressed down to 11° C.

The amphibian larvae are to a certain degree warmth-loving (thermophilic) and accordingly seek out sunlit places of the vessel or pond (positive heliotaxis). Only when the water has warmed up to a temperature of 20–25° C. does the endeavor come to light to return to cooler, shaded places (negative thermo- and heliotaxis).

A change of water is undertaken only when the water, through turbidity and bad odor, betrays its spoiled condition, no longer favorable for the gill-respiration of the larvae; in the partial or complete renewal of the water, care must be taken that no abrupt temperature difference exists between old and fresh water. Yet it has proved favorable for operated larvae, before the establishment of the wound-closure, when the renewed water was 2–3 degrees cooler than the old. — The bottom of the experimental vessels is covered with a layer of cleanly-washed sand or fine gravel.

For the **feeding** of the frog-larvae [Froschlurch-tadpoles] there serve green algae-masses (especially *Spirogyra*), such as are found floating on most pools; but one must take care not to drag in along with these algae-masses any predatory insects (swimming-beetles, water-bugs, dragonfly-larvae, etc.), which would gnaw at the tadpoles and kill them. Further, there serve for feeding pieces of raw flesh, which one lets hang into the water bound to a string, in such a way that they come to lie at the edges or corners of the vessel, where they are most easily found by the searching tadpoles, since these for the most part move forward along the edges and walls and settle to rest in the corners (thigmotaxis). The flesh must on no account be left lying in the water longer than a few hours; food-remnants are to be carefully removed by means of a pipette [Stechheber].

The larvae of the tailed amphibians [Schwanzlurche] are exclusively predators: they receive red brook-tube-worms (*Tubifex rivulorum* d'Ud.) and small crustaceans (Daphnies, *Cyclops*) and, in case of need, are also to be nourished with small, raw flesh-fibers, which one moves slowly back and forth before their snout, skewered on a wire. Once the morsel has been seized, one draws the wire back by careful turning.

A feeding arranged three times weekly suffices for the achievement of a normal nutritional state. If the development is to be accelerated, then one must feed daily and as much as the animals accept at all; only toward the onset of metamorphosis let the nourishment be suddenly scant, for in this stage hunger brings about a quicker execution of the transformation [1]: one feeds only once weekly, anuran larvae one restricts to the vegetable diet. If, on the contrary, the development is to be slowed down, then the reverse course is taken: scant feeding in the first period of larval life, plentiful feeding at the approach of the metamorphosis [11, p. 54 of the separate-print].

As soon as the negative **hydrotaxis** makes itself noticeable in the larvae during the transformation-process, one must offer them, by means of floating cork-bark or by means of stones projecting above the water, an opportunity to leave the same. The fully metamorphosed animals are transferred into simply furnished terraria [11, pp. 12–19 of the separate-print], whose floor-covering consists of gravel (for *Pelobates* a high layer of sandy earth for burrowing) and a few moss-cushions spread over it, and in which a cleanly-kept water-basin must not be lacking. The feeding of the transformed animals (for all the experiments here under consideration as plentiful as possible) takes place with meal-beetle larvae, flies and small earthworms.

During my stay in Johannisbad I kept the tadpoles of *Rana temporaria* worked upon there partly also out in the open, in order to attain a quite normal developmental course — in captivity it is always more or less accelerated —, namely I placed the tadpoles, after the operation had been carried out, into rectangular, closely-latticed wire cages and sank these into a pond with inflow and outflow, which was animated by many thousand tadpoles of the same species and from which I had also taken the material. For the purpose of accelerating the metamorphosis I set these cages at sunny places, which were shallow enough to permit the tadpoles, within the wire-lattice whose upper wall thus stood above water, the unhindered reaching of the surface, and where the temperature in the forenoon sun rose up to 28° C. For the purpose of retarding the metamorphosis I set the cages in the vicinity of the inflow, where the water was coldest (11° C.), namely half a meter below the level.

At this place (in the vicinity of the inflow) the water was not completely still, but rather showed a slow current: this gave occasion for the observation that the tadpoles always set themselves with the head against the current and seek to overcome it through their swimming-movements (negative rheotaxis).

The carrying-out of the operations took place in the simplest manner with the help of a small, somewhat curved pair of scissors, which before each cut was swung a few times through a spirit-flame. The blood which emerges after the operation and settles upon the bottom of the vessel is wiped cleanly away from there by means of a small sponge. The amphibian larvae are very resistant: I do not recall a single case in which the death of a larva was to be ascribed to the consequences of the amputation performed on it. One therefore manages with a comparatively very small amount of material, and only in order to escape the danger of a generalization of individual phenomena is a larger number of experimental animals desirable. Yet such individual deviations occur relatively rarely, and the experimental results, even in the presence of a large amount of material, show such a regularity that on the other hand even single observations must be accorded a certain evidential force.

The amputated limbs and, so far as it appeared necessary, whole animals in various stages and at the close of the experiments were fixed in 10% formol for 24 hours and then transferred into alcohol of increasing percentage, finally preserved in 95% alcohol. This conservation-method is intended only for macroscopic purposes; for a possible later histological investigation, which is to be designated as very desirable, it is hardly likely to be suitable, yet the material necessary for that can, if required, at any time easily be produced anew by experiment.

## D. Description of the experiments.

Nr. I. 20 normal, one-summer-old tadpoles of *Rana septentrionalis*, 81–97 mm long, with small hind-legs whose upper- and lower-leg still form an obtuse angle to one another, and which thus have not yet reached the ontogenetic boundary which Barfurth [3] found for the extinction of the regenerative power in *Rana temporaria*. — Four series of experiments, of five specimens each:

1) Amputation of the **left** hind-leg between insertion and knee, in the middle of the upper-leg or close proximally to the knee.

2) Amputation of the **right** hind-leg at the same place.

3) Amputation of **both** hind-legs.

4) (Control-series) no amputation at all.

5. July: Setting-up of the experiment and carrying-out of the operations. The animals stay quiet during the same, lying on the hand; they thrash about only at a clumsy cut which does not sever the leg at one stroke. A bleeding is perceptible only with larvae having already somewhat thicker legs, but lasts only a few minutes. Directly after the operation some of the larvae press out their intestinal-contents; with that the last sign of discomfort is over, and the animals gnaw soon thereupon at water-plants and flesh-pieces. With their swimming-movements there makes itself a small equilibrium-disturbance noticeable: the trunk, otherwise held horizontally in straight-line swimming, is inclined toward that side where no amputation has taken place.

Almost all larvae possess a more or less defective tail, since they, during the transport or earlier as a consequence of insufficient nourishment, had gnawed pieces of it off one another mutually.

10. August: Two specimens of the 1st series, three of the 2nd series, all five of the 3rd, and none of the 4th series have completed their metamorphosis.

Of the two transformed specimens of the 1st series, the one possesses a complete and well-formed (Fig. 3 Plate VII), the second a likewise indeed complete, but in consequence of irregular wound-surface mal-formed regenerate of the left hind-leg. — Of the three still in the larval stage situated experimental-animals of the 1st series, two have already regenerated the left hind-leg completely, the one specimen normally formed (Fig. 1), the other with teratological torsion (Fig. 2); with the third the leg is still engaged in normal regeneration, which however has already progressed up to over the knee.

Of the three transformed animals of the 2nd series, two have regenerated the right hind-leg completely; the third specimen has regenerated upper- and lower-leg, of which the latter however ends in a completely healed stump: tarsus, metatarsus and phalanges were also not newly formed even later. — Of the two larvae of the 2nd series, one has a complete, normal regenerate of the right hind-leg, the second is engaged in typical regeneration, which likewise has already advanced over the knee.

Of the all-together already transformed specimens of the 3rd series, four show a cone, bulging-out from the amputation-site, overlaid with skin, hence regenerative wound-healing, but no regeneration of the limb. Only one specimen has formed an incomplete, mal-formed regenerate of the left hind-leg, whereby the distal parts were laid down first; the right hind-leg is not renewed, but rather shows only wound-healing, and indeed merely in the form of skin-overlay of the wound-surface, without cone-shaped proliferation of the injured tissue (Fig. 4).

The all-together still in the larval state situated specimens of the 4th series possess no regeneration, since the hind-legs have grown along in size, their thighs form, oriented toward each other, a right angle.

Of the larvae of the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd series almost all possess front-legs, with the exception of that larva of the 1st series which has the left hind-leg already complete and normal: with this one the front-leg is indeed already perceptible, through the delicate skin which over-vaults the gill-cavity, but not yet broken-through; the front-leg is perceptible, through the delicate skin, on the body-side that was at that time still un-broken-through, opposite-set to the one already broken-through, but not yet broken-through; the right front-leg is normally formed, oriented toward the bulging-forth of the right side, and lies, in symmetry with that of the right side, not at that place denoted which the gill-hole indicates, but rather at the place which the gill-hole indicates: this left front-leg is not yet entirely visible (Fig. 1). With the remaining larvae a front-leg is indeed already broken-through, and at that time still un-broken-through one regenerated at the same place, so that the amputation took place while the front-leg-renewal-process was still in progress.

All experimental-animals which still possess larval-tails have their damaged places so much grown over un-evenly — the eye is more or less able to recognize these defects. The already metamorphosed experimental-animals have, however, regenerated the disappearing swimming-tail more or less before its complete dissolution, before the dissolution of the gill-parts, which stands in contradiction to the observation made by Barfurth [2] with *Rana temporaria* (= *fusca*).

11. August: At this point also the second front-leg is everywhere broken-through out through the gill-cavity, but likewise not yet in function, whilst the previously visible front-leg already takes part in the forward-motion and participates in the locomotion.

12. August: The front-legs now resemble one another in respect of formation and use.

22. August: All experimental-animals of the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd series (as well as all larvae, on which one could undertake no amputation) have completed their metamorphosis. Those specimens which on the 10. August were ready at the new-formation of their hind-extremities possess their complete regenerates.

The specimens of the 4th series, all of which are still in the larval state, as yet possess no forelimbs; only the hind limbs have increased in size, their thighs forming a right angle to one another.

The larvae of the 1st, 2nd and 3rd series already possess forelimbs, with the exception of that larva of the 1st series which has already regenerated the left hind limb completely and normally: in this one a forelimb is indeed already perceptible through the delicate skin which arches over the gill cavity (on the right side, opposite to the completed regenerate), but it has not yet broken through; the skin is bulged out at the place where this breaking-through must shortly be expected, whereas the weaker bulging of the left side, not quite symmetrically situated relative to that of the right side, does not point to the presence of the left forelimb, but marks the place where the gill-hole lies: the left forelimb is still entirely invisible (Fig. 1). In the remaining larvae a forelimb has already broken through, and indeed in those with finished regenerates on the side opposite to them, in those with unfinished regenerates on the same side where the amputation took place and the renewal process is still in progress.

All experimental animals which still possess larval tails have regenerated the damaged places of these so beautifully that nothing more of these defects is to be recognized with the unaided eye. The already metamorphosed specimens, however, did not regenerate the missing tail-parts before the resorption, which seems to stand in contradiction to the observations made by Barfurth [2] on *Rana temporaria* (= *fusca*).

11. August: Everywhere the second forelimb too has broken through the gill-cavity skin, but it is still quite adducted to the trunk and not yet in function during locomotion, whereas the forelimb which had already appeared the day before stands off from the trunk and takes part in the locomotion.

12. August: The forelimbs now resemble one another in respect of posture and use.

22. August: All the experimental animals of the 1st, 2nd and 3rd series (that is, all the larvae on which any amputation at all had been performed) have completed their metamorphosis. Those specimens which on the 10th of August were not yet finished with the neoformation of their hind extremities possess complete regenerates today.

The larvae of the 4th series are still not yet engaged in metamorphosis; yet the thighs of their hind limbs form a sharp angle to one another, and besides this the forelimbs have appeared, in four specimens simultaneously, in the fifth the right forelimb first, but the left one followed it within an hour.

27. August: The larvae of the 4th experimental series begin to resorb the tail and leave the water. —

Of the results of Experiment Nr. I, I will for the present only point to the one for whose establishment the experiment was carried out: **hind extremities and tail of the larvae of *Rana septentrionalis* are capable of regeneration.** These larvae belong to the largest that exist within the order of Anura; of the European anuran larvae only those of *Rana esculenta* subsp. *ridibunda* and of *Pelobates* sometimes attain the same size, namely: 1) neotenic (two- or several-summer) larvae, these sometimes already at a stage at which the thighs of the hind limbs likewise still form an obtuse angle to one another, 2) under certain circumstances (favorable nutritional conditions) also the normal (one-summer) larvae of the same species, but these only shortly before metamorphosis, when the thighs of the hind limbs already form a right or sharp angle, and have thus passed beyond the boundary stage of regenerative potency found by Barfurth [3] for *Rana temporaria*, which boundary stage, as I must remark by anticipation, has validity not only for *Rana temporaria* but also for most of the remaining Anura, with the exception of their phyletically lowest-standing families.

Nr. II. 6 neotenic tadpoles of *Rana septentrionalis*, 84–103 mm long, about 3 years old, with small hind legs, whose upper and lower segments still form an obtuse angle to one another. — Two experimental series, of three specimens each:

1) Amputation of the left hind limb, between base and knee.
2) Amputation of the right hind limb, at the same place.

15. August: Setting up of the experiment and execution of the operations. The same accompanying phenomena as in Experiment Nr. I.

These larvae too lack, like those at the beginning of Experiment Nr. I, pieces of the upper and lower seam of the tail fin, as well as the end-piece of the tail.

23. and 24. August: Completion of the wound closure.

29. August: In some specimens cone-shaped, in others only dome-shaped growth of the injured tissue of the hind limbs: regenerative wound healing. The damaged places of the tail, on the other hand, have completely regenerated. The thighs of the non-amputated hind limbs form partly right, partly sharp angles, varying even within one and the same specimen. Forelimbs are not visible.

25. September: The larvae in the same state. Of regeneration of the hind extremities not a trace is to be remarked.

Nr. III. 8 normal (one-summer) tadpoles of *Rana esculenta ridibunda*, 68–74 mm long. The hind extremities are in three specimens already fairly large, their thighs forming a sharp angle; in the rest they are still small, with an obtuse angle between upper and lower segment. — Two experimental series, the first of six specimens (among which the three already further advanced), the second of two specimens:

1) Amputation of the left hind leg, between base and knee.
2) Amputation of the distal half of the tail.

12. August: Setting up of the experiment and execution of the operations.

13. August: In the three further-advanced larvae of the 1st series the left forelimb (on the amputation side) is broken through, while the second is indeed already visible but still situated under the gill-cavity skin (Fig. 5).

14. August: The younger larvae of the 1st series have completed their provisional wound closure, a delicate epithelial covering of the wound surface [Born, 6]. By contrast, the operation sites of the further-advanced larvae, whose thicker thighs had also shown a stronger bleeding, lasting about ¼ hour, during the operation, are still open.

16. August: In these three older larvae the right forelimb too has now emerged through the skin of the gill cavity, but is still adducted to the trunk (not yet in function).

18. August: Both forelimbs of the older larvae now resemble one another in respect of posture and use; these larvae too have their wound closure finished.

28. August: The larvae of the 2nd series have completely regenerated the amputated half of the tail. The younger larvae of the 1st series are all on the point of regenerating the left hind limb, yet the regenerates have not yet advanced as far as the formation of phalanges. The older larvae of the 1st series are, without having regenerated, on the point of metamorphosing.

24. September: The younger larvae of the 1st series possess complete regenerates of the left hind limb, scarcely inferior in size to the non-amputated leg. On the right side they too already possess forelimbs. The older experimental animals (in the meantime metamorphosed) show only regenerative wound healing in the form of a fairly sharply tapering cone.

Nr. IV. 8 neotenic (and indeed about two-summer) tadpoles of *Rana esculenta ridibunda*, 81–93 mm long, with small hind legs, whose upper and lower segments still form an obtuse angle to one another. — Two experimental series, the first of six, the second of two specimens:

1) Amputation of the left hind limb, between base and knee.
2) Amputation of the distal half of the tail.

12. August: Setting up; execution of the operations.

15. August: Completion of the provisional wound closure.

17. August: Although the hind limbs are throughout still in a very early stage of development, the left forelimb (that is, on the amputation side) already pierces, in the larvae of the 1st series, the body integument lying over the gill cavity.

21. August: In the 1st series the right forelimb too appears.

23. August: Only today do the thighs of all the non-amputated hind limbs (in the 1st series the right ones, in the 2nd series both) take up the right-angled position to one another.

27. August: The larvae of the 2nd series have completely renewed the amputated half of the tail. They still possess no forelimbs; the hind limbs are somewhat further advanced in growth. — The larvae of the 1st series already show the urge to crawl onto land, without having regenerated.

25. September: The experimental animals of the 1st series are in the meantime metamorphosed, with regenerative wound healing (blunt cones 3 mm long) on the left hind extremity. — Those of the 2nd series receive forelimbs today, which everywhere broke through simultaneously: only one larva shows in so far a right-sided preponderance, in that, of the two simultaneously appeared forelimbs, the left one is at first still drawn against the body and not in action; only after the lapse of several hours do the forelimbs of this larva too become alike with respect to posture and function.

Nr. V. 8 normal (one-summer) tadpoles of *Pelobates fuscus*, 71–76 mm long. The hind extremities are in one specimen already fairly large, and their thighs form alternately now a right, now a sharp angle; in the rest they are still small, with an obtuse angle between upper and lower segment. — Two experimental series of four specimens each (the one more-advanced one in the 1st series):

1) Amputation of both hind limbs between base and knee, and of the distal half of the tail.
2) No amputation (control series).

23. June: Setting up and operation.

25. June: The larvae of the 1st series have already formed the provisional wound closure, both on the hind extremities and on the tail, in the form of the thin epithelium overlying the wound surface. One larva of the same series, in consequence of a sudden temperature drop that occurred through an inadvertent water-change, meets an almost instantaneous death, while the others recover.

11. August: The three surviving experimental animals of the 1st series possess complete regenerates of both hind extremities as well as of the distal half of the tail, and indeed also the specimen with hind extremities already developed further, namely beyond the boundary-mark of regenerative potency established by Barfurth on *Rana temporaria*. This fact gains in significance with reference to the low phyletic position of the family of the toad-frogs (Pelobatidae).

12. August: Appearance of the forelimbs (simultaneously and on both sides) and onset of the negative hydrotaxis in the experimental animals of the 1st series.

21. August: Appearance of the forelimbs (simultaneously and on both sides) in the experimental animals of the 2nd series. In two specimens of these the right hind limb is now amputated in the middle of the lower segment, with regard to the experiments of Ridewood on *Alytes obstetricans* Laurenti, which showed that hind extremities cut off shortly before the metamorphosis in the middle of the lower segment still regenerated after the metamorphosis [15]. Although the family of the Discoglossidae, to which *Alytes* belongs, is phyletically older than that of the Pelobatidae, the same behavior would nonetheless be conceivable in this likewise low-standing family, especially as it has already shown a stronger regenerative capacity than all the other higher anuran families hitherto examined in this respect (see this experimental protocol, 11. August).

22. and 23. August: Beginning of the tail resorption and onset of the negative hydrotaxis in the two animals operated yesterday.

25. August: These complete their metamorphosis. Beginning of the tail resorption and onset of the negative hydrotaxis in the non-amputated animals.

26. August: The latter too complete the metamorphosis today and are operated on the right hind limb (in the middle of the lower segment), with regard to the fact that in a phyletically low-standing family like that of the Pelobatidae the extinction of the regenerative power, even after the metamorphosis, may not be regarded as certain from the outset. The operation was accompanied by a relatively strong bleeding, not yet quite stanched after 15 minutes. One specimen croaked during the cut, which utterance of sound sounded quite different from the mating call of *Pelobates fuscus*: this is of interest in so far as in other frog-toads too sounds of fear and pain are known which possess no kind of similarity to the mating calls in question in respect of their phonetic character [8, 13], and further because other frog-toads, shortly after the metamorphosis, a long time before sexual maturity, are altogether incapable of vocal performances.

30. September: Neither in the animals operated before nor in those operated immediately after the metamorphosis does a trace of regeneration show itself, but only wound healing. In the former a dome-shaped growth of the tissue is perceptible, in the latter this too is not the case, but rather the wound surface is, without having formed any proliferation, immediately and definitively covered over with epidermis, which already possesses the same pigmentation as the rest of the integument surrounding the amputation site.

Nr. VI. 48 neotenic (judging by size: two-summer) tadpoles of *Pelobates fuscus*, 84–109 mm long. Ten specimens above, the others below the repeatedly designated Barfurthian boundary-mark of regenerative potency. — Five experimental series, four of them of ten specimens each, the fifth of eight specimens. Of the ten further-advanced specimens, two come to each experimental series:

1) Amputation of the left hind limb between base and knee.
2) Amputation of the right hind limb at the same place.
3) Amputation of both hind limbs at the same place.
4) (Control series) no amputation.
5) Amputation of the distal half of the tail.

23. June: Setting up of the experiment and execution of the operations under the same accompanying phenomena as in Experiment Nr. I. Among these, the disturbance in the equilibrium-keeping of the body makes itself especially noticeable, and indeed in all the experimental animals operated only on one side, as well as on the tail (1st, 2nd and 5th series), most strongly in the one-sidedly operated specimens with already thicker, larger hind extremities. ## E. Occasional earlier observations.

Besides the experiences described in the preceding section, gained from deliberately, purposefully devised experiments, there are now still some other observations to be communicated, made at an earlier time occasionally and by chance, which likewise bear on the chief question of the present work. In distinction from the described experiments, these observations are not recorded by date and by figures, but are nevertheless suited to confirm and supplement the results of the former in several points.

Above all I attained, from chance observations, the conviction that the regenerative capacity of the tail and of the hinder-extremities is a property belonging to all anuran larvae. I have therefore, besides those species to which the material used for the experiments described in the preceding section belongs, also reared tadpoles of *Rana esculenta* L. var. *lessonae* Blngr., *Rana agilis* Thom. and *R. arvalis* Nilss., such of *Bufo vulgaris* Laur. and *B. calamita* Laur., *Hyla arborea* L., furthermore of *Bombinator igneus* Laur. and *Alytes obstetricans* Laur.; the living material thus comprises, together with the species enumerated in connection with the experiments — although, apart from the North American *Rana septentrionalis* Bd., containing only Central-Europeans — sixteen different forms, which distribute themselves among five families (Ranidae, Bufonidae, Hylidae, Pelobatidae, Discoglossidae); the families again group themselves, according to Boulenger [7], into two anatomically sharply separated series, the series of the Firmisternia (hereto the family of the Ranidae) and the series of the Arcifera (hereto the remaining families). The material is thus sufficiently manifold to permit general conclusions.

Now it happens almost always, especially with intermittent feeding, that the tadpoles kept en masse in the basins gnaw at one another's tails and extremities; this goes, once a beginning has been made with it, so far that finally— 

—only a short stump of the limb concerned remains over. Tails that have been lost in this manner regenerate with ease, as long as the forelimbs have not yet appeared. If these are, on the contrary, already present — which normally is a sign of the metamorphosis setting in very soon — then the tail is no longer formed afresh; rather, its resorption begins forthwith. The hinder-extremities regenerate only as long as they are still small and their thighs form an acute angle; if, on the other hand, these already enclose a right or even an obtuse angle, then the regenerative capacity has ceased: frogs robbed of their limbs in such late stages remain cripples even after the metamorphosis. An exception to this rule is made by the two lowest families of the closed-chest frogs (Arcifera): the Pelobatidae and Discoglossidae renew the hinder-extremities even then still, when at the time of the loss they were already larger, when upper and lower leg already enclosed no longer the obtuse but rather the right or acute angle. In the discoglossid *Alytes* the regenerative potency reaches, as already mentioned, according to RIDEWOOD'S [15] investigations, even beyond the first period after the metamorphosis.

I have also observed earlier already that the forelimbs, when only one hind-leg or unequal pieces of both hind-legs had been lost, came to light at unequal times; but I made no records as to on which side of the body the forelimb (the hind-leg) broke through, [the limb] which in the Anura overlies the gill-cavity.

Also that, in undamaged tadpoles, the right forelimb sometimes breaks through first and the left follows it only a few hours later, I have occasionally — though only in isolated cases — perceived. —

The larvae of the Urodeles are still more inclined to cannibalism than those of the Anura: if the necessary food-supplies cannot be procured for even a single day, then forthwith a large part of the material is mutilated. That at least the newts, both in the larval and in the adult state, regenerate not only the tail but also both pairs of extremities and the lens, is indeed well known; but I was able to establish that the regeneration-process proceeds incomparably more slowly in transformed specimens than in the larvae, but that neotenic larvae regenerate just as slowly as same-aged, already metamorphosed animals.

Whereas, furthermore, the regenerative capacity in the normal (single-summer) larvae is in all developmental stages a uniformly vigorous one — that is, smaller as well as larger larvae renew the said regeneration-capable parts of the body equally fast, or at least with scarcely ascertainable differences of time — the growth-velocity of the parts of the body to be regenerated diminishes, in the already transformed animals, with increasing age: even very old animals still regenerate the tail, the limbs and the lens, but it happens within a much longer time than in young ones; the regenerative capacity remains the same, but the regeneration-force diminishes. The neotenic larvae now behave, in this respect too, exactly like the transformed animals: such, for example, as have become a year old ("two-summer") regenerate with the same growth-velocity as one-year-old, metamorphosed newts; multi-summer larvae already considerably more slowly.

Only in one case is the regeneration-force of the larval form — which, on account of gill-breathing, is always bound to a sojourn in water — whether of the normal or of the neotenic larval form, always greater than that of the transformed form: namely, when the latter keeps itself not in water but on dry land. This is found in free life among the newts regularly after the completion of the metamorphosis until the next spring, as well as in sexually mature animals usually for some months after the spawning-time. In captive life many individuals keep themselves in water year in, year out; others again cannot be induced to seek out the water; these permanently lead the life of land-salamanders (*Salamandra*) and drown, when they get into water against their will, just as quickly as the land-salamanders, if they cannot quickly get out again. The regeneration-force of the land-dwelling newts is now a very slight one. To be sure, it is more considerable than that of the land-salamanders, which — so far as is known to me — at a more advanced age are able to regenerate only the tail-tip, and shortly after the metamorphosis besides this also the limbs; but the new formation of the lost parts of the body proceeds extremely slowly and requires months until its completion. The phenomenon is easy to explain: the tissue lacks the possibility of taking up water abundantly, which is of very essential influence on the velocity of cell-growth. Besides this, a second circumstance may also carry weight: one and the same animal stands, so to speak, at different developmental heights, according as— 

—it keeps itself in water and exercises the more original functions linked to water-life, or whether it lives on the dry land and has adapted its organs to this secondary, later-acquired possibility of sojourn; life in water is, according to the hypothesis, the more original, consequently the regeneration-force in water the greater one. — In fact the functions of water-dwelling individuals are lower ones than the functions of land-dwelling individuals of the same species and the same age; this holds especially for the proposition concerning respiratory activity: it is exercised, for example, in the newts staying in the water by the entire body-skin, and during a longer sojourn beneath the water-surface exclusively by the latter, whereas on dry land the lung takes over the conduction [of respiration] and the share of the integument in respiration becomes small.

One sees this at once in the great structural difference of the skin of one and the same newt, according to whether it lives in water or whether it lives on dry land: in the first case the skin is always coated with mucus and consequently feels smooth; in the second case it appears dry, rough and granular. —

The observations on Urodeles enumerated up to now I have been able to make, besides on *Triton cristatus* Laur. and *Triton alpestris* Laur., which figure in the experiments described in the preceding section, also occasionally on *Triton vulgaris* L., *Montandoni* Blngr. (Carpathians), *marmoratus* Latr. (France), *Waltli* Michah. (Spain), *viridescens* Raf. (North America) and *pyrrhogaster* Boie (Japan), as well as on *Amblystoma tigrinum* Green. (North America).

That the regeneration-force of neotenic Urodele larvae is slighter than that of normal larvae and corresponds to that of same-aged, transformed specimens, I recognized especially also on the land-salamanders (*Salamandra maculosa* Laur. and *S. atra* Laur.). The salamander-larva replaces quickly and easily the gills, the tail, both pairs of limbs and the lens; — admittedly this is, in the case of the larvae of the more highly differentiated *Salamandra atra* freed by operation from the maternal uterus, the case to a less complete degree. When the time of the metamorphosis has been overstepped, but the animals are nonetheless still in the larval state, then the tail and the limbs regenerate uncommonly slowly and very often incompletely, the lens not at all any more; exactly the same is now to be stated for the freshly transformed land-salamanders [11, pp. 45, 46 and 77 of the offprint].¹

## F. Results and conclusions.

### I. Results that stand in immediate relation to the theme:

1) The regenerative capacity of the amphibian larvae is dependent on the developmental stage:

   a) The hinder-extremities of the anuran larvae regenerate as long as upper and lower leg still enclose an obtuse angle (first observed by Barfurth [3] on *Rana temporaria*, here set up as a rule within the order of the Anura).

   b) For the Discoglossidae and Pelobatidae among the Anura this limit does not exist: they regenerate the hinder-extremities even then still, when the legs already enclose a right or acute angle. The regenerative capacity ceases — at least in *Bombinator* among the Discoglossidae, in *Pelobates* among the Pelobatidae — only with the entry of the metamorphosis.

   c) The tail-tip is, in the anuran larvae, as a rule renewed only as long as the forelimbs have not yet appeared. Afterwards no renewal of the tail takes place any more; rather, there enters the resorption — proceeding during the metamorphosis — of the remaining piece of tail. — Exception: If the development of the larva is strongly slowed from the appearance of the forelimbs on, then a renewed formation of cut-off tail-parts can take place.

d) The Urodeles regenerate all regeneration-capable parts of the body in the fully formed bodily condition more slowly than in the larval state.

2) The regenerative capacity of the amphibian larvae is dependent on age:

   a) Neotenic (two- or multi-summer) anuran larvae, still at the same stage at which normal (single-summer) larvae regenerate the hinder-extremities completely, are no longer able to renew these.

   b) Yet neotenic anuran larvae regenerate the rudder-tail just as well as normal larvae.

   c) Neotenic Urodele larvae, still at the same stage at which normal larvae regenerate very rapidly, exhibit just as low a regeneration-velocity as same-aged, metamorphosed specimens.

3) The regenerative capacity of the amphibian larvae is independent of the size of a species.

### II. Results that stand in no immediate relation to the theme:

4) The regenerative capacity of the hinder-extremities and of the tail is, within the limits indicated under 1, a general property of the anuran larvae.

5) The regeneration-velocity is dependent on the respective sojourn of one and the same specimen: it regenerates considerably faster while it is in the water than while it is on the dry land.

6) An amputation or other injury which has taken place only on one body-side brings it about that the left and the right body-side of the specimens concerned develop at unequal speed:

   a) As long as a one-sided wounding has not yet definitively healed — e.g. the one hinder-extremity in the Anura (forelimb in the Urodeles) is still engaged in regeneration or regenerative wound-healing — the forelimb in the Anura (hinder-extremity in the Urodeles) always regenerates first on the side of the injury.

   b) If, on the other hand, the wound-healing or regeneration has already become completely finished beforehand, then the forelimb in the Anura (the hinder-extremity in the Urodeles)— 

—appears first on that side where no injury has taken place.

   c) If operations were undertaken on both body-sides, but in unequal degree, then the side more strongly taken into demand by the operation develops faster.

7) In undamaged or bilaterally uniformly operated amphibian larvae the second pair of extremities appears as a rule all at once; a small percentage of the larvae, however, shows a preponderance of the right body-side.

8) Injuries of whatever kind act, in the case of the Anura, accelerating, but in the case of the Urodeles retarding, upon the metamorphosis; that acceleration and this retardation stand in direct proportionality to the extent of the injury.

9) The amphibian larvae mentioned in the present work show thigmotaxis (movement along firm objects, e.g. of the aquarium-walls), negative rheotaxis (the endeavor to position themselves against the water-current), positive thermotaxis and heliotaxis up to a water-temperature of 20–25° C., negative thermotaxis and heliotaxis from the named temperature upward.

10) *Pelobates fuscus*, like several *Rana*-species, lets loud laryngeal sounds be heard out of fear or pain, which in their phonetic character are completely different from the mating-call, and which are at the animal's disposal even long before the entry of sexual maturity, namely already right after the metamorphosis.

Of these results only those enumerated under No. 6 and 8 still require a special discussion.

For the just-described unequally fast development of the body-sides in one-sided injuries I should like to attempt the following explanation: In order to repair the injury that has taken place, be it merely by wound-healing, be it by regeneration of the lost parts, the place concerned requires a more ample nourishment, a stronger in-streaming of nourishing juices. This, however, extends not only to the needy place, but to the whole body-side on which that place is situated. Once the wound-healing or new formation is completed, a reaction sets in: now the body-side that has remained uninjured, which in consequence of the stronger drawing-upon of the nourishing activity of the vascular system by the injured— 

—body-side had been neglected for a while, is more strongly nourished, and now in turn the developmental processes here proceed faster. For the latter case an example is also found in Barfurth (one not specially explained by him): on Pl. VI Fig. 7 of his work "Are the limbs of frogs capable of regeneration?" [3] a tadpole of *Rana temporaria* is depicted which has completed the regenerate of the right hinder-extremity, and in which consequently the left forelimb has appeared first, while the right one still sits beneath the skin of the gill-cavity. — That one-sided, local organic disturbances and their consequent phenomena extend not merely to the part concerned, but to this entire body-side, of this there are, in bilaterally-symmetrical animals, owing to the bilateral structure of the vascular and nervous systems, many analogous examples; most of all it is shown by human pathology that the two body-sides possess in this respect a certain degree of independence from one another.

A different reciprocal relationship than that which exists between the left and right body-side seems to be present between the anterior and posterior body-half. On this there are experiments by KOCHS [12] on *Salamandra maculosa* [*Salamandra salamandra*]. The hypertrophy preceding the completion of regenerative processes does not take place on that body-half where the injury is present, but on the opposite one: upon removal of an anterior limb, that is, an injury on the cranial body-half [12, Pl. XVIII Fig. 8], there occurred in KOCHS' experiments, before the regeneration of that limb, a compensatory hypertrophy of the hind limbs and of the tail, that is, on the caudal body-half (Figs. 11–13 on KOCHS' plate). This behavior too, and why it is opposite to that of the left and right body-side, should presumably be explainable from the course of the vascular system and of the innervation.

The sometimes unmistakable preponderance of the right body-side has likewise been observed by BARFURTH l. c. [3, p. 123, in the explanation of the illustrations], and indeed apparently in a larger percentage of specimens, judging from his statement: "Usually the right limb breaks through first."

It is striking that injuries of every kind exert a quite contrary influence on the onset of metamorphosis in urodeles and anurans, as the experiments show with the greatest clarity. Whereas in a newt or salamander larva one readily achieves neoteny if one removes merely a limb or a piece of tail, the same measure in a frog tadpole releases the rapid onset of the metamorphic urge. A real explanation of this phenomenon I am unable to give; I can only point out that it is teleologically very well founded: the organization of the urodeles directs this amphibian order, in general, far more than that of the considerably more highly and more specially differentiated anurans, toward finding all its conditions of existence permanently in the water. For the urodeles it is, for the preservation of the species, in general quite immaterial whether the animals persist for life in the larval form, which is adapted exclusively to a sojourn in water — in which form they are indeed also able to attain sexual maturity — or whether they transform into the lung-breathing form that lives at times on dry land. Among other things, this is also quite particularly a consequence of the mode of nutrition, which from the earliest larval stage onward always remains that of a carnivore.

Quite otherwise the anurans: their fully formed bodily condition is of such a kind that they depend on the water to a far lesser degree than most urodeles. The anurans never attain sexual maturity as larvae. Chiefly this is again the consequence of the mode of nutrition, which is fundamentally different from that of the urodeles: the anuran larvae are omnivorous; they are eaters of plants and carrion, but not predators, whereas the fully formed anurans are exclusively predators. Correspondingly we find, at the time of larval life on the one hand, and in the metamorphosed condition on the other, a quite different organization of the digestive tract.

Now it appears in the larvae of both amphibian orders that, in the case of neoteny (which, as noted, in the anurans can only be partial neoteny), only the decisive larval characters — gill-breathing and the tail fin-seam — are retained [11, pp. 54 and 79 of the offprint], while the structures of other organs do not remain at a standstill in their development, but approach the structure proper to the fully formed form. The latter holds, among other things, also for the development of the intestinal canal. In the urodele larvae this is of no consequence for the possibility of existence, for the mode of nutrition is, in all free-living developmental stages, lifelong the same; whereas the nutritional conditions for the anuran larvae begin to become very unfavorable from the moment when the regular time of transformation has been exceeded: the spirally coiled larval intestine, arranged for the assimilation of vegetable and decaying matter, assumes more and more the shorter form and coarser structure of the imaginal intestine, and thus becomes incapable of functioning under the conditions of existence in which the larva continues to live, since the jaws, still clothed with the larval horny edge and unfit for seizing living prey, are unable to supply it with suitable nourishment. Multi-summer anuran larvae are correspondingly always hunger-forms.

If now — as, for example, when the animal has sustained an injury — a regulation in the organism is necessary, then there comes to light the organism's endeavor to attain, or respectively to maintain, that bodily condition in which the disturbance can most easily be borne or eliminated, by virtue of the most favorable conditions of life given for it. But the most favorable conditions of life the urodeles find within, the anurans outside the water; therefore injured urodele larvae retain the water-form, while injured anuran larvae strive to attain the land-form. And in the anurans this endeavor is carried through even at the cost of the possibility of completing the regulation: the metamorphosis is accelerated as much as possible, even though, after its onset, as a rule only wound-healing, but no genuine regeneration any longer takes place. The bearing of the disturbance is precisely the precondition of the preservation of the individual, hence the primary, more important moment; the elimination of the disturbance is taken into account only in the second place. The former is now, in the anurans, as has been set forth in detail, more promising in the fully formed, land-dwelling condition than in the water-dwelling larval condition, and indeed still more promising even when the affected specimens, owing to the cessation of the regenerative capacity, must remain cripples. This view is confirmed by the behavior of such frogs and toads as had, in consequence of an amputation, transformed very rapidly before the production of the regenerates: although one or both hind limbs remained stump-shaped, they readily and abundantly accept the living nourishment offered, thrive and grow visibly thereby, and possess, in connection therewith, even a degree of mobility which one would from the outset not have credited to their crippled limbs.
