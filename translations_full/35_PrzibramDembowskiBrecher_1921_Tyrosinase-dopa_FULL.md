## 1. Introduction: Bloch's "Dopa".

In a series of significant works, Bruno Bloch, supported by various collaborators, has established by means of hand-sections, cell-sections and cell-detritus of mammals that in living pigment-cells it is "Dopa" that produces the pigment. This short term "Dopa" denotes dioxyphenylalanine, in so far as it, in the warm-blooded animal, is to be regarded as the actual pigment-former [chromogen] and as an indicator of the cells. Bloch holds the view that the "Dopa" out of which the melanin arises is identical with that which the comparatively rarely occurring specific ferments [enzymes] he was able to demonstrate condition — just as the tyrosinase gives occasion to the formation of melanin from the "Dopa" or another chromogen to be invoked.²

> ¹) An abstract of this work appeared under the title: Communications from the Biological Experimental Institute of the Academy of Sciences, Zoological Department, Director H. Przibram. 42. Action of Tyrosinase on "Dopa" (at the same time: Causes of Animal Colouration IV) in the Academic Gazette of the Academy of Sciences in Vienna, No. 18, 1919.

I must cautiously emphasize that Bloch in all his publications (e.g. 1917 *Problem* p. 199) declares the theoretical character of this theory, which has the melanin formation be effected by tyrosinase and similar substances by way of dioxyphenylalanine (and, as he puts it, only one-sidedly: the cells and cell-detritus that have been treated with Dopa blacken). With such Dopa-press-juices Bloch determined that dioxyphenylalanine could indeed serve as an indicator for the pigment-forming. That it really arises in the warm-blooded animal — such a direct demonstration in the living tissue Bloch has, however, not yet been able to furnish, and it is also, as he himself emphasizes, still a question whether, just as in their own skin-cells (Przibram and Brecher 1918, 1919; Przibram and Dembowski 1918, 1919), the discovery of the actual melanin formation succeeds. We were able to obtain melanin from the most concentrated Dopa-solution as well in the living eggs of vertebrates as in the warm-blooded animal, and Bloch in particular believes he has demonstrated, for the Addison disease, that here too an enzyme is at work (Bloch and Löffler 1917). Finally, however, the question of the Dopa-reaction is precisely this: whether, when it gives at the vertebrate skin its most intense reaction where the colour pigments at the surrounding pigmentation are present, namely in the Dopa (Przibram and Rybiner 1917, p. 238), we should not now have to assume a further kind of melanin formation for this organ. For this reason let the investigation of the eyes be brought to bear in the present work.

If the assumption of a proper Dopa-oxydase should prove superfluous, then the Dopa is nonetheless to be regarded as an extraordinarily sensitive indicator for weak pigment-forming ferments [enzymes], which, even though it is not the natural chromogen of the vertebrate, may yet be a reachable chromogen. Against the Dopa as the actual starting-point for the pigment-forming, Bloch namely emphasizes the failure of its demonstration in the animal body, in particular in the skin of fish (Bloch and Rybiner 1917, p. 174); the meeting [of the two], however, would, in the brown skins of mammals (likewise p. 186) be rendered invisible precisely through the consuming action of the oxydase. So it does not appear in these brown skins or in the yellow and black places (Przibram and Brecher 1918, 1919; Przibram and Dembowski 1918, p. 206); in connection with this, Bloch has rendered suspect, through the supposed chromogen-deficiency — if I believe rightly — the Addison-disease melanin formation (Bloch and Löffler 1917). Finally, the comparison of the Dopa-reaction, which gives precisely now at the vertebrate skin its most intense reaction, makes it appear necessary to assume this Dopa in particular for this organ. Therefore let the investigation of the eyes be brought to bear in the present work.

## 2. Action of press-juices and tyrosinases on "Dopa".

### a) *Salamandra maculosa.*

Jan Dembowski's experiments first established the action of press-juices from de-pigmented skins of the fire-salamander. These press-juices, of *Salamandra maculosa*, came in their own pre-treatments from the black *forma typica* and the yellow *forma taeniata*. With the press-juices of these animals — the black *forma typica* and the yellow *forma taeniata* — we always brought tyrosin together. By the comparison begun here we wanted to determine whether the black *forma typica* and the yellow *forma taeniata* give differing dispositions to melanin formation.

For the recovery of the tyrosinase a method worked out by von Fürth (1904) for press-juices was sought. Whether it is the customary yellow places of the vertebrate skins, one would never let new dispositions be expected in the salamander-juice with 0.05 % caustic soda.

Despite the deep-black colour of the yellow places of the salamander-skin, one nevertheless always sets in a weak darkening through the addition of press-juice or of the ferment-solution prepared therefrom to tyrosin. Indeed, a distinct difference set in, to be ascertained when, according to our procedure, all at one and the same time, one uses the blacker *forma typica* or the yellower *f. taeniata*: since the solutions obtained from the *taeniata*-skins coloured the tyrosin-solution yellowish up to an inner zone, the analogously concentrated solutions from skins of the typical form already showed a distinct excretion of melanin. Such a difference now also between the dark and light solution of the *forma typica* one remarked, while in the wider precipitation the inner brown zone in the yellow and black zone first set in, as our preliminary report shows (Dembowski's experiment from 31 X.–3 XII 1917).

The first experiment with dioxyphenylalanine itself gave a stronger melanin formation than the one set in the tyrosin-solution. ### b) *Mus decumanus* and *Cavia cobaja.*

Since the elevated alkalinity favours the melanin formation, it lies near at hand to attribute the lacking pigment-formation in white hide-places [Hautstellen] and at albinotic skins of warm-blooded animals to their high acidity.

This investigation on press-juices and on the ferment set up from the customary skins of normal-pigmented skins gives the comparative values compiled in the following table, to which there are then appended those for the analogously treated eyes. As experimental object there served first the brown rat, *Mus decumanus.*

In all the experiments set up by Dembowski in 1917 and 1918 with the rats, at the hides of the albinos a lesser number of drops was required for the disappearance of the colour of the phenolphthalein situated in alkaline solution than at those of the wild-coloured; consequently the albinotic hides are in fact more acidic, and indeed this shows itself both in both sexes and also at the various further processings of the hides to press-juice or ferment-extract. Also the hides tested with the viscostagonometer yield fewer scale-divisions for the albinos, which means a more strongly acidic reaction.

Also at the warm-blooded animals, just as at the salamanders, the Dopa was hindered in the melanin formation by the acidic extracts, over against the unset control-samples with time, and was always weakened from the very beginning over against the samples charged with 0.05 % NaOH-drops alone.

I have already pointed out, in a lecture before the Anthropological Society in Vienna (1918, p. 50), that for the absence of the Dopa reaction in the eye we have been able to hold responsible the known acidic reaction of the eye tissue, which is heightened in particular after illumination. The measurements of eye press-juices confirm the connection insofar as the eyes both of the albinotic and of the full-coloured rats show acidity grades similar to, or even greater than, the albinotic skins of the same series of test animals.

With this there again agrees the considerable weakening of the melanisation which the eye-extracts call forth in the Dopa samples. In doing so it comes at last to the appearance of a green colour, which we had earlier addressed as the "minimal effect" of melanisation (Przibram and Brecher 1919, p. 112). We have observed this green initial colour, besides in the eyes of the rats, also in those of the guinea-pig. Of the connection between the green colour, the acidic state, and the minimal effect of the tyrosinase, the experiments of Dembowski of 26. III. 1917 bore witness, in which tyrosin samples had been charged with a tyrosinase prepared from rat-albino-skin-tyrosinase + Halimasch [honey fungus, *Armillaria mellea*]. Here a hindering of the Halimasch action through the rat-extract occurred. But if now to the tyrosin samples of 1 cm³ a higher alkalinity was imparted by the addition of 1 to 2 drops of n/10 NaOH, then it came to a browning.

Conversely, a lowering of the alkalinity through the addition of ½ cm³ of distilled water or of 0.5 NaCl solution brought about the disappearance of the yellow tone out of the green colour; 1 drop of H₂O₂ deepened the pea-green tone into a dirty dark green.

According to our previous experience, we can trace the failure of the pigment formation back to the reaction state of the pigment-forming site, namely to over-acidification (yet in certain cases also to too great an alkalinity, Brecher 1921 VI; Przibram 1919, p. 226). In the skin, which lifelong has to deliver pigment to the renewing epidermal layers and hairs, the respective pigment sites coincide with the pigment-forming sites and there will these be distinguished by alkaline reaction before the more acidic white ones. In the eye, on the other hand, the pigment-formation process falls into embryonic and early post-embryonic stages: here the early appearance of it is to be referred to the beginning acidification, which leads to the precipitation of the melanin formed on the alkaline preliminary stage.¹ With the onset of sight, however, it then comes to a far-reaching acidification of the eye tissues and to the extinction of the intense pigment formation. In old age, moreover, it comes also in the remaining body tissues to over-acidification, with which the whitening of the hair is probably connected — which apparently can be reversed by alkalinising processes, e.g. certain irradiation (cf. Przibram 1919, p. 233), which again points to the fact that not the melanin-formation factors have been lost, but that a further state must have acted in a hindering manner. The sudden greying after strong emotional shocks may likewise be traced back to an over-acidification of the tissues with extinction of the pigment formation that is no longer reversible (the eyes here probably do not lose the pigment that is in any case deposited at an acidic site).

### c) *Phalera bucephala* und *Deilephila vespertilio*

If for the pigment formation in warm-blooded animals the reaction state is so decisive that one can regard the Dopa at the same time as an indicator for pigment formation and for the alkaline reaction state, then we can dispense with the assumption of a specific Dopaoxydase: the black-colouring through Dopa indicates to us the reaction state of the relevant tissue- or cell-portion. But since Dopa, also without any ferment, through mere addition of weak alkali is at once driven to melanin formation, we must still examine whether at all an oxidation-ferment is capable of [bringing about] the black-colouring [in order] to accelerate, or whether the black-colouring of the Dopa is not already determined through the reaction state of any arbitrary site — [a site] which indeed needed to contain no ferment. For these experiments there served tyrosinase from the just-available pupae of the Buff-tip [moth], *Phalera bucephala*, of 22. IX. 1917, prepared by the customary method. There were set up eprouvettes with ½ cm³ each of a ½‰ Dopa solution, and also such with saturated tyrosin solution, to which at 10ʰ 15′ in the forenoon 1 drop each of *Phalera*-tyrosinase was added. As controls there served uncharged Dopa samples, and with 1 drop of 0.05% NaOH charged Dopa samples; besides, tyrosinases of albinotic and wild-coloured rats were still in use with both named chromogens.

The effectiveness of the *Bucephala*-tyrosinase showed itself, in the tyrosin samples of 10ʰ 15′ forenoon of the same day, in a deepening of the initially weakly yellowish colouration; at 11ʰ 30′ a violet tone appeared at the bottom of the eprouvette; by 3ʰ in the afternoon the whole sample was deep-violet; on the following day at 9ʰ in the forenoon deep-black; the rat-extracts had during the whole time not reacted. Even before the tyrosin samples had changed their colour, namely at 10ʰ 20′, in the Dopa samples charged with the same tyrosinase a weak browning had occurred, which at 10ʰ 25′ had turned over into a "Grauilla" [*sic*], by 11ʰ 30′ raced through the violet stage which in the tyrosin was only reached in the afternoon, at 3ʰ in the afternoon became black-violet, and on the following day looked just like the analogously charged tyrosin sample. In contrast to the tyrosin samples, the Dopa samples set with rat-extracts had also, slowly to be sure, but gradually, browned, and by the 25th were blackened. That here no specific Dopaoxydase was in play, the control samples — without extract, but with 1 drop of 0.05% NaOH — demonstrated, since these ran through the same blackening grades, only a trace faster. Neither in the Dopa samples charged with rat-extracts, nor in those charged with NaOH alone, did a violet initial colour appear, and nowhere was there to be seen, in the forenoon of the setting-up, a distinct colour-effect, as with the samples charged with pupa-tyrosinase; of Dopa or also of tyrosin there was nothing to perceive.

It therefore remained beyond all doubt that tyrosinase, just as upon tyrosin, also exercises a specific action upon Dopa, which expresses itself on the one hand in an acceleration of melanin formation, on the other hand in the retention of the "specific initial colour." This is, moreover, a further fine proof for the proposition demonstrated by us (Przibram and Brecher 1919, p. 127) on several chromogens and tyrosinases: "For the initial colour the tyrosinase used is accordingly decisive. From the initial colour of the tyrosinase in tyrosin we can foresee which colour a chromogen of other provenance will assume under the influence of the same tyrosinase." This confirmation is so much the more valuable to us, as it here concerns a chemically exactly characterised chromogen, distinct from tyrosin — the Dopa — with which the same tyrosinase yields a strong colouration. That, in the action of the pupa-extracts upon Dopa, it concerns just as little as upon tyrosin a ferment action [as opposed to a mere temperature-effect], could easily be demonstrated through temperature experiments. On 24. X. a *Phalera*-pupa-tyrosinase was warmed to 45° and at 11ʰ 30′ added to Dopa samples, while other Dopa samples were charged with the not pre-warmed tyrosinase kept at 18° C. By 12ʰ the latter were violet, the former, however, in consequence of the heat-damage of the ferment, had not yet started; only on the following day were both samples black, the uncharged control samples first grey. A similar result gave repetitions of the experiments on 25. X. This time too a tyrosinase pre-warmed to 70° had been used; this could not, even after one day, call forth any colouration in the Dopa samples. Now 70° is the upper boundary of the tyrosinase activity in tyrosin, and between 40 — 50° lies the first weakening (Przibram and Brecher 1919, p. 104).

In order to eliminate still some misgivings against the experiments just discussed, performed with *Phalera*-pupae, there were later employed pupae of the bat hawk-moth [bat-sphinx], *Deilephila vespertilio*, a butterfly-species of near relationship to the wolf's-milk hawk-moth, *Deilephila euphorbiae*, employed at one time by Fürth (F. u. S. 1901).

It could namely still be objected that the action of the employed *Phalera*-tyrosinase, dissolved in 0.05% caustic-soda, upon Dopa was to be ascribed merely to this solvent, to which then admittedly a "fermentative" character would have to be conceded. This objection had to fall, when ferment-extracts also without alkali-addition acted strongly acceleratingly, or significant alterations of the alkali-grade yielded less difference in the melanisation than the addition of the ferment.

On 10. April 1919 there were set up by L. Brecher two samples each of 1 cm³ 1‰ Dopa solution with 2 drops of H₂O bzw. [respectively] n/20, n/40, n/80 NaOH, or with 1 drop of these additions plus 1 drop of *Phalera*-pupa-tyrosinase dissolved in distilled water, further n/160 NaOH plus pupa-tyrosinase. The result was striking: the samples charged with tyrosinase showed, even those of lower alkali-content, decidedly stronger reaction than those charged without ferment with the stronger alkali-grades. Already 1 hour after the setting-up this was noticeable, very distinctly after 24 hours; after 48 hours all the differences were obliterated by the completed blackening.

### d) *Agaricus melleus*

A quite analogous series, set up at the same time, with Halimasch in place of the pupa-tyrosinase gave an analogous result at somewhat slower action; very beautifully the red initial colour of the Halimasch again stepped forth as against the violet of the pupae.

A second objection against our interpretation — that the influence of the tyrosinase upon Dopa was to be ascribed to contamination with peroxides — I have to thank Herr Professor Schlenk for the kind suggestion to test, [namely] whether the dopa-blackening action of the alkali, even without ferment-addition, might perhaps be traced back to traces of peroxide, since such are for the most part present in commercial alkali. For this examination a test-series of 4 eprouvettes may be drawn upon, which had been set up with the addition of 1 drop of 1‰ Dopa to 1 cm³ each of tyrosin solution (for other purposes) on 6. VI. 1919. It showed itself that in this mixture, on the addition of one drop of n/40 NaOH, a strong melanisation occurred after 24 hours. If this alkali-drop was replaced by one of hydrogen-peroxide of such a concentration that the same quantity of OH-groups was added, there occurred during the same time only a weak yellow-colouration. If one lowered the concentration further, then the visible action, on lowering to the half, became a trace, on lowering to a quarter already markedly stronger; in place of the yellowish a brownish tone appeared. If, however, in place of the H₂O₂ distilled water was used, then the action was still better, the initial colour distinctly violet; the intensity of the colouration, however, allowed no comparison with that on n/40 NaOH-addition.

In another test-series a 1‰ Dopa solution was charged with 1 drop of n/80 NaOH (= 0.05%) bzw. 1 drop of the mentioned H₂O₂-solution, or one twice as strong and one half as strong, finally with 1 drop of distilled water (4.—5. VII. 1919).

Here too H₂O₂ failed as a melanin-promoter even as against H₂O. Hydrogen-peroxide therefore exercises no action corresponding to the alkali. Its presence even diminishes the action as against an equal addition of distilled water. One can, however, also convince oneself directly of the melanisation-damaging action of the hydrogen-peroxide if one adds, to the mixture tyrosin + 1 drop of Dopa, simultaneously into the same eprouvette 1 drop of H₂O₂ and 1 drop of n/40 NaOH; then the melanin formation is suppressed and does not appear even after 3 days. There speaks therefore nothing in favour of tracing the action of the caustic-soda back to an admixture of peroxide, for already small quantities of the same hinder this action.

## 3. Examination of Chromogens for Their Suitability for Melanin Formation

### a) Behaviour of natural and artificial chromogens towards Millon's reagent.

If we can manage without a specific "Dopa"-oxidase, then it still remains a question whether in vertebrates, in place of the tyrosine postulated as chromogen in certain fungi and invertebrate animals, we ought to assume the "Dopa." Dembowski tested the animal chromogens obtained by the crude procedure for tyrosine by means of Millon's reagent (experiments of 21 March 1917; Przibram and Brecher, p. 120). Both the press-juices from salamander skins (*Salamandra maculosa forma typica*) and those from the rat skins (*Mus decumanus*) gave a vigorous carmine staining, and indeed just as strongly as the saturated tyrosine solution which had been set up as a control. From the rats, besides the dark skin-patches of the wild-coloured ones, their white belly-patches too were treated, and showed just as strong a reaction as the dark upper side — a further proof that it cannot be the absence of every chromogen that is responsible for the white colouring (cf. on this Przibram and Brecher 1919, pp. 128 and 172; Przibram 1919, p. 226). Albinotic males and females likewise gave an almost equally strong reaction. If, however, by the procedure described earlier (Przibram and Brecher 1919, p. 126), traces of egg-white are still eliminated by heating to 90–100° and filtering off from the press-juice — which had in any case already been precipitated with ammonium sulphate — then the presence of tyrosine (or of another monoxyphenyl) in the animal chromogen can no longer be demonstrated. Such experiments we have so far carried out, with the same success, on the three differently coloured portions of the guinea pig, on the fire salamander, and on pupae of the great peacock moth, *Saturnia pyri*.¹

> ¹ It may be recalled that Fürth, too, after very careful elimination of the egg-white by precipitation with hydrochloric-acid-containing phosphotungstic acid, found no Millon reaction in the pupal chromogen, although the tyrosinase produced very strong melanin formation in the purified fraction. One might therefore believe that tyrosine, as natural chromogen, must be excluded not only in the vertebrates but also in the invertebrates. Yet this conclusion would be premature, for it has still to be investigated how tyrosine behaves when one subjects it to the same procedures under which the natural chromogen is indeed attacked by tyrosinase but is no longer coloured pink by Millon's reagent. I caused L. Brecher to test a tyrosine solution, treated just like the natural chromogens, [continued overleaf] for Millon's reaction, and it turned out that a tyrosine solution treated in the same way as the natural chromogen receives no trace of a pink colouration, but on the contrary takes on a green-yellow colour, just as Fürth had also stated for the pupal chromogen. A non-pretreated tyrosine solution of half the concentration, set up at the same time as a control, gave a splendid carmine staining.

A Dopa sample equimolecular with the treated tyrosine solution underwent, on the same treatment, a browning, and of course, even when treated, gave no Millon reaction.

From these experiments we must draw the conclusion that tyrosine, on precipitation with ammonium sulphate and boiling, is converted into another body which no longer gives a Millon reaction, and hence can no longer be a monoxyphenyl. It would now lie near at hand to think of the "Dopa," which, as a dioxyphenyl, can from the outset give no such reaction. There would then be the possibility that, in the natural chromogens, the tyrosine which without doubt always arises from the splitting of egg-white is, in the animal body too, partly converted into Dopa, and that this always furnishes the basis of melanin formation. But the behaviour of the natural chromogens does not speak for this: on treatment they yield water-clear fluids, whereas a "Dopa" occurring in them ought to make itself recognizable by browning. For the further testing of this question we have sought to force the named natural chromogens to melanin formation, partly by adding tyrosinase solution (2 drops to 1 cm³) with or without caustic soda (0.05 %), partly with drops of caustic soda alone; and it turned out that only the ferment-containing samples reacted, while those treated with caustic soda alone remained, even after 2 days, completely water-clear. But now we have seen above that Dopa is driven to blackening by caustic soda alone almost as well as by tyrosinase that contains no caustic soda.

It remains possible after all that small traces of Dopa might be present in the chromogens, but the great bulk of the natural animal chromogens can scarcely be supplied by it, either in the invertebrates or in the vertebrates — unless the pretreatment of the chromogens so alters the Dopa that it now forfeits its ready alterability by alkali, which, indeed, after several samples, seems to be the case. But this way out is from the outset rendered improbable by the different behaviour of the Dopa, as against that of the natural chromogens, during the pretreatment itself.

There thus still remains in any case the greatest probability that tyrosine forms the basis of the animal melanin.

Further experiments will have to bring the definitive decision.

### b) Melanin formation by artificial and natural chromogens.

If we regard tyrosine as the basis of the natural melanins, then we must still satisfy ourselves whether not other of the chromogens hitherto taken into consideration are also capable of forming melanin with tyrosinase. In order to obtain comparable strengths of the blackenings, I have prepared a series of solutions equimolecular with saturated tyrosine solution of the following substances:

Tyrosine, Dopa, pyrogallol, brenzcatechin [pyrocatechol], hydrochinon [hydroquinone], resorzin [resorcinol], phenol; furthermore saturated solution of the same (with the exception of Dopa, in order to economize with that substance, which moreover already gives blackening in the solution equimolecular with saturated tyrosine solution) and not exactly determined solutions of tryptophan, adrenaline, Halimasch [honey-fungus] chromogen and chromogen of a butterfly group (*Deilephila vespertilio*). Homogentisic acid, which would also be very worthy of attention (cf. Przibram and Brecher 1919, p. 119), I had unfortunately not been able to procure.

The initial colour of all samples of the equimolecular series was, except for the yellowish pyrogallol, nearly colourless; the concentrated solutions, on the other hand, showed various colour-tones, which are to be taken from the appended table; quite corresponding tones appear, without ferment addition, in the equimolecular solutions when they are exposed to the air for a few days; with brenzcatechin and adrenaline a dirty-brown precipitate even forms. Typical melanin-blackening appeared spontaneously, at the same time, only with the Dopa.

On addition of Halimasch tyrosinase or pupal tyrosinase, there again become noticeable in all solutions the "hooking-colours" characteristic of each of these ferments, in that Halimasch yields an orange-to-pink, *Deilephila* a tone shifted more towards blue. With the equimolecular brenzcatechin and with the tryptophan this colour adds itself, in the yellowish solution, to dirty-green; in all the others the blue-violet hooking-colour is distinct. But only in tyrosine and Dopa, as well as in Halimasch and pupal chromogen, does it come further to a typical blackening and melanin formation; in all the other chromogens used, whether they had been taken equimolecular or more concentrated, the blackening went scarcely beyond what was also attainable without ferment addition. To be sure, with brenzcatechin bulky precipitations had appeared, but these do not give the impression of natural, black melanin, and do not constitute the end state, which consists in a yellowish-brown solution with a turbid precipitate. In concentrated pyrogallol a pale precipitate appears. Since these two precipitations are induced only by *Vespertilio*-, not by Halimasch tyrosinase, it is probably a matter of a precipitation by acid reaction, which would have nothing further to do with the ferment action.

After the samples of the various chromogens, without and with tyrosinase additions, had stood for a longer time and had partly evaporated, differences showed themselves in the figures, among which dark precipitates had appeared with the various chromogens. These figures, traceable partly to crystals, partly to microcrystallized melanins, remind us of those formations which we had earlier observed, in various colour-races of *Dixippus* (cf. Przibram and Brecher 1919, pp. 133, 136, 175), in their chromogens. They are of significance in so far as they appear, with or without tyrosinase, in the form characteristic of the chromogen in question, just as had been the case formerly with the *Dixippus* races. There, however, it had not been a question of different forms of the melanin, but of dark-blue or light-blue figures, which were clearly recognizable only under a particular artificial illumination. At all events we see once again that, in contrast to the influencing of the hooking-colour by different tyrosinases, the precipitate-forms depend on the use of different chromogens. Particulars are to be taken from the two last columns of our table. Worthy of emphasis is the splendid development of "melanophore"-like forms in concentrated pyrogallol, the reddish dendritic "melanin"(?)-formation in concentrated brenzcatechin, and the separation of concentrated phenol, under the influence of the tyrosinase additions, into a water-clear fluid with red, slanting drops. Without ferment addition the concentrated phenol remains a uniform red solution. This substance therefore constitutes an exception in so far as its fate turns out differently without and with addition; but in the case of the concentrated solution it is not a matter of precipitate-figures. On the other hand, in the non-concentrated solution, with tyrosinase addition, brown palisade-shaped precipitates appeared along the old surface, which in the untreated solution were scarcely to be noticed even after complete evaporation. Both in the concentrated and in the diluted solution the action of the Halimasch and of the pupal tyrosinase was quite the same.

Since, of the investigated artificially prepared chromogens, only the tyrosine and the Dopa had undergone essential melanin-formation through tyrosinase addition, it was a concern of ours to investigate whether equimolecular solutions of these two substances are influenced equally strongly by the same tyrosinase, without any side-effect such as alkali, for example, exerts on Dopa, or whether the Dopa is at an advantage. Now, Dopa differs from Tyrosin solely through the presence of one OH group more, namely that one in the meta position to the side chain (Bloch 1917, p. 231). For the ferment attack, which uniformly attacks equimolecular Tyrosin and Dioxyphenylalanin, this meta group thus does not come into consideration as the point of attack; rather, a group common to both chromogens must form the target of attack. Such a one is the side chain, since this is likewise attacked in the yeast fermentation of Tyrosin (Gortner, cf. Przibram and Brecher 1919, p. 120). On the other hand, the point of attack for the "Dopa reaction," conditioned by spontaneous oxidation or by addition of alkali, must lie precisely in the OH of the meta group; for, in the absence of this group in Tyrosin, or also upon its replacement by OCH₃ (Bloch 1917, p. 420), that reaction fails to appear.

This time too it has become probable that in the spontaneous (or alkali-)blackening which proceeds without influence of light, [the point of attack lies] in the nucleus [ring], whereas in the ferment reaction—rendered light-influenceable indirectly through the ferment—"the point of attack is to be sought in the side chain." We see here again that the entry of a hydroxyl group, and this time in the nucleus [ring], on the Dopa makes "relatively difficultlyoxidizable substances often readily oxidizable," as was known for the entry into the side chain (Fürth and Jerusalem, cf. Przibram and Brecher 1919, p. 121).

## Summary.

1. Dioxyphenylalanin, Bloch's "Dopa," blackens of itself, even in very dilute solutions, spontaneously in the air, and is therefore essentially more readily oxidizable than Tyrosin, which, even in concentrated aqueous solution, blackens spontaneously only after a very long time.

2. "Dopa" can be brought to intense blackening by a very slight addition of alkali, without an organic ferment [enzyme] needing to be present.

3. The same result is not attained upon addition of hydrogen peroxide, which rather acts inhibitingly in increasing amount; the action of the alkali can therefore not be traced back to an unintended contamination with traces of peroxide.

4. "Dopa" is weakened in its pigment formation by a very slight addition of acid, thus for example already by the press juice of salamander skins.

5. Saturated "Dopa" solution is induced to pigment formation more rapidly by Tyrosinase than saturated Tyrosin solution, and here the effect of the alkali addition can even be surpassed.

6. With equimolecular solutions of "Dopa" and Tyrosin, the same blackening is achieved by the same strength of Tyrosinase.

7. The initial colour of the "Dopa" modifies itself in an analogous manner as with Tyrosin and all other investigated chromogens, according to the Tyrosinase used; with honey-fungus [*Hallimasch*] tyrosinase it has a red, with butterfly pupae a violet tone.

8. While the initial colours are governed by the Tyrosinase, with different chromogens characteristic forms of precipitation appear, which do not change with the Tyrosinases.

9. Albinotic skins of rats react more acid than those of fully-coloured ones.

10. Light skin areas of guinea-pigs react more acid than black ones of the same specimen.

11. Eye press juices of both albinotic and fully-coloured rats react at least as acid as the press juices from albinotic skins.

12. These eye press juices produce with "Dopa" a green colour, which had earlier been repeatedly observed as the minimal action of Tyrosinase in Tyrosin.

13. To distinguish a separate "Dopa-oxidase" from the Tyrosinase is not necessary, for "Dopa" is an excellent indicator for sites of active Tyrosinases.

14. The failure of the "Dopa" reaction in the albinotic skins, skin areas and in the eyes of mammals depends on the acid reaction-state, which has weakened the Tyrosinase (in pigmented eyes, after the separating-out of the Melanin).

15. There is nothing against Tyrosin as the basis of the animal Melanins, not even the failure of the Millon reaction in the natural chromogens purified of traces of albumen [egg-white/protein], for artificial Tyrosin too gives, after corresponding treatment, a negative result of this test.

16. The investigated natural chromogens of vertebrates yielded neither the browning characteristic for "Dopa" under analogous treatment, nor blackening by alkali.

17. Two processes can lead to melanin formation, of which the one acts through alkali attack on the hydroxyl group in the meta position to the side chain in Dioxyphenylalanin, while the other, in Di- and Monoxyphenylalanin ("Dopa" or Tyrosin), attacks the side chain through ferments [enzymes] (e.g. Tyrosinase).

## Postscript [Nachschrift].

1. Recently Herr Prof. v. Fürth was so kind, at our instigation, as to subject the Tyrosin detection in the pupal chromogen—which in 1901 had turned out negative—to a renewed examination: the chromogen, freed completely of albumen after coagulation of the pupal blood and precipitation with hydrochloric-acid-containing phosphotungstic acid, this time, upon cautious addition of the Millon reagent and warming, gave the reaction characteristic for Tyrosin. Let it therefore here, in accordance with the wish of Herr Prof. v. Fürth, be corrected with respect to his earlier statement (cf. Fürth and Schneider 1901), in the sense of the more recent investigations, to the effect that free Tyrosin can be detected in the chromogen of the pupal blood. According to this positive detection, Tyrosin is no longer—as v. Fürth had hitherto assumed—to be excluded as a natural chromogen; indeed there exists, as v. Fürth too now admits, a great probability that the chromogen of the pupal blood is Tyrosin, even though for the time being the direct confirmation possible from an analysis of the chromogen alone cannot yet be given.

The negative result of the Millon reaction in the earlier investigation (v. Fürth and Schneider 1901) v. Fürth ascribes to too copious an addition of the reagent, whereby the green-yellow colouration of the xanthoprotein reaction, which thereupon set in, had concealed the Millon reaction.

Further details are to be mentioned only in a forthcoming publication.

2. As regards the failure of the Millon reaction in the natural chromogens treated by us with ammonium sulphate as well as by boiling, and in Tyrosin, recent investigations on the Tyrosin too have fully confirmed this behaviour: a Tyrosin solution half-diluted with saturated ammonium sulphate solution shows, in comparison with one half-diluted only with water, after addition of the Millon reagent and boiling, a lasting red colouration only at a number of drops larger in proportion to the amount of Tyrosin. Before this limit, this colouration process characteristic for the Millon reaction is disturbed by another reaction, which yields a weak green colouration. The further details likewise remain reserved for a later publication.
