### Regeneration Processes in the Host.

In the preceding communication (Arch. f. Entw.-Mech. Vol. 99, p. 150) a report was given on the successful free transplantation of developed limbs in salamander larvae. There it was principally the fates of the transplant that were considered. But since in our experimental animals we are dealing with good regenerators, which can answer an amputation with organ-replacement, and since from the interlocking of regeneration and transplantation manifold complications arise, in this second part of the work the relation of transplantation to regeneration must also be discussed, in connection with a few characteristic findings — all the more so since precisely concerning this relation the most contradictory views are current.

In operations 01 and 028 (see the previous work), after the transplantation forward or backward of the limb the amputation site remains open close against the body. Here a regeneration cone soon rises, and after a few months a new complete limb has regenerated, which is equivalent to the one lost at the site in question and which also in size and function is not to be distinguished from a normal one. The same process takes place at the amputation sites that remained free after operations 065 and 071; differently, however, the matter stands at those amputation sites of these latter experiments at which a transplantation had been undertaken.

Now it appears that in such cases *no regeneration takes place beside the transplant*. By this I do not mean the new tissue which forms at the boundary between transplant and host animal, which proceeds from the implant in just the same way as from the other free amputation sites; rather *the regenerate* in the proper sense is meant, which fails to appear, while at most a stunted little stalk in the form of a bow grows out of the implant and ends in merely two toes.

In the remaining four animals of 071, however, beside the implant *no regeneration whatever* takes place; here these animals differ from all the five-toed ones treated hitherto by the absence of the regeneration processes at the leg, while arm and leg behave normally. The arm, in any case, retains throughout the characteristic five-toedness, and one searches there in vain for the characteristic regeneration cells; and since the backward-displaced toes are smoothly and completely incorporated, the healing-in has indeed taken place here¹). Only in one animal does one recognize the former graft site, in that a small annular constriction of the limb has remained. Here the joint sits on the attachment site of the removed limb's upper-arm, similarly to how body-parts grow out in axial elongation of the transplant.

In operation 065, where an arm was to be transplanted onto the amputation site of the leg, despite the most careful preparation of the anatomical character of the amputation sites it was not possible to set the transplant truly exactly onto the cut surface; for if the shoulder-bone of the transplant were set, as ordinarily, against the upper-arm bone, then the surface lying between transplant and shoulder-skeleton remains so thin that the proximal cut-surface of the transplant, although it could heal in, would nevertheless find no proper attachment; one might therefore designate the proximal cut-surface of the transplant as a foreign body inserted into the body in place of the covering; situated so close to the defect region, it is, however, drawn into the proximity of the regeneration processes. Here too the arm of the transplant retains throughout the five-toedness, and in these animals too nothing of it was regenerated. In two of the most successful cases of regeneration the regenerate set in even in size and full perfection; the others fell apart, but all the regenerates remained behind the described defects in their formation. In four of these animals the transplant remained permanently free, i.e. unconnected with the regenerate. In the remaining four animals, however, in each the transplant was *crowded out* by the regenerate —

### Regeneration-Inhibition through Transplantation.

If we summarize these findings, we recognize, indeed, that one and the same phenomenon, only in various degrees, is expressed in all of them: namely the *inhibition of organ-regeneration through the supervening transplantation*. The matter is in detail as follows:

An arm was amputated, and an equivalent limb is replaced by the salamander larva itself under »normal circumstances« from a »normal« amputation-surface. In the experiments, however, this otherwise free wound-surface was always set against a foreign limb. As a consequence of this it was seen that the regeneration was either *wholly suppressed* or, when the regenerate was indeed formed and further developed, then in its formation either retarded and delayed in size, or that the differentiation also lagged behind (four cases of 071: failure to appear of the regeneration; at the leg of 065: covering of the local regenerate by the healing-in); the others fall apart, but all the regenerates remained behind the described defects in their formation.

These facts stand in no slight connection with the question under which conditions a successful transplantation occurs, namely the better and more thorough the healing-in occurs, the fewer points — these are about 12 to 16 in number — from which one might draw the surmised conclusions, as soon as the observational material of various authors, viewed under a like point of view, is compiled, namely from the older works of *Detwiler* (1920, 1922) and of *Grüper* (1922). Both had undertaken transplantations of limb-attachment-material; in doing so, by contrast, *Detwiler* in his experiments did not work with already differentiated organs, but with embryonic material, and *Grüper* played but a small role; his findings are with mine not entirely comparable.

*Detwiler* (1920) extirpated the embryonic attachment-material of a forelimb in *Amblystoma* larvae and transplanted it onto a body-segment lying caudal from the original position. If the extirpation-site remains *open*, then there, in the further course of development, the normal limb is set there (*Harrison* 1918). This process, as one must — since it does not concern the further-development of the original site of the normal limb, but rather a replacement-formation from the surroundings — is in fact to be regarded as regeneration. If the wound is closed with indifferent ectoderm, the replacement-formation remains *suppressed*. Now the breadth of the transplanted attachment-material amounts to 3½ segments, so that, with transplantation 1, 2 or 3 segments caudal from the extirpation-opening, a part of the latter remains free, while the remaining part is incorporated and covered over a stretch by the transplant. Now from the experiments of *Detwiler* the following results:

With transplantation a whole segment caudal, so that also a slit of the breadth of a segment remained free, a regenerate sprouted forth from this slit in 48% of the cases; yet it was not viable, i.e. it dissolved approximately as the transplant became sparsely interwoven.

With transplantation onto two segments caudal, where the opening is already broader, there too 48% regenerates originated; although here too the outgrowths again retrogressed, they nonetheless held themselves three days longer than the previous ones.

With transplantation onto three segments caudal, finally, regenerates formed in 43% of the cases, and indeed of continued existence, although they too retrogressed about 10–13 days later; they came forth from the slit from which regenerates had gone forth.

With removal-displacement of the transplant nowhere caudalward of the one or other segment, the regenerate grew completely in such a case at the extirpation-opening, and was permanently retained.

*Detwiler* summarizes his findings in the following words (p. 151): »It is readily seen, therefore, from this observation that there is an inhibitory influence of the transplanted limb upon the uncommonly remote portion of the regimen which attempts to regenerate, so that the more remote the transplanted limb bud becomes, the longer the time required for total disappearance of the regenerating nodule of cells.«

A similar »inhibitory influence« has then also been demonstrated in a later work of the same author (*Detwiler* 1922): there, namely, the transplantation of the embryonic attachment-material of the forelimb cranialward from the normal attachment site was undertaken. Here, however, it found itself within the gill-formation-region, and one must, from this, as in the first case could be evoked, somewhat in such a manner as also with more nourishment more teeth would be developed. Just as with weaker nourishment and the more impeded forward-pressing of the growth-appearances which occur, the indicated formations did not reach the requisite size by overpowering the counter-forces of the formation-process working against the outgrowth (counter-resistances [tissue-elasticity, surface-tension, etc., *Przibram* 1909]); the already weak forward-thrust and the indicated formations differ from one another not slightly, but rather essentially from those in which from the outset hypotypical formations were set; and the phenomenon of the atypia is caused, just as also by segment-attachment-conditioned factors, while the conditions during the further development remain uninfluenced; the appearance and coming-about of the atypia of the developed regenerates have no part on the leg. Here I have already mentioned the leg more emphatically than the other limbs, at which a standstill sets in; I searched the remaining limbs, the transplant proper; now I scrutinize the entire nourishment that earlier stood at the disposal of both limbs, which the arm-regenerate now gets to use; for had it earlier had the arm and the nourishment for itself, and must the outgrowth therefore set in, then the leg, where now no atypia comes forth, would suffer no premature developmental standstill and lack of nourishment. So, however, it does not now happen; the amputated transplant regenerates fast and vigorously and becomes once again equal, in size and differentiation, like the others — not so. Hitherto we have discussed three kinds of *regeneration-inhibition through transplantation*: firstly the *under-pressing of the breakthrough forward and outward*, secondly the *inhibition through nourishment*, thirdly the *impairment of the form-formation-process*. All three kinds had in common that it had come to the setting-in of the regeneration-processes and that the transplant exerted its inhibiting influence therein first upon the *further-development* of the regenerate. Now one might with reason raise the objection that against the setting-in of the regeneration-inhibition no direct counter-evidence ever lies. Yet it appears probable that, in such regeneration-inhibition through transplantation too — where indeed only the *under-pressing of the breakthrough forward and outward* is given, which underlies every attachment of a *regenerate* — also organ-formation-processes do not set in. And yet here too it could be possibly more precisely so: a replacement is created from the surroundings of the wound-site that the distancing transplant brings forward.

It is, then, also not the deviation from the typical limb form that brings about the atypia of the regenerate in the cases described, but rather, conversely, in these cases the same factors are at work that as a rule produce the atypia, namely the conditions of nutrition. As I have set forth in the discussion of the cases already mentioned, the development of the atypia and the coming-about of the atypia of the fully formed regenerates depend in no small part on the regeneration process itself, in that during the regeneration there occurs a standstill at points where the stronger growth of the rest of the limb has already used up all the nutritive materials that were earlier available to both limbs; the regenerate then comes too late, and now nutrition would also have to provide for the formation of new tissue, but is no longer sufficient, and atypia is the consequence of this lack of nutrition. This happens also here; only that here the amputated stump-limb regenerates quickly and vigorously and could again attain its full size, were the site of regeneration not, in turn, impaired in its nutrition.

So far we have discussed three kinds of *inhibition of regeneration through transplantation*, firstly the *suppression of the regeneration-capacity from outside*, secondly the inhibition through *nutrition*, thirdly the *impairment of the form-building process*. All three kinds had this in common, that they let the inhibition of the regeneration processes come about only after the insertion of the transplant, and that they made the transplant effective only *afterwards*. Now I take leave to put forward, against the view here represented, yet a further conceivable kind of inhibition of regeneration, which alone seems to me to come into consideration to refute the conception advanced. Yet it seems to me probable that even in this case no regeneration-inhibition is to be sought from outside, since indeed *every disposition of a regenerate develops only by itself*, so that no formative processes from the side of the organism need set in at all. And it is precisely in this that one is then perhaps able, more correctly: in the surroundings of the wound-site, to seek the distant cause of why no replacement-regenerate is created from the wound-surfaces of the transplant.

Let us think of the regeneration processes as being triggered by the abrupt tearing-apart of the developed tissue-connection in the amputation site. Then the organism would feel a sudden injury, an empty space; reactions would set in, replacement would be created; the mobilization would, however, in any case last a certain length of time. Could now, in the case of this mobilization of the *organ*regenerative repercussions on the tissue-continuity, in the full measure of re-establishment, be brought about, were the conditions for the re-establishment of the normal tissue-connections present from the start; were the wound, so to speak, immediately again provided for in time before mobilization, then there could be no occasion at all for the re-establishment of the normal tissue-connections by way of regeneration. It is, namely, not the deviation from the typical individual species-character that is here expressed in regenerative form, but rather the *interruption* of the developed individual cohesion as such. The pseudopodia of an amoeboid protist can, namely, without injury to the individual itself, be withdrawn again into the body, as soon as their continuity with the rest of the individual is re-established. They were once tissue-elements of one and the same individual, but became artificially detached. For re-establishment of this character-likeness of individuals one needs only the re-establishment of the developed continuity, since within the boundaries of one and the same individual there can be no *character-difference* of the single elements. Within the boundaries of the organism, the smallest particle has, with regard to every other, just the same character. As soon as, however, an artificial separation of the differently characterized tissue-portions has taken place, then a character-difference of the single elements at once becomes manifest, and the organism feels this as a reciprocal *otherness* of the originally co-belonging tissue-portions, and a violent displacement of the originally co-belonging tissue-portions out of their developed connection found, by way of regeneration, a strong reaction, in that replacement was created and only then did re-establishment of the connection take place, after the gap had been bridged anew.

Yes, even more: not only does its own character make the developed tissue-character of the single character-likeness in the course of development pass over into separated parts, the opportunity to find connection to its own kind sinks. The single tissue-elements take on, in the course of the embryonic development, their own character; how out of the phenomenally uniform starting-material, step by step, the complicated manifold of differentiation of the end-formation works itself out, about this we can, precisely in the limbs, make quite well-supported general statements.

Experiments by *Peebles* (1910) make it probable, even if not yet with the desirable clarity, that at very early stages only the general limb-quality, but not yet the determination whether fore- or hind-limb, has taken place. *Braus* had shown that later, upon interchange, a transformation of the one into the other kind of limb no longer set in, and *Harrison*'s experiments have further taught that the fixation of the specific limb-quality is likewise carried through already at a very early developmental stage. At this time, in further experiments of *Harrison*, there seems, according to his interpretation, still to prevail around the limb-axis a morphologically and physiologically centric symmetry ¹), and only in the further course is this abolished by a differentiation that is different in the different diameters of the limb; the fixation of the side-quality takes place, *Gräper*'s "cross-section determination." While this centrically asymmetric formation strikes the eye without further ado in the autopodium and zeugopodium, the morphologically concentric arrangement of the tissues in the stylopodium might perhaps tempt one to the assumption that here the original symmetry has been preserved in the single diameters; that, accordingly, if we distinguish, in correspondence with the already external asymmetry of the distal sections, an extensor side and a flexor side, in the proximal, symmetrically appearing section no differential significance should attach to this distinction; that, rather, the musculature of the extensor side is distinguished by no personal character physiologically from that of the flexor side, that both are of equal value to one another and capable of substituting for one another, and that further in this proximal section there also exists a like homogeneity of the skin in the whole circumference. All this, however, by no means holds true: as the function of mirror-image implanted limbs teaches (*Weiss* 1922), the musculature of the extensor side is not in a position to behave physiologically like flexor musculature, but rather behaves still further exactly as before, prior to the transplantation, so that thus the muscle-groups lying at the two ends of a limb-diameter must be regarded as

With regard to the skin, too, the experiments of the first part of the present work (S. 166) have shown that even a mere displacement of the proximal parts of the skin of the original ventral or dorsal side retains its character-typical particularities, so that even there, after conclusion of the corresponding differentiation processes, a difference of character remains, hence a non-equivalence and a non-equal-valuedness of the cross-section areas lying in different directions has been formed.

If we now wish to draw these considerations into play for the interpretation of the transplantation results: they yield, provisionally without bearing upon the facts of the present work, as a preliminary confirmation, through agreement, of the results, the factual findings, with the calculation of the consequences. From the *non-equivalence of differently directed cross-section areas* there resulted of itself: firstly, the *spatial approximation* of the differently characterized parts to be joined for unification, since one always had to seek, in the coming-about of the atypia, chemical or other proximate, but in no case action-at-a-distance effects; for from a distance, that is, from the body across, no chemical influences (substances and findings) may reach across, the more so as all things lie too far apart, so that they could exert their differentiating action only in too short a distance; in the more richly such disturbing influences could find occasion, the unification to thwart.

Both basic requirements stand and fall, then, with the *complete congruence* of all directions in the *like-directed implantation*. Also biologically the centric symmetry of the cross-section areas comes, in *like-directed implantation*, not once at all into play, and even were the rotations of the symmetrical surfaces inwardly always like-directed, the two equal-valued symmetrical areas would, with respect to one another, offer the more favorable union-possibility. The asymmetry of the cross-section areas, on the other hand, makes only *one such position of the transplants possible*, in which a part for the one cross-section orientation of both sections is the same. With stronger pre-rotation of the one against the like tissues of the other, the cohesion of the tissues which were originally not joined together becomes the more impossible, the longer such reciprocal alien-getting, *immediately*, that is, to fuse, lasts; the *pre-rotation*, which is admissible, without the tissues of the one surface falling out of the like-character-range of the opposite side, will be all the larger the greater the like-character-differences themselves are, that is, but, the less far the auseinander-differentiation has progressed, briefly, the *younger* the animal is.

If now, in the transplantation of an organ onto a cross-section area capable of *regeneration of a whole organ*, such a rapid and *unmediated fusion* (through local growing-into-one-another) of the like-character tissues on both sides of the cross-section area can be achieved, then, on my view, the *organ-regeneration-process is not initiated at all*. Are the conditions for an unmediated growing-together of the tissues not given, then the regeneration-capable amputation site reacts exactly as after every simple cut-surface, only that, in the presence of the proximal cross-section area of the transplant, the regeneration processes organize themselves at the presence of the proximal cross-section area of the transplant.

With all these considerations the experimental results now agree very well! First I point to my own findings communicated above (S. 169), only [I am] far from wanting to impute to anyone, given their small number, that he ascribe to them full proof-power. In four out of six experiments carried out, a *like-directed* transplantation exactly to the amputation site succeeded on 071, and in all these four cases every trace of *regeneration has stayed absent*; the part of the [original limb] that remained in the body during the operation has developed further and has grown out of the body's interior, at the grafting-site, however, which even now upon sharp observation is visible, say, through a slight constriction of the skin, there is lacking precisely the easy constriction of the skin in the prolongation of the protruding transplant; one can, indeed, recognize on it precisely that *pigmentation* (extensor side), so that one would now have to examine exactly, by sharp observation, whether one might also detect, in the prolongation of the protruding transplant, any trace of joint-region-character or the like, which would point to begun or suppressed regeneration. The recently appeared work of *Gräper* (1922) might win me much testimony, even if also with not very numerous material.

*Gräper* has carried out host-different and host-like-arranged transplantations on limb-buds and in quite general fashion again taken up the like-directed cross-section orientation (homopleural or, conversely, heteropleural) of the transplant from the underlay directly, and no site-regenerate appeared, that, on the other hand, with non-like-character covering of the cross-section areas, there always developed from the body a regenerate beside the transplant. *Gräper* has likewise correctly recognized the connection, and how very much his findings support my views developed in the foregoing, and how completely his and my mode of explanation agree in this point, even if in single words [they may] in the foregoing [diverge], may [the following] make clear (S. 305): "If the cross-section organization (of a naturally arisen, or by transplantation orthotopically transferred, member) agrees with the cross-section organization that the factors proceeding from the body normally [build up] in [the naturally arisen member or in] the transplant, then there develops only a simple, host-like-character limb and never a further regenerate. If, however, these cross-section organizations do not agree, then either, under the influence of the factors proceeding from the body, a re-tuning is set in train, and then there likewise arises only a simple, host-side-correct limb without secondary regenerate, or else this re-tuning is impossible because of too strong a fixation of the cross-section organization in the transplant, then this grows further as something independent and the factors proceeding from the body seek out a new field of action and form a host-side-correct regenerate."

In order to be able to fuse together, the cross-sections must not merely be geometrically, but rather *physiologically congruent*; that is, the corresponding axes of the two surfaces must be like-directed (naturally only up to a certain degree of likeness). Here an analogy from sense-physiology strikes one: *Mach* has pointed out that two geometrically similar figures are by no means always recognized as related by our sense-apparatus, but rather only then when they are both oriented alike to the symmetry-plane of our sense-apparatus; thus, e.g., a square standing on its point and one resting on its side possess no physiological likeness at all, and only intellectual means help us to recognize the objective relatedness. Just as alien do the two cut-surfaces of one and the same cross-section also appear, when the *axis-system* of the one is rotated by a more-or-less large amount against the corresponding one of the other. The magnitude of this amount fluctuates, as we have seen above, with the *age*. On the magnitude of the physiological likeness of two cross-sections that are to come to fusion depends the possibility of a direct fusion.

Besides through physiological dissimilarity, however, the direct fusion can also be prevented through *oppositely directed growth-tendency* of the two cross-section areas, e.g. upon polarity-inverted bringing-together. This establishment is, properly, no extension at all of what has hitherto been said; for even if we, for the sake of the more vivid presentation, in the foregoing discussed only the cross-section and demanded for it likeness of the axis-orientation as a precondition of direct fusing, so may we yet not overlook that the organ is indeed a three-dimensional formation and that the axes perpendicular to the cross-section plane, with their polar non-equivalence, likewise in both components must be like-directedly oriented, if otherwise an unmediated unification is to be possible. One can compile a whole series of works of various authors, from all of which it emerges that it may succeed only in the rarest cases, upon planting like-poled cross-section areas one on another, to suppress the regeneration permanently. After a shorter or longer time there then form, after all, regenerates at the union-site, which often lead to the complete separation of the joined constituents. So found, e.g., *Wetzel* (1895), that the healing-together of transversely halved Hydras with the like-named cross-section areas (about oral on oral) succeeds easily, but that after some time regeneration-processes set in at the union-site, which finally lead to a separation of the components. To the same results came *Peebles* (1900) and *King* (1901) in Hydra, and something similar occurred in the polarity-inverted graftings of *Joest* (1897) on the earthworm. So all these findings teach that, with manifold orientation, the *external healing-in*, a wound-healing process, can proceed smoothly, without one's being permitted to see therein a guarantee for a lasting persistence of the connection; that, rather, only with physiologically correct bringing-together of the components can it be prevented that at the cross-section areas regeneration-processes set in, which would inevitably have as a consequence a pushing-away of the graft-pieces.

So I have, in the foregoing, taken pains to lay out clearly the conditions of when, beside a transplant, a regenerate will arise, when not. That in many cases regenerates arise is nothing that ought not from the outset to have been expected; what appears remarkable and in need of explanation is solely and alone why in *some cases of transplantation the regeneration precisely does not* set in. I have, on the basis of a small number of research results, wished to make probable the view that the absence of the regeneration in certain cases finds its explanation through an *unmediated fusion of the tissues*, but that this fusion is possible only with physiologically like-sensed orientation of transplant and attachment-site. Whether one may now accept this explanation or not, on the fact that after like-directed transplantation a complete regeneration-suppression sets in, nothing is altered by that. Already *Morgan* has remarkably found that one may, upon interchange of the head-end after detachment and re-insertion of one and the same Tritone tail-piece, achieve that there nevertheless arises a regenerate, and yet possesses every other cross-section area in itself the capacity to regenerate in the highest measure. *The activation of this capacity* will then through the grafting-on of another tail-piece *inhibited* (1907, S. 255) ¹).

### Impairment of the Transplant by Regeneration Processes.

It must appear strange, given such a state of affairs, when it is even asserted that the regenerative capacities are *not inhibited* by the *Transplantation*, but on the contrary are *activated*, as was recently asserted by v. *Ubisch* (1922). Since such a view stands in sharp contradiction with the findings presented here, this must still be addressed. The source of the contradiction lies entirely at the surface, and from its resolution the actual findings of v. *Ubisch* remain quite untouched.

*Ubisch* has grafted parts of earthworms onto one another in various combinations, above all also components of differing age grafted onto one another, and now establishes the percentage of permanent healings-together; and indeed he finds it higher with the union of unequally-aged components. This far the actual results reach, and up to this point everything is very pretty and interesting. But now comes the interpretation: It rests chiefly on the assumption that the prospects for a successful *Transplantation* are the more favorable, the better the *regeneration-capacity* is; »the healing-in of the transplant is precisely a regenerative achievement.« This assumption v. *Ubisch*, as he himself states, has taken over from *Schöne* (1912). Now he has thereby become the victim of an unfortunate scientific terminology, which designates quite different processes with one and the same word. *Schöne* is a surgeon, and by surgeons and pathologists, since their views are gained chiefly from humans, no sharp separation is made between wound-healing, scar-formation, healing-in and regeneration. According to such a terminology, every proliferative process after the setting of a wound would then be a »regenerative achievement,« e.g. also the connective-tissue encapsulation of an introduced foreign body. Upon more precise examination, to be sure, even in the human — where we have to do almost only with »*tissue-replacement-new-formation*,« but not with »*organ-replacement*« through regeneration — sharper distinctions would arise, roughly between healing-in and wound-healing; for these are, for example, quite obviously different processes, when on the one occasion a tissue, as it is, heals in and is joined to the organism, and when on another occasion a tissue does indeed heal in, but is gradually replaced by the regenerating tissue of the wound-surface.

But this is not the place to go into the conditions in poor regenerators, in which indeed often not even a real tissue-regeneration occurs, but only scar-like wound-healing, and this is then given the title »regeneration.« In any case it is not permissible to transfer, without further ado, views gained in such a restricted domain to cases in which we are dealing with animals that not only repair tissue-defects, but demonstrate the capacity to restore whole organs, indeed even the greater part of their body. Let us, in order to escape provisionally from the terminological difficulties and to undertake a delimitation against wound-healing, contrast this capacity, as »*organ-regeneration*,« with simple tissue-proliferation. And let us now for once look at how such a capacity for »organ-regeneration« relates to *Transplantation* — whether it really is so favorable for the healing-in of a transplant, as v. *Ubisch* thinks, so that one might then, as he does, infer from especially good *Transplantation* successes also an especially vigorous effect of such »regenerative potencies.«

First of all, I again summarize here what has shown itself in my experiments: let one read, say, the factual report on p. 169; there one finds that everywhere where the transplant had been placed in the vicinity of a regeneration-capable wound-surface, when a regenerate was actually laid down, this *displaced* the transplant. Healed in the transplants always were, in my case, whether I had transplanted positionally-correctly and at a regeneration-site or not; it is quite beyond question that in such a manner a union of originally mutually foreign tissues must have come about; how this takes place cannot be said — perhaps the growing-together is preceded by a certain assimilation of the tissues in question through loss of certain of their properties, similar as in the explantation-experiments of *Erdmann* and *Gassul* (1922). However the union of the not entirely same-type tissue-pieces may finally take place, in any case they need much more time than the fusion, in the sense set out above, between two same-type tissue-pieces. But during this time the organ-regenerative processes already set in; unconcerned with the transplant, the new-formation of a limb organizes itself, and when the transplant is then already too firmly anchored in the body to be thrown out entirely, then there arises precisely the *struggle between transplant and regenerate* described above. Thus we find the transplants finally often sitting directly upon the regenerate, because they have been pushed out of the body.

So too the transplants that *Detwiler* set into the gill-region have often been pushed away by a considerable distance from the implantation-site by the vigorously regenerating gill; in other cases again the fusion of the arising regenerate with the transplant has not allowed the latter any proper development.

So too in the above-mentioned investigations of *Wetzel, Peebles, King, Joest* many cases have arisen in which the setting-in regeneration completely undid the *Transplantation* result again — once more by the fact that it brought about a *displacement* of the already grown-on transplant. Indeed, it is precisely through these latter cases that the question is especially clearly illuminated: first a fine healing-in of the transplant; then after some time, at the site of union, regeneration sets in after all, and the transplant is cast off; so it is precisely the »activation of regenerative potencies« that signifies the end of the transplant, and one sees: when the transplant holds, this is above all to be thanked to the circumstance that through the *Transplantation* the regeneration was suppressed, which, in the case of its occurrence, would have had a displacement of the transplant as its consequence. Consequently: *the better the organ-regeneration-capacity, the more unfavorable the prospects for successful Transplantation,* not the reverse.

For further illustration I have here compiled also the statistical results from the mentioned works of *Detwiler*; they speak a clearer language than all words. He obtained:

For transplantations into the *regeneration-weak* region caudalward from the normal attachment-site of the foreleg, 82% healings-in.

For transplantation into the gill-region after extirpation of the entire normal gill-formation-zone (the gills can *regenerate* from the surroundings of this district), 63% healings-in.

With only partial removal of the gill-formation-zone, that is, under still *better regeneration-conditions,* 55% healings-in.

Furthermore, among the developed transplants there were:

For transplantation of the anlage-material of the foreleg three segments caudally, that is, into *regeneration-weak* territory, 75% normal.

For transplantation of the anlage-material of the foreleg three segments cranially, that is, into the territory of the excellently *regenerating* gills, only 15% normal.

It is almost superfluous to add any commentary to these figures.

The percentage of successful healings-in is, in the zones of which we *know* (have not *inferred*!) that they possess considerable regeneration-capacity, far smaller than in the regions in which organ-regeneration-capacity is lacking.

Here the experiments of v. *Ubisch* can now be appended without constraint, only he must step back from his dogma, which arose from a misunderstood terminological obscurity. Indeed, I should like to hold up to him one of his own results, for which perhaps only now, through the foregoing discussions, a contradiction-free and unforced explanation will emerge for him: He finds, namely, among other things, that graftings nearer the hind-end succeed far better than more toward the fore-end. Proceeding from the standpoint of his above-named basic assumption, he now thinks he must immediately conclude that, since the *Transplantation* successes increase from front to back, the »regeneration-capacity« in the earthworm too increases from front to back. But it is precisely to the opposite result that, according to v. *Ubisch*'s own statement, *Morgan* and still other researchers have come, who have established a decrease of the general regeneration-capacity in the earthworm toward the hind-end, and v. *Ubisch* now believes that through his experiments he has come into contradiction with these establishments. By no means: precisely the fact that the graftings at the hind-end succeed better than more in front proves indeed that there the regeneration can be more easily suppressed by the *Transplantation*, that there too, therefore, the regeneration-tendency must be weaker — quite in agreement with the findings of the other researchers; for it is only to the *weaker* regeneration-tendency that v. *Ubisch* here owes his *better successes*.

Thus, I believe, one can summarily establish that a regeneration proceeding from the body can only be *disadvantageous* for the healing-in of a transplant, and that the activation of regenerative potencies very much calls into question the success of a *Transplantation*. At the same time, however, we also recognize that under certain conditions the *Transplantation* itself already represents the effective means of *inhibiting* the activation of that capacity. To know the conditions under which this occurs is therefore very essential for the success of a *Transplantation*. The main thing in this seems to me to be the *positionally-correct orientation,* through which the physiologically related territories are brought into coincidence. That the healing-in of course proceeds with tissue-growth and -new-formation no one will doubt, nor that these processes have much in common with the ordinary wound-healing processes; only against this — that the complicated organ-regenerations too be treated without further ado together with these processes, and experiences from the one domain be at once generalized to the other — did I wish to turn. For the rest, I had here only to go into these questions in so far as they stood in connection with the *Transplantation* conditions.
