## I. Introduction.

*Connection and statement of the question. — Normal reproductive act of the frog-amphibians in general. — Normal reproductive act of Alytes in particular.*

The present investigations form the continuation of my work (1906): "Experimental Alteration of the Reproductive Activity in the Midwife Toad (*Alytes obstetricans*) and the Tree-Frog (*Hyla arborea*)," namely only of the first part, dealing exclusively with *Alytes*, in which I had at times observed, in this brood-tending anuran genus, a considerable approximation to the typical developmental courses of other, non-brood-tending frog-amphibians.¹

> ¹ The exact citations of the works mentioned are to be found in the bibliography (Chapter VII)! The words printed in italics refer to the likewise italic-printed catchwords of the bibliography.

Why *Alytes* sometimes refrained from brood-care — about this I had, in my work of that time (p. 70 ff.), been able to express myself only conjecturally; it was therefore the purpose of further experimentation to ascertain the causes of that lapse of parental instincts, that is, to be able to bring about arbitrarily the abandonment of their tasks on the part of the breeding animals. It was, further, the goal of further breedings to ascertain whether those reproductive alterations acquired in the individual life of the first experimental generation are capable of transmitting themselves to the descendants — that is, whether the midwife toads brought to development without brood-care, after the mode of the remaining European frog-amphibians, would again propagate themselves identically or similarly to their parents and to the ordinary frogs and toads, and dissimilarly and unlike their grandparents.

Before I describe these new breeding experiments in detail — two preliminary communications about this (1907 *Fortpflanzungsveränderungen* and 1908 *Erworbene Eigenschaften*) have already appeared —, it will be necessary once more to sketch the regular mode of propagation of *Alytes obstetricans*, also in comparison with other tailless amphibians, further to recapitulate briefly the content of the first cited work (1906 *Alytes-Hyla*), in order to call back into the reader's memory which reproductive alterations, whose heritability is to be tested, actually lay before us.

The European genera of the tailless amphibians — apart from *Alytes* — deposit, as is well known, their eggs, which number in the hundreds and thousands and which are mostly united by sticky gelatinous envelopes into clumps or strings, into stagnant waters. The deposition takes place in such a way that the male embraces its female in the loin- or armpit-region and, in hours-long or days-long labor, presses the egg-burden out of its body by means of the pressure of the fore-extremities; as soon as the eggs leave the cloaca of the female, the male squirts its semen over them. In doing so its hind-extremities are alternately drawn in forcefully and again stretched out, so that toes and feet involuntarily often dip into the welling-up spawn-mass and, upon the stretching of the leg, must promote its emergence, since it is thereby drawn out, as it were, directly from the cloaca. Thus not only the genus which has received from this the name ("midwife toad") exercises a kind of obstetric aid, but also that of other anuran genera, according to Héron-Royer (1878) especially pronounced in *Bufo*, *Pelobates* and *Pelodytes*. In the water the gelatinous envelopes presently begin to swell up, thereby lose their stickiness and remain lying at the place of deposition, without the parent animals concerning themselves further about it. The freeing of the embryos ready to hatch occurs mostly simply through maceration of the egg-membranes; their tearing is sometimes assisted by the larvae repeatedly and rapidly stretching out their hunched-up trunk. The developmental stage at which the leaving of the egg takes place is indeed not, in all genera and species, the same, yet it can in general be characterized thereby that no gills are present as yet; the gill-clefts are still closed and are formed merely by entoderm-folds which touch the skin. Only after some days do branched little gill-trees grow outward upon the gill-arches; some further days thereafter these are replaced by inner gill-lamellae seated in rows on the hind-margin of the gill-arches. Only now, too, do the larvae come into possession of horny jaws, by means of which they gnaw at firm objects and attach themselves to them, whereas they were able to do the latter, at the time of hatching and immediately afterward, only with the help of two suction-pits situated ventral to the lower-jaw bulges.

The genus *Alytes* spawns not in the water, but on the land, and its eggs, which appear connected by a sticky gelatinous envelope into single-rowed strings, are only 18–86 in number, but of outstanding size, which is conditioned by a special wealth of yolk. The male clasps its female about the loins and renders it obstetric aid, in that it not only presses the spawn out of the cloaca, but also assists its emergence by pulling and bracing with its hind legs. Since the eggs, after leaving the maternal body, do not come into water, their gelatinous envelopes cannot swell up; consequently they also do not at once lose their stickiness, but remain adhering to the hind-thighs of the male and, by virtue of the movements which the male unceasingly performs with its legs, wind themselves tightly around the latter. Within some hours after the delivery the gelatinous envelope has indeed here too no longer remained sticky, but instead has shrunk, has become tough, almost hard, so that it now after all does not fall down from the thighs of the paternal animal, but sits upon them like a fetter (hence the name "fetter-toad" ["Feßlerkröte"]). In this position they remain until the end of embryonal development. They are dragged about by the male for so long ("egg-bearing toad") until the embryos are ready to hatch out; one of the frequent baths of their begetter finally gives them the opportunity, with the additional aid of trunk-muscle activity, forcibly to break through the envelopes, which they had already previously worked upon with their horny jaws, and to escape into the water. Here they complete their post-embryonal development like other frog-tadpoles; only they require for this, as a rule, more than a full year, whereas those mostly become four-legged frogs and toads already in the first summer. The developmental stage at which the *Alytes*-embryos leave the egg is a far more advanced one than in our remaining frog-amphibians; the gill-less stage and the larval stage with outer gills (and therewith also the suction-pit stage) are here still passed through within the egg and are distinguished by the possession of a mighty yolk-sac; the larvae are already, before hatching, in possession of the horny jaws, which, as mentioned, render them important services in the breaking-through of the envelopes, as well as in possession of inner gills.

## II. Recapitulation of results important for the present work from earlier works.

The alteration of the reproductive activity of *Alytes* just described, insofar as I had achieved it in 1906, consisted in its partial **leading-back into the more original generative conditions of the remaining Ecaudata of Europe**:

1) The eggs forcibly taken from the paternal animals develop, without the brood-care of the male normally accorded to them, just as well and rapidly as with that brood-care.

2) Moisture, brightness and higher temperature — just as well each of these three factors by itself alone as combined with the other two — act acceleratingly; dryness, darkness and low temperature act in the same way retardingly upon the development of the *Alytes*-embryos.

3) The freshly hatched *Alytes*-larvae find themselves, independently of the light- and moisture-conditions to which the eggs had been exposed, and independently of the exercise or omission of male brood-care, approximately at one and the same developmental stage; however, with the bringing-in of raised temperature in combination with dry surroundings, it is possible to hold the *Alytes*-embryos fast in the egg up to more advanced degrees of formation.

4) Individual eggs are able to develop even when one does not keep them on the land in merely moist surroundings, but lays them, immediately after deposition, into the water; their gelatinous envelopes then swell up just like those of other anuran eggs.

5) *Alytes*-eggs situated in the water release their larvae at a relatively low stage, namely while they still bear outer gills. Their liberation occurs, since they do not yet possess horny teeth, solely through hunching-up and re-stretching of the trunk, in consequence of which movement the envelopes burst apart.

6) The larvae which have developed in such water-eggs require, for their post-embryonal development, even without other accelerating influences, only 3–4 instead of about 15 months. Acceleration of the **metamorphosis** can moreover be effected through brightness, high temperature, slight quantity and mechanical movement of the water (air- and water-currents, eddies, undulation), as well as through copious nourishment and scanty nourishment preceding intensive nourishment.

7) The *Alytes*-embryo can, at the stage provided with outer gills and still without horny jaws, be taken by operative intervention even out of a land-egg situated in normal surroundings, and be transformed into a larva living freely in the water. All specimens so treated grow up into partially neotenic animals, that is, they need longer to reach metamorphosis than normal, persist for 2 years and longer in the larval state. Retardation of the **metamorphosis** can moreover be effected through darkness, low temperature, air-richness, great quantity and rest of the water, intensive nourishment preceding scanty nourishment.

8) In all the larvae prematurely escaped from the egg — both those mentioned under 5 and those mentioned under 7 — the outer gills, the **integument** and other parts of the body are subject to adaptive **alterations** which are conditioned by the water-life. The most distinct of these alterations concern the **gills**, which adapt themselves to water-breathing through shortening — namely pure resorption or accompanying dropping-off of the tips (diminution of the respiring surface) — through epithelial-strengthening, pigment-increase and capillary-vessel-diminution.

9) The larvae arisen from the land-eggs, already provided with inner gills, remain — when they reach, instead of the water, only moist earth-soil — alive there for up to 4 weeks. Through skin-thickening, precocious development of the skin-glands and lungs, lateral strengthening of the muscle-portion and narrowing of the seam-portion of the tail, as well as through purposive reactions of purely physiological nature, these "land-larvae" adapt themselves to the air-medium. The remainder of their post-embryonal development must run its course in the water and is distinguished by compressed brevity. The horny jaws are cast off and the wide frog-mouth gapes open, even before the fore-legs appear.

10) Several times, captive midwife toads of a particular find-place (Münster i. Westf. [in Westphalia]) deposited their egg-strings of their own accord into the water instead of onto the land; the eggs were drawn out of the females' cloaca by their males and fertilized, but the winding around the male thighs and the carrying-about failed to take place.

The causes which, in these cases, had brought about the spontaneous abandonment of brood-care could not at that time be regarded as established; they were referred only conjecturally and quite superficially to the complex, general alterations which are unavoidably bound up with the transference of the animals from free life into captivity — alterations the impulse to which was perhaps already given in free life by the fact that the midwife toad, a specifically West-European species, is engaged in spreading toward north and east, and thus the regions where it must originally have acquired its peculiar mode of reproduction it must already in part have left.

As I already indicated in two preliminary communications of the years 1907 and 1908, I succeeded ere long **in ascertaining a factor under whose action *Alytes* gives up its brood-care**: the spawn was at first usually still deposited on the land, but the male exercised no brood-care, indeed it kicked off the egg-string when this wanted to remain sticking to its thighs, and let it lie unheeded; finally, however, the eggs were deposited into the water-basin, where an adhering, in consequence of the immediate swelling of the gelatinous envelope, does not take place anyway. Nonetheless, as is likewise already mentioned, in this case too a certain percentage of larvae are able to complete development well, and out of the water-eggs there slip such resistant and adaptable larvae at an earlier stage than otherwise, namely still provided with outer gills and not yet with horny jaws. The factor, however, which guarantees the entry of this altered reproductive course — already become almost identical with that of the other frog-amphibians — provided, namely, that the experimental animals proceed to propagation at all —, consists in **constant raising of the normal temperature to 25–30 degrees C.**

Herewith it was at the same time established that my supposition (1906 *Alytes-Hyla*) is borne out, that the altered captivity-conditions were to blame that some among the many midwife toads which otherwise retained their normal brood-care, suddenly gave up this brood-care at that time. A factor, namely, which is almost always bound up with the captivity of animals of our geographical latitudes, is the temperature-raising which is already brought about by the falling-away of winter; and precisely this factor appears, isolated, concentrated and in such mode of application, prompt and regularly effective for the cessation of brood-care in my new experimental series. The other supposition (1906), according to which the tendency to giving-up of the brood-care could already be derived from free life — from the free life in regions which are remote from the original center of distribution of the midwife toad —, will probably turn out to be erroneous; for the wandering of *Alytes* takes place from west toward east and north, leads consequently into regions whose climate can bring them no temperature-raising; on the other hand, the midwife toads of the south (Spain and Portugal) just as well exercise their characteristic brood-business as do those of the hypothetical starting-point, the west (of France and Belgium). Also, as I in the meantime learned, there lie not the slightest points of support which could make a giving-up of brood-care in the midwife toads of Westphalia — in which I once noticed such a deviation — appear frequent, or even increasing and regular. Naturally the possibility remains open that, besides warmth — as I had established for *Salamandra* (1907 *I. und II. Mitteilung*) — still other factors, e.g. moisture-raising, collaborate in the de-differentiation of the reproductive process.

Yet of these further experiments the following section shall report.

## III. Suppression of Brood-Care by Experimental Means.

### 1. Temperature Experiment.

The observation (1906 *Alytes-Hyla*) that, of thirty-five midwife toads brought on 18 April 1905 to Münster in Westphalia, on 21 April only two males loaded themselves with eggs, while these [eggs] were left lying by the remaining 12 males — this observation directed my attention even then to those factors which had occasioned the abandonment of the brood-care behavior, and to the cause of its reappearance. As material for these experiments I nevertheless did not use the Westphalian midwife toads, since these, without intended application of definite factors, showed just as scanty a brood-care behavior as in the case described here, where, on the contrary, the males loaded themselves immediately and surely; the isolation of those factors which were to be brought into action would on that account have become too uncertain. Rather, I used here quite different *Alytes*-material, which was already known to me as healthy from an earlier breeding-trial, namely the newly purchased *Alytes* which I had myself brought back home in 1903 from Appenzell (Switzerland); they had already at that time done me good service in the breeding-experiment, and what is more, they were caught by myself; thus the conditions of their place of origin were known to me; I knew, too, that brood-care takes place there normally and regularly (since the two midwife-toad pairs caught in spring 1905 had already three times — in spring 1903, in spring 1904, and in autumn 1904 — reproduced and thereby exhibited no deviation phenomena, but were furnished with normal brood-business, which corresponded as exactly as possible to that of free life).

At the time of the experiment's beginning (25 April 1905) these animals had not yet spawned, but the males had already for weeks let their glassy voices resound, and from the bodily girth of the females one could conclude that until the onset of this year's laying period no long wait remained. The animals were housed in 3 batches of 15 males and 15 females each (which were now very easily recognizable as such, whereas the distinction of the sexes by external characters during the remaining time of the year is a more difficult one, but with the help of the marks given by v. Bedriaga (p. 350) is entirely possible), and indeed in carefully and strictly uniformly arranged containers, so-called terraria, as I have described them in earlier works (1904, p. 176 ff., 1906 p. 54, 55). (On this point see also the literature-reference Przibram 1908/9, p. 249 ff.) — A fourth batch, the stock culture, which contains several hundred midwife toads and into which all newly acquired *Alytes*-material flows, was housed in the open.

The one terrarium remained standing at its previous place, where the animals had three times completed a normal reproductive period. The temperature of the room in question, unheated in winter, is a fluctuating one, but, being situated in shade, rises even in summer only seldom above ordinary room temperature (17 degrees C.), so that the room is then felt as cool; in winter the temperature often sinks down close to the freezing point, so that its inhabitants then fall into torpor — keep a winter sleep, which is only somewhat shorter and more often interrupted than in free life.

The second terrarium came into a room which receives artificial heat-supply summer and winter, day and night, and where the temperature fluctuates only between 25 and 30 degrees C.

The third terrarium — after a one-week acclimatization period in the aforementioned room — came into a room constantly heated to 37 to 40 degrees C.

The fourth culture came, in order to copy the normal conditions still more completely than is at all possible within a building, into an open-air terrarium (for the description see Kammerer 1907 I and II Communication, as well as Przibram 1908/9). This is at the same time the stock culture, the place of material accumulation, and the material source for all newly to be initiated experimental cultures.¹

On the results in Terrarium IV (garden) we need not hereafter particularly enter. Although in an open-air enclosure, on account of its extent and numerous hiding-opportunities, the monitoring is considerably more difficult to exercise and is namely bound up with a disproportionately greater expenditure of time, the findings move within the variation-range illustrated in the above table from Terrarium I. They are at all events very welcome as a revision [re-check], [confirming] that here as there entirely normal conditions actually subsist.

The same holds also of the following laying periods, those of autumn 1905, spring and autumn 1906, 1907 and 1908, spring 1909; and likewise the same holds of the descendants from those cultures which had in the meantime, by spring 1907 and earlier years, become sexually mature and to no small part had recently propagated themselves. At the same time one sees from this exposition that the midwife toads, at least under the conditions of captive life that approximate the aquatic ones, complete two laying periods in the year, of which the one falls in the spring (mostly in April), the other in the autumn (mostly September). In free life this is also the case in mild climates and during long summers, otherwise not; correspondingly, the actually rightly subsisting twofold yearly laying period would be indicated by various researchers who have followed the free life of *Alytes*, (e.g. Héron-Royer 1878), and denied by others (e.g. Dürigen). With those in the warm rooms, the yearly number of the same remains at two; only there sets in a gradual retrograde shifting [rückläufige Verschiebung] of them, so that finally the spring period is displaced into the late winter, the autumn period into the late summer. Even greater irregularities of the natural laying periods have, in the *Alytes*-breedings to date, not occurred, so that the periodicity is preserved longer and to a higher degree than for instance in the salamanders, where, in old captive-breedings, one is at no time of the year safe from suddenly occurring births and must therefore control much more sharply and uninterruptedly.

In Terrarium II (25—30 degrees) the laying activity began on the evening of 30 April 1905. It was at once to be seen that here there could be no talk of a proper loading with egg-strings on the part of the males. Two males made timid attempts at this: in them the spawn adhered also only for a while amounting to 3—4 hours, but finally slid down to the floor, as had already happened at once in the others. In the very earliest time of the laying period one found, on every side, stripped-off, tangled strings strewn about everywhere in the container, both inside and outside the water. Gradually, however, the spawn-masses concentrated onto the water-basin and were no longer balled up, but lay in the shape of unrolled strings, well recognizable as such, on the bottom of the basin.

The males did indeed, through the well-known movements of their hind extremities, render birth-assistance, but not in that degree which is most highly developed among all European frog-amphibians, as it is characterized in *Alytes*, but only in that less perfect degree such as is also to be encountered in other anurans, e.g. *Bufo* and *Pelobates*; the females therefore repeatedly proceeded to self-help, when they succeeded in fastening a little piece of spawn-string that had been pressed out of the cloaca to some object — most readily a plant-stalk or an edged stone — by pressing it down (sticking it on), so that they then, pulling at it in the opposite direction, drew the whole spawn-string out. In order to secure the initially difficult adhering to the object serving as supporting backing, they crept in a circle around it and wound it round with the whole string, which finally came to lie in well-ordered, at first close, then wider windings. We find in this *Alytes* abnormal procedure the purely mechanical explanation why many frog-amphibians, especially the Bufonids spawning in long strings, so gladly wind their eggs in clew-form around water-plants, brushwood lying in the water, stakes and the like; repeatedly there has been seen in this a fore-care for the spawn, which is hereby protected against being swept away to unfavorable places; but that act is already understandable from the endeavor of the maternal animal to free itself as quickly as possible and in a mechanically expedient manner from its burden.

We ask now after the grounds which induced the *Alytes*-pairs to refrain so promptly in high temperatures from all brood-care, and why this effect of warmth set in both in those midwife toads which in captivity had previously already repeatedly reproduced with maintenance of the normal brood-care, and also in those which had been freshly brought from free life into the higher captivity-temperature (Kammerer 1906 *Alytes-Hyla*, p. 69) — then we shall once again first see purely mechanical causes responsible for it. That here no direct influence of the instinct through the high temperature, in general no instinct-variation, lies present would be likely to follow already from its exceedingly swift effect: for midwife toads, which previously had demonstrably exercised brood-care in their reproductive business, ceased — at a laying period which began already four days after the transfer into the warmth, and which therefore must already have been preceded by the most varied periodic manifestations of the sexual passages and sexual instincts — suddenly to let any provision be bestowed upon their brood.

Now there was already, above, in the description of the normal reproductive-mode and in the recapitulation of the results of earlier works, attention given to letting the exposition bring out the important role which the condition of the gallertous egg-covering plays: its initial stickiness was emphasized, which in the normal land-eggs ceases through drying-in and becoming dry, in the experimental water-eggs through swelling-up, some time after the laying. During the drying-out process in the air, the stickiness remains preserved, under normal conditions, much longer than during the swelling-up process in the water, and that circumstance favors the initial adhering of the string to the thighs of the male, and the subsequent shrinking of the mass is here suited to make that initially only loose adhering really secure and durable.

These conditions are not merely inferred conjecturally from the transformations in the quality of the gallert-covering, but I have tested them experimentally on other anuran species which exercise no brood-care. The gallert-coverings of all anuran eggs are, before they swell up in the water or dry out in the air, sticky to a certain degree, though by far not to the degree as in *Alytes*. Nevertheless one can often see, on frogs and toads which in captivity, with a water-basin that is too small or lacking, have copulated outside the water, how parts of the spawn-mass remain clinging to their rump and hind legs for days on end. This I turned to my own advantage in such a way that I myself wound the fertilized egg-masses of several anuran species of both sexes around the hind thighs and, as far as practicable, prevented them from stripping them off. Least suited for this are the forms that spawn in clumps and all those that move forward in wide leaps, thus namely the genuine frogs (*Rana*), but on the other hand also the almost only creeping forms, such as the earth-toad (*Bufo vulgaris*), whose long strings indeed let themselves very comfortably wind up, but easily come to be lost when the hind legs, during the slow forward-movement, are stretched out behind to their whole length and often even for a time persist in such a position. Better suited then are the alternating-toads (*Bufo viridis*), which move forward in short leaps, in which the hind legs are indeed likewise stretched out, but at once again drawn up. The best object, however, for experiments with artificial brood-care à la *Alytes* is the garlic-toad (*Pelobates fuscus*), whose egg-strings, to be sure, on account of their thickness and shortness are not so well suited for winding-round as those of *Bufo*; but *Pelobates* moves itself — and that is the principal thing — so little, that a sliding-down of the string once taking up the desired position appears almost excluded. The garlic-toad, a species similar in many respects to the midwife toad through convergence, namely digs itself at once into the earth; it does this, to be sure, with the hind-extremities, namely with the outer metatarsal knob, but presses the upper thigh thereupon so closely against the rump that the eggs cannot fall off. Once buried, the animal often does not move at all for weeks together, if only the earth offers it sufficient moisture. Otherwise it wanders nightly to the water-basin. But all its movements take place so calmly, leaps and far-reaching creepings are so seldom, that the garlic-toad must be granted a downright ideal disposition for brood-care. It came in fact to the formation of burdens of developing eggs, similar to those normally borne by the male of the midwife toad. I intend to publish these experiments, should I be able to bring them to more complete, more clear-cut results, in a special treatise; for the present they shall only serve me as proof that I have correctly recognized the significance of the stickiness and its subsequent cessation.

If now the temperature is an above-normal one, then the drying-out process of the *Alytes*-eggs, in case these have been deposited on land, proceeds so rapidly that the stickiness, while the male is still occupied with drawing the egg-string out of the cloaca for the female, no longer possesses the desired degree. Still more quickly than the actual egg-enveloping gallert, that of the connecting-strands between egg and egg hardens: it cuts into the thigh-skin of the males, which are in the act of loading themselves with it, leaving behind deep stripes and bulges there. The winding-round of its thighs consequently causes the male, in high temperature, considerable difficulties, if not even pains, and leads to an abandonment of the business that appears unworkable.

In order to let this abandonment eventually become the starting point of an instinct-variation, a special (5th) terrarium was set up under the same temperature and moisture conditions, but without a water basin, and was stocked with a few midwife-toad pairs from the parent culture (open-air terrarium [Freilandterrarium]). For without withdrawal of the water basin there was, for the following reason, no counting on an instinct-variation resulting from it: namely, because toward the end of that same [period], and during all the later spawning-periods, all the spawn-strings were always deposited into that [basin]. Now, however, this was excluded in the special culture, where the toads had to content themselves — for the moistening of their skin — with the artificially dispensed dew, and — for the laying of their eggs — with the soil that had remained weakly moist from this. The special experiment was instructive, and not undertaken in vain, insofar as it permitted one to see a repeated recurrence of the spawning-process described above: each time, the high temperature brought about so rapid a setting of the jelly that its attachment to the thighs became a torment to the males concerned and was omitted by them. Meanwhile, of a progress of the experiment up to a stage further than this one there can be no question: the animals soon showed symptoms of illness, because they had to do without the baths to which they were accustomed; in particular, without baths they could not, at the moult, completely get rid of the epidermal layer to be cast off; the ulcerous wounds, so pernicious in many amphibian cultures, also appeared. There were two ways to keep the animals healthy, both of which were equivalent to a failure of the actual aim of the experiment, the attainment of an instinct-alteration: either the granting of greater moisture in the overall container, without a water basin — at most the keeping-wet of one piece of the terrarium floor; but then — especially since the animals always sought out the moistest part for laying — the jelly did not dry in so quickly that the males would not have succeeded beforehand in carrying the winding through to a good end. Or the granting of the water basin during the rest of the year, withdrawal only just before the onset of the spawning-period: then, however, once again no instinct-variation was at hand, as became sufficiently evident from the fact that the failed attempts of the males to force the egg-strings, which had become too hard, onto the thighs at any price renewed themselves undiminished every time.

We saw that, in the course of the spawning-period spent at high temperature, more and more egg-strings came to be deposited not on the land at all, but into the water basin. For this phenomenon too, and for its consequences consisting in the elimination of brood-care, one is not at a loss for a mechanical explanation: it is precisely the temperature itself, unusually high for the amphibians in question, that drives them to seek out the water far more often and to remain in it longer than their almost exclusively terrestrial mode of life otherwise prescribes for them. For it is indeed quite generally observable that animals which have to endure temperature-levels disagreeable to them seek cooling in the water. So extended a sojourn in the water further brings it about that in the basin — where now the whole company of rutting males and females finds itself assembled — the copulations and deliveries also take place; and that here, owing to the immediate swelling-up of the jelly coming into contact with the water, no adhesion at all comes about, and therefore not that so important first attachment around the males' thighs either — this was already emphasized in the previous section (II. "Recapitulation ..."). Even when a male loads the string onto himself, it nevertheless always slides down again; and even when, soon after the female's successful delivery, it leaves the water with the successfully wound string — where opportunity would be given for it to dry out afterward — the heat drives it back into the bath again too soon for the drying-process to be able to advance sufficiently.

All these attempted explanations claim validity in their full extent only for the first spawning-period in high temperature described above. Already during the second spawning-period, which played out in Terrarium II from 2–14 September 1905, still more with the third (18–25 March 1906), and in completed fashion with the following spawning-periods (29 VIII–5 IX 06, 10–21 III 07, 27 VIII–2 IX 07, 27 II–4 III 08, end of August 08, and 23–28 II 09), it became evident that the deviations of the spawning — which at first rested on a purely mechanical alteration — had now already become an immanent instinct-alteration, by virtue of which a spawning upon the land no longer occurred at all, but rather all the animals purposefully sought out the water basin for the spawning-act (no longer in order to cool themselves, since in the meantime they had become fully accustomed to the high temperature), where the act then proceeded quite uniformly, without any loading-attempt on the part of various males, entirely in the sense of the original anuran reproduction.

The spawning-mode of these Alytes-pairs thus copied, roughly, that of the closely related fire-bellied toads (Bombinator), and still more exactly that of the more distantly related toads (Bufo), because Bombinator has clump-shaped, but Bufo string-shaped spawn, the latter of which is readily wound around plants and stones — which is precisely what the discussed Alytes-pairs now regularly did.

Let it also be remarked at once here that, when one transferred a few pairs of these midwife-toads — with the instinct-alteration in the direction of non-brood-care firmly imprinted — back into lower normal temperatures, the next spawning-period nevertheless did not bring with it the re-entry of brood-care; rather, the acquired reproductive change continued through several spawning-periods even in the lower temperature, and only then became subject to fluctuations of such a nature that, at first, weak beginnings toward the original behavior showed themselves, which still led to no definitive result; that then individual males carried out the carrying-about of the egg-strings again up to the release of the brood; until finally all of them did this again. But at least four spawning-periods elapsed [before that]; indeed, a few of the youngest males — so far as the previous following-up of the later spawning-periods permits one to say — did not do it at all any more. In this whole process — the cessation of brood-care, first through mechanical influences, which then lead to the instinct-variation, finally to the persistence of the variation despite the cessation of the mechanically influencing factor — in this overall process one will be most vividly reminded of those regularities, those memory-functions of the protoplasm, which Semon (1908) has so thoroughly and convincingly presented in his "Mneme."

Before we concern ourselves with the fate of the eggs brought to deposition in Terrarium II in the manner described, we shall first include the results of Terrarium III (37–40 degrees C.). In the manner intended by me at its setting-up, this part of the temperature experiment did not succeed. Despite the week of acclimatization that had preceded the exposure to the highest temperature, the animals at first did not endure those high degrees of warmth, but rather, already on the day after the start of the experiment (that is, on 26 IV 05), a mortality began among them, by which the males were affected less than the females, which were bursting with eggs. If I did not wish to lose the entire stock of this experimental series, I had to bring them, three further days later (hence on 29 IV), back into the 25–30 degree room, in which Terrarium II also stands — the results of which we have just learned in detail. Up to then no spawning had come about, since the females were partly dead, partly limp and incapable of performing the muscle-contractions necessary for laying; and the males too seemed to have lost every mating-urge for the spawning-period of that time.

The employment of the experimental series in question — which, from 15 males and just as many females, had melted down to 11 males and 9 females — therefore took the following shape: with the females, care had to be taken that the spawn-masses present in their oviducts should somehow be emptied, if they were not now, after they had escaped the heat, still to perish from egg-binding [Legenot]; but with males of the same experimental series no delivery was possible, for their rut was definitively over. So I had 3 females delivered by males of the previous experimental series (from Terrarium II, at 25 to 30 degrees), which still showed themselves eager to mate and which solved the task assigned to them from 2–5 May 1905; the spawning-act proceeded in the water basin and without any indication of a brood-tending endeavor, and the three egg-strings thereby obtained therefore differed from those of the experimental series of Terrarium II only insofar as the mother-animals had been exposed for 4 days to a still higher temperature — a circumstance which, as could later be shown after the rearing of the young, was of no consequence.

With a further three females I induced an artificial abortion, which, with some practice, succeeds without difficulty through massage along the flanks and the ventral side (cf. the concluding paragraph of the second experiment on neoteny, p. 489), and I sought to fertilize these three egg-strings with the aid of the semen of a male of the same experimental series killed for the purpose. It failed, since the spermatozoa were evidently no longer capable of fertilization; under the microscope one could only establish that they did not move — the cessation of the rut was indeed evidently only a consequence of their dying-off. Thus the artificial abortion here had only the success that the females in question remained alive.

With the three females not yet discussed I had undertaken nothing further, but had left them with their unwilling males, in the weak hope that the latter might perhaps after all reconsider in good time and regain their procreation-urge. But this was not so: one female was able, in the manner already described — forcible pressing-out of a little end of spawn-string, attaching of the same to a stone, then pulling at the opposite end — to free itself; the two others nevertheless perished in the end after all from egg-binding [Legenot].

In any case, however, I had kept alive from this experimental series 10 males and 7 females, of which latter three had been delivered through artificial abortion, three through males of another experimental series, and one through self-liberation. With them the experiment was repeated a year later (spring of 1906), this time under more favorable auspices, since by then, through careful, quite gradual training, a complete acclimatization to 37 degrees and somewhat more had been attained. There were thus now no more deaths in the hottest room. The result was nevertheless the same negative one as before: **the animals simply did not proceed to reproduction**; this time the females were not even burdened with eggs — it seemed that no ovulation at all had taken place, so that even with the calling-in of other males, or by artificial means, it was useless to wish to obtain offspring here. In this culture, adapted to the heat but sterile, the experimental animals remained until the end of the winter of 1908; then I transferred them into the 25–30 degree room, for after the unused lapse of four spawning-periods (one each in the spring and autumn of 1906, spring and autumn of 1907) I could scarcely still hope that the heat-adaptation would advance up to the resumption of reproductive activity. But even in the 25–30 degree room, where the experimental animals of Terrarium II reproduce regularly, those of Terrarium III, in the three spawning-periods that have since elapsed, made no preparations to do so: the sojourn in the heat seems to have definitively paralyzed their procreative power. No better did it fare with experimental specimens freshly taken from the normal cultures and gradually and in perfect fashion accustomed to the heat: as soon as they had lived for some time in the hottest room, their copulation- and laying-drive was annihilated. As a result usable for the further breeding of an additional generation there accordingly remain, from the heat experiment (Terrarium III), only three egg-strings from females that had lived for 4 days at 37 to 40 degrees, and which were fertilized by males of the warmth experiment (Terrarium II).

Let us now turn our attention to **the eggs themselves**, which were laid in Terraria II and III, and to their development. The number of the eggs presented, in the first spawning-period — during which the cessation of brood-care had been enforced only through physical means — nothing extraordinary: it fluctuated between 20 and 49, and thus keeps itself within the limit between the minimum (18) and maximum (86) that have become known in the literature.

The constitution of the eggs too at first presented nothing special. In the course of their further development, however, phenomena then appeared in them which claim great interest. The reader will recall that during that first spawning-period — of which alone there is now to be talk — the eggs had not yet been laid without exception into the water basin, but to a good part still upon the land. The eggs were now in each case half left in that medium in which they had been laid — those deposited on the earth thus being further treated as "land-eggs," those deposited in the basin as "water-eggs"; the other half — the former being transferred into the water, the latter onto dry ground.

### a) Eggs laid on the land, further treated on the land.

In my work that appeared in 1906 (Alytes-Hyla), I had found that moisture and light, each factor by itself just as when combined with the other, act in an accelerating manner, and dryness and darkness likewise in a retarding manner, upon the development of the Alytes-embryos; but that the freshly hatched larvae are at one and the same developmental stage, irrespective of under which of those external factors the eggs had been kept. This was now confirmed also in all those eggs which I either transferred immediately after laying into a moderate temperature (17 degrees C.), or, while indeed leaving them in the high temperature of the experiment, also left lying on the moist ground of the laying-place. Here only the developmental tempo was a faster one, the stage reached up to hatching nevertheless the same. It was otherwise when, to the remaining in the warmth, a relative dryness joined itself.

I had at that time (1906) the temperature-factor, which in the present experiments to be reported by me today plays the chief role, not taken into consideration at all, at least not in immediate connection with the subject of my work of that time: the cultures had at that time all been exposed to the same, moderate temperature of about 18 degrees C.

But I had already spoken of preliminary experiments which, in the combination of the development-promoting factor "warmth" with the development-retarding factors "dryness and darkness," sounded favorable for the possibility of a retaining of the embryos in the egg up to advanced developmental stages, whereby an approximation to the direct, metamorphosis-less mode of development of Hylodes martinicensis, Hyla goeldii, Rana opisthodon, Pseudophryne vivipara appeared to be accomplished. An older statement, according to which the retaining of the Alytes-embryos in the egg had likewise already succeeded experimentally, derives from Jourdain, which statement I defended in 1906 (Alytes-Hyla) against objections of Héron-Royer (1878), who declared such an influencing to be impossible; but unfortunately Jourdain says neither by which factors, nor up to which stage, he had prevented the embryos from hatching.

As regards my own experiments, I brought these — which had originally been undertaken with arbitrary Alytes-spawn — only to clear results from the time when I had already kept the parent-animals in high temperatures and let them lay there. The land-eggs in question were further cultivated in the same room, that is, at 25 to 30 degrees C. — still higher degrees they did not tolerate — and indeed one half in the light, the other half in complete darkness. They were, moreover — for which the high temperature already provided, without any especially strong supply of moisture — kept as dry as is at all compatible with the moisture-requirements of an amphibian egg. They lay in glass dishes on sterile blotting-paper [Fließpapier] and were sprayed weakly with a fine atomizer once each morning and evening. As proof that the most indispensable minimum of moisture was thus actually offered them, let it be stressed that, in spite of this, most of the eggs dried up and only 61 of 155 eggs furnished the result which I shall describe at once.

Of these 61 eggs that remained capable of life, 48 belonged to the **culture in the light**; here the developmental conditions were after all still more favorable, and the whole duration of exposure could be a shorter one, since — as may be sent ahead at once — the hatching of the larvae here took place at no essentially more advanced stage than otherwise.

Different is the case for the eggs likewise kept dry and warm, but in darkness, of which I was able to keep only 13 alive up to the hatching of the larvae: the warmth made their embryonic development rush forward, while darkness and dryness hindered not so much the course of development as such, but rather the timely entry of the hatching-instinct of the larvae. So that no misunderstanding may arise, let it be expressly emphasized: as control experiments constantly prove, darkness and dryness naturally also hinder the developmental course as such — for otherwise, if free play had been granted to the temperature-factor, the stage reached in the same time would necessarily have been a far more advanced one. The chief hindrance, however, concerns after all only the moment of leaving the egg-membrane: the phasogenous engram in question was, to speak with Semon (1908), ecphorized very belatedly.

In the 8th week after the laying (date of laying: 30. IV. 05, in the evening) the egg-membrane had become so stretched by the embryo growing within it that one could conveniently perceive the latter through it — which is otherwise difficult on account of the brownish-yellow coloration of the shrunken jelly; Héron-Royer (1883 *Embryonnaires*) obtained this view only by removing the jelly-membrane. Now, however, the latter was not merely translucent, but downright transparent, and whereas the egg, as noted above, ordinarily attains only 5 mm length at 3½ mm breadth (Fig. 2), here its maximal extent amounted to more than double, namely 11 mm length at 9 mm breadth (Fig. 9 and 9*a*). Already before, finally — on the 66th–70th day, that is on the 5th–9th of July 1905 — the hatching took place, I therefore knew that very large larvae with fully differentiated hind-limbs would emerge, which were at approximately the same stage up to which I was able to bring the larvae in my previous work (1906 *Alytes-Hyla*, S. 87 ff., Fig. 5 of Plate V) on moist soil outside the water. Only, with the former "land-larvae" (Fig. 15) the total body-size was a more considerable one, the development of the hind-limbs on the other hand a less advanced one (51 mm total length, 8 mm hind-limb length), than with the present "embryos in land-eggs" (Fig. 10), which measured 31 mm from snout to tail-tip but possessed hind-limbs which, from the attachment of the thigh to the tip of the longest toe, measured 10½ mm. The developmental duration up to the attainment of the said stage is, in both experiments, on the average the same.

From Peters' data one can gather that ready-to-hatch *Hylodes* eggs exhibit a diameter of 7.5 mm. The *Alytes* eggs artificially prevented from hatching by me are therefore still considerably larger than the eggs of the Antilles-frog, which have become so well-known on account of their size. In part this may arise from the fact that the specific size of full-grown *Alytes*-females, at 50 mm, is a somewhat more sizeable one than that of *Hylodes* at 40 mm; in part probably also from the fact that, in my experimental results, we do not yet have before us, in the *Alytes*-egg, a constitutive, completed adaptation, in the course of whose completion secondarily — see the following section IV: "Inheritance" — in turn a diminution of the eggs and a reduction of their now excessively forced growth can take place.

Kathariner (S. 297, 298) had retained the embryos of the grass-frog (*Rana temporaria*), which normally leave the egg before they have gills, so long in the egg-membrane — by keeping them in moving water whirled around by means of an air-current — until they had developed not only outer gills, but already completely separated sucking-cups, a mouth opened into the gut, and a long oar-tail. In accordance with this experiment I undertook to set my *Alytes* eggs too into constant movement, in the hope of being able to heighten still further the result mentioned earlier. The land-eggs selected for this purpose I exposed partly to a strong air-current obtained from the central air-supply-line of our institution, whereby the eggs, after they had assumed a stable position, did not themselves come into movement, but yet were surrounded all around by moving medium; partly I let them roll about in a glass dish clamped onto a clockwork-klinostat; partly, finally, I centrifuged them weakly and during the day at one-hour intervals, each of five minutes' length. Only in both the latter cases, namely where the eggs themselves were in movement, was a result in agreement with Kathariner's observed, which, however, with respect to distinctness, could neither vie with the result obtained by warmth, dryness and darkness, nor — in combination with all these, or with a part of these three factors — was able to heighten the total-result: egg and embryo have evidently, upon reaching the aforementioned stage bearing vigorous hind-limbs, arrived at the high-point of their present capacity; if the hatching, when the stage is reached, still does not take place, then — as it naturally also had to experience more often — death sets in. Outside the combination with the other factors, with isolated application of the movement-factor — that is to say with moist and bright keeping of the eggs centrifuged hourly and rotating in the klinostat, in a space of ordinary room-temperature (17 degrees C.) — the result was about equal to that which was observed with bright keeping of dry and warmly accommodated eggs: the embryos remained in the egg until they had become quite large (20–25 mm total-length), but hatched out before a trace of the hind-limbs came into view.

Although this dwarf-growth already makes itself noticeable in the juvenile stages, it is yet most pronounced with the full-grown toads — the more pronounced, the more energetically and the more by way of several combined factors the retention in the egg was effected. The size of the grown toads stands in inverse proportionality to the length of the time which the embryos spent in the egg, or, otherwise expressed, in inverse relation to the developmental height of the stage which they had reached in the egg-membrane. The larvae that crawled out of land-eggs at the right time, but which are reared further outside the water, likewise yield dwarfish full-toads. These facts will, in the course of our exposition, in later treatises, gain still further in significance, in that they shape themselves into a general law, which reads as follows: The longer an animal is, during its development, surrounded by liquid medium, the more considerable a body-size does it attain; conversely it dwarfs itself, when an abnormally long stretch of its development runs in a dry medium. The more abundant possibility of water-uptake on the part of the cells, which favors their division-tempo and hence the tissue-growth, appears to be the cause underlying that experience. It also lets itself already be documented in fairly rich measure from the literature, which we will catch up on at a later opportunity.

With regard to my experiment published in 1906 (*Alytes*–*Hyla*), "Rearing of the *Alytes* larvae outside the water, on damp soil" (p. 85 ff.), it seemed desirable to repeat that experiment on those larvae which had remained in the egg until the stage with fully differentiated hindlimbs, in order thereby to drive their independence from water, if possible, still further. At that time I had such larvae at my disposal only from ordinary land-eggs, from which they crawl out with inner gills but footless, and I had been able to keep them, in a damp earthen hollow, up to the appearance of small hindlimbs and up to the maximum duration of 29 days (Fig. 15); a further four weeks afterward, in such larvae, the metamorphosis was already completed amid manifestations of a monstrous forward-drive in development. But now I had, as a just-hatched germling, approximately the same stage which at that time represented the outermost limit of development outside the water. As regards the technical arrangement of this very difficultly succeeding experiment, I must refer to the original of the repeatedly cited earlier work; here I confine myself to mentioning and establishing the durability of such larvae — when fed with wafers and frothy egg-white, in a basin filled with hollow-kneaded clay-soil — during a further 16 days (as a maximum), and to stating that the phenomena of precocity already enumerated once more in Chapter II ("Recapitulation . . .") of the present work showed themselves in heightened measure in the new land-larvae (Fig. 16), this however with the exception of the appearance of the forelimbs, which here, as a rule, broke through before the casting-off of the horny jaws and the appearance of the broadly split frog-mouth, and not only afterward. Unfortunately every attempt to bring the land-larvae to metamorphosis outside the water as well came to nothing; at any rate, when the surviving experimental animals were placed into the water after the expiry of those 16 days, it took only a further 12–18 days, so that the dependence of the frog-amphibian occupying us upon stay in water really does appear already depressed to a minimum. With their 16–17 mm the freshly metamorphosed (Fig. 17), with their 28–30 mm the sexually mature toads of this experiment are to be called dwarfs to a still higher degree than those of previous land-experiments.

### b) Eggs laid on land, further-treated in water.

If the eggs are transferred into the water-basin immediately after their laying on land, scarcely any difference whatever is to be perceived in their developmental manifestations as compared with those which were laid in the water-basin and left there. And if the eggs, before they are transferred into the water, have lain exposed to the air for a shorter or longer time, then their development is a more or less complicated resultant between pure land-eggs and pure water-eggs. Into the analysis of this we do not wish to enter more closely; first, because the experimental variations undertaken in this direction (superposition of land- and water-stay of the eggs) are, in number and in frequency of execution of each individual variation, too few for their results to appear clearly enough and to be used for general interpretation; then also, because these results do, after all, represent only transitions to those of pure land- and water-eggs, show in comparison with these two only differences of degree, not of essence, and consequently may fittingly be dispensed with for the rounding-off of the picture in the exposition. ### d) the eggs laid in water and further-treated in water.

It was already said that the rutting midwife-toads, already in the late days of their first, brood-care-free spawning-period spent at high temperature, sought out the water-basin more and more in order to rid themselves of the egg-strings, and that in the spawning-periods following thereupon this occurred exclusively, whereby the reproductive habits of the other European frog-amphibians appeared to have been established. With this deviating behavior of the parent animals there went hand in hand a corresponding one in the constitution of the eggs themselves and of their development. Whereas the normal *Alytes*-(land-)egg is distinguished by a considerable size, which in turn is conditioned by a special richness in yolk (Fig. 1a), and whereas the eggs laid into the water for the first time naturally as yet show no difference in this respect, the size of the eggs deposited voluntarily — in consequence of the alteration of instinct — into the water-basin during later spawning-periods decidedly diminishes, and it is the reduced quantity of yolk that brings this about. In order to recognize the decrease in size, one may of course measure only the actual spawn-grain; for since the jelly-envelope of the water-eggs swells up quite as mightily (Fig. 18a) as in the eggs of other anurans spawning in water, but in the land-eggs shrinks and nestles closely to the egg-surface, the water-eggs together with their jelly appear larger than the land-eggs, although without the jelly they possess a substantially smaller volume.

While the size and richness in yolk of the eggs decrease, their number and resistance-capacity increase in the medium new to them (the water). Whereas otherwise the female of *Alytes* lays 18–36 eggs, the quantity of eggs laid during the later, brood-care-free spawning-periods always moves at the uppermost limits of this range of variation and above it; for example, one string was deposited with 90, one with 99, the longest with 115 eggs. The general rule — the higher the differentiation of the begetting-process and the richer in yolk each individual egg, the fewer the eggs detaching themselves at each ovulation from the ovary and entering the oviduct — finds itself, then, confirmed here too in its inversion. It is, furthermore, to be repeated that the *Alytes*-eggs, frail as they at first show themselves in the water — so that in some strings none at all, in others about 3–5% of the eggs reach development — get on quite well there in the course of further water-spawning-periods; in the end scarcely more fertilized eggs perish in each individual string than in spawn-clumps or -strings of any arbitrary other frog-amphibian species under natural conditions.

It is no longer anything new to us that the embryos from water-eggs (Fig. 19) find the way into the open sooner than those from land-eggs, because, namely, the envelopes in the water are subject to a maceration-process which makes it possible for the larvae — without application of the gnawing-activity of their horny jaws, which at this time are not yet developed at all, but merely through strong curving of the body and snapping back into a more stretched position — to tear apart the obstacle. The from the outset much smaller, hence more quickly absorbed yolk-sac probably also imparts to the larvae earlier the mastery over their movements, and, through the hunger that sets in, the impulse to those movements. At this stage (Figs. 20 and 20a) they still bear their only, long external little gill-tree (Fig. 20b), which — although it stands in use only briefly and after a couple of days already makes room for the inner gills — undergoes an adaptation from embryonic to water-respiration, namely in the form of a shortening through partial resorption and of a switching-off of capillaries (diminution of the respiring surface), of a thickening of the epithelium and an increase of the pigment (raising of the resistance-capacity against mechanical insults).

In those larvae prematurely escaped from water-eggs which owe their origin to the first spawning-period without brood-care (Fig. 20), the shortening of the gills proceeded, besides through resorption, also through crumbling-off of the tips of the gill-bodies and gill-threads; in those from later spawning-periods (Fig. 21) only through pure resorption: thus here too, down into the finest ascertainable details, a perfecting of the aquatic adaptation, step by step with the advancing consolidation of the instinct-variation!

Attempts to hold back the embryos in the water-eggs too until late stages may be adduced for the sake of completeness, although they, not uninteresting in and of themselves, yet scarcely come into consideration for the main question of the present work. They yielded a positive result insofar as, with the help of Kathariner's method, it succeeded in fettering the larvae in the water-eggs up to that stage up to which they otherwise remain only in land-eggs: namely, by keeping them constantly hovering back and forth by means of a current of air driven into the water, and thus withdrawing them from the directing influence of gravity. One might think that here the unavoidable contact with the large air-bubbles sent through the water had brought about an approximation to the conditions of the land-(air-)eggs; however, also through rolling about in a glass dish (here, of course, completely filled with water) in the clockwork clinostat, as well as through hourly repeated, each time 5-minute-long weak centrifuging, that result was attained somewhat less completely; in what degrees, the following table (pp. 480, 481) gives information.

Through light and dark there was likewise only a little to be effected here, where the factor "moisture" is not variable. For whereas darkness in essence forms only a retarding moment for the act of hatching and only incidentally also retards the course of development as such, this latter undergoes a mighty acceleration through the high temperature. But the larva nevertheless hatches at no later stage than at that one with inner gills, which embryos in land-eggs always, in quietly lying water-eggs otherwise never, reach. Through combination of water-movement (air-current, clinostat-rotation, and centrifugation) too, no more was attained — a proof of the irresistibly dissolving influence of the water. There was, however, attained with the help of this combination yet one deviation in the mechanism of the becoming-free of the larvae: water-movement and darkness delay the release of the hatching-instinct, so that the larvae, instead of practicing as otherwise their trunk-bending and -stretching for the purpose of bursting the softened envelope, remain quite still. But since the envelopes in the water completely disintegrate within a short time, the larva must become free even if it does nothing toward it; and this wholly passive becoming-free — as it otherwise takes place most pronouncedly in *Bufo* and *Pelobates* — constitutes precisely the deviation attained and at the same time the insurmountable obstacle to keeping yet later stages surrounded by the envelopes. ### 2. Neoteny experiment.

Strictly taken, the neoteny experiment has little to do with the "suppression of brood-care by experimental means": namely, only in so far as the eggs employed for it were taken away from the male and forced to develop without brood-care, hence not in the sense of an instinct-variation impressed upon the male. It was, to be sure, from the outset not to be excluded that such an instinct-variation would nevertheless be achieved indirectly through the deviant course of development, which however did not come about. Since the neoteny experiment attaches itself organically to the discussion of the developmental duration of the larvae stemming from water-eggs, let it be inserted into our presentation at this point.

The developmental shortening of the *Alytes*-larvae provided with external gills and stemming from water-eggs sets in, curiously enough, only then when the larvae really actively free themselves out of the egg and when the envelope is in a state of maceration which permits them this, without, however, the envelope already disintegrating of itself. If one brings about the liberation before the entry of the motor reactions of the creeping-out instinct by an operative intervention, if one therefore cuts the larvae out of the egg-envelope — whereby one probably always takes out *earlier stages*, even though in their external characters they seem to occupy one and the same step as the spontaneously hatched ones — then one obtains an admittedly only small percentage of larvae which remain capable of life after that act of violence; on these larvae, however, one now perceives not, say, a developmental acceleration, but on the contrary always the phenomenon of *partial neoteny*, that is, they persist far beyond the to-be-expected time of transformation in the larval condition (Fig. 27). Through the operating-out already from normal land-eggs (only for such had I published it in 1906 [*Alytes-Hyla*]), still more from water-eggs, where it succeeds altogether more easily and a somewhat larger percentage of larvae so treated remains alive, one thus gains a larval material which is from the outset predisposed for that phylogenetically so interesting phenomenon, precisely neoteny. If we now, for good measure, apply together still all those factors which I already in 1906 had ascertained to be favourable for the prolongation of the larval life, namely: *darkness, low temperature (10–12 degrees C.), richness in air, great quantity and repose of the water, intensive supply of nourishment* when the normal term of metamorphosis approaches, after preceding strictly regular but somewhat scanty nourishment — then one obtains giant-larvae which live several years in the water without preparing themselves for metamorphosis. With the application of the last-named factor, the heightening of the nourishment, one must take care not to miss the right stage, because afterwards, as *Looss* has ascertained, there follows a stage at which the gut cannot function and at which, on account of the development of the bony jaw-arches, which put the movement-muscles of the horny larval jaws out of action, neither the gnawing at firm objects nor the seizing of the prey of the grown frog is possible; this stage marks itself externally roughly by the moment of the breaking-through of the fore-legs. The heightened supply of nourishment, and indeed a mightily heightened supply of nourishment, has therefore best to set in just when faint protrusions begin to show themselves at the place where the fore-legs are to break through. If this moment has not passed by unused, then in neotenically disposed larvae *no interruption* of the food-intake sets in, but rather, as soon as the bony jaw-arches have developed and, in place of the larval narrow horny jaws, the wide frog-mouth gapes, there begins abruptly, without transitional period, without fasting-time, the manner of nourishment of the finished frog, notwithstanding that the oar-tail persists in over-normal length and breadth. The neotenic giant-larva then nourishes itself from the prey of living quarry: it eats insects and worms living in the water or such as one has thrown into the water, seizes them in a quick thrust with its jaws, and helps larger morsels along into the gullet partly by bracing them against hard objects — the glass wall of the container, stones — partly by stuffing them in afterwards with the fore-legs.

In my work of 1906 (*Alytes-Hyla*) I had described the developmental course of a larva (pp. 78ff.) and depicted it at the high point of its neotenic development (there Pl. V Fig. 3, here Pl. XVII Fig. 27), which larva, merely in consequence of being operated out of the egg, without purposeful application of further factors, had lived 4 years and 2 months as such and had only then transformed itself into a full toad. Yet there had beforehand been no sign of sexual maturity noticed on it, so that this case was still to be ranged with partial neoteny. Since then, however, it has succeeded for me — again only in a single case, which however, on account of its positive results, is almost as valuable as a possibly far more numerous material of such — through the combined application of all the just-mentioned factors which stand at command for the purpose of postponing the metamorphosis, to achieve in an *Alytes*-larva total neoteny in the sense that sexual maturity was attained even before the metamorphosis (Pl. XVII Fig. 28). That ultimately the metamorphosis did nevertheless still set in does indeed somewhat impair the unambiguousness of the case; but, in accordance with the definition, one must nonetheless, on account of the attainment of sexual maturity in the larval condition, designate it as total neoteny, and it represents the first unobjectionably known case of total neoteny in a frog-amphibian. Although a statement is to be found according to which an "almost grown" *Rana temporaria* with tail is said to have been found, that statement (*Schläpfer*) is yet too incomplete to be able to gather from it whether the size of the grass-frog in question was really of such a kind that sexual maturity may be presupposed.

As regards my case, the sexual maturity is not only inferred from the size, but proved by the (even though only artificially brought about) act of reproduction, and the progeny gained in the manner now to be detailed more closely was of the greatest importance in regard to the inheritance-problem.

The parents of the tadpole that afterwards became totally neotenic still stemmed from the oldest *Alytes*-material that had ever at all been at my disposal: from my first sojourn in Appenzell, where I had caught them in the summer of 1894 at Weißbad. It is not superfluous to remark this, since the most strongly neotenic specimen described in my previous *Alytes*-work (1906 *Alytes-Hyla*) was a descendant of Freiburg (Breisgau) midwife-toads: the difference of the locality is at all events desirable, for otherwise it might seem as if precisely the animals of the one locality were already by nature disposed for neoteny; as if it were accordingly a matter of no character first to be acquired and impressed upon them by the experimental conditions, but rather of a germ-variation.

The Appenzell midwife-toads had already several times reproduced in a completely normal way. On 15 March 1902 this had again come to pass, and I took four males which in the preceding night had loaded themselves with egg-strings, relieved them of their burden, and let them develop on without brood-care, the one half in the normal medium (hence outside water, in moist surroundings), the other half in the water. In order to procure for myself a comfortable insight into the developmental processes and embryonal characters, I removed from all these eggs the gelatinous envelope, as *Héron-Royer* too had done for this purpose with the eggs of *Alytes*, so that these were thus now enclosed only by a single, very transparent envelope, the egg-membrane; thus there could not easily escape me that stage which, in earlier experiments of the kind, had proved itself to me the most favourable for the operation. Externally it resembles, as I have already said, that stage at which the larvae from water-eggs are wont already of themselves to hatch out: the external gills stand at the height of their unfolding, the tail is already of stately length and provided with a narrow fin-seam; the mouth is opened into the gut but bears as yet no horny jaws; the yolk-sac is still large, so that the larvae, despite their oar-tail, are immobile and lie motionless on the bottom; the total body-length amounts to 12 to 13 mm.

This stage was reached between 28 March and 3 April 1902 in all land- and water-eggs; the operating-out was therefore undertaken within the indicated days: it yielded with the water-eggs far better results than with the land-eggs, and although also from the latter a few larvae remained alive and without exception became partially neotenic, our further data-based determination concerns nevertheless only the larvae taken from water-eggs. From 7–13 IV 02 the external gills here disappeared again; from 8–15 IX the hind-legs were, with differentiation of upper and lower leg, foot and phalanges, fully developed, the whole animals around the same time 50–55 mm long. Up to here the delay over against the norm is not even so striking; but now the stage with the hind-legs persists in all specimens until the beginning of May 1904, but in that specimen, however, of which there is predominantly to be talk, until 12 May 1906! With the others, namely, from 5–9 May 04 the fore-legs broke through, whereupon the metamorphosis (13–23 VII 04) did not let itself be long awaited; they had their larval development concluded with a total length of 85–88 mm (measured before the beginning of the tail-resorption). From here on, then, we are concerned only with the developmental course of the individual that had remained over as a two-legged larva at that term. It receives, on 12 May 1906, its fore-legs and has by then grown to 119 mm in length; around 20 July 1906 it measures 125 mm, loses the tadpole-beak and receives in its place the widely cleft mouth of the full toad (Fig. 28). A further longitudinal growth no longer shows itself now, but as yet also no resorption of the enormous oar-tail. For this, however, the belly of the animal swelled up mightily, which in such an extension could only be interpreted as a sign of rut: ripe ovarian eggs must have entered the oviduct and here urgently awaited being pressed out by a midwife-toad male. An equivalent male, that is, a likewise neotenic one, was unfortunately not at my disposal; thus the purity of the experimental conditions could not be wholly preserved, and I therefore endeavoured to bring a male full toad to copulate with the larval female. On 10 September 06 I accordingly placed the aquarium in which the neotenic larva lived into a larger terrarium, which I laid with earth and turf up to the upper edge of the completely filled aquarium, and put into it at first a few Appenzell males caught in 1903, which had already been employed with success in the experiment of bringing about an abandonment of brood-care by means of high temperature, and in which the brood-care instinct had indeed quite gone missing, for which they had exchanged the instinct of seeking out the water at pairing-time and there carrying out the copulation. That was indeed also the advantage which I expected precisely from these males, for with another male I should have had no prospect at all that it would seek out the larva, which was bound to the water, and embrace it in the water. For this advantage I would have taken into the bargain, if need be, two disadvantages connected with it: the one, smaller one, that the males came from the same locality as the neotenic female, which again would have brought nearer the objection of the possible existence of a local germ-variation; the other, greater one, that the purity of the experiment should suffer still more if I bring in animals which have already been influenced by differently constituted factors in a heterogeneous direction. Only it did not come so far at all, for the males, although they bathed regularly in the aquarium, did not seek out the female larva. I tried it with other normal males, which had not laid aside their brood-care instinct; only these did not even seek out the water-basin. Forced thereto by drought, it still came to no copulation, although I had of course in all cases selected pronouncedly rutting males. The cause of the failure lay in any case not in the fact that the males shied away from the unaccustomed larval form of the female; for, as experience shows, it is at least to the tailed amphibians a matter of complete indifference whether they have to do with a sexually mature larva or a full newt, provided only that the rut-marks stand out strongly. To be sure, here both the developed and the larval forms bear a tail, whereas there, in the frog-amphibians, only the latter [do]. But one will be allowed to ascribe to the anurans in this respect all the less sensitivity, since the madly rutting males at pairing-time, without further ado, clamp themselves fast even on fishes, indeed on lifeless objects. Rather it probably became the insurmountable obstacle that the male, in order to embrace that neotenic female, would have had to dive down some 20 cm deep in the aquarium. I lowered the water-level as far as it went, without endangering the persistence of the neoteny in the female and bringing about an undesired metamorphosis, by introducing a layer of gravel — all in vain. I finally tried yet a last thing: males of the Appenzell series of 1903, which were accustomed to copulating in the water, though only at a depth of at most 5 cm, to which measure it could here for the conceived reason not be lowered — such males, then, I let mount their own females, that is, such of the same experimental series, and then detached them, before the emergence of the eggs and the discharge of semen had taken place: they were then so lustful that they without further ado clasped the fingers of my hand and carried out with their hind legs the rubbing copulatory movements. In this state I dipped them under, brought them to the neotenic larva and sought to fasten them to it. At first they also seemed to want to clasp it, but as soon as the larva, in a sudden swimming-thrust, with vigorous beating of its oar-tail, sought out the deepest places of the aquarium, this was too much for its rider, and it let go.

In consideration of the failure of a natural copulation, I pressed — when on 19 September the body-girth of the neotenic female had assumed an alarming size — by means of suitable, stroking hand-movements (similar to the way one proceeds in the stripping of fish-roe, a manipulation which, with some practice, is quite easily to be carried out also on gravid anuran females) the ripe egg-mass out and inseminated it with the sperm of a south-French male in full rut: I obtained in this way 18 fertilized eggs, which were treated as water-eggs and developed on quite well. About their further fate the fourth main part of the present work, "Inheritance of the Reproductive Change," shall report.

From the day of the forcible delivery onward, and possibly as a consequence of this rough disturbance, there nevertheless appeared in the larva — whose total neoteny had been demonstrated by its possession of mature, fertilization-capable and development-capable eggs — the preliminaries of an eventual transformation into the full toad. On 12 October 1906 it left the water, contrary to expectation, even though its tail still possessed a considerable length. The spiraculum was still open for another week, but on 20 October it was closed. The tail was now resorbed with great rapidity and by 1 December was perceptible only as a short, conical stump. If one takes this point in time as the end of larval development, then the animal was a larva for 4 years and 8 months, reckoned from the day of the (artificially induced) leaving of the egg-membrane — four months longer than the neotenic larva presented in 1906, which, however, was by far more greatly surpassed by the one now described with respect to body size and other characters of a more strongly pronounced neoteny than the relatively small time difference of 4 months would lead one to suppose. — Unfortunately it was never again possible to obtain offspring from this animal, which had been neotenic, even after its transformation.

As one of the controls for the neoteny experiment, and as a test of whether neoteny might not also be induced thereby, artificial insemination of *Alytes* eggs was also carried out. These were taken from the oviduct of the female in question, the sperm from the vesicula seminalis of a male. The male must be killed for this purpose, the female not, if the eggs can be stripped out by abdominal and lumbar massage. The eggs, however, developed — depending on the medium — exactly as water-eggs and land-eggs otherwise do, and likewise the larvae that arose from them, thus without neoteny.

## 3. Moisture experiment.

In describing the temperature experiment, care was taken to show that in the first-time suppression of the brood-business, purely physical factors are decisive: warmth drives *Alytes* to seek cooling in the water; here, therefore, the copulations take place, and here the spawn-strings brought out in the process cannot adhere to the thighs of the males, because their gelatinous envelope soon swells up and loses its stickiness.

It now lay near at hand to bring about this effect more simply, without first having to make use of elevated temperature as intermediary. Through isolation of the factor "moisture" it had to succeed equally well in preventing the adhesion of the egg-strings. I proceeded here in a twofold way: first, by keeping the pairs entirely in the water from the outset; second, by giving them in their container a more spacious water-basin than usual, alongside it admittedly also a small land-section, the floor of which, however, was kept constantly very wet by strong spraying several times daily. As the experimental temperature, what is understood here throughout is a fluctuating one — low in winter, so that uninterrupted hibernation set in, in summer scarcely ever exceeding 17 degrees C., as already stated in the normal cultures of the temperature experiment. These normal breedings are also to be taken as controls of the moisture experiment.

### a) Keeping entirely in the water.

The technical basis of this experiment is quite similar to that in the experiment "Hatching of the *Alytes* eggs with brood-care by the father in the water" of my 1906 work (*Alytes-Hyla*, pp. 58–60): I placed the midwife toads in an aquarium that was filled with water to a height of 2–3 cm over a layer of clean-washed sand. A deeper water-level is impossible, since the animals, unaccustomed to sustained swimming and diving, would drown in it; a deep water-level with only a quite small spot above the surface, say on a piece of floating cork-bark, is likewise impossible, since in this case the animals prefer to keep the island occupied for weeks on end, pressed tightly together or crouching on top of one another, rather than venture into the water. In shallow water, however, where they are nowhere forced to swim but everywhere find firm ground and can therefore always conveniently draw breath, they endured their fate with calm and perseverance, especially when I constructed dark caves out of flat stones.

But they simply did not proceed to reproduction. Even females in advanced stages of egg-laying ripeness found no male that would have been ready to drive out and fertilize their egg-burden, so that they either expelled it spontaneously — in which case the spawn, being unfertilized, naturally had to perish — or else they themselves succumbed to egg-binding [Legenot].

In three different ways I was nonetheless able to expose development-capable eggs to the medium of this experiment: 1) By transferring into the aquarium only after the males — which in the normal cultures had already become loaded with eggs and had thereby begun to exercise brood-care — had done so. There is then merely a repetition of the negatively ending experiment "Hatching of the *Alytes* eggs with brood-care by the father in the water" from my 1906 work (*Alytes-Hyla*): the water, by which the egg-clumps are constantly washed around, does not let any drying of their coating come about; this therefore remains soft and elastic, so that all the males, already after 1 to 2 days, lose the egg-packets without any action on their part. — 2) By taking the spawn-clumps away from those males at the very first beginning of their brood-care and bringing them loose into the aquarium: there is then merely a repetition of the experiment "Hatching of the *Alytes* eggs without brood-care by the father in the water" of my 1906 work (*Alytes-Hyla*), into which experiment the previous one had in any case already passed over, owing to the non-sticking of the submerged spawn-strings to the thighs of the males. — 3) By transferring pairs that had begun to copulate in the normal or warmth cultures into the shallow aquarium water. It then happened a few times, especially with pairs from warmth cultures — where they were anyhow already accustomed to copulating in the water — that despite that relocation they did not release each other but brought out and fertilized their eggs, and that the egg-strings, of course, owing to the action of the water, did not remain on the thighs, even when males that had previously belonged to normal cultures made weak attempts to wind them on. Usually, however, they too refrained from the outset, since — evidently sobered immediately after the discharge of sperm — they first became conscious of the unaccustomed surroundings and then showed only the endeavour to climb out of the water without regard for the offspring. Males that had previously belonged to warmth cultures naturally, by virtue of the instinct-variation immanent in them, refrained from the outset from any attempt to load on the eggs.

It is clear that all these experiments could not lead to any instinct-variation produced by the factor "moisture" **alone** — at least certainly not in the first generation of the experimental animals — because spontaneous reproduction in the aquarium basin remained entirely excluded everywhere. Naturally nothing is altered in this if the eggs obtained in the water culture by the said artifices were not throughout treated further as water-eggs but in part also as land-eggs. If I describe all the experimental series in this, as in the preceding main experiments, so circumstantially despite their failure, this is done in the belief that it is not unimportant also to learn to survey and to judge the detours which mediately lead over to the choice of the right paths, and which are at any rate suited to allow a renewed erring in the same direction to be avoided when the experiments are repeated.

### b) Keeping in wet surroundings.

*Alytes* in general loves dryness more than moisture. Like every amphibian, it is true, frequent moistening of the skin for the purpose of maintaining its respiratory function is indispensable to it, but for this purpose it contents itself with quite transitory daily baths in dew or puddles, whereas during the remaining time of day it seeks out dry localities. In captivity, too — even in the warmth experiment first set up here — it is only conducive to its well-being if the interior arrangement of the terrarium, that is, if earth, moss and stones, are dust-dry — provided only that there always remains an opportunity to seek out the basin at dusk or, in greater heat, also during the day. This is very much in contrast to the earth-salamanders, for which one must always secure a quite ample measure of air- and ground-moisture, especially if they are to endure higher degrees of temperature; therefore the role of the temperature factor in the experimentally produced de-differentiation of the procreative course is a much purer and more independent one in the case of *Alytes* than in the case of *Salamandra*.

*Alytes*, conversely, first requires a certain training before it can permanently endure great wetness without taking harm. In the experiment now to be discussed, the floor-filling of the terrarium land-part — consisting essentially of stones, earth and moss-turf, which remained over from the water-basin here chosen especially large — was kept continuously very wet; the ground-moisture of course communicates itself to the point of saturation also to the air, all the more so as the ventilation flaps here were kept closed and were opened daily only briefly, during the tending manipulations (feeding, eventual cleaning operations). Such a culture, stocked with the dryness-loving *Alytes*, was at first associated with distressing loss of material, and in a finally well-acclimatized stock the same difficulties with regard to reproduction seemed to make themselves felt as in the water culture: the animals did not become sexually excited [brünftig].

Yet at least by two better means than those that might be applied in keeping entirely in the water, offspring could at once be obtained in the wet culture: 1) By transferring pairs that in normal cultures had begun to show signs of sexual heat [Brunft]. These signs did not regress again in the wet terrarium but increased to the desired climax. — 2) An even better approximation to the success of the breeding experiment in question was achieved when it happened to bring *Alytes* pairs from this same culture into heat by transferring them, a few weeks before the expected onset of the rutting period, into another, normally arranged terrarium; then, after the females there had documented their laying-ripeness through increasing body-girth, and the males their readiness to copulate through frequent uttering of their call-cry, transferring them back into the wet terrarium, where they engaged in the business of reproduction. —

The process took place as follows: although only very little dry land was at the toads' disposal, it never occurred to them to seek out the basin; at most, an animal in the urge of movement or during the impetuous motor copulation-reactions might involuntarily plump into the water, only to leave it again at once, even accepting an interruption of the copulation begun. Since the land-section offered enough moisture for the moistening of the skin, only a very high temperature could have driven the animals into the water — which, however, in the present experiment, for the sake of isolating the moisture factor, was to be avoided. And so the deliveries also took place on land, and the males wound the string in the accustomed way around their hind-thighs.

In consequence of the excess of moisture, however, their remaining here was not for long. The gelatin sucks itself full of water from the wet ground, the connecting string between the individual eggs conducts it onward, so that soon even the highest-lying eggs lay in swollen envelopes. It is true that it took somewhat longer than with submerged eggs before they slid down, owing to the cessation of their stickiness and the non-onset of their capacity for contraction, but after 2 to 3 days no male any longer had spawn-strings around its thighs.

The experiment, however, could not lead to an instinct-variation and a developmental deviation: first, not, because an interruption of the stay in moisture-saturated surroundings was always first necessary to allow the reproductive drive to arise at all; second, because the spawning always took place **outside the water**, so that one could not speak of a complete leading-back of *Alytes* reproduction into the original spawning-mode of the remaining anurans. The failure of the instinct-variation to occur thus also expressed itself clearly in the fact that the males, at each newly occurring pairing, at least again made the attempt to exercise the well-known brood-care, and were always held back from it only secondarily by the purely physical obstacle. Even the males of the warmth experiment that had been transferred for the carrying-out of the reproductive act into this experiment (naturally, however, into separate experimental containers) — which there had no longer exercised brood-care — relapsed in time, that is, in the course of a few spawning periods, into their old habits; they thereby furnished a further proof of the fact that with moisture alone, without temperature action, nothing is to be accomplished in our case.

Finally, it is to be considered that the eggs laid here remained lying on the moist ground, without reaching the water; transferred into separate culture vessels (glass dishes) with substrate made approximately equally moist, they developed according to the character of normal land-eggs. The plus of moisture to which they were here exposed was nonetheless unable to imprint upon them the tempo and the other peculiarities of the development of water-eggs; the larvae left the envelopes at the proper time, already provided with internal gills, and in the course of their post-embryonic development also differed in nothing from those of the normal control breeding. A part of the eggs, which was not left on the moist ground but laid into the water, developed according to the character of the water-eggs, but without this developmental deviation leading, in the toads that arose from them, to an instinct-deviation.

### c) Dryness experiment.

At the beginning of the first spawning period in high temperature, it had repeatedly happened that the egg-laying still took place on land; and the cause that here, too, induced the males to refrain from brood-care was essentially different from the causes operative in the water: in the water it was the all-too-rapid swelling, on land — provided that the temperature is high enough — the all-too-rapid drying-out of the gelatinous envelope that held it, together with the eggs surrounded by it, fast to the thighs of the males.

It was therefore, for a good conclusion, still necessary to produce the last-mentioned inhibition — which in the temperature experiment could become the cause for the suppression of brood-care — without the mediation of a second factor, and thus without the aid of high degrees of temperature. Whereas the hitherto dispatched series a and b of the moisture experiment endeavour — in vain, to be sure, as we saw — to call forth the cessation of the brood-care instinct through **excess** of moisture, the experimental series c now to be discussed does this through **absence** of moisture — likewise with negative success, as we shall presently hear.

*Alytes* requires, like all amphibians, a certain measure of moisture, otherwise the skin-respiration vital to the animal can no longer proceed. As was already emphasized, however, that indispensable quantity of moisture need not be supplied to the animal uninterruptedly, but absolute dryness can alternate with it. The toads even visibly feel very comfortable under such conditions, that is, when they have it quite dry, while on the other hand they can, as often as it pleases them, bathe in a bowl set ready. The experiment was now handled in such a way that the animals came into terraria that were covered only with calcined [ausgeglüht], dry sand. As hiding-places, stones and pieces of bark laid with their hollow side onto the floor served, which were likewise completely desiccated and remained dry. A small water-basin took account of the need to bathe.

Two assumptions regarding the course of this culture — in which the animals thrived well and became sexually excited at the proper time without further artifices — appeared at first justified: 1) the excessive dryness could, as the excessive warmth had been able to do, drive the animals into the water during their love-play; here, then, the egg-layings could possibly take place, but, owing to the swelling of the gelatin, could not lead to brood-care; 2) the egg-layings could nevertheless take place on land, but again not lead to brood-care, here owing to the too-rapid drying-out of the gelatin.

Corresponding to these two premises, the procedure was then carried out accordingly, whereupon it at once became apparent that the dryness of the land section was by no means capable of confining the toads to the water during the decisive phase of their spawning act. They simply attended to the moistening of their skin beforehand and then copulated on the land. To this there was added, in case the water basin remained standing in the container throughout this whole period of time, a further inconvenience: in the surroundings of the basin the sand became moist, because the toads climbing out of it always carried over a little water, which here dripped off them. And precisely this single, moist spot of the terrarium was the most beloved by the copulating toads; here they accomplished their spawning business, loading on the egg-strings, in entirely normal fashion. By covering the basin's surroundings with gravel and a thick layer of filter paper changed daily, as well as by the fact that germinating dishes [Keimschalen] with a double rim were used as water basins, where the greatest part of the water adhering to the toads flowed off between the outer and the inner rim of the dish, this difficulty was fairly well remedied, but it changed nothing in the negative result of the procedure pursued up to this point.

Therefore, from now on the water basin was removed from the terrarium after the onset of the toads' rut and withheld from them for 1–2 days. Longer than this is impossible, for already within this short time their skin becomes wrinkled and shrivelled, the so well-nourished animals look as if they had eaten nothing for months, and they all press themselves together into one corner, in order to eke out a living from the moisture which the glands of their skin secrete. In the process, parts of the belly skin, the palms and the soles still become reddishly and bluishly suffused, the eyes, otherwise protruding and glistening, are drawn back dull into their sockets. After two days they had become so hungry for moisture that they immediately sought out the water basin restored to them. But this measure too availed nothing: for the rut-phenomena had come to a standstill in the dry surroundings; neither had copulations taken place on the dry land, nor did they now take place when the basin was put back into the container. The toads bathed greedily, then only thereupon did the sexual drive reawaken in them, and they accomplished pairing and binding-on on the land, whose dryness, in this fully soaked condition of the toads, remained a matter of indifference for the fate of the eggs. But even when the pairing did not take place immediately after the bath, but only hours later, after the animals had again become entirely dry, it turned out that the complete dryness of the medium was not capable of bringing about so rapid a drying-in and hardening as had succeeded so well with the aid of high degrees of warmth. Since the cessation of the stickiness — which goes hand in hand with the shrinkage of the egg-jelly — here only ensued after the males had had ample time not only to wind the strings around their thighs but also to glue them on thoroughly, the subsequent drying-in of the egg-membrane, precisely because it occurred at any rate somewhat more rapidly than in normal culture with moderate moisture-content of the surroundings, brings about only an all the firmer seating of the fetter. The experiment therefore ends along all lines with fully accomplished brood-care on the part of the males; in its accompanying phenomena it resembles the experiment ›Maturation of the *Alytes* eggs with brood-care by the father on dry land‹ of my work 1906 (*Alytes-Hyla*), p. 53 ff.

## IV. Inheritance of the Reproductive Change.

The same difficulties which opposed themselves to the undertaking of rearing the young earth-salamanders to sexual maturity and the exercise of sexuality also arose when the same was to be striven for with respect to the young midwife toads. The rearing as such is easy, especially since it has become possible in our institute, through the mass-breeding of suitable small fodder-means, particularly small flies (*Drosophila, Psychoda*, Culicidae), beetles (*Gnathocerus*) and worms (*Enchytraeus*), to satisfy the nutritional need of such young beasts of prey in a more natural and less time-consuming way than was formerly the case by means of surrogates in the form of artificially moved fibres of raw flesh and the like (compare on this Przibram 1908/9, Chapter ›Feeding-cultures‹). Only when the rearing in room-containers, with their limited spatial conditions, proceeds, there readily arises a disproportion between movement and nutrition, a too strong displacement in favour of the latter, which has as a consequence a fatty degeneration of the sexual glands.

Therefore, in the rearing of sexually capable individuals, the free-land terraria [Freilandterrarien] once again played a great role. Their description the reader finds in my work ›Inheritance of Enforced Reproductive Adaptations, I. and II. Communication‹ (1907, pp. 27—28), as well as in Przibram 1908/9, Chapter ›Land-installation‹, with photograph.

Since in the garden of our institute, at the time of the commencement of the inheritance-experiments [Vererbungsversuche], there were only four such free-land terraria suitable for breeding-purposes, it was necessary to unite the *Alytes* breeds [*Alytes*-Zuchten] with those of *Salamandra*, which, however — given the size of those cages and in view of the experimental procedure — could damage the breeding neither with respect to its progress nor with respect to its purity. More easily controllable, to be sure, are the always strictly isolated room-breeds [Zimmerzuchten], on which account this time too I employed the precaution of bringing a portion of the animals, in pairs, into containers of the building's interior as soon as the pairing-drive had set in outside.

Just now I spoke of the difficulties which opposed themselves to the becoming-sexually-mature of the young *Alytes*; nevertheless these difficulties are considerably smaller than in *Salamandra*. Such pairs, for example, as had once become rutting in the open and had thus indicated the attainment of their reproductive capacity, can then calmly remain in room-containers and yet become rutting again with each new spawning period [Laichperiode]. Indeed, a certain percentage of the *Alytes* raised from the egg does not need the free-land culture at all: a superior success as compared with *Salamandra*, which on the one hand is certainly grounded in constitutional [konstitutionelle] differences between *Alytes* and *Salamandra* — with *Salamandra* nothing of the kind has succeeded for me up to now —, and on the other hand is based on the progress in the procurement of natural fodder.

A further moment which makes the breeding-success of *Alytes*, in comparison with that of *Salamandra*, easier, is the more rapid onset of sexual maturity in the frog-toads [Froschlurch]. The salamanders needed at a minimum 3½ years before they became gravid for the first time; the midwife toads attain the analogous stage at the age of 2 years, and in high temperatures — whose stimulating influence on the maturation of the sexual products is, after all, also known from other experiences — even a year earlier still. So it comes about that the breeding-results now already at hand, in the second and in part third generation of the experiment-animals, are incomparably more complete and more many-sided than I was able to publish in my year-before-last salamander-work (1907, *I. and II. Communication*).

There lies before us the offspring of animals from the following experiment-categories:

### 1. Normal Control-Cultures.

The eggs are carried about by the brood-tending males, wound around the thighs, up to the slipping-out of the larvae (Fig. 1), and during this time — apart from daily, minute-long baths which the fatherly animal lets be bestowed on itself and thereby at the same time also on the eggs — they are exposed to the air (›land-eggs‹ [›Landeier‹]); after the elapse of this time the larvae slip out on the occasion of one or several of those baths and spend the developmental period up to metamorphosis in the water.

It was already remarked at an earlier place that we have succeeded in making the conditions of the normal breeding so very like those of free living that no deviation from the normal brood-business [Brutgeschäft] occurs. Essential in this is the maintenance of very moderate temperatures — in winter so low that the animals at least temporarily fall into torpor.

Under these circumstances there also lie before us the offspring of the control-breeding, of which since 1903 three uninterrupted generations are at hand, completely normal in their spawning- and brood-care-habits [Brutpflegegewohnheiten]. The midwife toads caught near Appenzell in the summer of 1903, among which there were already at that time egg-bearing males, yielded — from these eggs already fertilized in the open — the second generation, become sexually mature in the summer of 1905; the eggs laid by the latter in 1905 had become, by the early autumn of 1907, the sexually mature third generation; and the now-living young generation, which is to become sexually mature by autumn 1909, is the fourth generation of this breeding, or the third bred and developed in captivity in normal fashion. This breeding is therefore also the parent-breeding [Stammzucht] from which the material is drawn when it is a matter either of setting up new experiment-series or of crossing animals from already existing experiment-series with normal animals¹).

> ¹ On the crossing-experiments [Kreuzungsversuche] and the Mendelian behaviour of the newly acquired characters, report will be made — in order to await their fuller completion — only in a later work.

Most of the experiments, for which initially animals freshly obtained from free life were used, have already been after-checked [nachgeprüft] with specimens from the second or third generation of the control-breeding, which offers the inestimable advantage that here all the spawning periods have taken place under the supervision of the observer, that it is therefore precisely known that they ran their course normally, and that the objection — that instinct-variations [Instinktvariationen] and developmental deviations [Entwicklungsabweichungen] already originating from free life might be at play in the breeds — is excluded. To take account of the possibility of such an objection at all, I had been led by several midwife toads originating from Westphalia, which — as I should now like to recall from my work 1906 (*Alytes-Hyla* p. 69 ff.) and from the beginning of the present work, temperature-experiment — immediately after their capture had not exercised the brood-care otherwise to be observed in *Alytes*.

### 2. Offspring from eggs which were forcibly taken from the brood-tending males and reared in the normal medium (under moderate moisture, apart from water),

from experiment Nr. 3 of the work 1906 (*Alytes-Hyla*), p. 60 ff.

It was not out of the question that such eggs — which, indeed, behaved according to their embryonal and post-embryonal phenomena entirely normally, but developed without brood-care — would yield a toad-generation [Krötengeneration] which behaves deviantly with respect to reproduction, perhaps exercising no brood-care. For of the little town of Iglau in Moravia, whence the metropolis of Vienna had from time immemorial drawn a great part of its requirement of wet-nurses, the saga goes that the girls reared there meanwhile artificially [i.e. bottle-fed] are later incapable of suckling, on which account wet-nurses from Iglau can at present no longer be used.

Nothing similar, however, showed itself in the male midwife toads, which exercise a kind of wet-nurse-activity [Ammentätigkeit]. Although the experiment in question already reaches through three generations — in that those males which had developed from untended eggs, and had then taken the egg-strings from females likewise [so developed], had these too once again loosened by half — the males proceeding from the latter likewise again loaded themselves with spawn-packets [Laichpakete] and carried them faithfully until [they became] empty.

### 3. Offspring from eggs which were taken from the brood-tending males and matured in the water,

from experiments Nr. 2 and 4 of the work 1906 (*Alytes-Hyla*), p. 58 ff. and 69 ff. respectively.

To the inheritance-possibility [Vererbungsmöglichkeit] to be presupposed at the outset in the previous breeding-category there was here joined yet a second one: there are deviations in the developmental course [Entwicklungsgange] itself present, which consist principally in this, that the larvae, in consequence of membrane-maceration [Hüllenmaceration], leave the egg already at an earlier stage, namely the one with external gills; further, that all the midwife toads originating from water-eggs [Wassereiern] attain a supernormal size.

The first possibility mentioned in the previous category did not occur here either: the giant *Alytes*-males which emerged from such water-eggs gave their still more gigantic females midwife-assistance [Geburtshilfe] and exercised brood-care for their normal-looking eggs. This as well in the second as also in the third generation of the experiment-animals [Versuchstiere].

On the other hand, with respect to the inheritance-possibilities [Vererbungsmöglichkeiten] which are specific to the category now discussed, positive data seem to lie before us. Only these are too little distinct to be allowed to count on all-sided recognition. The larvae, namely, which emerge from such land-eggs [Landeiern] tended by the giant-males sprung from water-eggs and treated normally, are decidedly smaller than entirely normal larvae. Whereas these latter are always 16 to 18 mm long, those were at maximum 16, mostly only 15 mm long. But since they already possess inner gills, they do not differ from one another, apart from the perhaps too little significant size-difference: both stand at a developmental stage which is externally characterized by the same features. The full toads [Vollkröten] which became from these larvae turned out without exception quite large, but just as without exception they remained smaller than their begetters, so that their body-measurements [Körpermaße] indeed move in the upper limits, but yet still within the limits of that which has become known as the variation-breadth in this respect in the systematic literature: Dürigen gives 4–5 cm as the size of sexually mature animals, whereby the smaller animals are to be understood as males, the largest as females. The males proceeding from our breeding, however, are 47–48 mm, the females 49–50 mm long.

The whole weight of the achieved instinctive and morphological changes, and of their hereditary transmission [Übertragung], concentrates itself on the following category:

### 5. Offspring from eggs of the first main experiment ("Temperature-experiment" ["Temperaturversuch"]) of the present work.

a) Offspring from eggs which were left in the lurch by the males on the land, in consequence of too rapid drying-in [Eintrocknens] of the egg-jelly [Gallerte].

From the first laying-period of the parents influenced by high temperature there originate land-eggs [Landeier] which were granted no brood-care.

Since this case, as we then saw, has led to no instinct-variation, it is no further to be wondered at that the offspring behaved normally with respect to their reproduction. Normal behaviour is also to be reported with respect to the development of the eggs laid and tended by those; this, however, appears once again comprehensible, since their begetters themselves had developed entirely normally out of eggs which, partly set back into normal conditions immediately after laying, partly left lying in the high temperature on the moderately moist ground, had here shown only a somewhat more rapid tempo of their embryogenesis, but no morphological changes. Not so self-evident is it that the such-like land-eggs further-treated in the water too — although they released larvae with external gills and delivered giant full toads [Vollkröten] — proved themselves worthless for the inheritance-question [Vererbungsfrage] on the occasion of the reproduction of the latter. The reason for this lies, as we shall hear more amply in the following experiment-series (b), in the decisive, hereditary potency of the instinct-variation [Instinktvariation], over against which the influence exercised by the external developmental medium recedes: larvae from water-eggs [Wassereiern] always slip out with external gills, those from land-eggs [Landeiern] always with inner gills; the former always yield especially large, the latter rather small full toads, even though in the previous generation the reciprocal may have been the case. These characters are precisely all too labile, all too easily inducible anew again in every generation.

Those of the land-eggs last mentioned, deposited at high temperature, which — withdrawn from brood-care — were not, immediately after their laying, brought into moderate temperatures, but rather were further cultivated in the warmth and besides under maintenance of the greatest possible dryness, have, on the other hand, contributed some fine material to the solution of the inheritance problem.

Insofar as the further culture of the land-eggs kept warm and as little moist as possible took place in the light, I had attained only a difference of size relative to the norm: the hatch-ripe egg had become 6 mm long and 4½ mm broad (instead of only 5 mm long and 3½ mm broad); the freshly hatched larvae measured 20–24 instead of 16–18 mm. On the other hand the fully developed toads had remained smaller, with 21–21½ mm just after metamorphosis, 39–40 mm in the species-adult state, than is normally the case (23–25 mm for freshly metamorphosed, 40–50 mm for adult individuals). In the next generation, kept in the open, there likewise appeared only quantitative differences, measurable by millimeters and fractions of millimeters, which in consequence appear little distinct, especially since they set in together with the weakening obligatory upon return into normal conditions. The eggs, laid in normal size from the 8th–14th of September 1906, attain before hatching 5½ mm length at 4 mm breadth; the just-emerged larvae are 17–19½ mm, the freshly metamorphosed full toads 22 mm long. In April 1908 they reproduce for the first time anew, and indeed in quite normal manner, without a trace of the quantitative deviations stated, and have up to the present (spring 1909) become 40–43 mm long.

It is much more clearly that inheritance can be seen in the reproduction of those midwife toads that originated from land-eggs kept not only warm and as dry as possible, but in addition in darkness. Such land-eggs had indeed grown to 11 × 9 mm before hatching, their freshly emerged larvae were already provided with hind extremities and 31 mm long, their just-metamorphosed full toads nevertheless only 17–18 mm, their fully grown toads only 28–32 mm long. These dwarfs carried out their first reproductive act from the 16th–20th of September 1906. Since the parent animals, with respect to their brood-care instinct, had not yet been influenced in producing these descendants, but had only failed to take up the spawn because they had been prevented from doing so by too rapid drying-out of the jelly, the dwarfish descendants (Fig. 13) did not deviate from brood-care, but loaded themselves (Fig. 14) with the — strikingly few (16–19)! — eggs, which at the moment of being laid were 4 mm large. The size which they reached before hatching was not so considerable as in the previous generation: at 7 mm length, 5½ mm breadth. And the freshly hatched larvae, which measured 21 mm, found themselves at a developmental stage that was indeed still advanced relative to the norm, but lower relative to the parents: the hind legs, which in the parents were already fully differentiated, here showed themselves only in the form of bud-like rudiments, on which one could not distinguish the individual sections of the extremity (Fig. 11). Their embryonic development lasted somewhat more than 7 weeks, their post-embryonic development about 10 months; their total body length attained immediately after metamorphosis amounted to 20, the same after attainment of sexual maturity to 43–44 mm. At their most recent spawning period, from the 26th–29th of March 1909, the animals left in normal conditions allowed scarcely any differences relative to the norm to be recognized any longer: their eggs were not noticeably larger either at laying or in the hatch-ripe state than one sometimes encounters even in midwife-toad eggs found in the wild; their larvae left the egg-membrane after 6 weeks with internal gills, but not provided with hind legs or with their outwardly protruding buds.

Unobjectionable testing of whether an acquired character has been transmitted to the descendants is possible not only by returning the descendants to normal conditions, but often just as well by letting the modifying experimental conditions continue to act upon the second generation also. Upon return to normal conditions, the inheritance that has taken place expresses itself through the reappearance of the acquired character, sometimes in equal, mostly in more or less weakened form. With continued action of the transmuting factor, the inheritance can express itself through the unchanged persistence of the character; but then it is not recognizable to us as inheritance, for the factor itself could have brought about anew in the second generation the change brought about in the first generation. But the contribution of inheritance as such is recognizable when the acquired character reappears immediately in heightened degree; for now to the newly effected modification has been added a second, like-directed one, which can only stem from the previous generation. In the case of the Alytes held back in the egg by warmth, dryness, and darkness, we are in the fortunate position of being able to perceive such a heightening. Of the eggs laid, as reported just before, on the 16th–20th of September 1906, only half were left to the males for carrying under normal external conditions; the other half were treated as the ancestral generation had been: taken from the males, brought into high temperature (25–30 degrees C.) and here brought to term under the mere granting of the most indispensable minimum of moisture with exclusion of light. Thus the eggs lay 69–78 days, until they had reached 11 mm length at 10 mm breadth: only then did the larvae contained within gnaw and push their way out — insofar as they had not perished in the meantime (which happened to a large part) — into the open, and came into the world with 31–32½ mm length and well-developed hind legs. Left in high temperature, they attained their sexual maturity as early as September 1907, which the males indicated by croaking; since, however, at this point in time they were in normal conditions, they came in the autumn of 1907 to no copulations as yet. Only from the 13th–20th of April 1908 did this happen, and the males loaded themselves with eggs which, significantly, were once again fewer in number (maximum 14, minimum 10) and, on account of the increase of yolk, already at laying decidedly larger than the normal Alytes egg: the latter has 3½–4 mm, those had 4⅓–4½ mm in diameter. The size which they reached before hatching was not so considerable as in the previous generation: at 8 mm length, 6⅓ mm breadth. Nevertheless the larvae, freshly hatched after 61 days (Fig. 12), which measured 24 mm, found themselves at exactly the same developmental stage as the parent animals had occupied at hatching: the disproportion between their fully differentiated hind limbs and the, as it were, growth-retarded, fat trunk thus appears here still more striking. From their embryogeny the abnormally long persistence and the monstrous size of the yolk-sac are to be emphasized. Since the larvae thus remained in the egg up to an equally advanced developmental stage as in the previous generation, I believe myself justified in the assumption that in the decrease in size of the hatch-ripe egg there is to be seen this time no weakening of the acquired character, but rather, together with the increase in size (= increase in yolk-richness) of the freshly laid egg, a kind of compensation, a phenomenon of adaptation to the newly acquired mode of reproduction. Self-evidently, these eggs had to be carried by their father much longer than otherwise, for almost 9 weeks elapsed (as against 66–70 days in the previous generation) before the larvae gnawed through the membranes. A normal carrying is, after all, usually finished within 4 weeks, and only exceptionally lasts longer. The males took this extra performance upon themselves with self-evident devotion: one more proof, to be added to the facts brought forward for this in my work of 1906 (*Alytes-Hyla*), that the male is not guided by intelligence or by an instinct of its own when it brings the eggs into the water "in good time"; but rather this happens quite by chance during one of its regular, as a rule nightly, baths. Since the male by no means, as many authors stated, lives concealed until the hatch-ripeness of the larvae and only then goes into the water, but retains its otherwise habitual mode of life and movement throughout the whole period of its caring activity, the dissolving influence of the water and of the swimming movements of the male helps to free the larvae precisely at the moment when they have become ripe for this. — The larvae of which we have just now been speaking attained in the newly metamorphosed state 18, and to this day (spring 1909) only 27–28 mm length. In this dwarf form they are almost to be regarded as adult.

Some descendants I also still possessed from those cultures where the holding-back in the egg was effected by means other than dark-keeping and dry-keeping in high temperature, namely where the embryos had been prevented from timely hatching by mechanical agents: by surrounding the eggs with a moving medium (air current), or by setting the eggs themselves in motion by means of clinostat rotation or centrifugation. Where the mechanical agent was applied alone, the consequences of this were too weak to allow an after-effect in the daughter generation to be expected; that one accordingly failed to appear. And where the mechanical agent came together with all the other factors, all my larvae had perished as a result of this too-strong combination. But there remained larvae which had likewise been held back a little in the enlarged egg by high temperature and dryness on the one hand, by the moving medium or self-movement on the other, but with omission of the exclusion of light, hence in bright daylight. By comparison of eggs cultivated only warm-dry-bright and of eggs additionally strongly blown upon, rotated, or centrifuged, it had actually scarcely been possible to perceive that the supervention of the mechanical agent meant a prolongation of the embryogeny: in many data (cf. the table on p. 472/3) rather the opposite is reflected. But the descendant generation of those two experimental series indicates that the mechanical agent had nevertheless not remained without effect; in toads which proceeded from the eggs treated without that agent, only a trace of aberration was to be observed in their reproduction and in the development of their eggs; the fairly numerous eggs, however, which were laid and "incubated" by the animals treated during their embryogeny with the mechanical agent, retained their embryos throughout for 5–6 weeks, and at hatching the latter, with their 20–23 mm, always exceeded the length of normally hatched (16–18 mm long) larvae. The result brings me to the thought that perhaps, in the direct, metamorphosis-less development of frog-amphibians such as *Hylodes martinicensis*, which deposit their eggs on swaying plant-shoots (Amaryllidaceae, Bromeliaceae), movement plays, or originally has played, a certain role, through which the eggs placed there are, at every slight draught of air, withdrawn from the orienting influence of gravity; likewise in the at-least-delayed hatching of frog-amphibians such as *Alytes* and *Hyla goeldii*, which carry their eggs about with them and thereby set them in frequent motion.

To a lesser degree the holding-back of the germlings in the egg by darkness and by the mechanical agents named had succeeded also in the case of water-eggs (cf. the table on p. 480/1). If the larvae are to hatch from these while they still have external, not yet any internal gills, then through the contrarily acting factors there had secondarily again resulted a remaining-in-the-egg up to the stage with internal gills. Although the discussion of the water-eggs is only the subject of the next section (b), I believe that I must after all send the result of those water-eggs on ahead — since it most fittingly attaches itself here and, on account of its negative outcome, does not come further into consideration: namely, of the comparatively weak retardation achieved in the first generation no after-effect of this kind in the second generation was to be ascertained; from water-eggs the larvae here always hatched again provided only with external, not yet with internal gills.

## b) Descendants from eggs which were abandoned by the males on account of too rapid swelling of the jelly in the water.

Here we have before us the descendants from the single experiment which, among so many other experiments, had brought about the acquisition of an immanent instinct variation, namely in the sense of complete abandonment of brood-care and laying of the eggs in the water, where there hatch from them larvae still provided with external gills, which grow up into above-average-sized full toads.

The testing of whether these acquired characters are capable of inheritance or not had at first likewise again to take place by returning the animals begotten and developed in the deviating manner to normal conditions.

As such conditions I would allow only the breeding-containers of the garden to count. For although in the case of *Alytes* room-rearing also succeeded in some cases, and these rearings, with those of the open ground, yielded concordant results, the example of those certain Westphalian midwife toads (1906 *Alytes-Hyla*, p. 69 ff.), which had turned away from brood-care without deliberate application of experimental factors, hovered before me as a warning. There can be no doubt that the higher temperature bound up with room-keeping — notably, indeed, through the elimination of hibernation — accomplishes that same change of the brooding process more slowly than it sets in so quickly under deliberately strongly raised temperature. Midwife toads kept captive in close confinement undoubtedly possess a certain inclination toward the back-differentiation of their highly developed reproductive procedure, analogously to the way in which here the earth-salamanders display an inclination to bring forth their young at ever earlier stages (1907 I. and II. Communication, pp. 14, 38). Through suitable, cool placement of the experiments it is indeed possible, as we reported rather near the beginning, to establish, even in the interior of the building, rearings that remain permanently normal; but in order to forestall all objections from the outset, the following data refer one and all to specimens from the open-ground terraria, which each time were taken into the house and isolated only just before the beginning of spawning, for the purpose of more precise observation. Yet there is also no lack of observations on the spot, in the open-ground terraria themselves, which observations stand behind the isolated ones only with respect to numerical control, but otherwise prove with all desirable exactness that the perceived phenomena are not perhaps connected with the transfer of the experimental animals, but are the same here as there. The number of the actual, large open-ground terraria was by far not sufficient for the requirements of the *Alytes*-rearings, for there are, in all, only four, of which one was already taken up by the normal stock-rearing. For their supplementation there served various walled pits and cement troughs which are situated in the garden of our institute, as well as simple, weatherproof terrarium-containers which hitherto had found use only in the rooms, but now were likewise set up out of doors.

In rendering the data of a rearing where male and female stem from a like experiment, it was not found necessary to refer the descendants to a definite pair of the parent generation, even where this is known: the animals from one and the same spawning period of their progenitors behave, at one and the same spawning period which they themselves carry out, in such a manner similar, and without noteworthy individual deviations, that the spawn obtained each time in gratifyingly large quantity, and its course of development, need not be ordered according to parent animals, but only according to spawning periods.

In the autumn of 1906 the descendants of the very first egg-laying period — which had taken place in the 25–30 degree room — had become sexually mature. That this happened so quickly is certainly to be attributed to the influence of the high temperature, which at least accompanied and stimulated the maturation of the genital products in the body of the parents and the very first beginnings of embryonic development; then, however, the eggs were, after a time-span counted at most in hours after laying, immediately brought into normal temperatures. Despite this, their first egg-laying already took place in October (12 to 20 October 1906); but without the slightest deviation with regard to the place selected for this purpose and the instinct-reactions exercised in doing so. This appears, especially in analogy with the negative results enumerated hitherto, readily understandable when we recall that, after all, back then, in the egg-laying period (April 1905) to which the now-laying toads owed their origin, in their begetters the laying without brood-care had become only the compelling consequence of physical impediments, but not an instinct-variation. In the subsequent egg-laying periods of these oldest of the midwife toads of the second generation, matters naturally proceeded likewise; the same with their descendants, who came to reproduce for the first time in the autumn of 1908, the grandchildren of the influenced experimental generation. The same too in the case of those specimens and their descendants which originated from three egg-strings whose mother animals differed from the others coming into consideration here in that they had lived for 4 days, instead of only in 25–30 degrees, in 37–40 degrees C.

Already the descendants from the second, warmth-influenced laying period of the same parent animals (7–14 September 1905), which came to their first egg-laying in the spring of 1907, yielded a different result: from 9 to 16 April 1907 they laid, to be sure, on land, but without troubling themselves about the egg-strings. The same was the case with the descendants from the third influenced laying period of the parent animals (18–25 March 1906), when they laid for the first time from 15 to 21 September 1907. Nevertheless there results, in favor of a better fixedness of the hereditarily transmitted instinct-alteration in the latter, a difference between the two batches: the first has already in its second egg-laying period (23–26 September 1907) again forgotten the new mode and exercises regular brood-care; the latter, on the contrary, retains it in its second (19–22 April 1908) and, for a large part of the individuals, up to the third (20–28 September 1908) egg-laying period, abandoning it entirely only in the fourth (5–11 April 1909). Number, constitution, and development of the eggs are, up to this point, everywhere — whether brood-care was exercised or not — indistinguishable from the norm.

Now there follow the descendants from those laying periods in which the parent animals had already climbed the highest level of instinct-variation, a level on which the parents themselves, set back into normal conditions, did not so easily let the new mode drop. All these descendants lay at first in the water: the cement basins of the freeland terraria, the glass and earthen vessels of the room terraria, into which the animals were transferred in part from the freeland terraria, fill up with eggs to which no care is given, with eggs of essentially different quantity and quality (Fig. 18) than unimpaired midwife toads produce.

Let us follow the results in detail. From 29 August to 5 September 1906 had taken place that laying period of the parents which was for the first time permitted to claim for itself that complete degree of variation; from 31 March to 3 April 1908 took place the first laying period of the descendants, which had emerged from that parental laying period one and a half years before. The number of eggs in one and the same string, at 70–89, stands decidedly high above the average of the norm (18–86); their size (2½–3 mm diameter) stands deep below the average of the norm (3½–4 mm). All eggs are deposited into the water, and about 90 % develop; of the remainder it may be assumed, in analogy with other water-laying small frogs, that the fertilizing Spermatozoen did not reach them — a damaging effect of the foreign medium itself already appears to be excluded. The larvae (as in Fig. 21–21 b) slip out on 14 April 1908 with external gills, which immediately after slipping out are still long and tender, but are already strongly resorbed in the next-following hours; a shortening in another way, for instance by breaking off of the tips, does not take place. They complete their post-embryonic development in August of the same year, and the freshly transformed full toads (as in Fig. 24), which afterward still show a very considerable growth, not yet come to a standstill at the conclusion of the present work, characterize themselves by a size unusual for this stage (27–30 instead of the normal 23–25 mm total length). Of these phenomena let only those of the egg-laying, as well as the characteristics of the spawn, be written to the account of inheritance; the developmental phenomena are, after all, of no different nature than those which already proceed upon first influencing with eggs lying in water, and are each time anew inducible afresh by renewed sojourn in water.

In the *partie* of descendants discussed last that became capable of reproduction, too, the gradual sinking-back of the acquired and inherited character is unmistakable, although its appearance in the first-laying period of the descendant generation was, despite the setting-back into normal conditions, scarcely an attenuated one. The next laying period (17–20 September 1908) brings, however, a laying without brood-care on land; the period most recently observed for the time being (24 April 1909) brings a like egg-laying, whereby size and number of the eggs have regained their normal average.

Such a fading-away has hitherto, in admittedly already two laying periods, not yet been established among the descendants from the parental laying period of 10–21 March 1907. These lay for the first time from 12 to 21 September 1908, for the second time from 23 to 24 April 1909, both times under complete adherence to the reproductive alteration forced upon their begetters. That the developmental alterations undergone by the latter likewise occurred again is, at the conclusion of the present publication, with respect to the period of the spring of 1909, established only on early larval stages.

The laying period of the parent generation which took place from 27 August to 2 September 1907 is the last from which the descendant offspring already propagated up to the writing of the present treatise: it occurs from 25 to 29 April 1909 with all the marks of abandoned brood-care and those of the non-brood-tending frog amphibians under conditions become identical.

The positive results presented up to now all relate to descendants of such eggs deposited by the parent generation as had been left at the place of their deposition (in the water). The results of the water-eggs that, after deposition, were brought onto moist ground — that is, made into land-eggs — need after this to be dispatched with only a few words. I possess only few descendants from this not-often-repeated special experiment: decisive for their egg-laying behavior is here likewise solely the formative degree of the instinct-variation which the parents had attained when they deposited the spawn in question, not its further treatment. If that variation had already been a high-grade one, then the descendants deposit their eggs into the water, even when they themselves had come from eggs that had not remained in the same medium where the parent animals had deposited them. Decisive for the egg-laying behavior of the second generation and the developmental mode of the third generation is thus only the question of the egg-laying behavior of the first generation, not also, or only in a subordinate rank, the question of its developmental mode: for the latter is each time freshly induced by the external medium, whereas the former is inherited at first independently of it.

All the breeding-successes reported hitherto with descendants of eggs that were left in the lurch by the males in consequence of too-rapid swelling of the gelatinous matter were obtained under setting-back of the freshly laid egg into normal conditions. There remain for us to discuss breeding-results of the named category in which that setting-back was not undertaken, but rather the second and possibly further generations were also left in the experimental conditions, which here everywhere represent high temperature (25–30 degrees C.). The second generation used for this stems from the egg-laying period which the parent animals had completed from 18 to 25 March 1906. The male offspring originating from this laying period, which had been set back into normal conditions and which had reproduced after 1½ years, in the autumn of 1907, had indeed not yet been brood-tending, but the eggs had been left lying on land instead of in the water. Quite otherwise behaved the other part of the descendants from this very same parental laying period, which part remained in high temperature; already within one year the animals here were sexually mature: the first egg-laying period proceeded from 29 March to 4 April 1907 and yielded egg-strings deposited into the water, each with 98–106 eggs, and each egg only 2¼ mm in diameter. The habitus of the eggs (Fig. 22) is a different one than in the water-eggs of *Alytes* obtained hitherto (Fig. 18 and 18 a); this comes about because the gelatinous envelope has become considerably thicker, and indeed the gain in substance of the actual gelatinous egg-envelope, of the spherical shell therefore that surrounds the eggs, has evidently taken place at least in part at the expense of the now-shortened connecting strings between the individual eggs. Whether in addition the secretion of the gelatinous matter by the oviducts has also become a stronger one, must this time remain undecided. The eggs stemming from the laying period of March to April 1907, which of course developed in the manner and at the rapid tempo of water-eggs, yielded a third generation, coming to reproduction already at the beginning of May 1908, again left in the warm room, whose eggs provided with thick gelatinous matter showed only 2 mm in diameter and instead occurred in quantities of 110–117 per string.

The larvae leave the egg, according to the rule holding for water-eggs, at the stage with external gills and are 11½ to 12½ mm long; but whereas in the larvae from land-eggs, which still spend this stage in the egg, as well as in all hitherto considered larvae from water-eggs, which already spent this stage freely, only on the first gill-arch a gill develops (in the egg distinguished by special length, delicacy, and rich branching) (Fig. 20 and 21), in the present larvae of the fourth generation (Fig. 23 and 23 a) external gills formed on all three free gill-arches (Fig. 23 b), which at once, without an adaptation-process having first to set in in the water, were much coarser and shorter, more pigment-rich and more blood-poor, and did not have so many branchings (gill-filaments) as the normal *Alytes* gill. The originally so long gill of the foremost gill-arch, however, has by now become the shortest; the newly added gills of the second and third gill-arches surpass it in length. Therewith the usual relation of the three amphibian gills is established. Again left in the high temperature, there develop out of these larvae the full toads, which again already one year later (April 1909) proceed to egg-laying activity and are just now occupied with setting a fifth heat-generation into the world, whose larvae bear three little gill-trees, which is, after all, in so far as no setting-back into normal conditions took place, really self-evident. Yet I had treated one part of the eggs as land-eggs at normal temperature; in a further part in the same place, by letting [them] be whirled about in the water set in motion by a current of air, I held the embryos back beyond the slipping-out time of those in ordinary water-eggs; despite this, in both these special cultures there developed embryos with three gills, which, however, secondarily began already again to adapt themselves to the embryonic respiration, in the first place to lengthen. The maximum of the secondary lengthening, however, shows itself not, as is the case with the primary *Alytes* embryo, in the gill of the first, but in that of the third gill-arch.

Simultaneously with the alterations of the gills there proceed such of the entire body-form, namely of the thickness-relation between trunk and tail, of the width of the tail-fin seam, and of the pigmentation. If one compares, on Plate XVI, the larvae that slipped from the egg for the first time with external gills — which back then was brought about by the transfer of an originally quite normal land-egg into the water (Fig. 20) — with such a larva that still descends from the first, the parent generation of the experimental animals, after the voluntary depositing of the eggs in the water had already become habitual to the latter (Fig. 21), and finally these two larval forms with the larva last occupying us, of the fourth and fifth heat-generation (Fig. 23), there results a successive increase of the dark dye-stuff in the whole skin, a likewise advancing widening of the tail-fin seam, a blurring of the originally so sharp demarcation between trunk-region and tail-region — the latter a consequence of the vanishing yolk — and a reduction of the total body-size. The larvae have in the end become quite similar to a *Rana* species situated at the same stage.

There shows itself, however, on the sexually mature males of the fourth generation a further morphological variation: as repeatedly mentioned, *Alytes* lacks distinct secondary sexual characters and rutting-marks (Fig. 25 and 25 a). FATIO (cited after v. BEDRIAGA, p. 350) does indeed state that the male sometimes possesses a weak callosity-formation on the hand; on my material of *Alytes*, numbering many hundreds, of the first generation still stemming from free life, and indeed from the most diverse localities of discovery, nowhere is even a trace of it to be remarked, and in the literature, too, otherwise only the absence of any such formation is ever mentioned. One may therefore certainly be permitted to assume that the occurrence of rutting-callosities in *Alytes* is in nature a rare exceptional case. In my breeding, however, of the midwife toads influenced in the fourth generation by the warmth, the males that had become rutting (Fig. 26 and 26 a) were all provided with black-gray discolored callosities on the upper side of the thumb and on the thumb-ball, and likewise there shows itself in them a hypertrophy of the forearm musculature, in consequence of which the whole limb receives a somewhat different position: it is carried more curved inward, the palms are propped against the ground nearer the median line. Made attentive by this, I examined rutting males also of the previous and the generation before the previous, and found in the latter still nothing at all, but in the former indeed, at the very same places of the hand, the skin rougher than in the surroundings, as if rubbed sore, but not discolored, rather of the same color as the surroundings.

The rutting-callosities of the male anurans are, as is known, not interpreted as sexual stimulants, but rather they serve their bearer for being able to hold the female fast better. The same purpose is naturally served also by the hypertrophied musculature of the arm. Whoever has ever seen the differences which the skin of an amphibian exhibits, according as it finds itself, even if only briefly, on land or in the water, will not doubt that the roughnesses at those places where the extremity comes into most intimate contact with the body of the female are very necessary in order to make the clinging-on in the water possible, and that to this also belongs a considerable expenditure of muscular force; for every amphibian skin, however warty, becomes, in the water, within a few hours wholly smooth in consequence of the mucus-secretion; whereas the callosities and muscles could regress in the normally on-land copulating *Alytes*, because here the skin remains so rough that a further aid is not at all needed. When we therefore see that the rutting-callosity, which had been lost to the land-copulating midwife toads, reappears in water-copulating animals in the third generation and, in the fourth generation, comes to the fore intensified together with the muscle-hypertrophy, then the mentioned fact obtains the significance of an inheritance of a functional adaptation.

## 6. Descendants of larvae from the second principal experiment ("neoteny experiment") of the present work, including the larvae obtained through artificial insemination.

In the previous chapter it was described in greater detail how, in a female *Alytes* larva originating from Appenzell, I had induced total neoteny by experimental means (Fig. 28), and how on 19 September 1906 I obtained from this animal, through artificial stripping, 18 eggs and fertilized them with the sperm of a male full-grown toad originating from southern France. The eggs were treated further as water-eggs, and only two perished. The remainder released the larvae on 1 October 1906, whose external gills persisted until 19 October. This interval is an unusually long one, for the maximum duration of the persistence of external gills observed hitherto (and that only in neotenic larvae) amounted to 10 days. One may therefore well discern a neotenic phenomenon in this, and say that **neoteny this time manifests itself clearly already at an earlier stage than in the parental generation**. In the process, however, the larva, which by then had grown to 15–16 mm in length, was kept under conditions which ought rather to have been conducive to a rapid development than to a developmental inhibition: in moderate temperature (17 degrees C.), in full daylight — even shone upon for a short time daily by the direct sun — and with good feeding on filamentous algae and softly-beaten raw muscle fibers, in an aquarium only 32 × 25 × 18 cm in size, at a water level 12 cm high, that is to say shallow. *Alytes* larvae from the control rearing, which were kept in identical aquaria set up alongside, transformed at the proper time or even somewhat earlier. In the larvae of interest to us, however, the hind legs come into view only on 20 March 1907, in the form of bud-shaped rudiments (the animals possess at this time a total length of 42–44 mm), and become fully differentiated only after a further two months (total length 58–61 mm, hind-leg length 13–14 mm). In this stage, the one with fairly large hind limbs but without forelimbs, the larvae — of which eight have in the meantime perished — still find themselves at present (Fig. 29), while I write these lines, in the spring of 1909. A faint bulging of the skin covering the gill cavity indicates that the forelimbs concealed beneath it are pressing outward. The total body length has now reached 82–88 mm. Normal midwife toads ought by this time, within 2½ years since birth, to have long been sexually mature; in the neotenic larvae nothing is to be seen externally, neither in a morphological nor in a physiological respect. Dissection of such larvae yields the presence of well-developed ovaries (Fig. 32) or testes (Fig. 30), respectively, which, however, judging from their smallness, will probably not yet have attained the condition of their functional capacity (Figs. 33 and 31).

Apart from that single, totally neotenic larva, whose descendants begotten with a normal animal had again become neotenic, there is also present a further generation, namely of animals which had become **partially** neotenic and had reproduced only after the transformation. Among these are both animals that had become neotenic with the aid of being operated out of the egg, and also such as became so through darkness, low temperature, large quantity and stillness of the water, and fattening following a preceding scanty diet. For this reason it was here possible to pair the formerly neotenic animals with one another, without bringing in an individual that had probably attained metamorphosis without neoteny. In the production of those descendants there also participated the specimen described in my 1906 work as the most strongly neotenic, likewise a female (Fig. 27), which had lived as a tadpole for 4 years and 2 months. Of the remaining specimens of the parental generation, none was a larva for less than 2¼ years. Nevertheless, no single case of metamorphosis-delay shows itself in the descendants, neither when the eggs were brought to maturity on land nor when they were brought to maturity in the water. In the latter case the metamorphosis proceeds, as is usual in larvae from water-eggs, after about 3–4 months, in the former case, as is normal, after 14–15 months.

The generation reared through artificial insemination (parallel culture to the neoteny experiment) had already itself yielded no neotenic specimens; it is superfluous to say that this was likewise not the case among its descendants.

## 7. Descendants of the "land-larvae".

In this last series of experiments, coming up for discussion today, a distinction is to be made between the descendants of those *Alytes* larvae kept out of water which emerged at the proper time from normal land-eggs, and of those which were held back in untended land-eggs up to an abnormally advanced stage.

### a) Descendants of the land-larvae from normal land-eggs, from Experiment No. 6 of the 1906 work (*Alytes-Hyla*), p. 88 ff.

The examination of whether some or all of the in part very striking morphological and physiological characters by which the land-larvae are distinguished are inherited was carried out, again, first of all through transfer into normal conditions. These were here evidently only to be brought about in such a way that those larvae which came from eggs that had been laid by the full-grown toads — representing the parental generation, formerly land-larvae — after their metamorphosis, were now left to their original medium of residence, the water. This indeed came about in a manner that resulted of itself from the normally-proceeding reproductive business of those full-grown toads that had formerly been land-larvae. Most of the experiments of rearing *Alytes* larvae out of water, in earth-hollows kept moist, had been undertaken in the summer and autumn of 1903. Accordingly, the sparse full-grown toads rescued from them — dwarfed in consequence of the long dry life — attained their sexual maturity in the autumn of 1905, which the males brought to notice through frequent calling and copulation attempts. But only from 24 to 30 April 1906 were egg-strings deposited, which possessed a normal appearance and were carried by the males around the thighs until ripe for hatching. The larvae escaped, on the occasion of baths taken by the males, into the water-basin between 18 and 21 May.

During their development it turned out that most of the characteristics of the land-larvae no longer came into view. With regard to the narrower tail-seam in the latter, one need not even emphasize this, since already the fin-seam of the parental generation, narrow as it had at first become, broadened out again amply once the larvae had come into the water after the expiry of the maximum duration of dry life endured by them. Also the **muscle-part of the tail**, which was thicker in the land-larvae, here allowed the laterally-compressed oar-form to come to expression again in the usual way. The integument is of normal thickness and constitution; the **skin-glands**, whose development and function represent a character which, according to whether the residence is in water or on land, is very labile — we have already learned to appreciate this in the present work in the discussion of the functional significance of the nuptial callosities —, develop, in the larval descendants of the land-larvae, no earlier and function no more strongly than in other water-larvae of *Alytes*.

As regards the lungs, however, there was present a deviation from the norm which must be interpreted in the sense of the inheritance of a functional adaptation. The lungs are laid down, in the land-larvae, abnormally early, grow extraordinarily fast, and take on the honeycomb-like architecture (Fig. 36) which otherwise distinguishes only the lungs of the full-grown toads (Fig. 37). If one observed the aquarium in which the descendants of the land-larvae were swimming, and a neighboring aquarium which housed normal *Alytes* larvae, then within a few minutes it was conspicuous that the former came up to the surface much more often and here, similarly to carp, which must live in oxygen-poor water, snapped for air with a gulping sound. Occasionally the other tadpoles indeed do this too, but nowhere near so often. From this phenomenon I at first inferred only a poor constitution of the water; but renewal of the water altered almost nothing in it, and even leading a stream of air through had a mitigating effect upon the breathing only when the tadpoles betook themselves directly into the zone of the rising air-bubbles, that is, could consume the air itself before its dissolution in the water. The preparation showed that, despite the water life, the lung of the land-larva-descendants is already relatively large and does not represent the smooth-walled, simple tube (Fig. 34) as in other tadpoles, but rather that its wall had already, to a similar extent, everted numerous small vesicles, as in the land-larvae (Fig. 36) and otherwise in the full-grown toads (Fig. 37).

The inversion of the normal temporal relationship between the passagethrough of the forelimbs and the shedding of the horny jaws was here indeed once more abolished, yet not to such a degree that the latter, as is the rule, came up perceptibly earlier in turn, but rather that both phenomena occurred simultaneously or almost simultaneously.

The total duration of the post-embryonic development, which in the land-larvae had been reduced to about 8 weeks — whereas otherwise, in larvae crept from land-eggs, it is wont to last about 15 months —, was also here, in the descendants of the so-rapidly metamorphosed land-larvae, a comparatively short one, yet took nevertheless a good 9–10 months, which in normal larvae also occurs at times (although it never occurred in the control experiment), so that the inheritance here at any rate does not come to expression so clearly delimited as in the remaining cases, where we were still able to ascertain it.

The land-larvae had yielded abnormally small full-grown toads, indeed so much so that in this respect they remained below the minimal limits of the range of variation known for *Alytes*. The full-grown toads that became of the land-larva-descendants were also quite small, but with their 41–43 mm move within the normal limits of variation.

Finally, it remained still to investigate whether the capacity of the land-larva-descendants **to endure on land** had not become greater than in the parental generation. To this end the back-transfer into the normal medium, the water, naturally had to be omitted, and the larvae were placed, some immediately after leaving the egg, some still at later stages, after a considerable time of water life, into moist earth-hollows and covered with a moist, inverted (with the budding-poles directed downward) cushion of moss, that is, treated just as their respective begetters were. Since these, when kept out of water immediately after hatching, were able to live in the air at the maximum 29 days, and when taken out of the water only at the two-legged stage, at the utmost 14 days, while their descendants, however, under like circumstances held out up to 34 and 20 days respectively, the question as to an enhancement of the capacity is to be answered in the affirmative. It forms, moreover, a support for the view which I expressed in 1906 (p. 94) on the basis of comparative experiments on the larvae of other Central European frog-amphibians: that the capacity of the spawn and of the larvae to spend a certain time on dry ground is a functional adaptation to the periodic drying-up of the spawning waters.

### b) Descendants of the land-larvae from land-eggs in which the embryos had been held back up to advanced stages, from Experiment No. 1a of the present work.

For 16–20 September 1906 the first spawning period was announced of the dwarfish *Alytes* which had remained in the egg up to the stage with two fully differentiated legs, and it had turned out that the eggs produced by them again retained the embryos long within themselves, namely up to the stage where the differentiation of the two first-appearing limbs had already begun. Into almost the same time, from 18 to 21 September 1906, falls also the first reproduction of those dwarf-*Alytes* which, after their very much delayed hatching from the egg, had still not been transferred to their proper element, the water, but had developed further for a further 16 days on merely moistened ground.

On examining the inheritance-capacity of these animals with respect to the morphological-physiological peculiarities acquired during their land-larva life, the "back-transfer into normal conditions" could not proceed in such a way that the eggs laid by them were at once entirely normal again. For we have indeed already learned that the eggs of such *Alytes* again grow to considerable size and retain the embryo long within themselves. This happened here too: until the hatching of the eggs, present only in a number of 16–18 and 4 mm in size, it took 50–54 days, and the freshly-crept larvae were already 20–21 mm long and furnished with hind-leg stumps. But now one part came at once into the water, another part again onto moist loamy earth with a covering of moss over the densely-swarming heap of tadpoles.

In those in the water the phenomena of the rearing experiment 7a just discussed above repeated themselves: all the adaptation-phenomena to land-life had vanished, with the exception of two: the shortening of the post-embryonic developmental time, which however was already favored by the long lingering in the egg, to 2–3 months (instead of about 15 months in normal ones, 12–18 days in the corresponding previous generation, and 5 months in the larvae not treated as land-larvae, which slipped out of the egg with delay); further, a premature formation of the honeycombed lung composed of many small vesicles, of complicated structure. The forward-shifting of the time-interval between the appearance of the forelimbs and the shedding of the horny beak does not in any case come into consideration here, since this phenomenon, already in the parental generation — in distinction from the land-larvae that proceeded from normal land-eggs —, had not been observed.

A renewed transfer of the larvae onto land (only immediately after the delayed hatching — the other experimental variation, transfer onto land at later stages, is omitted here) yielded a mighty enhancement of the vital capacity in this unaccustomed medium, bound up with an enhancement of all the adaptation-characters that come into consideration here. The enhancement of the vital capacity expresses itself less in a prolongation of the outermost term at which the continuously very great mortality among the land-larvae becomes so great that the remainder, on account of otherwise impending danger of total dying-out, must be put into the water, but rather in this, that that mortality, right from the very beginning of the larval life on land, by far did not assume those dimensions as in the previous generation. Whereas there about ¾ of all the larvae had perished, this now befell only about one fourth. And this percentage refers only to the epoch of the very last days of the dry-residence, after the elapse of which the remainder had to be put into the water. The whole period of the land-life, from the crawling-out of the egg up to the transfer into the water, amounts to 19 days; the subsequent post-embryonic development up to metamorphosis then lasted only a further 14–16 days (against 12–18 days of the previous generation). If the enhancement of the acquired and inherited vital capacity out of water, together with its accompanying phenomena, namely the accelerated metamorphosis, is no greater, then this lies precisely in the fact that here the most extreme demands are made on the performance-capacity of the animals, demands which they could probably fulfill in the full measure desired by the experimenter only after breeding through some further generations. But today I no longer doubt that it lies within the realm of the possible to force upon *Alytes* a development independent of water-residence and without metamorphosis.

## V. Theoretical Evaluation of the Results.

The principal objection which — among other places, in an excellent review by PLATE of my previous inheritance work (1907, I and II Communication) — was raised against the interpretation of my results in the sense of an inheritance of somatogenic characters, is this: that the characters called forth by me do not represent new ones, but rather a reactivation of old characters latent in the germ-plasm.

In my I and II Communications (1907) on the inheritance of enforced reproductive adaptations, the atavism lay openly evident only in the one breeding series, the one with *Salamandra atra*, where the more highly differentiated reproductive mode of full-newt-bearing was led back to the more lowly differentiated mode of larva-bearing. In order also to be able to assume atavism in the second, reverse-running breeding series, the one with *S. maculosa*, and thus to present the enforced full-newt-bearing as an old character vis-à-vis the larva-bearing that is normal here, one must indeed, as PLATE does, draw upon the behavior of *S. maculosa* at the boundary localities of its vertical distribution, where transitions to the normal mode of generation of *S. atra* sometimes occur, i.e. the depositing of only a few larvae at advanced stages; one must accordingly make the assumption that the bearing of advanced stages was transferred from the salamander population of those sporadic high-altitude localities to all the remaining salamanders of the entire distribution range — an assumption that could after all hardly be defended.

I have therefore, in a critical author's-abstract (1908), held to the view that the full-newt-bearing of *S. maculosa* is a property **newly impressed** upon this species, and have conceded the reversion only for the larva-bearing of *S. atra*.

In the III Communication presented today I should like to register expressly as such **three results** which do not admit of the objection that it is a matter of atavism: firstly, the heritable suppression of a piece of the larval period and the thereby accomplished approach to direct, metamorphosis-less development, which arises from the fact that a few more stages are transferred into the egg than is already the case in *Alytes* anyway; the act of generation, more highly differentiated in comparison with other frog-toads [anurans], thereby becomes still more specialized and has become similar to that which only very few of the amphibians [Lurche] we know possess. Inasmuch as *Alytes* is raised up into their exceptional position, the character bound up therewith must count as newly acquired, may nevermore count as a reversion. Secondly, for the same reason, the heritable enhancement of the viability of *Alytes* larvae on land, together with the characters bound up with it. Thirdly, the heritable neotenny, this for reasons that are to be set out still more precisely further below.

To the remaining results published in my present work, the principal objection that it is a matter of atavism — insofar as therein there is at all an objection against the inheritance of somatogenic properties — may fit. We have before us above all, just as in *S. atra*, a de-differentiation of a complicated course of generation: when the midwife toad, which copulates and spawns on land and then in the male sex is brood-tending, adopts the primitive spawning habits of other frog-toads, accomplishes copulation and egg-laying in the water and leaves the spawn lying there, then there is thereby evidently again attained a condition from which the species once set out, abandoning the accustomed ways of the remaining species. Granting the atavistic nature of this character produced by experimental means and passing into inheritance in the next generations, I should only like once more to point out that its usability **in favor of** the inheritance problem is thereby not in the least impaired; I should like to point to the words of SEMON (1907, p. 26): "Is the germ-plasm altered by conduction-stimuli or not? That is the question. The nature of the alteration, however — whether it manifest itself in the appearance of a new disposition or in the reappearance of an old one that has become latent — is, for the fundamental decision of this physiological problem, of course of no significance whatever. If, then, it is demonstrated that the disposition to deviate from the ordinary developmental course and to turn into the path of the primordial forefathers can be acquired through external influences, and further that this disposition thus acquired is transmitted by the conduction-pathway to the descendants and becomes manifest in them even upon the absence of those external influences, then thereby at the same time the inheritance of a somatogenic property is irreproachably proven."

SEMON (1907) uses these words chiefly in order to defend one of the oldest-known, direct cases of proof of somatogenic inheritance: the case of v. CHAUVIN with the Mexican axolotl, *Amblystoma* (p. 385). Of the cross-toothed-salamanders [Querzahnmolche] *Amblystoma mexicanum* and *tigrinum* there exists a sexually mature land form and a sexually mature (neotenic) larval or water form. In order to induce young animals of the latter to metamorphose into the former, severe coercive measures were necessary; on the other hand, the larvae descending from the enforced land forms transformed themselves without coercion under conditions under which such a transformation would otherwise never have taken place. SEMON considers this case, which he has repeatedly discussed (1907, pp. 24, 25; 1908, p. 173 ff.), to be one of the most demonstrative, because the selection objection and the objection of direct co-influencing of the germ cells are excluded. I have (1907, I and II Comm., p. 44) even opposed this latter view of SEMON's, since I held a direct, physical, not physiological further effect of the factor "moisture" coming into consideration both in CHAUVIN's and in my *Salamandra* cases to be possible, by the osmotic route through the skin and the tissues lying beneath it, as well as by the route through the cloaca and oviduct. SEMON (1908, pp. 175, 176) replied to this that osmotically no incisive difference could result for the germ-glands — which are in any case always bathed by lymph — from whether their bearer lives on land or in the water. The oviduct, however, was under normal conditions, i.e. in the healthy animal, hermetically and fluid-tightly sealed off from the outer world. The at most slightly increased tissue-moisture of the germ-glands could not be responsible for the fact that the instincts and functional changes called forth in the parents are repeated in the descendants. In my author's-abstract (Zeitschr. f. indukt. Abstammungs- u. Entwicklungslehre 1908) I have declared myself in agreement with these arguments and accordingly now myself consider a direct co-influencing of the germ cells in CHAUVIN's *Amblystomas* and in my salamanders to be improbable.

HATSCHEK once (orally) prompted me to repeat the CHAUVIN experiment — production of the land form and testing of the inheritance with neotenic *Amblystomas* — on a larger scale; SEMON prompted me, in connection therewith, to undertake the reciprocal: production of neotenny and testing of the inheritance, on our native water-salamanders or tritons. I believe I can best meet these suggestions for the time being by presenting a case of inherited neotenny not in a tailed amphibian [Schwanzlurch, urodele] but in a frog-toad [Froschlurch, anuran]. For in most tailed-amphibian species neotenny is not all too rare even in the wild and at no locality can it be flatly excluded. Neotenny is here, too, a very labile character which, called forth in one generation, in the next, without the continued action of the factor that calls it forth, only too easily vanishes again. At least, then, several generations would be required to raise irreproachable, clear evidential material. If the frequent occurrence of neotenny in free life brings near the objection that it is a matter of germinal variations corresponding to the somatic variation (SCHNEIDER), and if the fluctuation of the neotenny-character further hampers the proof of inheritance, then over and above this, in the Urodeles, the objection that "it is a matter of atavism" — which is after all decisive for many researchers and must accordingly be taken into account — is irrefutable. Even in a tailed amphibian which, so far as is known, never manifests complete neotenny in the wild, the objection is not entirely without force; but in such a tailed amphibian as e.g. *Salamandra*, it has on the other hand also never yet succeeded, even in captive life, in producing total neotenny.

On the other hand, all reservations had to fall away once it had succeeded in **letting a frog-toad retain the larval form on beyond sexual maturity**. No one will indeed seriously maintain that the sexually mature larva of *Alytes*, teeming with cenogenetic characters, is atavistic. No frog-toad has, after all, in its phyletic development passed through a stage that even approximately looks like the one depicted in Plate XVII, Fig. 28. In the inheritance — even if weakened, yet distinct — of such neotenny there is therefore attained a case of somatogenic inheritance which contains the whole evidential force championed by SEMON for the CHAUVIN case, and which moreover does not admit the objection of atavism, which there remained unrefuted.

But a second principal objection — perhaps better expressed, only a suspicion — namely of **direct co-influencing of the germ cells**, has here, in another form than before, again presented itself. In that form, partly theoretical, partly inferred merely from incidental facts which have nothing to do with the experimental course itself, in which I had left open the possibility of direct germ-alteration in *Amblystoma* and *Salamandra*, it has been refuted by SEMON (1908) in a convincing manner. In the present case, however, the results are after all constituted as follows: numerous *Alytes* larvae, hindered by whatever factor from timely metamorphosis, beget among themselves, after their belated transformation into the full toad, a progeny that never shows even a trace of abnormally long post-embryonic development; a single *Alytes* larva, on the other hand, which was not partially but totally neotenic — i.e. which transformed itself not already before, but only after attainment of sexual maturity — this *Alytes* larva begets, before its belated transformation, with a non-neotenic animal, a progeny which one and all grows up into giant larvae without a timely impulse to transform.

One might interpret this result simply thus: that already in the specimen itself whose descendants became neotenic, neotenny was much more strongly pronounced than in the others, which had non-neotenic descendants; and that therefore neotenny was inherited in the former case, not in the latter. The explanation could, however, also lie deeper and in principle furnish a parallel case to certain results of TOWER on leaf-beetles of the genus *Leptinotarsa*. *Leptinotarsa* is, for the decision of the question whether in the inheritance of acquired properties a direct transmission or an indirect stimulus-conduction onto the germ-plasm takes place, an extremely favorable material, inasmuch as the sexual products in that beetle genus only become ripe when the coloring-out process [Ausfärbungsprozess] of the imago already appears entirely carried through. TOWER's results therefore yield the following picture: he let temperature, moisture, and atmospheric-pressure extremes act upon his experimental animals and obtained aberrations in not-yet-fully-colored beetles, no aberrations in already-colored ones. If the aberrant ones were reared on under normal conditions, then the next generation was not again aberrant, but typical; if the non-aberrant ones were reared on, then the next generation shows the same aberrations as had been produced by direct influencing of not-yet-colored animals. The germ cells can thus take up transmuting influences only during their ripening period; before the ripening period those influences can indeed be taken up by the somatic cells — these become and remain altered, — but are not in a position later, on the entry of ripeness of the germ-plasm, to transfer the alterations onto the latter. Conversely, at that time the somatic cells themselves can no longer take up any transmuting influences, and thus could again not function as intermediaries between the outer world and the germ-plasm; nevertheless it shows itself, in the form of the transmuted descendant-generation, distinctly influenced. Hence the germ-plasm must have been altered by the transmuting external influence **directly, independently of the soma**.

Identical with TOWER's results, in my breeding of neotenic *Alytes* larvae, is only that case where partially neotenic animals, which reproduce only after their metamorphosis, do not inherit the neotenny. They themselves show considerable aberrations, but only at a time when the sexual organs could not yet have been functioning.

Since such larvae, as larvae, exceeded, in part considerably, the age of animals that already reproduce, that circumstance does indeed signify a (with neotenny often to be observed) striking **delay in the development of the genital glands**; and one might doubt whether sexual maturity had not, despite the omitted reproduction of the larvae, already set in, were the dissection-finding not likewise to speak against it (Figs. 30–33).

No longer identical, but presumably yet homologous with TOWER's results is my other, case that has remained isolated, where a totally neotenic animal, which reproduced already before its metamorphosis, did inherit the neotenny; those factors which call forth neotenny acted here, precisely, still during the ripening period of the germ-plasm. Herein the case is like those of TOWER, but it differs from them in that the parent animal did not, as in TOWER, remain unaltered and thereby permit the somatic transmission to be excluded, but rather was itself strongly aberrant. Therefore in my case the possibility remains open that the aberrant soma exerted a conduction-stimulus upon the germ-plasm, to which the soma of *Leptinotarsa*, being itself not aberrant, was naturally not capable. On this interpretation we should be allowed to present as a doubtlessly justified conclusion only the one, that unripe germ-plasm in *Leptinotarsa* and neotenic *Alytes* cannot be influenced, neither by the directly physical nor by the indirectly physiological route. Influenced can be only ripe germ-plasm, in *Leptinotarsa* certainly by the direct route, while in *Alytes* this remains for the time being undecided.

But in the *Alytes* breedings still other results are contained which constitute parallel cases to those of TOWER. The **sexual maturity** of *Alytes* sets in, at normal temperature, around the completion of the second year of life. *Alytes* reared in high temperature, however, become sexually active already in half this time and inherit the more rapid entry of puberty in weakened degree upon their descendants, which, set back immediately into normal temperature, become sexually capable within 1½ years. Now, breeding animals with which a warmth experiment is newly initiated, and which derive from a normal stock-culture, can no longer themselves be influenced in the direction of more rapid entry of sexual maturity — this epoch of their life is, after all, long past. The only reactions in them — apart from the instinct alteration, which consists in the abandonment of brood-care — are these: that they faithfully keep to their two spawning periods in the year, whereas in free life they are often prevented, on account of unfavorable weather, from holding the second; and that these spawning periods shift slowly retrogressively in the sense of a somewhat earlier entry of the same. If one now brings the eggs which the warmth-kept midwife toads deposit, immediately after their deposition, into moderate temperature, then the toads issuing from them nevertheless become sexually mature already at 1½ instead of at 2 years, quite the same as the descendants of the *Alytes* that remained in the heat from the egg onward, here having become sexually mature within 1 year. There must accordingly, in the case of the uninfluenced toads — uninfluenced because they were placed into the experimental conditions as old, long sexually mature animals — the influence of high temperature upon the ripe egg- and sperm-cells have sufficed to awaken, in the germ fertilized by their union, the tendency to more rapid unfolding of its later germ cells.

The heritability of the acquired property thus dates, in TOWER's *Leptinotarsas*, in neotenic and in such *Alytes* as become sexually mature too rapidly, only from that moment at which the altering factors could make themselves felt upon ripe germ-products. And this speaks further in favor of the view that here the transmission of the acquired property took place directly upon the germinal part [Germinalteil] and — at least in the cases just enumerated — neither required the mediation of the personal part [Personalteil] nor could have availed itself of it, in *Leptinotarsa* and in the *Alytes* that become sexually mature too rapidly.

Also in most of the other previously ascertained experimental examples of the inheritance of acquired characters, the sensitive stage falls within the maturation period of the genital products, coinciding with the period of action of the altering external factor. This holds in all inheritances of enforced reproductive adaptations in *Salamandra*, in instinct variations in *Alytes*, in neoteny in *Alytes* and *Amblystoma*, in the transformation into the land-form in *Amblystoma*, finally in the inheritances of color variations in butterflies demonstrated by Standfuss and Fischer (where the pupa was influenced), as well as in the variations of diverse character in beetles demonstrated by Tower.

Not so certainly can we ascertain the going-hand-in-hand of external action and maturation of the genital products in the following examples: in the heritable instinct-, size-, and color variations in butterflies demonstrated by Pictet (where the caterpillar was influenced), in the heritable instinct variations ascertained by Schröder in *Phratora vitellinae* and *Gracilaria stigmatella*, finally in the heritable remaining of the embryos within the egg, beyond the stage normally ripe for hatching, which I observed in *Alytes obstetricans*. Here the transmuting factor acts everywhere upon larval stages, that is, in a developmental period in which the genital organs are indeed already laid down and in part — as in the insect larvae — even already fairly well developed, but are certainly still far from their maturity. Either the difference between the first-enumerated and the now-enumerated cases is only an apparent one, in that here we merely do not know the critical stage of egg- and sperm-maturation at which the germ cells are receptive to direct influencing from without, and which may very well be different in different cases; or else, from case to case, both paths of stimulus-transmission are possible, there the direct and here the indirect: the latter, namely, in cases of heritable influencing *before* the maturation of the genital substances.

Most of the previously ascertained proofs for the inheritance of acquired characters are cases of direct adaptation, that is, of such where the external factor in question has produced the heritable changes immediately by physico-chemical means. But now I should like to point out particularly a few cases from my results, where what is involved is the inheritance of **functional adaptation**, hence of such where the external factor has called forth the heritable changes mediately, by way of functional reflexes. These changes appear scarcely explicable and conceivable through a direct influencing of the germ-plasm — without the mediation of the soma in the form of nervous conduction or internal secretion or both. Already in my previous inheritance work (1907, I. and II. Communication) some such results were on hand, concerning which, however, I had not there discussed further with respect to their theoretical significance: in particular, the adaptive changes in the gills and the tail-fin seam of the salamander larvae, according as these remained in the uterus or reached the open water, were to be conceived as inheritance of functional changes and functional intensifications. From my present work there is to be reckoned hither: the reappearance of the male copulatory calluses [Begattungsschwielen] and hypertrophied arm muscles in the *Alytes* copulating in the water, in the third generation, their strengthening in the fourth generation; the histological change, shortening of the gills and their increase from one on each side to three on each side; the development of the tail-fin seam, heightened with the extension of the water-life; in the tadpoles the heightening of the capacity to live on land, together with the morphological-physiological accompanying phenomena of this change of abode; finally the altered distribution of the gelatinous envelope and its increase in thickness around the water-egg. To be sure, these functional adaptations (with the exception of the next-to-last) are again of atavistic nature; and to be sure (again with the exception of the next-to-last) the impulse to them is given by such changes as could arise through direct influencing of the germ-plasm. But in their further development they are entirely independent of the latter; and since they concern only definite parts of the body, from which the germ-plasm can be reached in no other way than by the stimulus-conduction path, the objection of atavism would thereby also fall away, even if the functional change in question actually possesses an atavistic character.

In my previous inheritance work (1907, I. and II. Communication) I had had to halt before the question: **"How do the heritable characters come to the germ-plasm, by physical or physiological conduction?"** I did so in the consideration that the answering of it possesses only a relatively still subordinate significance for the answering of another question, "How do new species come into existence?", once it had been demonstrated that species-forming characters originate from the external world and can be inherited, no matter by what path.

In my present work I attempted to separate the cases into those where the purely physical path must be regarded as possible, and into those where only the physiological one remains probable. Often I have thereby spoken, as it were, as advocate for those investigators who would always let only the first path hold; although the phenomena of living nature, and likewise those of the artificially altered conditions, press me toward the opposite conviction. But I hope thereby to demarcate more sharply the positive results hitherto on hand, which still leave open the one or the other objection, still contain the one or the other obscurity, as against those which bring us a step nearer — one not to be retracted — to the final answering of that much-disputed question.

## VI. Summary of the Results.

*(In the following points of the summary, under **a** in each case the condition in the parental generation is designated, under **b** the corresponding one in later generations of the experimental animals. With the exception of Point 7, where it is expressly stated otherwise, the inheritance results are to be understood as obtained through breeding animals of the same experimental conditions.)*

**1 a.** *Alytes obstetricans* copulates on land and lays in the open, at the same place, 18–86 large, yolk-rich eggs; during the copulation the male draws the egg-chains by means of its hind legs out of the female's cloaca and at the same time winds them around its thighs, where they remain adhering in the form of a clump until the embryos (as a rule after 4–5 weeks) are ready to hatch. To their adhering, the constitution of the gelatinous envelope contributes, which immediately after laying is very sticky, later contracts, and thereby draws the fetter tightly together. The brood-tending male, in the time during which it carries the eggs, does not depart from its accustomed mode of life; among other things it frequently seeks out the water, and on the occasion of one or several of these baths the egg-envelopes are sufficiently softened to make possible for the larvae the bursting of them by bending and stretching of the trunk. They are then 16–18 mm long, already possess inner gills and horny jaws, with which they have already gnawed holes into the envelopes before hatching. Their postembryonic development lasts over a year.

**1 b.** This reproductive act, valid for life in freedom, can be maintained under captive life — provided natural conditions are preserved, and namely the temperature in summer does not climb above 17 degrees, and in winter brings on the torpor-sleep — not only in all the laying periods of the specimens captured in the sexually mature state, but also in those of their succeeding generations. Two laying periods are completed in the year, one in spring (mostly April), one in late summer or autumn (mostly September).

**2 a.** When kept at 25–30 degrees C., the male, during the copulation, draws the egg-chains out of the female's cloaca, but does not wind them around the hind thighs, instead letting them lie. This can happen on land, where too-rapid drying-out of the gelatinous envelope hinders the adhering, but mostly happens in the water, where the animals seek coolness from the heat, and too-rapid swelling of the gelatinous envelope prevents the sticking. — The eggs laid into the water develop only to a very small percentage; in consequence of the maceration of their envelopes the nearly colorless embryos become free already after 2 weeks at a stage at which they are 12–14 mm long, still possess outer gills (one on each side) and a yolk-sac, which does not permit them to move about swimming. The becoming-free is supported by trunk-movements of the embryos, which, without consequence within the firm envelope of a land-egg, in the water-egg bring about the tearing of the softened envelope; on the other hand the gnawing-activity at the envelopes is absent, since the horny jaws are not yet developed at this time. The outer gills, properly destined for embryonic respiration, are long, delicate, finely branched, blood-rich and poor in pigment; in the water they become reduced and simplified by resorption, with accompanying crumbling-off of the tips, [becoming] coarser, poorer in capillaries and richer in coloring-matter. The postembryonic development lasts there only 3–4 months. The toads that have proceeded from water-eggs are distinguished by giant growth.

**2 b.** The *Alytes* brought to development without brood-care, partly on land, partly in the water, which derive from the first laying period of their parents spent in high temperature, show on their part, on the occasion of their reproductive activity, no kind of deviation from the normal mode designated under 1 a and 1 b.

**3 a.** But if reproduction without brood-care has become habitual, then the sexes, at the approaching mating season, seek out the water without compulsion, copulate there, and there result 90 to 115 small, yolk-poor eggs, whose larvae, soon driven by hunger, are induced to bend and stretch themselves so violently that they forcibly burst the envelopes already at the stage provided with outer gills. In the adaptation of the embryonic gills to water-respiration, the reduction of the respiring surface now proceeds through resorption alone, no longer also through breaking-off of distal parts. Upon hatching, these larvae, somewhat more strongly pigmented than the previous ones, have no yolk-sac any more, and can therefore at once swim freely and nimbly in the water, for which they are besides made capable by the somewhat broader rudder-tail.

**3 b.** The *Alytes* brought to development without brood-care in the water, which derive from later laying periods of their parents, when the latter had, with respect to their laying instincts, already become permanently altered, again practice no brood-care and lay their eggs

α) without continuation of the experimental conditions, either on land, in case they still derive from relatively early periods, immediately following that first laying season ineffectual for inheritance; or in the water, if they already stemmed from relatively latest laying periods of their parents.

β) With continuation of the experimental conditions they lay their eggs always in the water, whereby the eggs, still always increasing in number, but decreasing in yolk-richness and therefore in size, with thicker gelatinous globe-shell but shorter connecting-thread, [and] the freshly hatched, intensively dark-pigmented larvae in the 4th generation with an again broadened rudder-tail, with three gill-pairs instead of one, the gills themselves without an adaptation-process appearing at once less branched, shorter, more robust, richer in pigment and poorer in blood. Sexually mature males of the third generation possess on thumb and thumb-pad rough, [and] in the fourth generation in addition black-colored callus-formation and hypertrophic arm-musculature.

**4 a.** The eggs laid without brood-care outside the water develop, when left lying on moist ground, normally and only, if they are besides kept warm, in accelerated tempo. But if one brings it about, through the high temperature, that they run through all stages faster than usual, and at the same time, through allowance of only the most indispensable moisture-minimum, that they do not hatch in good time, then one obtains very large eggs, in which the embryos lie 6–7 weeks and at hatching are already 20–24 mm long. If light-exclusion is added to this, then one obtains giant eggs, in which the embryos remain lying toward 10 weeks, up to a size of 31 mm and the differentiation of the rearward extremity-pair. The remaining development up to the metamorphosis proceeds accelerated, in about 5 months. The completed toads are distinguished by dwarf growth.

**4 b.** These dwarf-*Alytes* practice brood-care on their large, only 16 to 19 eggs:

α) Without continuation of the experimental conditions, there hatch from them after over 7 weeks larvae which at 21 mm total length already exhibit hind legs in the form of still undifferentiated, outwardly protruding buds.

β) With continuation of the experimental conditions there results a further increase in size and decrease in number of the just-laid eggs, but no increase in size of the hatch-ripe eggs, from which after nearly 9 weeks larvae nevertheless hatch at an equally advanced stage as in the first experimental generation, namely such with completely finished differentiated hind legs. To the outer factors, which hold back for quite a long time the movements preceding the bursting of the envelope, there is added as inner factor the extraordinarily large and long-persisting yolk-sac, which forces the larvae to rest and gives them so abundant nourishment that they feel no urge to move.

**5 a.** Also through air- and water-currents, which set into strong movement the medium surrounding the land- or water-eggs respectively, further through uninterrupted rotating in the clinostat or weak, intermittent centrifuging, which brings the eggs themselves into movement, the embryos can be held back in the envelopes up to a certain, lesser degree (in water-eggs e.g. up to the resorption of the outer gills). Through combination with warmth and dryness the result, which is already attained through the latter two factors alone, is scarcely heightened; if darkness is added, then the embryos perish.

**5 b.** Only where, in land-eggs, the mechanical agencies combine with warmth and moisture-deficiency, is there to be observed in the next, normally treated generation a hatching delayed by up to 5 or 6 weeks of the then 20–23 mm long, still footless larvae.

**6 a.** The larvae, both from ordinary land-eggs and from those in which the embryos were held back until beyond the normal hatching-time, can be kept alive for some weeks on moist earth instead of in the water: the former, until they have small hind legs, the latter considerably beyond this stage, since they already possess large hind legs when they leave the egg. Through integument-thickening, early development of the skin-glands and lungs, lateral strengthening of the muscle-portion and narrowing of the seam-portion of the tail, as well as through physiological reactions of appropriate kind, the land-larvae adapt themselves to the air-medium. The rest of their development proceeds in water and ends conspicuously early with the metamorphosis, after which the completed toads grow only a little more, hence remain dwarfed.

**6 b.** α) Without continuation of the experimental conditions these adaptation-characters disappear in the next generation, yet with the following exceptions: premature onset of air-breathing (quite especially frequent fetching of breath by the larvae living in the water at the surface) and of the cellular lung-structure; shortening of the postembryonic development as compared with its normal total duration.

β) With continuation of the experimental conditions there results chiefly a considerable heightening of the capacity to hold out on land, linked with a heightening of all the adaptation-characters coming into consideration thereby.

**7 a.** Through premature operative removal from the water-egg, light-exclusion, much cold, air-rich, calm water, and force-feeding [Mästung] following a preceding meager nourishment, it was possible to keep an *Alytes* larva for 4 years and 8 months in the larval state, and previously to obtain from it 18 eggs, which were artificially inseminated with the help of a normal male.

**7 b.** The larvae raised from these, kept without continuation of the experimental conditions, were distinguished by a conspicuously long persistence of the outer gills, are after 2¾ years still at the two-legged stage and make no kind of preparations for metamorphosis.

**8 a.** At 17 degrees C. and below, *Alytes* becomes sexually mature at an age of 2 years, at 25 degrees and above already at 1 year.

**8 b.** The descendants of the latter, kept warm, require — even when the just-laid eggs are already brought back into cool temperature — 1½ years until sexual maturity; and just as long, however, [require] also the likewise-treated descendants of such toads which were brought into the high temperature only as sexually mature animals, and reproduced after they had lived there for some time.

## 9. Inheritance of the Enforced Reproductive and Developmental Change Has Taken Place

α) in every case where the parents had already acquired an immanent instinct-variation, and

β) where the germ-plasm could be reached by the altering influence during its maturation;

γ) in one case, namely the growing of giant eggs, which contain embryos of an abnormally advanced developmental stage, also where the germ-plasm could be reached by the altering influence only before its maturation.

δ) In some cases of adaptation of a functional nature: that of the "land-larvae"; that of the "water-eggs" with their thickened jelly, increased and modified gills, broadened fin-seams on the larvae hatching from them; that of the sexually mature male arising from the latter, with its nuptial pad and strengthened arm muscles.

Upon return of the second and the following generations into primary conditions, the acquired change has reappeared in an attenuated and ever-more-fading degree, the longer the time that elapses between that return and the breeding-period in question. With continuance of the experimental conditions, however, the change has been transmitted to the next and to the later generations at least to an equal, mostly to a strengthened, degree.

## 10. Inheritance of the Enforced Reproductive and Developmental Change Has Not Taken Place

Inheritance of the enforced reproductive and developmental change has (with consideration of the exception mentioned under 9 γ) not taken place where it had not yet been engraphically fixed in the parents:

α) in all land- and water-eggs brought to development without brood-care, from the first breeding-period of the experimental animals, spent under elevated temperature;

β) in all breeding-periods that took place only under altered moisture conditions, not also altered temperature conditions;

γ) in artificial insemination of the Alytes eggs and their rearing on land or in water;

δ) in partial neoteny, where the reproduction of the experimental animals had taken place only after metamorphosis; ε) in all cases, even with deeply intervening developmental changes, of so labile a nature that they are capable of being freshly induced from the external medium in every generation: thus the stage at which the larvae hatch from land- and from water-eggs; the duration of their postembryonal development; the size which the full-toads attain from water-eggs, from land-eggs, and after a postembryonal development spent wholly or only partly in the water; those adaptations of the land-larvae which concern the muscle- and seam-part of the tail, the integument, the glands, and the motor expressions.

## VIII. Explanation of the Figures.
