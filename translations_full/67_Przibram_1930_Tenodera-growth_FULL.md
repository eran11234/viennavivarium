## I. The Rearing of *Tenodera aridifolia*.

On repeated occasions I have received egg-packets of a Japanese praying mantis from Japanese colleagues to whom I had communicated about my mantis experiments. Not always was the sender named, and not all cocoons had yielded successes. In order therefore to pass over no one, I here express to all senders collectively my heartfelt thanks for the consignments.

The animals belonged to the species *Tenodera* (= *Paratenodera*) *aridifolia* Stoll. (according to the naming in the 50th Delivery of "Das Tierreich", de Gruyter 1927). Their keeping proceeds entirely according to the conditions ascertained for other praying mantises, namely *Sphodromantis bioculata*, which are to be drawn from the earlier publications about the rearing (I. 1906, II. 1907, III. 1909, IV. 1912 etc.,

see 1929). The Japanese species is almost just as resistant as the Egyptian one, yet a resting period for the eggs seems to be necessary for it, so that reproductively capable offspring may arise, for from the 2nd generation reared on through the winter no copulations and egg-packets could be obtained. The colouring, however, was in no way different from that of the European *Mantis religiosa* observed by us under the same circumstances (II. 1907), as opposed to the summer generation. All imagines had brownish, less light colouring with a green lateral margin of the forewings. Green larval stages occurred, alongside yellowish-brown, rose-tinged, and longitudinally-striped ones; isolated specimens died off or even yielded typically-coloured full-grown animals; at most green colouring was observed on their legs and at the edge of the pronotum. A smaller-hatching race of the same species behaved likewise. It reached me via America through Dr. C. Ishikawa, College of Agriculture, Imperial University, Tokyo. In consequence of the lack of well-distinguishable colour-races and of the impossibility of rearing more than *one* fertile generation in the year, the attempt to investigate the inheritance of colours, as had been carried out for *Sphodromantis* (1909, S. 569), was abandoned. Regrettably, the attempts at artificial fertilization, in 1914 also undertaken with very promising mantises, led to no result, so that more detailed information about the occurrence of "pseudogamy" (das. S. 605) could not be furnished.

In contrast, the cast skins gathered in the year 1914 and since then kept dry have proved themselves usable for weighing just as well, those of 1928/1929 being weighed soon after their casting. The resumption of the rearing permitted weighings of whole animals as larvae, nymphs and imagines, to be made immediately after the moult, which, with regard to the deviations in the behaviour of the length growth, to be discussed, of the Japanese praying mantis as opposed to the Egyptian one, seemed advisable, in order to be able to ascertain with certainty the relationship of length to growth, which at the same time permitted most interesting glimpses. The cultures of the year 1914 were based on 10 egg-packets, those of 1928/1929 on the 2 received directly from Japan and the 3 of the generation reared by us. The cultures are designated, after the egg-packets, as A—F and N (= Nana, dwarf, yet later growing up); the two directly obtained packets of the later cultures were 28a and 28b. Of the hatched larvae of the 28acocoon, Nr. 1—5 reached the imaginal stage, of 28b Nr. 6—8. Of these, Nr. 1 and Nr. 4 were males. Only the latter proved suitable for mating. It produced for me, with female Nr. 2, three, with Nr. 7 two egg-layings. These packets were designated by the number of the laying mother with Arabic, and by the ordinal number of the sequence with Roman numerals. The individual specimens of the hatched offspring received "Ex." with an Arabic number added, while individual specimens from the directly imported cocoons always [received] "Nr." such-and-such.

The packets A, B, C, I, N, 28a, 28b, ♀ 2 I, 2 II, 7 I are reared in separate containers, mostly brought to hatching in the prismatic Müller egg-cage (cf. 1910, S. 251 or 129) and kept therein up to the isolation of the individual specimens; G and H were kept together, likewise D with E and F. Closer details are to be taken from the Tables, namely from which moult onward individual animals were kept entirely isolated. The cultures 7 I and 2 II were fed more poorly than the others, but without particular deviations from the moulting-number, weight, or duration of transformation coming here to observation. The latter series was moreover set up at elevated moisture, relatively 60% against about 25% for the others, and showed just as little a deviation. Of the *Tenodera* C and GH reared constantly at elevated temperature, 35°C instead of otherwise 25°C, we shall yet come to speak. The number of observed moults was almost throughout 8, seldom did 9 occur, without known factors of an external nature — temperature, moisture, feeding, injury — or of an internal one — sex, descent — being able to be made responsible for it.

## II. The Doubling of the Weight of the Animals from Moult to Moult.

The doubling of the body weight of the animals from one moult to another, found purely empirically by us in pursuance of the idea of a dependence of the length increase on the mass increase, has, for the Egyptian praying mantis *Sphodromantis bioculata* Burm. (I. 1912, S. 689), since also been found in the Indian stick insect (*Carausius = Dixippus morosus* Eidmann 1924, Titschak 1924, Teissier 1928, 842) (cf. below VII.). The occurrence of a doubled growth-step for the fourth stadium of the silkworm (I. 1912, S. 709) has likewise received its confirmation (Nobumasa Yagi 1926); the fifth stadium, too, seems likewise to be accompanied only by its doubling, as I previously surmised after the data of Luciani and La Monaco, not by *one* doubling but by two (of which, however, one is lost again at the pupation). A detailed discussion of the conditions in various arthropods I reserve for a further communication, in which I shall have presented yet the material of other mantids, which is in process of being worked up, and in which also, it is hoped, the weighings of successive moulting-stages in crustaceans carried out from another quarter will have been published (skins of *Carcinus maenas* — Teissier 1928, 297, Anm.). Weighings of the living animals in small aluminium cages covered with Müller's gauze, whose tare weight was naturally brought into deduction, took place in the culture ♀ 2. Cocoon I, however, only from the 7th moult onward. The intention was originally directed at the re-examination of the data made for the stick insects (Eidmann 1924, S. 578, Titschak 1924 I, S. 443), according to which, from the imaginal moult up to the deposition of the first eggs, again a weight increase of the whole animal occurs. Since no egg-deposition came about on the part of the offspring of the *Tenodera* 2, this re-examination remained unexecuted. We can, however, use the gathered data for the answering of the question whether, from one moult to the other, a doubling of the total weight is present.

Three specimens (2 I, specimen 2, 3, 15, see Table D), which were weighed immediately after the 7th and again after the 8th moult, without the transformation to the imago having yet taken place at the latter, gave in the cross-section a growth quotient of 2.070; twelve specimens, which had metamorphosed at the 8th moult, gave an average quotient of 2.274; three specimens, which only formed up to the imago at the 9th moult, gave the quotient 2.091 for the last interval. The general cross-section of all 18 quotients ascertained through comparison of the weights yields 2.189. For *Sphodromantis* the average 2.09 had been found from a much greater number of quotients, from the average moulting-intervals of 38 specimens 2.22 (cf. IV. 1912, S. 689 resp. 725). The two last moulting-intervals of *Sphodromantis*, 8./9. and 9./10., gave on average from all animals reared that far 2.05 resp. 1.81. The 7./8. interval had, owing to the more frequent occurrence of standstills following quadruplings, with *Sphodromantis* the average quotient 1.57. In the weighings of the *Tenodera*, neither standstills nor quadruplings occurred. The largest quotient, animal 11, 7./8. moult, amounted to 2.658, the smallest, animal 15, 7./8. moult, 1.803. As the cited numbers show, the doubling of the total weight in the Japanese praying mantis is at least just as clearly to be recognized as in the Egyptian species. We are therefore entitled, in case both the weighing of cast skins from series and of *Tenodera* doublings from skin to skin yield, in first approximation, a similar behaviour of the total weights, to assume [this] without having to weigh the living animals after each moult. It is precisely this that is a great advantage, that the cast skins can be weighed at any time, whereas the living animals may, for our purposes, only be weighed in the fasting condition immediately after each moult; moreover, each living animal must, at least in the later stages, be brought singly onto the scale, whereas with skins of the same moulting-stage it is possible by cumulative weighings, and in the early stages [it] even appears necessary, for the purpose of gaining a more correct value of the very small weights.

## III. The Weight Increase of the Cast Skins.

The attempt, undertaken in connection with the *Sphodromantis* measurements, to use, for the investigation of a doubling of their weight from moult to moult, old, admittedly moist-preserved cast skins of self-reared crustaceans, foundered on the impossibility of bringing the cast skins removed from the glasses to the same degree of dryness. So much the more joyfully was I therefore surprised, in the weighing of dry-preserved mantis skins, to find not the slightest difference compared with skins weighed shortly after the casting. So could the considerable material from the year 1914 be worked up together with that newly added in 1928. For *Tenodera* there stood at disposal 941 skins from 302 specimens. There have been ascertained 68 mutually independent quotients of the weight increase. Thereby the reproach of too great a heterogeneity of the data used for the ascertaining of the average quotient, such as was raised against the *Sphodromantis* experiments (Titschak 1926 II, S. 526), will hopefully be averted from the outset.

Let us look first at the cross-section from all quotients (Table B); it amounts to 2.216, agreeing thus with the 2.189 found from the animal weights still in the first decimal place, since on taking the correction it yields 2.2. This value lies thus likewise not far from a doubling, and one can, in first approximation, again speak of such a one from skin to skin, just as of that from the animal weight from moult to moult. One could believe that the deviation from 2.0 is insignificant and to be traced back to some chance or other. If we group all the quotients of the collective cultures in classes of 0.2 on either side of 2.0, then there shows itself a rather flat, approximately symmetrical curve with the summit 2.2. If we content ourselves with the coarser grouping in classes of 0.4, then the summit point appears already in the group immediately enclosing 2.0. But with the individual specimens (Table C) there appears, on just such a summarizing, the summit above 2.4. That this is not to be attributed to weighing-errors is already shown clearly by the analogous behaviour of the animal weights of the same specimens. Nevertheless one could here also think of the chance-quality of the material itself. Let us now look at the cultures individually, then we find the cross-sections of the moulting-quotients available for each particular [culture] mostly in the vicinity of 2.2 and only in the cultures C and G—H quite a little under 2. These two series, kept at 35°, could however not be reared up to maturity. There are lacking to them therefore the later moulting-quotients. Is this circumstance to blame for the lower value of the cross-section, then it would be to be expected that the first moulting-intervals would in general have a smaller increase-quotient than the later ones.

Von den Zuchten — Of the rearings, the most regularly proceeding one, A 1914, is best suited for a closer determination of the character of the curve which represents the increase of the mass of shed skins with advancing moult number. This curve is not a simple exponential curve, as is shown by the distinctly curved course of the line drawn through the logarithms of the quotients, whose graphical representation, however, did not let us recognize anything further with certainty. It could be explained as the sum of two processes proceeding exponentially with different velocity, of the form *mˣa* + *nʸb*, where *x* = 2 or somewhat less and *y* would be over 2. The quotients of the first moulting intervals, going below 2, are namely, with regard to Teissier's statement (1928, p. 299), possibly to be ascribed not merely to inaccuracies. Teissier found, on weighing the shed skins of the mealworm *Tenebrio molitor*, that these became only 1.8 times heavier from one moult to the next, while the larvae themselves exhibited a doubling of their weight. Therefore in the praying mantises too the weight of the skin need not be exactly proportional to the weight of the animal, as we had hitherto assumed in first approximation. Otherwise the "course" (»Gang«) in the quotients of the skins would always have to appear also in the total weights, which according to the data for *Sphodromantis* does not quite hold true. Also the quadruplings, standstills of the total weight need not come to expression in the shed skins, as the table for *Sphodromantis* (1912, p. 723 B a) teaches. ## IV. Correlations between growth quotients, temperature, and interval duration.

The rise of the quotients in the successive moulting intervals suggested examining whether the more substantial growth of the middle, and as a rule also the last, stages might not be connected with the likewise rising curve of the time used for each following moult. A glance at the table for *Sphodromantis* (1912, 723) shows us at once that the increase of the interval duration proceeds quite differently from that of the growth quotients for the skin weights. Only the 7th/8th moult shows an essential increase in both series. The preceding intervals, on the other hand, are of almost equal duration. The two last moults, which in various specimens lead to the imago, are precisely those that take the longest for preparation, yet do not have the largest, indeed even the smallest, quotients. Now these decreases are, to be sure, connected with the use of a part of the gained mass needed for the imaginal transformation, as can be concluded from the analogous behavior of the animal weight and from the well-known, only much more considerable, weight decrease of the holometabolous insects. But the temperature under which the *Sphodromantis* of the first weighings were reared was not rigorously constant, and therefore the lack of the correlation between interval duration and quotient might not have come to appearance clearly enough. The *Tenodera* of the 1914 rearings were kept in our strictly constantly maintained "temperature chambers" (cf. Przibram 1913, Five-Year Report), on the one hand at 25°, the mean temperature also previously valid for *Sphodromantis*, on the other hand at 35° C, namely the rearings C and G—H. Especially instructive is the comparison of the quotients at the values for C and G—H, which had dropped to almost half the interval duration of the 25°-rearings, for the 2nd/3rd moult, with the quite similar quotients at the same moults of, e.g., the A-series and also of the 2 I-series of 1928/1929 kept (admittedly somewhat less well) constant at 25° C. Shortening of the developmental duration through temperature elevation thus has no influence on the magnitude of the quotient. If we compare, within a single rearing, duration and

## V. The length- and breadth-growth of the prothoracic shield.
### (After measurements by Leonore Brecher.)

Under the assumption hitherto justified, that the weight is proportional to the volume in the skins of the mantids, with strictly proportional growth a given length would have to be proportional to the third root from the mass, and the quotient of analogous lengths at successive moults equal to the third root from the quotient of the masses. With exact doubling of the weight from moult to moult the latter is ³√2 = 1.26. ## VI. The Similarity of Growth in Both Sexes.

In the year 1912 I wrote concerning the Egyptian praying mantis (p. 697), following weighings of the freshly-hatched imagines: "The more considerable size of the female praying mantis as compared with the male thus stands in a certain correlation to the greater number of moults of the female sex. Yet it is shown in Table H, among the females with 9 moults, that these, although on the average smaller than their fellows of the same sex with 10 moults, have nevertheless by no means remained at the size of the male with 9 moults. Conversely, the males with 10 moults are the smallest of their sex, which is explained by their character as injured specimens."

In contrast to *Sphodromantis*, whose males mostly already stand out by their smaller size, I noticed in *Tenodera* only rarely such a sexual difference. It lay near at hand to ascertain the number of moults of both sexes of *Tenodera* and to follow up its connection with the similarity in weight and length of the *Tenodera* male and female. The examination of the records yielded the expected result, that the number of moults is the same in both sexes of *Tenodera*, usually 8, in contrast to the greater number of moults of the *Sphodromantis* females relative to the males of one and the same brood.

Concerning the heritability of the number of moults in *Sphodromantis* I shall report in a later publication. How slight the size-differences of the sexes of *Tenodera* are emerges from the weights of the just-hatched imagines of our 2 I-brood 1928/1929 (Table G): 3 females with 8 moults had only a 1.014-times greater average weight than 9 males with the same number of moults. 2 females with 9 moults had on the average a 1.383-times greater weight than 1 male likewise with 9 moults. In *Sphodromantis* the average weight of 3 imaginal females with 9 moults was 1.509-times greater than that of 6 males moulted 9 times. Since in this species, however, the female as a rule has one moult more than the male, we have to compare 10-times-moulted females with the 9-times-moulted males. 5 *Sphodromantis* females with 10 moults weighed on the average 1.656-times heavier than the 9-times-moulted males. The weights of the last exuviae of *Tenodera* 1914 DEF (Table E), probably with throughout 8 moults, gave ♀ : ♂ = 1.196; *Sphodromantis* (calculated according to 1912, Table C) with 9 moults ♀ : ♂ = 1.330; 10 moults : ♂ 9 moults 1.535; both sexes with 10 moults ♀ : ♂ = 1.208. Once again the correlation between the differing number of moults of the sexes and the heightening of the sexual difference comes to light. Besides the weighings, length measurements of the prothorax carried out on *Tenodera* imagines, for the purpose of comparing the sexes, are at my disposal. The parent-animals 28a and b, with throughout 8 moults, yield, from the comparison of 6 females with 2 males, a merely 1.06-times greater prothorax-length of the former. The analogous ratio-figure for their descendants 1928/1929 2 I, 5 females, 8 males each with 8 moults, calculates as 1.09; for 2 females, 1 male each with 9 moults as 1.07; from all the measured imagines 2 I, further 7 I and 2 II, again 1 male each with 1.07. In *Tenodera*, then, the sexes are, as regards weight and thorax-length as well as number of moults, much more similar to one another than in *Sphodromantis*. To the same number of moults there corresponds almost the same weight. On the other hand, the size-differences, just as in *Sphodromantis*, cannot after all be traced back to the number of moults alone.

## VII. Critical Remarks on the Significance of the Doubling.

Several authors have, despite exact confirmation of our discovery of the increase of insect-stadia in powers of two, felt prompted to dispute its more general significance.

Titschak (1926, p. 555) writes: "If body-length and girth increased, the insect had to strip off the rigid chitin — as a knight his armour. Proceeding from this view, Przibram had already established that in *Sphodromantis* a doubling of the weight occasions a moult." "For *Carausius morosus* an equally great ratio has been found by me and Eidmann. As fine as these discoveries were, they have nevertheless not withstood further re-examinations, such as my moth-investigations represent. As we have seen, 1. the moult of the caterpillars occurs, according to the experimental conditions, at very different body-enlargements; 2. the body-enlargement in the successive moulting-intervals becomes ever smaller during life; 3. too small body-enlargements can be made up for in the next larval stage, so that no definite body-enlargement is due to each age-stage either. There is also added the fact that the clothes-moth caterpillars on a certain nourishment can, through months, indeed years, regularly lose in size at each moult." Although now in his summary, p. 565, there is to be read, 7. "the animal seeks to make up again at the next moult for too slight an increase in weight", yet Titschak, by his own statement (p. 526), found the weighing "in the case of the moth-caterpillar, on account of its smallness, not possible". He "was therefore compelled" to "content himself with measurements". "From all the head-capsules that an animal strips off during its life", he "detached the praefrons + postclypeus (see Fig. 1)", there p. 527, "a fairly flat triangular little chitin-piece, glued these onto carrier-slides in sequence and protected them with little cover-glasses against dust; from base and height, which were measured, I then computed the area of the praefrons + postclypeus". Not the weight of the entire animal, nor that of the cast-off skin, but the surface of a restricted body-part had been measured. The average of all the values ascertained gave the quotient 1.6, which would correspond to a surface-increase (³√2)² = 1.59 or a weight-doubling (p. 529). If the magnitudes of the quotients exhibit a "course", then this surely means that the increase of the head does not proceed at the same velocity as that of the whole animal. Exactly the same thing has Teissier (1928, p. 297) observed in Titschak's first experimental object Dixippus, namely that the head increases less in relative length than the trunk, only ²/₃ as fast as the individual thorax- and abdominal-segments. Hence it would have to follow for Titschak that in Dixippus too the weight-increase declines with the successive moulting-intervals, which contradicts his own weighings as well as those of Eidmann and Teissier! For the rest, I described moults with weight-decrease under hunger-conditions already in crustaceans (1907) and have repeatedly called attention to this phenomenon, likewise said of *Sphodromantis* "in some cases there appears, in place of a doubling of the weight (namely of the animals), from one moult to the next a quadrupling, which is then mostly followed by a standstill during the next moulting-period" (P. and M. 1912, p. 712). Hunger-animals one may not, as regards the relation of mass to length, place in parallel with normally-nourished ones. Pirquet (System of Nutrition, 1917) set up the maintenance of a definite relation between sitting-height and weight in the human child as a measure of its health, since every illness, like poor nourishment, alters the relation. On the head-capsules of the mealworm Tenebrio, Teissier (1928, p. 903) likewise observed, under hunger, the strong shifting of the relation of mass to length.¹

The objections of Yagi (1926, p. 30) against the general applicability of my growth-law, which he confirms for the silkworm, refer to an analysis, undertaken by the Japanese investigator, of Eidmann's curve for the daily growth of Dixippus. He perceives on the latter's figure a depression of the weight-increase within each moulting-interval and means that for this reason two growth-cycles are present and, according to my theory, two cell-division-steps are to be expected, whereas only a single weight-doubling occurs. To me the weights ascertained between two moults in the non-fasting state of the insects seem unsuited for so exact an analysis. For the rest, I long ago abandoned the idea that it is throughout the "division-steps" that are decisive for the doubling (cf. Sztern 1914, Przibram 1922, Form und Formel 1922, p. 68, Deus geometricans 1923, Quanta in biology 1929). The fundamental phenomenon is the mass-doubling, which makes itself felt even without cell-multiplication.

## VIII. Summary.

1. The Japanese praying mantis *Tenodera aridifolia* Stoll. moulted (at 25° C) in both sexes 8, occasionally 9, times.

2. The weight of animals weighed sober after the 7th, 8th, and 9th moult was doubled from moult to moult (without there having resulted thereby any conspicuous irregularities, quadruplings, standstills, as in *Sphodromantis bioculata* Burm.).

3. The weight-increase of the cast-off skins likewise consisted in a doubling from moult to moult; a "course" was to be noted in the increase-quotients of the successive skins, in that these quotients increased somewhat toward the time of the nymph, but then sank again or tapered off into a not yet securely interpretable "Verdreifachung" [trebling].

4. The developmental duration shortened by keeping at 35° C had no influence on the maintenance of the mass-doubling of the cast-off skins; yet at 25° C, within one and the same brood, specimens with smaller quotient at lesser duration of a moulting-interval, and such with larger quotient at longer duration, came under observation; a direct relation between time-duration and quotient-magnitude in the series of successive moults did not exist.

5. The measurements of the lengths and breadths of the prothorax on the cast-off skins, carried out by Leonore Brecher, show, in contrast to analogous ones on *Sphodromantis*, that the lengths of the prothorax-skins in successive stadia do not exhibit the quotient cube-root-of-two, but rather an essentially larger one, 1.35 instead of 1.26; the breadth of the thoracic skin has a correspondingly smaller quotient 1.18, so that the surface of the prothoracic shield, with the quotient 1.59, is after all proportional to the square of the cube-root of the mass-quotient 2.

6. The sexes of *Tenodera* have, with equal number of moults, almost equal weight and almost equal thoracic length, in contrast to the more considerable number of moults, mass, and prothorax-length of the female occurring within one and the same brood of *Sphodromantis*.

7. The final weight of the cast-off *Tenodera* skins (and probably also of the animals) represents, on account of the increase of the quotients in the successive moults, despite the lower number of moults, the same power of two of the initial weight as in *Sphodromantis*.
