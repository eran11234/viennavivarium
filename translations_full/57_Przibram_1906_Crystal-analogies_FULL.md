## I. Introduction.

The relations between crystals and organisms are manifold. Many constituents of living bodies are formed from substances capable of crystallization, and sometimes genuine crystals are embedded in the animal or plant body. Even the genuine protein substances can, as we know from the investigations of Hofmeister and his pupils, be brought to crystallize under particular conditions. None of these relations come into consideration for the analogies between crystals and organisms. As such, only those phenomena can be regarded which exhibit a similar regularity in crystallization and in organic form-building.

Although the existence of far-reaching analogies had already become clear to me several years ago,¹) I nevertheless wanted to wait with the publi-

The comparison, promised therein, of the restitution processes in crystals and organisms shall serve as the point of departure for the presentation of the analogies in general. It is a pleasant awareness to me that in this I am sure of the agreement of a number of renowned researchers who, like Roux and Goebel, have already drawn conclusions from my experiments.

The numerous further points of comparison between crystals and organisms (growth, symmetry-relations, movement, self-division, etc.) will form the further continuation of the comparative investigation, and here I shall chiefly have to enter into the fundamental observations of O. v. Schron (Naples) and O. Lehmann (Karlsruhe), to both of whom I am obligated with great thanks for kind reception on the occasion of a visit to these cities and for the sending of their publications. Regrettably, v. Schron has so far not been able to bring himself to a pragmatic publication of his extraordinarily important and thorough investigations on the growth of crystals; thus the communications that have penetrated into the public have only been apt to evoke a false conception of his works. No one who is interested in the borderlands of the natural-scientific disciplines should, during a stay in Naples, neglect to have the developmental stages of the crystals demonstrated to him by Schron.

Completely independently of Schron, the well-known molecular physicist O. Lehmann, in consequence of his occupation with flowing and liquid crystals, has arrived at results which in many points confirm the observations of Schron (apparently spontaneous mobility, self-division of certain crystals). In the very most recent time O. Lehmann sent out a lithographed circular with an invitation to collaboration on the problems stimulated by him, which closes: »Whether the peculiar processes observed in the apparently living crystals are not merely outwardly similar to the corresponding processes in the lowest living beings, but rest upon a real kinship of the physical forces operating therein, the physiologist and biologist will have to decide. The interest of the physicist is chiefly captivated by the fact that a derivation of those processes from hitherto known [phenomena] appears impossible. United forces may well succeed in creating clarity in all these directions.«

My work thus comes, in a certain sense, as an answer to this invitation, although the first section of the theme, now presented here, was already concluded before receipt of the circular and at first predominantly treats points of comparison other than those last meant by Lehmann.

## III. The Problem of Proportional Form-Regulation.

### A. Presentation of the Analogies.

From of old one has been accustomed to set the crystals, as individuals of the inanimate world, alongside or in opposition to the "living individuals," animals and plants.

The "indivisibility," i.e. the undividedness, of organisms as well as of crystals may originally have been conceived as such, just as we now ascribe it to the molecules: namely that, although a division of them is indeed possible, its products (analogous to the atoms) — quite apart from their smaller size — no longer exhibit the same properties as the whole unit.

Today, now that we know that lower animals and plants can be multiplied by artificial partition, that under certain circumstances several reduced whole-formations arise from one egg, and that even injured crystals "heal," it is precisely divisibility that appears as an essential property of these bodies, since the development of the partial pieces into whole specimens lets us presuppose a "self-activity" working through internally located forces.

We can nevertheless retain the concept of the "individual" if we see its undividedness to lie in the fact that the parts do not remain parts permanently, but become wholes by their own activity.

For the practical recognition of the individual ones, we make use, as before, of their form, i.e. the arrangement of the particles in the system (both words used in the most general sense).

Form, as well as mass, also belongs to non-individualized, physico-chemical systems, and it may therefore be attempted to subordinate the phenomena of form-regulation, as special cases, under a general mechanics (statics and dynamics).

If, on a system, one perceives no change in successive periods of time, then one says the system is in equilibrium.

If a system returns, after a disturbance, back into the state that existed before that disturbance, then one calls the equilibrium a stable one (a).

If a system, after a disturbance, does not return back into the previous state, but passes over into another one than the one present during the disturbance, then one calls the equilibrium a labile one (b).

When a system, after a disturbance, finally does not return to the previously existing state, but instead remains, even after the disturbance, in the state as during (the last moment) of the disturbance, then the equilibrium is called **indifferent** (c).

A disturbance of (static) equilibrium can either leave the mass and form of the system unchanged, or only the mass, or only the form, or finally neither mass nor form.

I. If the mass and form of a system are to remain unchanged under a disturbance, then the disturbance pertains to the relation (the position) of the whole system to its surroundings.

As examples of these cases, the familiar cases of center-of-gravity support of rigid bodies may serve:

a) Stable eq., return after the disturbance: a body suspended above the center of gravity.

b) Labile eq., transition into another position: a body suspended below the center of gravity.

c) Indiff. eq., persistence in the disturbance position: a body suspended at the center of gravity.

II. If the mass of a body remains unchanged while the form is disturbed, then this means (according to our definition of form) the same as that the position of the mass-particles relative to one another is altered.

As examples serve the kinds of cohesion of bodies:

a) St., return after the disturbance: Elastic bodies.

b) Lab., transition into another position: Brittle bodies.

c) Ind., persistence in the disturbance position: Ductile bodies.

III. If the form of a system remains unchanged while the mass is subjected to changes, then this can only happen in such a way that, in two successive intervals of time, each departing mass-particle is replaced by one of the same kind (qualitative change of mass). But with the mere establishment of a common behavior of these three groups of bodies, little has yet been done for the understanding of "self-regulation." It is, after all, the endeavor of natural science to trace back more complicated processes to the cooperation of simpler factors, to sketch for us, through "complete description" (Kirchhoff), a vivid picture of them. We may regard the analysis of a natural process as concluded when we have convinced ourselves that the removal of one factor made responsible in each case (one already otherwise known to us) leaves over a system that now exhibits the operation of the remaining factors made responsible. Toward this end we are aided not merely by mental operations, but above all by the experiments, which permit us in this way more than an adequate description, namely an energetic mastery of nature.

Upon being dismembered, the part-pieces are at first capable of a certain rounding-off, but gradually — and indeed, as must be especially emphasized, without any nourishment needing to be available — they again take on the specific form (whereby mitotic nuclear divisions have not been demonstrated).

This phenomenon, designated by Morgan as "Morphallaxis," is distinguished from the analogous behavior of the "flowing" crystals by the fact that the restoration of the form requires a measurable time, whereas the flowing crystals react with almost immediate form-regulation. We shall hardly go astray if we trace this difference back to the fact that in the crystals, owing to the homogeneity, every particle can be employed at any arbitrary site for the restoration of the form, whereas in the inhomogeneous organisms different particles can come into use only at particular sites. Also, the still-less-fluid (more viscous) aggregate state may already prevent rapid rearrangements.

As is well known, the living plasma finds itself in a so-called colloidal aggregate state, which one formerly held to be opposite to the crystalline, until Hofmeister succeeded in furnishing proof of the crystallizability of protein bodies. These crystals have the property of swellability, i.e. of enlarging their volume by taking up water, without giving up their form. Since the living plasma finds itself in a similar swellable state, the question seemed interesting to me whether, from experiments on protein crystals, a further approximation to the phenomenon of "Morphallaxis" in crystals could be brought about (Przibram, 1904, p. 580).

Hemoglobin crystals from horse blood, prepared according to the method of Micko, yielded excellent experimental material. They form macroscopically visible, wine-yellow, rhombus-shaped thin platelets, which according to Lang belong to the rhombic system. A few crystals were brought, in their nutrient solution, onto a slide, burst apart by pressure of the cover-glass, and exposed to the action of water for so long until the at-first merely swollen-and-rounded burst-pieces had suffered a dissolution of their corners and finally only roundish pieces of the formerly perfectly rhombic crystal remained over. Only during the advancing dissolution was nutrient solution again substituted, in that the water was removed on the one side of the liquid accumulation by means of a strip of filter paper, and on the other a drop of hemoglobin solution was added with the pipette. The uninterrupted microscopic observation yielded the following (Fig. 8): the torso began to clothe itself with rhombus-faces from two opposite sides; in the interior of the crystal liquid movements were meanwhile visible, and the newly formed parts distinguished themselves by a lighter color from the original parent-piece. For the position of the latter, the small, spherical inclusions that occurred furnished welcome marks. With advancing restitution of the outer form, in place of the two still-unfinished corners convex (drop-like) bulges were visible, until these gave way, upon the completion of the rhombic shape, to sharp corners as well. In the course of the form-restitution the crystal had as a whole enlarged itself a little, and one might therefore have believed that it was here simply a matter of a regeneration of the corners.

Now, however (as already mentioned above), the coloration of the newly formed substance is a lighter one, and one sees, even after the completion of the rhombus-shape, the old crystal-portion lying within the new rhombic boundary. But this darker part too now possesses a rhombic form!¹

For the reshaping of the torso into a rhombus, 1 hour 20 minutes had been needed in this particular case; these swellable crystals thus no longer undertake any immediate form-regulation, but are able, with expenditure of time, to bring about a rearrangement of their particles into a whole-formation.

That, during this reshaping in the presence of nutrient solution, they moreover still grow, heightens still further their similarity to the animals capable of Morphallaxis (cf. e.g. Planarians, Morgan).

The capacity to fuse, under the formation of a unitary axis, like the "flowing" crystals mentioned above, has also been observed in organisms (Hydra, sea-urchin eggs), and again the condition is the possibility of setting the axes parallel; otherwise differently oriented "concrescence-twins" result.

Systems that contain solid, insoluble, and non-displaceable parts can fulfill the demand of a return of a part into the previous form-state only when at the same time an increase of mass is made possible for them. They then fulfill also the demand of the restoration of the mass (cf. p. 22: IV α, β). This phenomenon of "Regeneration s. str." has been known far longer than the processes hitherto discussed: already in antiquity one knew of the re-generation of broken-off lizard-tails and the like.

The regenerative capacity of the crystals we can resolve into two factors: 1) The capacity to draw molecules out of the nutrient solution, and indeed differently in different directions, in such a way that an enlargement of its form comes about, i.e. growth. 2) The capacity to deposit new substance preferentially at those sites where an injury has occurred.

The first point corresponds to the definition of the crystal given by Lehmann (1904, p. 182): "A crystal is an anisotropic body which is able to grow in its solution," or more precisely, since in the growth of the crystal the newly attaching layers are anisotropic in exactly the same manner as the growing nucleus, "which is able to exert molecular directive force." It therefore does not need, here, where it is a matter of regeneration, to be analyzed further.¹

The essential identity of the regenerative healing of crystals with their normal growth was demonstrated by Spezia through experiments on quartz: if a quartz crystal or a quadrant-shaped sector of such a one was notched at the edge, and indeed in such a way that some notches ran in the direction of the longest (principal) axis of the crystal, others perpendicular to it, and still others in intermediate positions, then those perpendicular to the principal axis were filled in most rapidly, because the growth of the crystal is precisely greatest in the direction of the principal axis, those parallel to the principal axis [were filled] most slowly, and the intermediate positions were healed in correspondingly intermediate times.

If we now return to the injured crystals in a supersaturated solution (the ordinary case of regeneration), then we have gained the insight for this that the crystal would, even without increase of mass, change its shape — and indeed in the direction of the injury, at the cost of the other parts — by depositing material. Since, however, it is supplied with new material, it will be able to maintain its form and the size of the other parts, or even to grow. In the direction of the injury the directional force of the crystal will be able to exert itself the more strongly in proportion as the fracture surface in question is the more remote from the corresponding crystal surface that would be in equilibrium with the surface tension.

Analogously to our procedure with the crystals, we decompose the regeneration-capability of the organisms into two factors: 1) the capability of undergoing, out of the nourishment (which, if it is not already fluid, is first liquefied by the organism, or at least worked up into an emulsion), an increase of mass of its own form, which appears differentiated in various directions through the appearance of chemically distinct parts (organs); and 2) again the preferential growth in the direction of the injury. The first point again contains the definition of the organism as a system endowed with organs and growth.

The difference as against the crystals lies in the composition out of organs; the presence of several active chemical constituents brings with it the peculiar dynamic equilibrium of the organisms.

The growth of the organisms can be decomposed into three processes (Davenport, 1899, S. 280):

"Growth is increase in size and can result from the multiplication either of the formed substance through secretion, or of the plasma through assimilation, or of the enchylema (cell-sap [Zellsaftes]) through imbibition." The increase of the formed (solid) substance is not autonomous (unless it happens to be a matter of crystalline precipitates); the capacity for imbibition, as we have seen, is, as a consequence of the state of aggregation, shared also by protein crystals; there remains, as the characteristic growth-form of life, assimilation. This consists in the fact that the various chemical plasmas are able to build their own substance out of a (possibly uniform) nourishment. It is evident that organized systems cannot be offered any nourishment that would contain the various substances ready-made and in the correct localization relative to one another for the formation in question — unless one wished to regard the fusion of two individuals into one as the uptake of nourishment; but here, then, a growth without preceding assimilation is also possible. In the ordinary processes of nutrition, mixed substances are offered which themselves contain only parts of the plasma-substances. For growth, the plasma must therefore first carry out an election among the various substances, and then build them up into the substance like itself.

The crystals which grow out of a uniform solution of their own substance can naturally carry out no election; a greater achievement it already is when, out of a mixture of different substances, only the isomorphic ones are used for the growth of a single crystal; finally, however, in special cases the crystal is able to draw the substances it requires out of other chemical compounds and to deposit them in the compound proper to itself. Ostwald (1896—1902, S. 766) writes: "Somewhat more involved than in the other cases are the phenomena exhibited by chrome-alum. As is known, fresh solutions of this salt change their color, becoming greener. On heating to boiling they become entirely green, and this color becomes bluer again on keeping at ordinary temperature. The processes derive from this, that in the solution a certain quantity of a green-colored salt of a complex chromosulfuric acid forms, which increases with rising temperature; the transformation forward and backward requires, at ordinary temperature, days and weeks." "If the green solution is evaporated at low temperature without alum-germs being added, then it transforms itself into a green varnish [Firnis], which probably consists essentially of the complex salt. If, on the contrary, one lets it evaporate down in the presence of a germ, then it passes completely into crystals of the violet germ." "The latter phenomenon explains itself easily, since through the separating-out of the solid salt the equilibrium in the solution is shifted in such a sense that a part of the complex salt must transform itself into the ordinary one; this continues, up to the complete transformation, in the measure in which the quantity of the solution becomes smaller."

In any case the achievement of the assimilating plasma is here still not reached, because indeed the substance obtained is not first subjected to a synthesis. Yet this further approximation too can presumably be attained through an appropriate experimental arrangement: In a concentrated solution there are the two salts a·b·c and d·e·f, of whose ions b·c and d·e are supposed to possess the capability of coming together. There will then be present in the solution: the free ions a, b·c, d·e and f, the salts a·b·c and d·e·f, and the compound b·c·d·e in a definite equilibrium relation. If now a crystal of the compound b·c·d·e is added, then the same will first deposit upon itself the phase b·c·d·e; the equilibrium is disturbed; b·c and d·e in corresponding measure come together again out of the free ions, and are split off again out of the compounds a·b·c and d·e·f; the newly arisen molecules b·c·d·e are again deposited upon the crystal, and the process will have to proceed up to the exhaustion of this compound (with progressing evaporation).¹

In this manner we obtain a picture which resembles organic assimilation even in the simultaneous formation of waste products (the remaining a, f and — if these can come together into a compound — also a·f). That the assimilating plasma possesses the capability of attaching the assimilated substance to itself in such a manner that a proportional enlargement of the form results, comes plainly to light in the Protozoa, in some other lower animals, and the plants, while in those animals which undergo a metamorphosis this at times appears obscured².

## B. Discussion of the views of various authors on the value of the analogies between crystal and organism¹).

When, toward the end of the eighteenth century, the regeneration of animals came to be more closely known by the experimental route, the comparison with crystals — which one could conceive as being composed entirely of smaller, similar crystals, and which possessed the property of growth — was generally invoked for the explanation.

As a representative of this "naïve" crystal analogy let Buffon be named (according to the views reproduced by Driesch in his "History and Theory of Vitalism"). The analogies adduced by the said author must be designated "naïve," since it has indeed turned out later that the points of comparison adduced — such as, for example, the composition of an animal out of nothing but smaller animals — do not hold water, and that elsewhere the deeper-going

analogies, such as could be drawn e.g. by the discovery of the regeneration of (artificially) mutilated crystals, were as yet unknown.

The authors of that time, too, who rejected the explanation by preformed parts, saw in crystallization a form-formative power [Formbildungskraft] analogous to the organic "formative drive" [Bildungstrieb], only less developed: Blumenbach, Maupertuis, Reil.

It could not fail to happen that the superficial comparison, at the basis of which lay as yet no thorough investigation of the compared objects, met with energetic opposition. When H. Jordan (1842) first undertook experiments on the regeneration of crystals, he already had to turn against the view evidently prevailing at that time, which had been expressed, for example, by E. H. Weber in Hildebrandt's Anatomy, that the formative [bildende] activity of crystals was very different from that of organisms.

It is certainly noteworthy that Jordan published his work in a physiological archive, and that in the following period precisely those authors who had at the same time been active as researchers in both the organic and the crystallographic field engaged themselves most warmly on behalf of the cogency of the analogy: Bütschli, O. Lehmann, Pasteur, Quincke, Rauber, Sachs, v. Schrön, Spencer — however diverse were the paths by which the named persons had approached the problem.

The cause of this lies in the fact that to every biologist who works his way into crystallogenetic experiments the "self-activity" [Selbsttätigkeit] of the crystal becomes strikingly apparent to an increased degree, so that the boundary between the inorganic and organic world no longer appears separated by that unbridgeable gulf as the opponents of the crystal analogy present it. The physicist Bernstein designates the force of crystallization as a preliminary stage of the organic formative drive [Bildungstrieb], as the chemist Liebig and the philosopher Schopenhauer had already done, in that they pointed out that the laws of mechanics are just as little transferable without further ado to chemistry [electricity, magnetism] and crystallography as to biology, but that, rather, proper laws of action are added.

To the purposiveness [Zielstrebigkeit] of every form-formative process, of crystals too, not only of organisms, Fischel (1903), Holmes (1904) and Friedmann (1904) have pointed; to the self-activity [Selbsttätigkeit] of crystals as of organisms Schultze (1876, p. 24); to equal division O. Schultze (1895, p. 288). Crystal analogies to biology have further been brought forward by a great number of other researchers, who, however, refer chiefly to processes of heredity and may therefore here (where it is for us a matter of the regeneration problem) for the present be cited only summarily, as friends of the crystal analogy in general: Bayer (1904), Friedmann (1902), Haacke (1893), Haeckel (1904), Kassowitz (1899), Krukenberg, Le Dantec (1896), Ostwald (1902), Zehnder (1899).

We can now turn to the discussion of those authors who do indeed admit a certain analogy, but yet perceive a very great and essential difference between the processes of form-formation in crystals and organisms.

Hans Driesch, in his writings which concern themselves with the rehabilitation of "vitalism," everywhere already concedes to crystals a special position over against the rest of inorganic nature: the crystals are, "in spite of their lesser complexity, already in themselves not strictly chemically-physically comprehensible" (1901, p. 839); "although the concept of the typically-heterogeneously composed plays no role with these classes of objects, and precisely for that reason the concept of the 'purposive' [Zweckmäßige] here cannot really come into question, but at most in a quite general sense . . ., crystallographic science nevertheless represents, in a certain sense, a transitional stage from physics and chemistry to biology: the concept of the typical, not 'accidental,' direction of happening appears in it, which was foreign to those sciences. There, properly, only the concept of rectilinearity came into question; now relations of direction become intrinsic lawfulnesses [Eigengesetzlichkeiten]. Already crystallography, then, represents over against physico-chemistry something quite new, thoroughly irreducible, however one may also turn the matter fictively or hypothetically" (1904, p. 164).

In his newest book (Vitalismus, 1905) Driesch repeats these views [p. 68, 230; the discussion of crystallization and purposiveness deferred on p. 3 to later, I missed in reading further]. At a later place (p. 239), however, he sets in an abrupt manner inanimate things in opposition to animate ones, under the presupposition that the "life-entelechies" [Lebensentelechien] behave quite differently over against substance than do the constants of physics, chemistry and crystallography; to this view, which is to be ascribed to Aristotle, however, he does not assent without reservation.

Since Driesch, only after he had formulated his vitalism, found himself prompted, through the objections of Bütschli, to declare expressly that he too would grant the crystals a certain autonomy, and since he himself seems never to have occupied himself with crystallogenesis, it is not to be wondered at if he ascribes to the "entelechy of life," over against the mechanical constants, a value of distinction far more significant in essence than [that ascribed] to the autonomy of crystals ranged among the other "autonomies" (e.g. that of chemical compounds).

As autonomous ("intrinsically-lawful" [eigengesetzlich]) processes Driesch defines, in a clear manner, those which cannot be traced back to otherwise-known natural phenomena or to combinations of the same. It must in fact be admitted that we are not in a position, for example, to demonstrate the properties of a chemical compound as a resultant of the components, but that "quite new" properties come to light; just as little can the attractive forces exerted by crystals in various directions be explained from the sum of the substance heaped up at any given time within a particular radius; let reference be made merely to Jordan's experiments on crystal regeneration, who himself had already drawn the conclusion that the quantity of mass-particles could not be decisive for growth, since indeed precisely in the direction of the loss the greatest attraction is exerted; and Lobry indeed showed, as cited above, that with equal surface area different weight increases occur according to whether an intact or an injured crystal is present, so that it is not, say, merely the mass-particles lying at the surface that determine the quantity of deposition.

That, entirely, in the realm of organisms, and again most of all in the case of animals, "intrinsic lawfulnesses" [Eigengesetzlichkeiten] occur, hardly needs first to be explained: most clearly this shows itself in "consciousness," which, according to the experiences gained on ourselves (awakening from sleep or faint!), we must regard as something supervening upon the living under certain external circumstances, without the same being "explicable" from the organs composing the body (say brain, nerves, etc.) and the external stimuli. The changes of the same [consciousness], to be sure, are quite lawfully linked with the changes of lower degree (which, not aptly, may be grasped by many under the catchword "psychophysical parallelism").

Driesch, however, rejects the gapless physical-chemical causality of psycho-physical parallelism (p. 221), as well as repudiates the view that always, when a series of certain complicated physical and chemical factors comes together, something living with new intrinsically-lawful [selbstgesetzlich] properties arises as a result, but rather: "beside the single composing element, indeed above it, steps the composedness as a 'single thing.' Its study has to proceed alongside the study of the single composing element: it led to our proofs of autonomy; it gave rise to the concept of entelechy."

What, now, is the situation with the proofs of autonomy?

The first proof, grounded on the differentiation of "harmonic-equipotential systems," rests upon experiments on sea-urchin eggs, on stems of *Tubularia* and on *Clavellina*. An arbitrary element (cell in the case of sea-urchin eggs, cross-section in the case of the other organisms) can undergo a different differentiation, according to whether it possesses a certain distance (a) from a particular chosen fixed point, and according as the magnitude (g) of the whole system is altered for the establishment of the proportionality (harmony), which Driesch expresses thus: "So we can, then, say generally that the prospective significance (S) of an arbitrary element of our systems depends, besides on the distance (a) from a fixed point, also on the absolute system-magnitude (g) generally: S = f(a, g)" (p. 204).

"We will designate the inner magnitude which gives common expression to all that has just been described — to the harmony, to the particular prospective potency, and to the species-character — as E; we can thus now set up the equation S = f(a, g, E), where E, in distinction from the two other magnitudes, represents under the function-sign a constant magnitude."

Driesch now believes himself able to prove, for the magnitude E, the exclusion of the "machine-like": "Every arbitrarily conceived part of the whole would thus have to contain that infinitely complicated machine in its entirety; indeed, since every 'absolute' part of the whole can play every relative role in the artificially produced part — since indeed the sections of the operation are arbitrary — so every system-element would have to contain every part of the infinitely complicated machinery, but in each case from a different machine. There would lie, one above another, infinitely many machineries, each displaced by an infinitely small amount, by a 'differential.' Yes, with objects such as *Clavellina* and *Tubularia*, there would be added to the infinitely many machines of normal magnitude, which overlie one another somewhat displaced, yet infinitely many others of infinitely many different magnitudes, which would again have to overlie those in infinite variation: for since indeed the absolute system-magnitude, not merely the distance a, enters into the formula, this would be the case."

"Such an analysis, however, abolishes the concept of machinery, of the physical-chemical manifoldness, as the basis of the differentiation of harmonic-equipotential systems. The machine-concept here becomes, in the true sense of the word, 'nonsensical.'"

To be sure — if rigid machines are thought of! Since, however, organisms, as discussed at length, are in no case to be regarded as rigid systems, but rather always predominantly and often almost throughout as fluid, a proportional diminution can be attained by re-ordering processes, without there needing to be present preformed machines of "infinitely many different magnitudes" (apart from the fact that "infinitely" many cases do not really exist).

That we do not go astray when we make the state of aggregation responsible for the capacity of transformation [Umbildungsfähigkeit], the crystals now demonstrate. Let us first consider rigid crystals with respect to their capacity to satisfy the demands set up by Driesch for the sufficiency of the "entelechy."

Naturally we are conscious of the difference, namely that in the crystal all particles are materially equivalent, whereas in the developed organism there occur variously "differentiated" organs, which are different not only in form and position in the whole, but also in chemical composition and in "function." This is precisely the difference in complexity between crystals and organisms.

Now we could indeed perceive therein a "category" different in kind, if the "life-entelechy" brought it about, from a chemically-identical initial product, to produce the organ-differentiation demanded by the harmony at each place, to let now this, now that chemism arise. Merely for the sake of completeness let the further two proofs of Driesch be touched on here in a few words; they are founded on the analysis of the organic motions. To our present theme they stand indeed only in a looser relation.

"The history of the individual furnishes only the elements for future actions. These offer themselves indeed in determinate combinations: but the reaction-determinant can combine them otherwise."

Precisely this distinguishes the organism from the phonograph and from similar machines, which can reproduce only given combinations in the specificity of their being-given. These machines are, in their reactions, not "co-determined on the ground of a historical reaction-basis, but firmly determined through the specificity of their history" (p. 219).

".. A quite extraordinarily small variation in the composition of the stimulus can fundamentally alter the composition of the reaction, and on the other hand the latter can remain almost or even entirely the same when the composition of the stimulus is fundamentally altered. When I call out to an acquaintance on the street: 'Dein Vater ist schwer erkrankt' [Your father has fallen gravely ill], or when I say 'Mein' [My] instead of 'Dein' [Your], hence alter only a single letter, then the effect is nonetheless entirely different; the 'stimuli' 'Dein Vater ist gestorben' [Your father has died], 'Tuo padre è morto', 'Your father is dead', by contrast, despite the complete difference of their combination, have the same effect — presupposing that the addressee 'understands' the three languages!"

"In any case no machine can be devised which could accomplish the individuality of the assignment.... 'The reaction-determinant' in actions is no machine, it is a kind of 'entelechy'; we will here speak of a 'psychoid', in order to reserve the word psyche for pure psychology" (p. 221).

The first quoted passage seems to me to assert nothing other than the rejection of the determination of the will, hence the affirmation of a "free will," which of course could nonetheless follow determinate laws not connected with the system's other behavior. As is well known, however, the complete determination of the will in the psychological phenomena (or, if one wishes to avoid the expression, of the action-reactions) can very well be defended (cf. Mach, further: Przibram, Exp. If "Mein" steps into the place of "Dein," then the triggered complex is another, under circumstances an entirely different one, and as such determines entirely different actions; in triggerings the effect is precisely not at all adequate to the stimulus.

By contrast the stimuli "Dein Vater ist gestorben," "Tuo padre è morto," "Your father is dead," "despite the complete difference of their combination," have the same thought-complex, when, as a result of the learning of the language, they have entered into connection with the relevant thought-complex (or singly with its single elements) and have in this way been incorporated into the "historical reaction-basis."

Driesch takes the last proof from the results of brain-physiology:

"Disturbances set up operatively in the brain can now be conceived as integrating constituents of the total state of the organism, which, together with its reaction-basis and together with the triggering stimuli, determine the reaction. If this turns out typical despite the disturbances — and that is certainly the case in many instances, after the direct injurious consequence of the operation has been overcome — then this shows that the central conducting-pathways can be used in an indeterminately variable and yet harmonious manner. Hence it is not, say, fixed machine-like-tectonic relations in the centres that are the basis of the movement-effects, but — something else." "The cerebral cortex, in so far as it admits substitutability, appears here as something that might well be fittingly designated as a functional harmonic-equipotential system" (p. 223).

Since, according to the preceding discussions, the harmonic-equipotential systems seem to me not necessarily to have any "entelechy," only the functional aspect need still be gone into: I conceive the use of new pathways after the loss of the old ones entirely in the sense of a compensation, as it emerges in a form-building relation, for example, in the *Alpheus*-claws, or still more distinctly in the taking-over of the function by the rudimentary operculum in the serpulids (*Hydroïdes* e.g.). Here too, accordingly, I can recognize only something marked-off from simpler modes of happening (intrinsic lawfulnesses) by the degree, through the complexity, but not by a wholly different category.

In so far, then, as Driesch's proofs are meant to posit a fundamental difference of the organic modes of action from the inorganic ones, I must reject them on the ground of the crystal-analogies.

E. Schwalbe (1906) does not believe that from the observations on the regeneration of crystals anything can be inferred for the relationship of the inorganic to the organic world, without, however, entering into his grounds.

Verworn expresses himself on the crystal analogy in his "General Physiology" (1901) as follows:

"In the comparison of organisms with inorganic substances one has by preference committed the error of setting the organism opposite a crystal, instead of comparing it with a substance which offers a similar consistency, in general similar physical conditions, as the living substance does, thus perhaps with a thick-fluid mass" (p. 124).

"We see: essential differences are not yielded to us by the comparison of the structural relationships of living and inorganic substance; and if we do not altogether give in to the inclination to compare the living substance always only with the crystal, but rather with fluid mixtures, then we find that, in its structural relationships, it no longer differs from inanimate fluid mixtures any more than these differ among themselves — indeed, not even as much as these differ from a crystal" (p. 125).

"One has finally also sought to establish a difference between organisms and inorganic substances in the manner of growth, yet with just as little success. To the setting-up of this difference, again, the unfortunate juxtaposition of the organism and the crystal has led. One said: the crystal grows through 'apposition,' the organism, on the contrary, through 'intussusception' of the particles — that is, the crystal grows by one particle after another being deposited upon its surface, whereby the interior remains fixed and unchanged, the organism, on the contrary, by the particles being taken up into the interior and intercalated among the already-present ones. If one sets a cell as a whole opposite a crystal, this is indeed not to be disputed; but not all inorganic bodies are crystals, and we already saw that we may, according to their physical properties, set the living substance of organisms essentially in comparison only with a fluid mass. Fluids, however, always grow only through intussusception — that is, if one adds a soluble body to a fluid, say salt to water, then the water dissolves the salt and intercalates the salt molecules of itself, through diffusion, among its own water molecules. Here, then, we have exactly the same process as in the growth of the organism" (pp. 127–128).

Verworn therefore recognizes the fluid state of the protoplasm as decisive for the different manner of form-formation of the living as opposed to the rigid crystals; he evidently rejects the crystal analogy only because the plastic crystals are unknown to him.

A. Weismann turns, in "Keimplasma" (1892), similarly as Morgan, against Spencer, only that this time the capacity to exert an influence upon their parts as a whole is denied not only to crystals but also to organisms:

"Were the 'unities' of the body capable of reshaping themselves arbitrarily under the influence of the whole and of crystallizing toward precisely the missing part, then they would have to be able to do this in all kinds, just as in all organs. But this is precisely not the case. The leg of the salamander regenerates itself, but that of the lizard does not. I shall, in the special part of this section, be able to show still more precisely that regeneration does not rest upon a general capacity of the animal body, but rather that it rests upon a special adaptation" (p. 139).

"From the phenomena of regeneration, as they lie before us today, it can, as I believe, be derived with certainty that the capacity for regeneration does not rest upon a primary property of the bion, but rather that it is a phenomenon of adaptation" (p. 152).

"We are thus led to the conjecture that the general regenerative capacity of all parts might be an achievement of lower and simpler animal forms brought about through selection, which in the course of phylogeny and of the increasing complication of the structure indeed sank, gradually, more and more from its original height, but which at every stage of its reduction could, with respect to particular biologically important and at the same time frequently lost parts, be raised again through special selection-processes directed upon these parts."

Weismann therefore rejects the crystal analogy in the case of regeneration, because to him regeneration in crystals evidently appears to be a general property, but in organisms a special one, "acquired through selection." Since, however, Weismann must push the arising of the regenerative capacity down in the animals as far as the protozoa, since these too exhibit the regenerative capacity, his view can be refuted only in particular, but for this very reason his view also remains an assumption that is capable of no direct proof. Now, as regards the particular cases adduced by Weismann, in which through the greater probability of loss of a member an increased regenerative capacity is supposed to have been brought about, or in which through diminished probability of loss (in animals belonging to otherwise well-regenerating animal groups) members are supposed to have lost the regenerative capacity, these have in part relied upon incomplete literature (such as the alleged incapacity of the cave-olm to regenerate, cf. Goette, Development and Regeneration of the Newts, 1879); in another part it has been shown through subsequent experiments that even in the lower relatives of the animals in question an analogous regenerative capacity exists, without the member in question ever being lost, and that it is therefore not a matter of an enhancement of a sunken capacity — for example, in the case of the regeneration of the beak of birds, adduced by Weismann as a particularly striking example in his writing "Facts and Interpretations in regard to Regeneration" (1899), since the jaws of the reptiles and lower vertebrates likewise regenerate easily (cf. I. Werber, A. f. Entw.-Mech. 1905, p. 248).

In the "Facts" Weismann adduces still other things against the crystal analogy, namely the facts of "Hypotypy": "In the face of such facts one evidently does not manage with the conception of a general regenerative force which restitutes the damaged 'life-crystal' in integrum when somewhere a part is broken off from it. Here the crystal completes itself according to an older model, and one does not see how it should come to do so, since indeed the whole is as little altered as in the case of a dodecahedral crystal from which one has broken off a vertex. If now suddenly the missing part 'crystallizes' in another 'system,' this can have its ground only in the fact that the elements from which this completion proceeds are not the remaining whole, but rather particular parts that belong to another 'system' — or, translated into the language of biology, that at the injured place regeneration-anlagen of older origin were present and came to unfolding through the injury. With this, however, the existence of 'anlagen' is proven."

The premises of this deduction rest upon the thought, already mentioned in Morgan, that the whole in the case of mutilation remains otherwise unchanged in the crystal; and the same is asserted, in direct opposition to Morgan, of the organism. That "morphallaxis" occurs both in organisms and in crystals refutes both. Besides, we saw in the flattening-out, as a consequence of mutilation, an alteration of the accruing crystal parts set in: to be sure, only possible faces of the same crystal system appear. But in the organisms too there appear indeed not forms of other kinds, but rather less highly developed ones of the same kind (upon the phylogenetic value of hypotypy I will not enter here; cf. Lit. Przibram, 1902).

Now, as regards the proof of "anlagen," such of a special kind have nowhere been demonstrated, but rather there is mostly present a far-reaching transformation of the parts lying at the injury-surface (cf. all the more recent works on the histology of the regeneration processes).

[Indeed, almost insurmountable difficulties seem to me to arise for Weismann's conception through the facts of compensatory hypertypy and of the hypotypy of the opposite side appearing in connection with it in *Alpheus*, because this process is again reversible and a "lagging-behind" [Nachhinken] of regeneration-determinants in Weismann's sense would entirely exclude this; cf. Przibram, 1905.]

For the third time, Weismann comes to speak of the crystal analogy in his "Lectures on Descendance Theory" (1902):

"Regeneration must therefore, in its deepest root, rest upon those 'affinities' between the particles which preside over their arrangement, which are capable of maintaining it and of calling it forth anew. Thus the organism appears to us, in this respect at least, comparable to a crystal whose broken-off tips complete themselves again out of the mother liquor according to the same crystallization-system, evidently likewise in consequence of inner directing forces, polarities, which, however, we here too cannot precisely specify. But the difference from the crystal lies not only — as one was hitherto inclined to assume — in this, that the crystal needs the mother liquor in order to grow and to complete itself, while the living-unit itself creates the material for its further construction, but at the same time in this, that regeneration is not possible in every organism and not at every site of such a one, but that it rather requires for this purpose special 'Anlagen' [predispositions/rudiments], without which the part in question cannot arise. The indispensability of these Anlagen, the determinants, however, seems to me to rest upon this, that here the new construction cannot happen simply through the procurement of organic material, but that for it there is needed quite specially and in every case differently-hewn building-stones [anders behauener Steine], which can only be procured on the basis of historical transmission, or: to step out of the image, because the life-particles, out of which the organ is to be re-established, possess a specific stamp and have a long prehistory behind them, because they thus can arise only out of the specific life-particles transmitted through generations, precisely the determinants."

Apart from the not unconditional dependence of crystal-regeneration on increase in the mother liquor, these utterances of Weismann coincide with the actual state of affairs, if one under determinants may understand organ-determining substances, that is, if the "differently-hewn building-stones" in each case are figuratively meant as, say, molecule-complexes.

Now, however, Weismann has admittedly, in the "Lectures" too, not withdrawn from the assumption of the connection between loss-probability and regeneration-height.

As the principal support of this theory, autotomy is always cited, although it had been demonstrated through experiments by Morgan (1898, 1900) and by me (1899) that the autotomizable extremities of the crabs [Krebse], cut off also proximally or distally to the preformed fracture-site, are able to regenerate very well, that also non-autotomizable and rudimentary appendages grow back again in the Crustacea, and so forth.

How little a direct connection exists between autotomy, the importance of an organ, and regeneration-capability, is most clearly to be seen in the Orthoptera:

In the lowest forms, not specialized in their legs (Blattidae, Forficulidae, Phasmidae, and Bacillidae), all three pairs of legs regenerate, which admittedly almost always exhibit autotomy; in the Mantidae, however, whose forelegs are transformed into grasping-apparatus, these regenerate, according to Bordage, although of greatest value to the animal, with more difficulty than the remaining legs, or not at all.¹) Bordage has attempted to explain this by the absence of autotomy in the forelegs.

Literature: Przibram, 1902; A. f. Entw.-M. 1906). ### Postscript [Nachschrift].

In several of his most recent writings¹) O. Lehmann adopts a rejecting position toward the Pfaundler-Curie theory and therefore also toward the explanation of my experiments on the transformation of crystals in solutions protected from evaporation. On the occasion of the 78th Assembly of German Natural Scientists and Physicians at Stuttgart, Herr Hofrat Lehmann was so kind as to discuss this point with me in conversation. It thereby emerged that O. Lehmann does not doubt the result of my experiments, but holds the unavoidable temperature-fluctuations, however slight, responsible for the possibility of transformation. Since, according to Pfaundler, the various molecules in the liquid already possess a different living energy (heat-tone [Wärmetönung]), Lehmann's conception holds only for a merely theoretical limiting case, which in practice also need not be taken into account for the explanation of the processes in free nature. [Still much less, of course, can a realized temperature-constancy be thought of in the case of organisms.] In the case of perfect temperature-constancy, the crystal would be in equilibrium with the liquid in every form, since according to Lehmann the surface-tension is the same in all faces, not a constant different for different faces. In this point too, however, there is by no means an unbridgeable opposition to the views of Curie, Pfaundler, and myself to be discerned, since Lehmann understands by surface-tension merely the energy which would have to be expended for the continuity-separation [Kontinuitätstrennung] of two surface-particles in the direction of the spreading of the surface, whereas he sharply distinguishes the effect of this surface-tension, which strives toward the assumption of the sphere as minimal-surface, as "surface-tension-pressure" [Oberflächenspannungsdruck].
