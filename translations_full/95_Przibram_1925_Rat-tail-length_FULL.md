### I. Statement of the Problem.

In the preceding Communication (The Environment of the Germ-Plasm XI) the proof of the direct dependence of the relative tail length in rats (and mice) upon the body-warmth was furnished. On that occasion it was already mentioned in passing that, parallel with the establishment of the tail length, the body-warmth of the females and males of these animals had also been determined. Whether for both sexes the curves obtained ran wholly identically (as in the Figs. 1—6 reproduced there) was not gone into. The thought already presented itself near at hand of comparing here also the magnitude of the relative tail length for the sexes among one another, in case a und M. (E.) rattus L., als fakultatives Geschlechtsmerkmal. **499**

difference were to be traced back to a difference in the body-warmth. A detailed treatment of this problem appeared to us all the more in place, since *Sumner* had traced the difference of the tail length of his mice back solely to the differing body-warmth itself; the most recent of the measurements which yield sex-differential body-warmths stems from the *Lipschütz* school, insofar as these are afflicted, in their accumulation, with methodical measurement-errors. It is therefore the duty to subject the comparative compilation of the sex-warmths given in the last own work (the "Temperature and Temperatures" 1923, Chap. 6 and Tab. A, p. 117) to a renewed critical reworking and supplementation.

### II. Material.

As the principal objects of our investigation the rats served, whose keeping, measurement and breeding at various temperatures was described earlier (Environment VI, VIII, and namely XI) in detail, so that here a presentation of the experimental technique can be dispensed with. Valuable material is offered by the works of *Sumner* over body-warmth (1913) and tail length (1915) on Albino-mice, for the tail length of the same animal-species only *Sundstroem*'s Communication (1922, II). For sex-differences in general reference is to be made not only to the work just mentioned (1923); the literature already cited there has been supplemented further by some, which was brought to my knowledge through the friendliness of Herren *A. Lipschütz* (Riga) and *A. Kanitz* (Leipzig). Professor *Hári* (Physiological Institute, Budapest) obliged me for the written particulars over the works carried out under his guidance by *Z. Aszodi* and *K. Goto*, which investigated internal temperatures of mice resp. rats at various outside temperatures, unfortunately without regard to the sex. The same holds for the thermoelectric measurements of the body-warmth by *Graham* and *Hutchinson* (1914), which, carried out on white rats, compared the results of this method at various outside temperatures with those of the mercury thermometer. *Donaldson's* "Rat" (1915) has again rendered excellent services.

####### β) "Philadelphia".

Does this difference established between the black house-rat "Semmering" and the agouti-coloured wander-rat "Vienna" lie now in the Species, the colour-variety, the locality, the experimental-condition or in a wholly other un-controllable factor? ### δ) From "Philadelphia."

Once again the comparison with the rats measured at the Wistar Institute in Philadelphia permits us to test whether the locality exerts an influence on the outcome of the K.S.-relation [body–tail relation] in both sexes. The data available are even more favorable for the albinos than those for the agouti-colored wandering rats. They relate, namely, not to freshly caught animals, but to *Decumanus* reared in captivity for generations (*Donaldson* [1915, S. 8] uses, incidentally, always the synonym *Epimys norvegicus* Erxleben, because this author had described the wandering rat one year earlier than *Pallas*, namely already in 1777), and a table („The Rat" S. 110, Tab. 64) gives the weight of these albinos at various days of age, after determination in experiments. By means of the weight one can now (S. 115, Tab. 68) find the body length at the age-weeks of interest to us, so that we do not first need to draw upon our rats for age determination. At the age of II weeks there results ♂ K : S = 78 : 49,4 = 1,580; ♀ 51,8 : 15,8 = 1,506; hence ♂ — ♀ = 0,074. This value, still somewhat higher than that for our albino crossing „St. × M." and *Rattus*, shows once more that no species characters are involved. For 58 days (VIII—IX weeks, S. 117) one reads: ♂ 148 : 119,8 = 1,232; ♀ 143 : 120,0 = 1,175; hence ♂ — ♀ = 0,057, which is again higher than our *Wiener* average from all albinos (namely 0,032). There can accordingly be no doubt that the albinotic races of the wandering rat, exactly like the black, wild house rat, agree in the great difference of the sexual tail length at the age of II weeks. In contrast to this stands the agouti-colored, wild wandering rat, which at the same age has no greater difference (sometimes even the reversed sign) than at the age of VIII—IX weeks, in which the difference for all examined and compared strains of both species is almost the same. Wildness or tameness cannot come into consideration for the contrast, for then *Rattus* and *Aguti* would have to agree, just as little [could] wild coloration against albinism. There would still remain, as a correlation factor for the sexual difference of the tail, the other coloration — in the wild *Decumanus* a coat strongly mixed with yellow, in the wild *Rattus* a more black coat. Unfortunately I lack data on the K.S.-relation of other rats or mice with just such pigmentation as those used for my experiments. It may, however, be discussed further the behavior of albinotic mice according to *Sumner* and *Sundstroem*, which at least allow us to compare the same pigment-less color race in two different species; here too, however, data on the age of 14 days are lacking.

### e) House mouse, *Mus musculus* L.,

#### α) After "Sumner".

*Sumner* (1915) subjected six groups of mice of the same provenance, at the age of 50 days, that is, somewhat over VII weeks old, to body-length and tail measurement (B₃ S. 372; C₂ S. 376; C₄ S. 388, each apportioned into a group in cold and warm surroundings). From his data I have calculated the K : S (Tab. XI and XVIII), and there results ♂ 1,0738 — ♀ 1,0630 = 0,0108. *Sumner* gives a further eight groups at more advanced age (90 days: C₃ S. 384; 105 days: F₁ 1909 in 1915, S. 396; 270—300 days: B₂ S. 364; 285 days: A S. 285, each in cold and warm surroundings). The average from the groups (not the separated litters, which he does not give) yields ♂ 1,1176 — ♀ 1,1008 = 0,0168. If we take the mean from the K : S-numbers for the age immediately before VIII—IX and that after IX weeks, then we obtain 0,0138 as a number comparable with the rats at the age of VII to IX weeks. This formation of a mean is admissible insofar as *Sumner* himself (l. c. S. 425) says: „Whether the relative tail length fluctuates at different periods in the life of the animal or not cannot be determined from my table. If such differences, dependent on age, really exist, they are of a much smaller order of magnitude than the differences between different groups of the same age. At least this is true for the period from 50 days to maturity."

In a later work (Umwelt XIV) I shall return once more to this point; as I already communicated in abstract in „Temperatur und Temperaturen", K : S does not remain constant, at least in rats, even during the period indicated by *Sumner*. The differences are, however, relatively small, so that the value determined for the time after VII weeks permits us a sufficiently exact comparison with the rats at the age of VIII—IX weeks. *Sumner*'s mice show a much smaller sexual difference than all rat strains (excepting „Aguti" without regard to temperature), for indeed the smallest number with VIII—IX weeks among the albinos was that calculated for the crossing „Ställe × Molkerei" with 0,0193. Does, then, perhaps *Mus musculus*, belonging to a different subgenus than *Epimys*, differ specifically in the K.S.-relation of the sexes from that of the same color race in the rats? The answer is given in the negative sense by the experiments of *Sundstroem*. Before drawing upon them, let reference still be made to our Tab. X, according to which (column 11) in 75% of the litter-groups of *Sumner* the male had the shorter tail than the female, and the positive deviation of the K.S.-relation for the male (column 19) amounted to 93,0%. The average value is therefore not without significance.

#### β) After "Sundstroem".

Fortunately *Sundstroem* (1922, II, Tab. 1, S. 418 ♂♂; Tab. 2, S. 419 ♀♀), for the measurement of body and tail length in two mouse-litter groups of the same descent, chose precisely the age of 60 days, which is therefore fully comparable with the period VIII—IX weeks. We obtain ♂ 1,348 — ♀ 1,316 = 0,032. This value falls within the most frequent rat values and thus shows that we may not perceive any specific difference in the small value of *Sumner*'s mice. In both litter-groups of *Sundstroem*, and in most of the individual specimens, the greater relative length of the female tail was realized. Without regard to the external temperature, then, in the compared rat and mouse strains the male has the shorter tail, excepting in the wandering rat „Aguti" of our *Wiener* breeding, where almost equality prevailed.

## IV. Body warmth of the sexes (in various external temperatures).

For better survey there are compiled on Tab. As far as the data reach, the number of experimental animals (column e—f), age (h), external temperature (i) is also taken into account (the depth of the thermometer insertion is discussed separately; cf. below VII).

### 1. Mammalia: Rodentia.

#### A. House rat, *Epimys rattus*.

A glance at the table (column m) shows us, for the difference between rectal warmth of the female and male, in all six considered constant external temperatures, positive values, which indicate higher body warmth in favor of the female. The measurements relate to readings made each on 10 successive days at the same time of day; only the male at 40° could be measured on only 2 days, since it then perished. The average value from the six differences for the six constant temperatures amounts to 1,4°C. We are dealing with old specimens of one and the same strain (cf. Umwelt VI, Tab. A).

#### B. Albinotic wandering rat, *Epimys decumanus albinoticus*.

Experiments at four different age-stages and in seven constant external temperatures, carried out by three experimenters, have yielded, in agreement, higher female body warmth, with the exception of one series (23—25 days old, 30°C), in which no difference between the sexes was to be found. Nowhere did the male have higher own-warmth than the female. Compared were, in the young animals always, in the older mostly, specimens of the same litter, in any case of the same strain. The number of measurements is to be taken from our Tab. XIV (column g).

#### C. Albinotic house mouse, *Mus musculus albinoticus*.

For sexually mature house mice in various external temperatures, *Sumner*'s (1913) measurements are available. His result is, just as with our rats, higher male rectal warmth. In none of his series is there an exception to this. State of movement, depth of measurement, provenance are observed. He examined 13 pairs and a further 20 males.

#### D. Guinea pig, *Cavia cobaia*.

*Steinach* (published in *Lipschütz* 1917) carried out 206 measurements on 8 sexually mature guinea pigs, whereby males and females of the same litter came to comparison. The difference amounted to 0,66°C in favor of the female. Over against this stands a measurement-series carried out on a single pair by *Simpson* and *Galbraith* (1905/06), which is recorded in our Tab. XIV with a negative value of 0,19. The English authors emphasize that this is the only case in which they did not find higher body warmth of the female (cf. below E, G—N).

*Lipschütz* (1923, S. 1262) and his pupils (*Bormann*, *Brunnow*, *Savary* (1923, S. 255) cite the work of *Ocaranza*, which appeared in 1922, as proof for the equal body warmth of the male and female guinea pig. I have, through the kind mediation of Professor *Lipschütz*, turned to Herr Dr. *Fernando Ocaranza*, Professor of Physiology in Mexico D. F., with the request for more precise statements about his temperature measurements, since it already seemed to me to emerge from his publication that it was merely a matter of a general impression, but not of measurements specially carried out. *Ocaranza* had the kindness to communicate to me in writing, under the date of 15 April 1924, that his conclusion — that there is no sexual difference with respect to the temperature of the guinea pig — was based on the fact that in both sexes the daily fluctuations, and those from one day to the next, keep within the same extreme limits of variation. The same was confirmed by his pupil *Margarita Delgado* (unpubl.). It is therefore possible, he says, that one may find differences of several tenths of a degree if one examines a male and a female simultaneously, without this being attributable to the sex. *Ocaranza* has thus carried out no experiments in which, by making all other circumstances equal, only the difference of sex would come to expression. His indirect conclusion is also unable to explain why, then, in larger experimental series the difference always falls in the direction of greater warmth of the female — which was, incidentally, still unknown to him. To be mentioned, further, is the review by *Romeis* (1923) of *Lipschütz*' and his pupils' work. *Romeis* maintains that these had refuted, on the guinea pig, the findings of *Steinach*; also that he can find, according to his own measurements, no difference between males and females, castrates and heterologized animals. The former assertion of *Romeis* is absolutely false. To be cautious, I turned again, after reading through the review, to *Lipschütz*, and he answered me, under the date of 8 XII 1923, that it had not been a matter of guinea pigs, but of rabbits (on this below E), [and that] he had also never said that with his new findings everything was now false that *Steinach* and he himself had communicated about this — which *Romeis* had put into his mouth — but rather had expressed himself much more cautiously. Own measurements *Romeis* has not published; it is therefore not possible, for the time being, to check the reliability of his second assertion. There is no occasion whatever to reject the measurements of *Steinach* on the guinea pig, which agree with the rat and mouse experiments, while the contradicting statements are of little significance for our question — partly because of the isolation of the case (*Simpson* and *Galbraith*), partly because of too great fluctuation of the values as a consequence of insufficient maintenance of all conditions (*Ocaranza* and *Delgado*), partly because of erroneous or vague statements (*Romeis*).

### E. Rabbits, *Lepus cuniculus*.

According to *Simpson* and *Galbraith* (1905/06), measurements on one male and two female rabbits yielded 0.10° C in favor of the females. Likewise in favor of the females are the data published by *Lipschütz* and his students, which refer to 4 ♀♀ and 1 ♂, which were subjected to measurement at different life-ages, 4—6 months, and belonged to the same litter or at least to the same race. The difference in favor of the female is 0.16° C in the general average. The same animals, however, showed later, at 6—7 months, no difference, and at 10 months 0.15° in favor of the male. The experimenters trace the difference in behavior, however, not back to age, but to the altered measurement method (about which more further below, VII). In a further measurement series of still older rabbits, this difference in favor of the males was even 0.4°, but here neither the same race nor the same age was present, but rather merely an approximately equal weight of the two pairs. Concerning this it says (1923, S. 253): "If, however, it is taken into account that the animals measured in series belonged to different races, then it is clear that these experiments too cannot be interpreted in favor of a difference between the sexes." Sometimes *Richet* is cited as the authority for the equal body warmth of the male and the female. But the relevant passage in the *Dictionnaire de Physiologie* 3, 96 reads in German translation: "In dogs and rabbits, as far as we can judge, according to the temperature determinations that we have carried out, and although we have carried out no special investigations on this point, the sex does not influence the temperature." Here neither a mean temperature of the sexes, nor a number of measured pairs, nor a number of measurements is given. *Richet*, evidently (likewise as *Ocaranza*), merely reproduced the general impression that no differences extending beyond the otherwise [present] extremes are to be found between the sexes.

*Lillian M. Moore* (1918), who unfortunately likewise made no numerical statements and who, as she had the kindness to communicate to me in writing (of 17 May 1924), also cannot supply them subsequently, because the sex is not recorded everywhere in the protocols, did, however, obtain from numerous measurements of the body warmth in both sexes of the rabbit the impression of a higher body warmth in the female. In the rabbit too, then, the data, insofar as they are usable, come out in favor of this higher inner temperature of the female.

### 2. Primates.

#### F. Human, *Homo sapiens*.

The data on measurements of the body temperature of the human in the male and female sex are remarkably meager. *Roger* (1845) found in 24 measurements on just as many boys and girls almost ¹/₁₀° in favor of the latter; *Davy* (according to *Simpson* and *Galbraith* 1911/12) found, in three adult men and women each, a higher temperature of the former. These data, namely the latter, are, on account of the small number of measurements, quite inadequate. Nevertheless, here again, in the larger number, the temperature is found in favor of the female sex. In a renewed investigation, careful attention would have to be paid to the scrupulously exact maintenance of equality in race, age, external temperature, nutritional and movement state, thermometer-equality, and measurement-depth equality.

#### Q. *Domestic Fowl, Gallus domesticus.*

In the fowl the statements about differing warmth of the sexes are contradictory. *Simpson* and *Galbraith* (1905/06) found, both in a pair of Dorking and of Bantam, differences in favor of the hen, whereby 40—50 measurements had been carried out in each case. #### 5. Columbinae.

#### R. *Pigeon, Columba livia.*

The same contradiction between the actual statements of *Simpson* and *Galbraith* (1905/06) in favor of higher female body warmth and the citation of *Hildén* and *Stenböck* is found again in the pigeon. The female gives the same average value as that of the Englishmen, but the male 1—2° more than the English. The Swedish authors themselves designate this unusually high temperature as "a quite individual phenomenon." Consequently there remains, under critical sifting, even in all the investigated birds, just as in the mammals, the sentence emphasized earlier, namely that under otherwise equal circumstances the female exhibits a somewhat higher body warmth than the male. This is shown also by our last example, the jackdaw.

### VI. Exclusion of errors in the establishment of the sex-differing tail length.

Since males and females in the mouse-like animals are distinguished, besides by the tail length, also by other external characters, the question must be examined whether these too, and in what manner, depend on the temperature. Already long ago I described the enlargement of the testis under the influence of heat (1909; Phylogenese 1910), which has been further investigated by several authors (*Sumner* 1915, S. 334; *Steinach* and *Kammerer* 1920; *Hart* 1922; *C. R. Moore* and *Quick* 1924). It is thereby a matter not only of a functional state, as *Sumner* meant, and therefore, in every tail and body measurement that is carried out on the male, the size of the testicles may play a part insofar as they influence the position of the anus to the tail root and thereby could shift the use of the same as tail starting-point. *Sumner* expressed himself, with regard to the following objection, literally in the following words (1915, S. 431): "The only really reliable difference between the sexes, with regard to the characters considered, referred to tail length; the tails of the females, as they were measured by me, were somewhat longer. This difference may depend entirely on the displacement of the anus, on account of the presence of the testicles, and may not concern the length of the tail itself."

and thereby the use of this same [point] as the tail's starting-point could become shifted. *Sumner* expressed himself, with respect to his mice, literally (1915, S. 431), rendered into German as follows:

"The only rather certain difference between the sexes, with respect to the considered characters, related to tail length; the tails of the females, as they were measured by me, were somewhat longer. Were this supposition correct, then the correlation between body warmth and relative tail length in the sexes would be a merely coincidental one. In that case the measurements of the tail lengths in our rat experiments too, which had yielded such [a correlation] for so many series, would not be usable in the sense of the dependence of the relative tail length on the sex-warmth.

The direct refutation of the *Sumner*-ian supposition by measurement of the tail from the dorsal side encounters difficulties, because a sharp delimitation of the tail-root can scarcely be hit upon. Also, the question of the same or different number of tail vertebrae, which would be present with different tail length, would first have to be solved, for which up to now only a brief datum on mice (*Sumner* 1909, S. 129) is at hand, whereas the analogous question for rats was indeed raised (*Toldt* in *Przibram* 1918 anthr. Ges.), but up to now could still not be brought to answer.

A simple consideration has, however, brought me (Temperature and Temperators, S. 70) to the conviction that no apparent, but a real tail-difference is present in the sexes. Since the testicles increase in size from the coldest to the hottest outside temperature, the difference between the tail length of the female and the male ought also to increase in this same manner, if the latter [difference] were due merely to the position of the anus pushed outward through the testicle. Now we saw, however, precisely that no such simple relation exists, but rather an exact correlation between the tail- and body-warmth-difference of the sexes. Thus, opposite the utricated heat-testicle [Hitzehoden] at 40° stands the least sex-difference in tail length, and so forth. Even though the different position of the anus may therefore furnish a source of error in the measurement of the relative tail length, the error can yet not be so great that it could overlie the warmth-influence. Quite excluded is the tracing-back of the tail-length-difference in the sexes to false measurement through anus-displacement, when we draw into consideration the quantitative agreement of the growth-promoting warmth-influence in the sexes on the one hand, [and] the constant outside temperatures on the other hand (Environment XIV, provisionally communicated [in] Temperature and Temperators, S. 69: Q₁₀ of the tail-growth).

## VII. Exclusion of Errors in the Establishment of the Sex-Different Body Warmth.

In the year 1917 there appeared a work by *Alexander Lipschütz*, in which he wrote (S. 178): "After the present findings, one may thus regard it as completely certain that males and females differ from one another with respect to their body temperature, and we shall, in the third part of this work, bring a further proof thereof at hand by very numerous measurements on guinea-pigs. *The body temperature is a sex characteristic like various other somatic or functional moments of the organism.* In doing so it must of course be left undecided how this difference of the body temperature comes about, whether it is a matter of another difference in the total metabolism, or only of another distribution of the warmth in the body. The latter possibility is not excluded, since indeed all measurements of body temperature are in reality only the measurement of the temperature of a narrowly delimited body region. One could think of it that the observed difference of the body temperature in the male and female sexes resulted from the fact that the blood-flow of the skin- and mucous-membrane covering differs in both sexes, which in turn could rest on a different pulse-frequency in the sexes." After consideration of the body movements (S. 181), of the animal hypnosis in the back position (S. 181), of the local difference at different measuring-depth in the rectum (S. 182), of the outside temperature (S. 183), it then reads, summarizing, on S. 191: "*The body temperature of females is higher than the body temperature of males. The difference amounted on the average to 0.6—0.7°. The body temperature of females undergoes through castration a lowering, which amounted on the average to 0.4°. The body temperature of males undergoes through castration no influencing. The body temperature of the castrated male is made equal through feminization to the temperature of a normal female. The body temperature of the castrated female seems to be altered through masculinization only little. The higher temperature of the female is in any case an effect of the female germ-gland.*"

Later, however, *Lipschütz* (1923), as already mentioned above, had measurements carried out on the rabbit by three candidates of medicine, and drew in such [measurements] by *Ocaranza* on the guinea-pig, from which he (S. 1262) concludes (translated from the French): "After all these results it seems to us very probable that all consequences which the authors have drawn over temperature-differences between the two sexes are false, evoked through an insufficient technique [applied] to so delicate a question on the disputed territory." The mentioned pupils, *Bormann, Selma Brunnow* and *v. Savary* (1923, S. 256) make the following further remarks:

"With insufficiently deep introduction of the thermometer, a difference to the advantage of the female could perhaps arise thereby, that in the female the position of the orificium ani with reference to the inner and outer sex-organs is another than in the male. We must, on the ground of our experiments, assume that the temperature-measurement indicates to us the real body temperature only then, when the animal is in normal position and the thermometer is introduced deeply enough, so that the influences of the outside temperature are switched off. *Rumpf* (5) had indeed already, years ago, pointed out that the result of the measurement depends on the depth into which the thermometer is introduced; but the various authors have not drawn from this relation the consequence of always introducing the thermometer so deeply that all influences from the side of the outside temperature would be switched off. Our own measurements show sufficiently how much the result of the measurement can be influenced through this moment."

After *Lillian Moore* is reproached that she (it reads there "he"!) introduced the thermometer each time to a different depth ("two or more inches"), left it in too briefly (2 minutes), and found too great daily fluctuations, it reads further (S. 255): "In the experiments of *Steinach* and *Lipschütz*, and likewise of *Przibram* and his coworkers on rats, the thermometer was most probably not introduced deeply enough", and (S. 256): "The same criticism that we exercised above on the findings of *Steinach* and *Lipschütz* seems to us justified also against the experiments of *Przibram* and his coworkers. *Bierens de Haan* (3) found in 24-day-old rats (*Mus decumanus*) a difference of 0.1 to the advantage of the female. In animals 38—54 days old the difference to the advantage of the female amounted to 0.73°. *Przibram* found in animals at the age of 92—242 days a difference of 0.3, and in animals which were 426—503 days old, a difference of 0.45 to the advantage of the female. *Sumner* (cited after 3) found in adult animals a difference of 0.76 to the advantage of the female. According to *Bierens de Haan* the temperature in his animals fluctuated between 34.8 and 37.2°, i.e. the maximal difference amounted to 2.4°. According to *R. Tigerstedt* (7) the mean body temperature in the rat, however, amounts to 37.9° and the fluctuations [to] only 1.1. It is therefore very probable that in the experiments of *Przibram* and his coworkers the real body temperature was not measured [i.e. it was not measured], because the thermometer was not introduced deeply enough and not to a constant depth."

Were *Lipschütz*, with his more recent view, and his coworkers right, then all our conclusions drawn from the coordination of K.S.-relation and body warmth would be untenable, insofar as they concern the sex, for then there would be no sex-differences at all in body warmth. It must therefore be examined what justification belongs to the criticism of our measurements of the body warmth. We must thereby distinguish two reproaches that have been made against the experiments on rats and mice. The one relates to the inexact maintenance of the experimental conditions within one and the same measuring-series, the other to the too-small measuring-depth in all series. If the first is founded, then the body-temperature-measurements would, even apart from the sex, have to be inexact, and the obtained absolute values would not agree with those of other authors. Fortunately, precisely in the latter time several works have appeared which give body temperatures for rats and mice at various outside temperatures. In Tab. XIX I have put together these values, ascertained without regard to sex, with the earlier ones given by *Sumner* on mice, [and] by my coworkers and me on rats. The works of *Aszódi* (1921) and *Goto* (1923) were carried out in the formerly *Tangl*-ian Laboratory under Professor *Hári*, hence in an Institute specially occupied with, and distinguished in, the warmth-production-problems. To the data of *Graham* and *Hutchinson* (1914) again special significance belongs, because these authors worked not with the mercury-thermometer, but with the thermo-electrical method of *Philipp* and *Demuth*, which at present is regarded as the most exact. Let one compare in particular my rat-values with *Goto*'s analogous ones, in order to recognize the quantitatively good agreement; further the means of *Graham-Hutchinson* with my maximal figures. Only at low outside temperatures do we find discrepancy several times, which is to be traced back to the fact that those investigators who bring un-acclimatized rats or mice into this extreme temperature-condition must reckon with the entry of rigor (Torpor), with which the temperature in the interior sinks far below that otherwise valid for the relevant outside temperature.

We have, just like *Sumner*, always taken the movement-condition into consideration, and are therefore free of this error: in one and the same series the same treatment and measuring-method was always applied. The back position in *Bierens*'s measurements has, by the way, not furnished lower values than otherwise; that may have lain in the age of his rats, or in the lesser suitability of this genus for falling into hypnosis.

The time-of-day of the measurement was likewise always the same for one and the same series (mean of morning and evening — *Congdon* 1910; 5—6 in the evening — *Przibram* 1917 Umw. VI, S. 38; evening temperature — *Bierens* 1922, S. 9; morning temperature ibid. 10—12), so that the daily fluctuations did not at all come into consideration for our questions. Also the measuring-depth was always measured the same for one and the same series. *Bierens* writes hereover (1922, S. 2): "It is of the greatest importance to push the thermometer in always equally far, for a somewhat deeper or less deep pushing-in can be the cause that the thermometer notes apparent temperature-differences up to 1°." Thereto D. *Finkler* (1882) had already, in measurements on the guinea-pig, drawn attention. *Sumner* (1913, S. 320) laid a rubber ring around the bulb of his thermometer, in order to hold to one and the same depth. He had even (S. 341) made against *Congdon*'s experiments quite the same reproaches which he now gets to hear from *Lipschütz*'s pupils. The agreement in the results in the rats and mice, which have been investigated at various outside temperatures, between the single authors further shows that the measuring-time in our and *Sumner*'s experiments must also have been sufficient. The latter, by the way, did not use clinical thermometers and [used] still much shorter times than we. With the clinical thermometer it depends entirely on the construction whether one must read off after 2 or several minutes; but always, of course, one and the same time must be maintained in a series. Of further circumstances, let [reference] be made to the wholly constant temperature of the surroundings in our rats, [and] to the respective measurement and registration of average-temperatures in *Sumner*'s mice, which makes possible a strict comparison of the body-warmths.

We now turn against the second reproach, [that] of too-small measuring-depth. *Lipschütz* and his pupils pay homage to the principle: the deeper, the better. Obviously they thereby start from the supposition that the body temperature increases steadily from outside toward inside, as for instance in a sun [Sonne]. This view, however, has been rejected by the already-mentioned D. *Finkler* (1882, S. 117), on the ground of the experiments carried out in *Pflüger*'s Laboratory, with the following words: "There prevail accordingly different temperatures at different depths. But no uniform decline of the temperature from a warmest kernel to the periphery takes place, as a body would have to exhibit in whose center the warmth-source lies, and which from there loses warmth through its various layers by conduction and at the surface by radiation. Firstly, the animal body is not a body which possesses a warmth-source at the middle-point. Rather, the warmth-sources are distributed through the whole body. All these innumerable warmth-sources give off very different warmth at different time, so that one can very well imagine that the preponderant amount of warmth becomes free now in the activity of the deeper organs, glands, [of] the intestine, now in the deeper- or more-superficially-lying muscles. But beside this there now plays, in the normal animal, yet a quite special warmth-source an outstanding role, namely the one which comes about in the musculature without the appearance of contraction." Further, S. 118: "*The equiwarmness is not absolute, because, on the one hand, under physiological performances of the warm-blooded animal not inconsiderable fluctuations of the temperature occur, and because, secondly, the various layers of the body can vary their temperature, even without the temperature proper to the deepest parts, or for instance the temperature of the most important provinces of the nervous system, needing to alter anything of their constancy.*"

D. *Finkler* has, for example, investigated the temperature of the guinea-pig at different rectal depth; the data are entered in our Tab. XIX. The maximum of the body warmth he found not at the greatest investigated measuring-depth of 90, but at the lower of 70 mm. From 25 mm to 70 mm a rise of the body warmth is to be remarked in the three outside temperatures employed. But at 25 mm the anomaly shows itself, that the rectal temperature in the middle one of the investigated temperature-intervals was higher than in the two others. It is a matter of the mean of many measurements, not for instance of an aberrant case. Were the insufficient insulation of the measuring-space in the rectum from the outside temperature alone decisive for the differences of the inner temperatures, then this result remains quite inexplicable. No otherwise than with D. *Finkler*'s guinea-pig does it behave in the rats: here the greatest depth hitherto measured out in the rectum (*Graham* and *Hutchinson*) shows 40 mm, but the mean of the measurements carried out by them lies rather still deeper than our measurement at 15 mm, which *Goto*'s measurements at 25 mm almost completely reach. Whether *Bierens*'s values obtained on younger animals are somewhat higher, because the relative sinking-in into the rectum is, at the same absolute [depth], to be rated higher on account of the smaller animal-size, cannot be said with certainty. But even if this were so, this temperature-increase cannot be referred to the better insulation of the measuring-space, because its distance from the environment is indeed not greater than in the larger [animals]. In mice, *Aszódi* measured at 25 mm depth smaller values than *Sumner* at 13 mm. From 9—17 mm a gradual rise shows itself on comparison of *Congdon*'s and *Sumner*'s experiments. In general we must therefore establish that, indeed, in the vicinity of the orificium ani a lower temperature is to be measured than further inward, but by no means such a distribution of the temperatures as the communication with the surrounding-warmth, as the sole factor, would presuppose. Above all, differences of the greatest rectal depth too remain in existence, as soon as the outside temperatures are varied. Let one compare in Tab. XIX the relevant values (for the three animal-kinds). It would be incorrect to ascribe to the penetration of the outside temperature no influence at all in the thermometric measurement. In fact two errors must be taken into consideration: the direct communication of the rectal air with the outside air between rectal wall and thermometer on the one hand, the conduction of the outside warmth along the thermometer-tube on the other hand. The communication of the rectal space with the outer world during the measurement was very much diminished in our rat experiments thereby, that the sphincter ani had to lay itself firmly around a constriction above the bulb; in *Sumner*'s mouse experiments, through the mentioned rubber ring. By the way, in *Graham-Hutchinson*'s thermo-electrical measurements the introduced wire was so thin, and the introduction of the thermo-needles so deep, that a very tight drawing-together of the sphincter [was] possible, and thereby probably every influence — with the exception of the conduction along the (insulated!) wire — appears excluded. Now, as to the influence of the outside warmth on the protruding part in mercury-thermometers: *Sumner* (1913, S. 321) carried out, with his three thermometers, direct experiments, in that he sank them either wholly into warm water of body temperature, or merely up to the rubber ring, while the outer air-temperature amounted to 1—2° C. Two thermometers let no [difference], one [let] a difference of less than ⁵/₁₀° to the advantage of the wholly sunken-in condition, be recognized. More exact agreement could therefore not be reached. In our rat-measurements I have attempted, by use of the "thermometer-thread-correction" [Thermometerfadenkorrektur] customary among the physicists, to gain a picture of the greatest possible error from this source. In Tab. XX, for albinotic migratory rats [Wanderratten], the observed body-warmths in two ages and at outside temperatures from 40—5° downward are taken from our earlier tables. To the left of them are placed those values which, with a thermometer-thread-correction (last column) for 10 protruding thermometer-degrees, would have to result as correct values, 35° outside temperature being taken as the starting-point; to the right of them, the values calculated according to our formula for the influence of the outside on the inner temperature. The formation of the differences between observed and calculated values on the one hand, between observed values and thermometer-thread-corrections on the other hand, and the formation of the mean in each case from all these differences for the various temperatures, shows a two- to twelve-fold as great deviation of the [body warmth] calculated by means of the thermometerthread-correction from the observation [as] of the body warmth calculated according to our formula. The difference becomes, as a rule, greater when we exclude the somewhat uncertain values at 40 and 5°, because [they were] obtained from another generation. In any case, in our rat experiments too, the thermometer-thread cannot be made responsible for the magnitude of the decrease of the body warmth at sinking temperature.

The difference between the two values is as a rule larger, the more accurately one measures; thereby it becomes clear in advance that the consistently higher body-warmth of the weibchen [female] at sinking temperature cannot be made responsible for the decrease in size of the thermometer-thread-correction.

With the rejection, through the foregoing arguments, of the two objections raised against us regarding variability and depth, the question is now at the same time answered whether our measurements of the sex-warmth [Geschlechtswärme] have any significance, insofar as it is a matter of the accuracy of the method. It was, moreover, clear from the outset that the consistently higher temperature of the weibchen [female] at all outside temperatures cannot at all be traced back to insufficient sealing of the measuring cavity against the outside world, for the differences between männchen [males] and weibchen [females] are maintained even at differing measuring depths. Thus it amounts, for *Sumner*'s mice according to our Tab. V, at an outside temperature of 21.5° and a measuring depth of 10 mm to 0.30°, at one of 17 mm even to 0.80°; at 13° outside temperature at 10 mm to 1.44°, at 17 mm to 0.28°. In the rabbit the difference at a measuring depth of 20 mm according to *Lipschütz* et al. (1923) amounts to 0.14°, at 40 mm to 0.20°, is distinct at 50 mm according to *L. Moore* (figures outstanding), amounts at 60 mm according to *Simpson* and *Galbraith* (1905/06) to 0.11°, and would, according to *Bormann, Brunnow* and *Savary* (1923), decline at the maximum temperatures at 80 mm to 0.05°, whereas at the middle temperatures it is even supposed to yield, on the contrary, 0.07° in favor of the männchen [male]. In the duck the difference according to *Martins* at a measuring depth of 80 mm is 0.347°, according to *Simpson* and *Galbraith* at a probably somewhat lesser one, to judge by the absolutely smaller figures, 0.34° C. In our rats the difference, as we set out above (V B), is dependent not directly on the outside warmth but on the inner warmth, which is only indirectly connected with the former, and therefore cannot be strongly falsified by communication and thermometer-thread. Unfortunately the values for the deeper measurements of the other authors are without indication of the sex, so that I could lay down no scale.

The impossibility of explaining differences in the sex-warmth by the influence of the outside temperature was the occasion for *Lipschütz*' pupils to bring in the position of the orificium ani as an explanation. I will at first disregard entirely in which direction a change in the thermometer reading might come about through the position of the orificium. We do not need to know this first in order to demonstrate the inconsistencies of this assumption with our previous knowledge. And indeed we need only let *Lipschütz* himself have the word to grant this: how could, in his castration and heterologization experiments, the temperature in the weibchen and the heterologized männchen on the one hand, in the männchen, the castrates and the heterologized weibchen on the other, be a similar one, if the position of the anus were decisive for these measurement results? Or does the position of the anus in the castrated weibchen perhaps shift in such a way that it comes to equal that of a male one? Let one not object that *Lipschütz* himself now rejects his measurement results on the basis of the measurements with greater measuring depth and lets both sexes, like castrates, be fully equal in body-warmth. The position of this orificium is drawn upon for the newer measurements to explain the smaller measuring depth as the cause of this result. But even in the sense of the "communication-explanation" the position of the orificium does not fit the rats, for the rats are with little to commend the introduction depth, since the muff and the broad anal cushion surround the rectum, into which the thermometer cannot be inserted as in the weibchen. The temperature of the testicular sac was recently determined by *C. Moore* and *Quick* (1924) raised in rabbits, guinea-pigs and rats. In the latter, *Carl Moore*'s figure of the difference at 16.4° C room temperature is 6.1°, at 32° C 3.8°. The scrotal muff thus, according to the degree of the outside warmth, gains several degrees. I hold it for not exceeded; the thermometer-thread correction comes into consideration here, the small difference in favor of the männchen [male] coming out, just as little as do the measurements taken close together, which probably present the corresponding influence of insertion. The measuring depth is here surely of importance for the determination of the body-warmth, but neither it nor the position of the orificium explains our found sex differences. Wherein actually this difference lies does not at all come into consideration: it depends only on whether the temperature can be determined free of methodical and technical errors, [the temperature] which from within is able to act upon the changing tail-root. For the question of correlation between inner temperature and tail growth, of course, the kind of this temperature difference does not come into consideration at all: it depends only on whether the temperature, free of methodical and technical errors, can be determined, the temperature which from within is able to act upon the changing tail-root. That, I believe, has indeed actually succeeded. Therewith enough is done for the defense of our chief result against the objections of *Lipschütz* and companions. Let there, at the conclusion of this section, be given only briefly a compilation of some facts which I have hitherto not mentioned, in order not to prejudice the reader from the outset against the investigations at the *Lipschütz* institute.

Whereas the measurements on mice were undertaken by *Sumner*, those on rats by independent researchers experienced in experimental zoology, likewise the experiments on guinea-pigs by *Steinach* and *Lipschütz* himself, we must, for the rightness of his data on the rabbit, rely merely on his three pupils, who still stand before the doctorate. With the accuracy of the data in the rabbit-works it likewise does not stand at the best: the introduction depth according to methods 1 and 2 is given by the three on p. 250 as 2 cm, by *Lipschütz* for the same measurements according to p. 1261 as 4 cm. The authority for the guinea-pig, *Ocaranza*, writes to me (15 April 1924) that he measured 6 or 7 cm deep. By contrast, *Sumner* and his coworkers had scarcely more than 1 mm difference within a measuring series. The Dorpat school (1923) has, furthermore, not found it worth the trouble really to consult the literature it cites, for otherwise the reference to the importance of measuring depth, owed to *D. Finkler* (1882), would scarcely have been attributed (p. 264) to *Rumpf* (1884), who merely remarks (p. 100) that in his experiments "the thermometer is always inserted to the same length"; the American Professor *F. B. Sumner* cited among my coworkers (1923, p. 256), *Lillian Moore* quoted as a man (1923, p. 255), *Simpson*'s experiments on hens, guinea-pigs and rabbits passed over (1923, p. 257), and yet the reproach of insufficient methodology was not made!

Certainly the technique of body-warmth determination is still capable of improvement. What we now need in order to win further insight is a thermometer that measures simultaneously at various depths and possibly registers directly photographically. Such a one is under construction. Then it will be possible to take up a precise "warmth-topography" of the rectum and to throw more light on the difference in the warmth-distribution between the sexes. In this sense a *Lipschützian* spur, through the however unjustified critique, is to be thanked!

So I hope that *Lipschütz* will now also, for his part, undertake further experiments and thereby take my objections and criticisms into account. It would be advisable that he then not take merely a single and comparable männchen [male], as has been the case in the year 1913 (with four weibchen [females]). That is, after all, somewhat little! *Sumner* worked on 33 male and 13 female mice; we worked on 35 male and 37 female wandering rats, on 6 male and 6 female house rats, which were in each case of the same stock and of known age.

## VIII. Exclusion of errors with change of the sex-condition.

The establishment of higher body-temperature in the weibchen [female] cannot without further ado be valued as cause of the different tail length, because in the periodically occurring conditions of the "heat" (English "heat") a higher temperature than in the männchen [male] occurs anyway. It would therefore have to be especially attended to, in comparative measurements, what the sex-condition of the weibchen is, as soon as it is a matter of mature animals. Even in advanced stages of pregnancy a small temperature-elevation seems to occur, which came to light in our measurements (*Przibram* 1917) and those of *Steinach* (in *Lipschütz* 1917, p. 184). Heat [Läufigkeit] and gravidity must therefore be taken into account, animals in heat and gravid animals must be excluded from comparison with the männchen, which has always been adhered to in my compilations. Otherwise we would falsify the state of affairs to our own advantage (on the cause of the temperature-rise in pregnancy cf. *Przibram* 1921, Umwelt VII, p. 171). Entirely excluded are errors of this kind in the determinations of the sex-warmth carried out before sexual maturity, such as were undertaken for the human being by *Roger*, for the wandering rat by *Bierens*. For the birds the [individual] measurements of *Martins* are at hand. The observation of Tab. XIV teaches us that the difference of the body-warmth between the sexes, which becomes small on the approach of puberty, rises in general with increasing maturity. It is not, say, the difference between mature and immature weibchen that is to be traced back to the periodic condition, but rather a constant divergence of the sexes, which makes itself more strongly felt with maturity.

## IX. Exclusion of errors with change of the body-size (K).

We must also take into account, as a possible warmth-source, the absolute body-size. It is not feasible to want to compare variously large animals through the same insertion of the thermometer.

Therefore it is necessary not to take männchen and weibchen of too great a difference in body-length. With the use of specimens out of one litter, where one is forced to make do with what the litter just offers, it must be checked whether length-differences occur that must be heeded. In rats and mice this will scarcely ever be the case, for, with respect to size, there tend to be at birth and also later no such differences between the sexes that they would carry weight against the accuracy of the thermometer-insertion. On the average, for various rat-stocks, the männchen are mostly somewhat longer than the weibchen of the same age (see *Donaldson* 1917); our Tab. XX brings the corresponding orientation for our house- and wandering-rats: in 62.5—92.9% of the litters of a stock the männchen predominate at the age of II weeks, in 52.6—100% at the age of VIII—IX weeks. The low percentage figure comes about only through the presence of a relatively large number of litters with completely equal male and female average length; the number of litters in which, in the same stock, the weibchen on the average outstripped the männchen amounted to only 31.6%. For our problems the predominance of body-length in the männchen is favorable, because thereby an objection that could be directed against the tracing-back of the longer tail in the weibchen to its higher body-warmth falls away from the outset. The tail is, at birth in rats and mice, relatively short. With increasing age, then, the relative tail length increases. If the weibchen ran ahead of the männchen in body-length, one could believe that the increase of its tail were to be traced back merely to the more rapid reaching of a particular absolute body-length, with which a larger relative tail length is connected. But since, just the reverse, the männchen that runs ahead in growth has at the same age (or even at the same size) the shorter tail, such an explanation would be untenable. Further details on the growth of the tail are reserved for "Umwelt XIV" (in heat above 30° the relation of the absolute size between männchen and weibchen can reverse; cf. there Tab. XLV and *Sumner* 1909, p. 141; but also the tail length).

## X. Summary.

1. In the albinotic wandering rats, *Epimys decumanus* Pall., the relative tail length at the same age is larger in the weibchen [females] than in the männchen [males].

2. This holds for every age and every environmental-temperature, yet the difference is smaller with rising outside-warmth.

3. This difference of the relative tail length thus presents itself as a tertiary sex-mark modifiable through temperature.

4. Since, according to earlier communications (*Przibram* 1915; *Bierens* 1920), the body-warmth of the young female rats is on the average higher than that of the male ones, but less so at higher outside temperatures, the larger tail length of the weibchen is to be brought, unforced, into connection with its higher inner temperature.

5. Analogous observations are at hand for the albinotic house-mouse (*Mus musculus* L.) in the experiments of *Sumner* (1913, 1915), and there likewise higher body-warmth and larger tail lengths were established in the weibchen (the latter also by *Sundström* 1922).

6. *Sumner*'s interpretation of the difference of the relative tail length as a measuring error owing to the displacement of the anus by the testes in the male sex cannot apply, since otherwise the strongly swollen testes would, precisely at higher outside temperature, call forth a larger difference, which contradicts the found decrease of the difference precisely at rising warmth.

7. In the wild black house-rat (*Epimys rattus* L.) too, corresponding to the higher body-temperature of the weibchen, a larger tail length is to be established in this sex.

8. By contrast, in the wild aguti-colored wandering rat a difference of the sexes in regard to relative tail length was not to be observed in all temperatures.

9. The relative tail length of this wild stock-race of *Epimys decumanus* is, in relation to that of the tame albinos of the same kind, much smaller; the latter may therefore be regarded as an artificial "warmth-race," or, conversely, the wild wandering rats as "cold-animals" in comparison with the albinos.

10. Accordingly, both with the action of raised and with the action of lowered temperature, an approximation of the sexes in regard to relative tail length seems to take place.

11. It lies near to assume that in the wildcolored wandering rats, at certain outside temperatures, the sexes no longer differ through the body-temperature, and that therefore the tail lengths in männchen and weibchen become equal.

12. Unfortunately no temperature-measurement was carried out in the wild aguti-colored wandering rats; *Lipschütz*, however, communicated to me in 1922 that in the rabbit (*Lepus cuniculus*) no difference in the body-warmth of the sexes is to be noticed, according to measurements by *Brunnow* and *Bormann* — that the body-temperature therefore seems to provide no generally valid distinguishing-mark of the sexes in warmblooded animals: body-temperature would thus be a facultative tertiary sex-mark like so many others.

13. The communications appearing in the meantime by *Lipschütz, Bormann, Brunnow* and v. *Savary* (1923), however, let such a difference — even though a small one — be recognized in the rabbit, as *Lillian Moore* too confirms. Altogether the review of the present literature speaks rather in favor of an obligatory, even though only small, body-warmth-difference of the sexes in the warm-blooded animals (mammals and birds).

14. The objections brought forward by *Lipschütz* and his pupils against the accuracy of the body-warmth measurements on the experimental animals by *Bierens, Przibram, Steinach* and *Sumner* have been examined in detail and rejected.
