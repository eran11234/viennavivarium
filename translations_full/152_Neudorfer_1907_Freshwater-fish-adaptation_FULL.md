## Experiments on the Adaptation of Freshwater Fishes to Salt Water.

By

Med. Dr. Arthur Neudörfer.

*(From the Biological Experimental Institute in Vienna.)*

Received on 23 February 1907.

*Archiv für Entwicklungsmechanik der Organismen*, vol. 23 (1907).

> **Full translation.** A complete English rendering of the running text of “Experiments on the Adaptation of Freshwater Fishes” (Neudörfer, 1907), including all tables, figure and plate legends, and footnotes. Numbers and table cells were transcribed from the page images, not the noisy OCR.

It is well known that some fishes are able to live both in river water and in seawater. It now appears striking how it should be possible that these animals — assuming the permeability of the gills — can endure so great a fluctuation of the osmotic pressure without harm, whereas for the higher vertebrates the constancy of the osmotic pressure of the fluid bathing the cells of their organism is positively a condition of life. This latter fact appears comprehensible at once, since it is known that living cells, upon a change in the osmotic pressure of the fluid surrounding them, are so damaged in the structure of their protoplasm by the uptake or release of water that cell death is the consequence. De Vries (1) made use of this property of cells — he worked on plant cells — in order to determine the osmotic pressure within them.

For if the salinity of the external fluid was only a trifle lower than that within the cell, vacuole formation occurred in the latter. The limit, now, at which this vacuole formation still failed to appear, corresponded exactly to the osmotic pressure of the cell contents. Balbiani (2) applied this method to infusorians and observed thereby that, already with a concentration of the external solution higher by only 0.04 %, a shrinkage of the body surface occurred, and death followed if the animals were not returned to fresh water in time. Now, if this law held good not only in highly organized animals, but also in such animals of the lowest animal class, it must appear all the more astonishing that some animals are able to pass over from an approximately 3 % salt solution into river water — which shows a freezing-point depression of on average −0.02° — without harm; the more so, as the blood circulating in the gill vessels is separated from the external fluid only by the delicate gill membranes. Since swollen animal membranes are permeable to crystalloid, impermeable to colloid substances, one would have to assume that in the gills there is no obstacle to the passage of water and salts. In the lower, sea-dwelling animals this indeed appears actually to be the case. Thus Krukenberg (3), who compared with one another the chlorine content of the seawater and that of the fluid dripping from the gelatinous discs of medusae, found a far-reaching agreement of the two values. Botazzi had established that, just as the blood, so too the glandular secretions of lower animals agree in their osmotic pressure with that of the seawater (cited after v. Fürth, 4). Since F. Frédéricq (5) had demonstrated, for the blood of certain crustaceans which also reside in brackish water, that the osmotic concentration of it is dependent on the density of the surrounding fluid, the proof appears established that the body envelopes of these animals are permeable both to water and to salts. An exception to this rule is made, according to the investigations of L. Frédéricq, by the crayfish. The salinity of its blood amounts, measured by the freezing-point depression, to δ = −0.80, whereas the freezing-point depression of the water in which the crab had lived was only −0.02. If, on the other hand, crab blood was dialyzed against water through a dead animal membrane, the pressure difference equalized completely within two days. This fact proves that the gill membrane behaves differently from a dead animal membrane, since it is able to retain the salts of the blood. Similarly to the crayfish, according to the investigations of Botazzi (7), certain cartilaginous fishes appear to behave, since he found that their gill membranes are half-permeable in the sense that they are indeed permeable to water, but not to salts. Even under these conditions the osmotic pressure of the body fluid and that of its surroundings must be equal, only the kind of salts may differ within and without. Some sea-dwelling animals from the series of the bony fishes have an osmotic pressure that is about half as great as that of the seawater, that is, δ = −1.03 (cited after v. Fürth, p. 110). Finally, let it be mentioned that sea turtles have an osmotic pressure that is completely independent of the medium surrounding them, in that the freezing-point depression of their blood amounts to −0.61, which is not further surprising, since these animals breathe through lungs.

Before I enter upon my own experiments, let it still be permitted to adduce, out of the wealth of interesting material on adaptation experiments, those which form the foundation of the following experiments.

The first to carry out experiments on the adaptation of freshwater amoebae to salt water was V. Czerny (8). He succeeded in acclimatizing the animals, which are very sensitive to salt solutions, to a 4 % salt solution. How far this habituation can go in individuals of the lowest animal classes is proved by investigations of Hennegoy (9), who succeeded in keeping alive an infusorian species *Fabrea salina* by gradual evaporation of salt-containing water, while crystals had already settled on the bottom of the vessel. Interesting and pertinent here are also the observations on the animal fauna of salt-rich lakes. Mollusc forms have been found in salt-rich lakes of North America, and even in salt-saturated pools flagellates and other infusorians have been encountered. Only in the Dead Sea, at a salinity of 15–42 %, is all animal life lacking. Whether this phenomenon is connected with the salinity or with the richness of this water in bituminous substances may be left undecided. As regards the adaptation in higher animals, Beudant (10) has adapted freshwater molluscs to salt water of 4 % concentration. As regards the reverse process, the adaptation of marine organisms to fresh water, here too both artificial and natural adaptations have been observed. Especially productive in this respect is the Baltic Sea, which from east to west shows all gradations from fresh water to seawater. While now, in the eastern part, which is almost completely freshened, a great part of the marine fauna has disappeared, yet some forms have been able to adapt themselves to the altered conditions of life, though under certain changes in their size and in their shape, K. Brandt (11). Further data on natural adaptation are found in Semper (12), Cuénot (13), and others.

From the foregoing observations it follows that the various water-dwelling animals behave quite differently toward changes of the osmotic pressure of their surrounding fluid. Long known and, before the development of physical chemistry, incomprehensible was the fact that a brusque transition from seawater to fresh water, or conversely, as a rule has the death of the animal as its consequence. Today it must, on the contrary, appear wonderful that some animals are nevertheless able to overcome such great differences of the osmotic pressure. Thus it is known, for example, of the salmon, that they ascend at spawning time from the sea into the rivers. Conversely, the eel seeks out the sea at spawning time, whence the young brood returns again into the rivers. Herrings have been found 120 km above the river mouths, sharks in the Ganges and Zambezi. All these observations concern fishes from the higher orders. Now it is, however, known that sea-dwelling cyclostomes, which in the animal series have their place close above *Amphioxus lanceolatus*, likewise ascend with the salmon into the rivers, and this fact appears so astonishing because one would have to assume that, owing to their position in the animal series, the salinity of their blood is dependent on the medium surrounding them. Since sea-dwelling cyclostomes were not at my disposal, a form living in fresh water, the larva of *Petromyzon Planeri* Bl., was used for the following experiments.

5–6 of these animals were placed in a vessel which contained so much sand that they could bury themselves. The quantity of fluid amounted to five liters of water. The plan was, by means of cryoscopy, to investigate the changes of the blood with the increase of the concentration of the external water, and so to demonstrate the permeability of the gills. In the first experiments 100 cc of seawater was added daily; but it soon turned out that already at a concentration of two parts seawater to three parts river water the animals perished. Although these experiments were varied in manifold ways, so that the concentration was raised only quite gradually, the result remained always the same, even in those experiments where it had taken 2 months until the above-mentioned concentration had been reached. The phenomena observed were the following: the otherwise very lively animals become slow and feeble in their movements. They are no longer able to bore into the sand, but remain lying on the sand. Striking is the respiratory distress, which begins fairly simultaneously with the other symptoms of disease. In the experiments as to whether such *Petromyzon* larvae, which had been kept for a longer time in dilute salt solutions, had become more resistant to salt solutions than unprepared ones, it turned out that this was not the case. It succeeded without difficulty in bringing such animals directly out of the river water into a solution of one part seawater to four parts river water, without their having shown any symptoms of disease. But as soon as the seawater concentration rose above δ = −0.75, the previously mentioned phenomena began, and the animals perished within a few days. As regards the investigation of the blood, this met with difficulties. The roughly 10 cm long animals yielded only a few drops of very rapidly clotting blood, so that the determination of the freezing-point depression of it appeared impossible. The attempt, too, to obtain the necessary quantity of fluid from a larger number of animals by pressing out after comminuting them, failed, as did another. For the attempt was made, namely, to let the animals freeze fast, to crush them in this state, and after thawing to wait whether the lymph would settle out. In both experiments I obtained only a sticky mass, which was unsuitable for the execution of cryoscopy.

For the solution of the question posed, a new way had to be sought. First I hit upon the idea of placing the petromyzonts in iron solutions, in order to observe, on sections of the gills by means of the Prussian-blue reaction, the passage of the iron under the microscope. For the experiments there were used, in turn, ferric chloride, potassium ferrocyanide, and potassium ferricyanide. A 0.1 % solution was begun with, and the concentration was gradually increased. When the animals were brought into a 0.1 % ferric chloride solution, they showed for about half an hour curious jerky, retrograde movements, such as could otherwise never be observed in these animals. After the cessation of these movements, they remained lying on the sand and showed only sluggish movements when they were disturbed. There was, on the other hand, no respiratory distress. Already in a 0.3 % solution the animals perished. In potassium ferrocyanide the animals remained fairly fresh up to a 0.4 % solution, but then likewise rapidly perished. Best tolerated was potassium ferricyanide. Even at a solution of 0.5 % the animals were fairly mobile, while the little food-fishes added to them had already died off at 0.3 %. On the sections of the gills of these latter no usable result could be obtained. A new attempt to demonstrate the permeability of the gills consisted in this, that a poison was sought which, brought only onto the gills, would have unfolded its toxic effect. Although it is known from the vertebrates that prussic acid is resorbed extraordinarily easily, and therefore the results to be expected might perhaps have been contestable, the first experiment was nevertheless undertaken with a two-percent prussic-acid solution, which was most kindly placed at my disposal by Herr Dr. Freund, head of the chemical laboratory at the Rudolfsspital. Thereupon there showed itself the most peculiar property of these animals, of being completely resistant to prussic acid. Into one animal, 2 cc of a 2 % solution was injected under the dorsal skin, without this — for so small an animal enormous — dose having produced any noteworthy effect. An animal so treated was still completely lively after 8 days. Another was killed after the injection of 0.04 g prussic acid, a drop of the blood was sucked up and injected into a mouse. This died in 30 seconds. Much more sensitive proved to be already young pike (*Esox lucius* L.). A dose of 0.02 g prussic acid sufficed to kill them within half an hour. It would be interesting to subject the action of prussic acid in the animal series to a comparative investigation. Another strongly acting poison for the experimental animals could not be found, so that this experiment too was not carried further.

Finally, it was attempted to investigate the individual constituents of the seawater, each for itself, in their action on the *Petromyzon* larvae.

Of the constituents of the seawater, the potassium chloride, the sodium chloride, the magnesium sulfate, the magnesium chloride, and the calcium sulfate were subjected to investigation. Toward potassium salts the animals proved extraordinarily sensitive. Already a concentration of 0.1 % sufficed to kill them within two days, one of 0.2 % to kill them within a few hours. Here the kind of the salts appeared to play no role. Potassium carbonate proved just as poisonous as potassium chloride and potassium sulfate. That in these observations it cannot be a question of the diminution of the osmotic pressure, but of a specific poison effect, is at once clear in view of the very low concentration. Actually the freezing-point depression of the potassium-hydrate solution used amounted to −0.08. Higher-organized fishes too — there were used young crucian carp (*Carassius carassius* L.) — indeed tolerated a 0.2 % solution of potassium chloride, but died at a 0.4 % solution within 24 hours. With sodium chloride the experiments on the petromyzonts were so conducted that a 0.2 % solution was begun with and every 4 days the concentration was raised by 0.2 %. Every 2 weeks the entire water quantity was changed. In the course of 8 weeks there could thus be obtained a concentration of 1.4 % salt water, in which the animals just barely still lived. δ amounted to −0.85. After 48 hours' sojourn in this salt solution, however, the animals perished. It was striking that they were still completely lively in a 1 % salt solution and that then fairly suddenly the symptoms of disease appeared. Here too, in the last days of their life, the respiratory distress was quite pronounced. Very well tolerated was the magnesium sulfate. In the period of 6 weeks a concentration of 3.3 % could be reached, at which the animals still kept alive. This concentration in and of itself is not very astonishing, when one considers that the osmotic pressure of MgSO₄, on account of its high molecular weight, is comparatively low. Thus δ of the just-mentioned solution amounted to −0.29. At this fairly high concentration of the magnesium solution it could be hoped that, if the gills were permeable to magnesium at all, a chemical analysis would yield an increased magnesium content in the body of the fish. The chemical analysis was carried out by Herr Dr. Freund, head of the chemical laboratory of the Imperial-Royal Rudolf Foundation, for which I should like to express to him my best thanks at this place. The animals were first ashed and then investigated.

The percentage figures calculated per 100 g of ash permit, as I believe, the conclusion that magnesium sulfuricum has passed through the gills of the investigated animals, since, by analogy with the experiments with eels to be mentioned later, it is not probable that the resorption from the gastrointestinal canal exerts an essential influence.

In the experiments with magnesium chloride, a concentration of 1.6 % δ = − 0.43 could be achieved. In this solution the animals perished.

Finally, artificial seawater was prepared following the specifications of Herbst. In each 100 g of spring water there were dissolved 3 g of sodium chloride, 0.07 g of potassium chloride, 0.26 g of magnesium sulfate, 0.5 g of magnesium chloride, and 0.1 g of calcium sulfate. To this was added a knife-point of calcium phosphate, after which the portion that remained undissolved was filtered off from the solution thus obtained. With this solution, experiments were carried out in the manner indicated earlier, with increases in concentration, with and without KCl. In both cases the result was the same. At a concentration whose freezing-point depression amounted to − 0.75 to − 0.78, the animals regularly perished, without the presence or absence of the potassium salt having made any perceptible difference.

Already in these experiments it may appear striking that, given the great sensitivity of the petromyzonts to potassium, the presence of the latter made no perceptible difference. This emerged still more clearly in a second experiment. *Petromyzon* larvae were kept in a 2 % MgSO₄ solution, and potassium chloride was added to this solution. In this form it was possible to reach a 0.5 % potash solution without the animals having shown any signs of illness. Loeb (14) has shown that the toxicity of monovalent ions is abolished by the presence of polyvalent salts, and indeed by trivalent ones more than by divalent ones. In our case, then, the toxicity of the potassium ion was abolished by the magnesium ion. As regards the concentrations stated, it must be noted that it was not possible to give a perfectly exact dosage, since the sand necessary for the life of the animals examined was always moist, so that in general the figures given may be taken as too low. For this reason the freezing-point depression of the fluid was always additionally stated.

Finally, let a fact already be mentioned here that may appear striking. If lampreys, which already appeared severely damaged by their stay in salt solutions, were brought back into fresh water, they recovered completely in the course of a few days and behaved entirely normally thereafter. One had the impression that the animals detoxify themselves in fresh water. Experiments in this direction cannot be described as entirely free of objection, since for understandable reasons very small quantities of water had to be chosen, and in doing so it was not excluded that other circumstances also played a role in the matter. For when diseased lampreys were placed into fresh water for detoxification and, after they had recovered, were removed and other healthy animals were placed into this water, the latter clearly showed signs of illness, such as reduced mobility and lack of appetite, without, however, perishing.

The striking thing about this — that it is possible to bring lampreys which had lived in salt water directly into fresh water — lies in the fact that, if the gills of these animals are permeable to salts, one would have to assume that fresh water for its part, not being isotonic, ought to have acted as a poison on the animals, which was by no means the case. As the later experiments will show, this phenomenon was also encountered in the experimental animals belonging to a higher class of animals, on which more will be reported later.

Since the form living in the sea, *Petromyzon marinus* L., was not available to me, larger experimental animals of other orders of fishes had to be drawn upon in order to obtain the blood quantity necessary for the investigations. *Acipenser ruthenus* L. (the sterlet) from the series of the ganoid fishes [enamel-scaled fishes] was chosen. An investigation of the normal blood of this animal yielded a freezing-point depression of − 0.44. A basin holding 200 liters of fluid was selected, and the concentration was now slowly raised by gradual addition of seawater. Despite repeatedly undertaken attempts, despite the slowest raising of the concentration, I never succeeded in getting beyond a salinity of the fluid that showed a freezing-point depression of − 1.10 without severe poisoning phenomena appearing. Here too the fact was striking that the functional disturbances appeared rather suddenly, after the animals had been completely well the day before. In 50 % seawater they were still completely lively. If the concentration was then raised further, the animals became listless within a few hours, behaved entirely motionless, and showed pronounced respiratory distress. In this solution they perished within 24 hours, if they were not brought back into fresh water. If animals were brought, which already lay on their backs and no longer carried out any active movements, back into river water, they recovered without exception, completely, in the course of a few days. Now an attempt was made to bring a sterlet from river water directly into 50 % seawater with a freezing-point depression of − 0.92, and the remarkable fact appeared that the fishes tolerated this sudden change without further ado. But if one attempted to transfer them into a seawater solution of δ = − 1.46, they perished within a few hours. As regards the changes in the blood: a sterlet which, under the slowest raising of the concentration, had in the course of 2 months been brought to δ = − 1.10, but already showed severe respiratory distress, was first rinsed off in spring water, then dried, and finally killed. The blood withdrawn showed a freezing-point depression of δ = − 1.03. Since during the withdrawal of the blood a drop of fresh water from the gill cavity had dripped into the dish, the slight difference is probably to be explained by this experimental error. It was striking that the blood appeared distinctly thinner-flowing and more lake-colored than that of a normal animal. Indeed, under the microscope numerous dissolved red blood corpuscles were found. A sterlet which had lived for 3 days in a salt solution of δ = − 0.81 and appeared completely lively showed in its blood a freezing-point depression of δ = − 0.69.

A sterlet which had been kept for only 24 hours in a salt solution of δ = − 1.46, but already showed severe respiratory distress, had a freezing-point depression of − 0.64.

These apparently mutually contradictory results find, as I believe, their simple explanation in the fact that the duration of the stay in the salt solution was too short to establish complete isotonicity. If one compares the earlier-mentioned experiments of Frédéricq with crab blood, this appears readily explicable.

Interesting results were shown by the experiments with bony fishes. The blood of the carp (*Cyprinus carpio* L.) has, as repeated investigations showed, a freezing-point depression of δ = − 0.44 to δ = − 0.46. Carp as well as pike were subjected to the same experiments as the sterlets. In this it appeared that those animals seem to be still more sensitive to salt solutions than the earlier experimental animals. Already at a concentration of δ = − 0.81, in which sterlets still appeared quite lively, carp and pike showed signs of illness, albeit of moderate degree. The blood withdrawn under the precautions described earlier showed an osmotic pressure of likewise δ = − 0.81.

Repeatedly repeated experiments always yielded the same result. After a stay of 8–10 days in an osmotically different medium, the blood concentration was isotonic with the external solution. Here let it be mentioned at once that, in consequence of the statements of Reclus (cited after v. Fürth), who had observed that fishes which were driven by the current into the very salt-rich Gulf of Kara Bugas in the Caspian Sea went blind in consequence of clouding of the lens, the eyes of the experimental animals were also subjected to an investigation. I now believe I have distinctly observed cloudings of the lens in such animals as appeared, in consequence of their stay in salt water, already severely damaged in other respects as well. A histological investigation of the lenses was not carried out.

Only a single species was available to me which dwells both in the sea and also in the rivers, namely the eel (*Anguilla anguilla* L.). It was in fact possible, even without any transition, to keep a very large specimen of this animal directly in a basin of seawater, which showed a freezing-point depression of δ = − 2.31, for 16 days, in which it remained completely lively. When killed, the osmotic pressure of its blood was δ = − 0.645. The blood of an eel kept in fresh water showed a freezing-point depression of − 0.59, which latter figure, however, is not entirely reliable, because it concerned a small specimen and I was compelled to work with an insufficient quantity of blood.

Finally, let a few words about the repeatedly described respiratory distress of the animals find a place here. Even though the change of the red blood corpuscles could already be a sufficient ground for this, it was nevertheless considered whether the partial pressure of oxygen might not be lower in seawater than in fresh water, which would have come to expression still more in the relatively small basin, although the latter was always aerated. From the literature on this subject let the following be mentioned: Gréhant (15) found in a liter of Seine water 6–8 ccm of oxygen, 13–17 ccm of nitrogen, and 20–30 ccm of carbon dioxide. Jolyet and Regnard (16) found in a liter of seawater 4.8–6.3 ccm of O, 12.5–14.1 ccm of N, and 43.6–71.2 ccm of CO₂, of which 2.1–9.2 free, 41.5–62.0 bound carbonic acid, whereas the investigation of the seawater in a basin yielded quite different results. There were found 3.28–5.74 ccm of O, 11.76–12.03 N, and 65.78–74.29 ccm of CO₂.

From the comparison of these figures it now follows, indeed, that for the investigation of the seawater contained in a closed basin not only the quantity of oxygen appears very small, but also the quantity of CO₂ appears strikingly high. Even though it follows from the observations of Boussingault (after v. Fürth, p. 124) that fishes are able to tolerate a very large quantity of carbonic acid without showing signs of poisoning (211 ccm of carbonic acid in a liter of water), on the other hand the lack of oxygen plays a very great role. To this is added, moreover, that the quantity of oxygen which water is able to absorb is at 0° approximately twice as great as at 20°. In a part of the experiments the temperature of the water was unfortunately not noted. In the last experiments work was done at a temperature of 10–12° C.

From the complete analogy of the phenomena in the fishes investigated by me, which were not capable of a complete adaptation to seawater, as well as from the nearly identical limit, in cyclostomes, cartilaginous and bony fishes, up to which the adaptation could be driven, I believe I may conclude that the gills of the cyclostomes [round-mouths] too are permeable to the salts of the external solution, so that the osmotic pressure of their body fluid is dependent on that of their surroundings, even though an exact proof for this last fact could not be found.

Summarized, the following conclusions may, in my opinion, be drawn from the experiments described.

I. The gills of the fishes *Petromyzon Planeri*, *Acipenser ruthenus*¹⁾, *Esox*, *Carassius* and *Cyprinus* are permeable to salts.

II. The freshwater-dwelling animals were not capable of a complete adaptation to seawater.

III. The limit of the possibility of adaptation lies at a salt solution whose freezing point lies in the vicinity of 1.0.

IV. The gills of *Anguilla* prove to be impermeable to salts and water.

> ¹⁾ According to Heincke (in Martin's Illustrated Natural History, vol. 2, part 1, p. 527) it is precisely this species that is stationary in the rivers.
