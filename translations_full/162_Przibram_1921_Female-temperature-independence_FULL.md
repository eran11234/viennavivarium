## I. Statement of the Problem.

In a preceding communication (Przibram 1917, *Environment VI*) I have once again demonstrated the direct dependence of the rectal temperature upon the external degrees of warmth in our two rat species, in that for this purpose I was able to use experimental animals reared at constant temperatures (cf. Przibram 1913, p. 164 ff.). These rearings now also permit one to pursue, with sufficient accuracy in a warm-blooded animal, the problem of the influence of temperature upon the developmental velocity of the embryos, just as it had been done in a cold-blooded animal in the same temperature chambers (Przibram 1917, *Temperature quotients … of Sphodromantis bioculata*). In particular, it can be tested without difficulty whether external temperatures bring about a change in the duration of the period in the female rats, which might well be expected given the changes of the rectal temperatures that run in the same direction as the environmental degrees. Kanitz (1915, p. 126) at least touches upon this problem in his comprehensive presentation of the temperature quotients of lifeprocesses, in that he predicts, for a higher body temperature, a decrease of the duration of gravidity in humans and other mammals. As evidence he adduces merely data on horses, according to which "the mean duration of gravidity of the mares that foal from July to September is a markedly shorter one (326 days) than in the mares that foal from March to May (343 days)," and further "a duration of gravidity shorter by a few days in the work-mares than in those serving merely for breeding"; but here it is a matter of one or a few days. If we examine the same rat species at different temperatures, such cases—rare in any event—will probably distribute themselves uniformly over the temperatures and therefore will not impair the means. There remains, however, the question to be raised: how great could the differences in the duration of the period or the duration of gravidity be that would be attainable through the available temperature quotients, on the assumption of a value of 2 for the ten-degree temperature quotient, as it presented itself for the development of cold-blooded animals. If a velocity is to double itself for a 10° C temperature difference, then there would fall (with neglect of the decline of the velocity quotient with rising temperature) upon 1° difference ¹⁄₁₂ and upon 4° difference (hence this number raised to the fourth power, computed logarithmically as 1.319 days [abbreviated 1.32]). That is to say, in the case of a body temperature which is 4° higher than another, an increase of the developmental velocity by almost exactly a third, or an abbreviation of the developmental duration by a quarter, would be to be expected.

In the case of a period of 24 days, the 4° elevation should therefore bring an abbreviation of 6 days, so that the period, or the time from one litter to the next, would amount to 18 days. Since under the action of various external temperatures only 4-degree differences of the body warmths have actually been established by us in any case, the direct temperature differences of the rectal temperatures would have to bring forth a measurable difference of the period or even of the gravidity; for the predominant variability of the period and of the gravidity itself produces irregularities that could outweigh those brought about by the aforesaid variability, indeed even the difference between period and gravidity itself. After setting out these various assumptions we can now proceed to the description of the experiments themselves.

It suffices then, for the duration of gravidity, to know the duration of successive litters of *one and the same rat-female*, in order to be able to give the duration of the female period, and to set the gravidity 1 to 2 days shorter. It must, to be sure, be remarked that exceptionally a postponement of the period is said to take place when the young of the earlier litter are still being suckled (King in Kirkam and Burr 1914), yet it is a matter of one or a few days. If we examine the same rat species at different temperatures, such cases—rare in any event—will probably distribute themselves uniformly over the temperatures and therefore will not impair the means. There remains, however, the question to be raised: how great could the differences in the duration of the period or the duration of gravidity be that would be attainable through the available temperature quotients, on the assumption of a value of 2 for the ten-degree temperature quotient, as it presented itself for the development of cold-blooded animals. If a velocity is to double itself for a 10° C temperature difference, then there would fall (with neglect of the decline of the velocity quotient with rising temperature) upon 1° difference ¹⁄₁₂ and upon 4° difference (hence this number raised to the fourth power, computed logarithmically) 1.319 [abbreviated 1.32]. That is to say, in the case of a body temperature which is 4° higher than another, an increase of the developmental velocity by almost exactly a third, or an abbreviation of the developmental duration by a quarter, would be to be expected.

In the case of a period of 24 days, the 4° elevation should therefore bring an abbreviation of 6 days, so that the period, or the time from one litter to the next, would amount to 18 days. Since under the action of various external temperatures only 4-degree differences of the body warmths have actually been established by us in any case, the direct temperature differences of the rectal temperatures would have to outweigh by far the irregularities produced, which could be brought about by the aforesaid variability of the period, indeed even the difference between period and gravidity itself. After setting out these various assumptions we can now proceed to the description of the experiments themselves.

## II. Experimental Results.

Results come first into consideration from all those rat-females which, during the rearing in the constantly tempered rooms in the years 1912–1914, actually brought litters into the world in at least two successive periods. In the accompanying table the times from litter to litter are entered in days, and indeed without regard to the individual females, merely according to affiliation to the room temperature (horizontal rows) and the rat strains used (vertical columns). In the last vertical columns the means from the wander-rats and from all rats are compiled for each external temperature.

Beginning with the house-rat, *Mus rattus*, we find at 20° external temperature entered once 23 and once 28 days. The average would be 25.5; further, at 40° external temperature a period of 25 days. If we compare Table A for body warmths of the female *M. rattus* given in 1917, p. 39, this had at 20° a rectal temperature of 35.05°, and at 40° one of 38.05°; the difference therefore amounts to 3°, and with a ten-degree temperature quotient of merely 2 an abbreviation by almost a fifth would have been to be expected, so that the duration should amount to 20 to 21 days; instead of this, the time at 40° external temperature is shorter than at 20° C by only ¹⁄₂ day. This difference too has no significance whatever, in view of the few cases, the interposition of the number for 40° external temperature among the cases established for 20° C, and the determination of the litter-time accurate only to whole days.

Passing on to the wander-rat, *M. decumanus*, we find first, in a wild-colored rat-female descending directly from captured parents, a period of 24 days at 15° external temperature, in confirmation of the figure given by earlier authors for the albinos long bred in captivity, at room temperature. Our own albinos of various provenances ("Ställe" [Stables], from a crossing of wild with tame white rats effected in 1905; "Molkerei" [Dairy], from animals bred on for many years merely as white; "Ställe × Molkerei," their crossing) likewise confirm the earlier statements and furnish usable data for the various constant external temperatures. A separate consideration is not required, since they exhibit no differences among one another. The mean figures show in the clearest manner that no abbreviation of the period has taken place with rising external temperature. From the overall mean for *M. decumanus*, which amounts to 24 days, the still most divergent data deviate by 1.3 (at 30°), by 1 (at 5°), and by 0.6 (at 15°) downward, and by 0.6 (at 25°) upward. Already from this presentation it follows that no "trend" whatever is present in the assignment of the period-duration to the external temperatures, for otherwise the greatest deviation from the mean would necessarily occur at the extreme external temperatures, namely at the lowest (5° external temperature) downward and at the highest (35° external temperature) upward; but precisely at 5° external temperature is one of the strongest downward-directed fluctuations, while at 35° external temperature the mean is exceeded by half a day.

The insignificance of these fluctuations can be accounted for by the relatively small number of usable data, and has also come to expression in the dispersion within each temperature chamber. It will be brought home to us still more clearly when we compare the rectal temperatures of the female wander-rats at the corresponding external temperatures. For 5° C external temperature we had found 33.45° C, whereas for 35° already 37.50° C (1917, *VI*, p. 39). The difference therefore amounts to a full 4 degrees, and given the applicability of a ten-degree temperature quotient for period and gravidity, we should have to expect an abbreviation by ²⁄₅, that is a reduction by 6 days; instead of this, rather a lengthening has occurred, upon which, however, for various reasons, as already mentioned, no value is to be placed.

## III. Interpretation of the Results.

As unambiguously as the results prove a complete independence of the female period, and with it of the gravidity, in rats from the external temperatures, so cautiously must we proceed in the assessment of the question whether we are thus actually dealing in these processes with phenomena that entirely lack the temperature quotients of other developmental processes and are therefore of an entirely different nature, in the sense, say, of the so-called "doctrine of periods" (Fließ, Swoboda, etc.). This latter cannot, to be sure, already for that reason be applied, because the onset of the periods and the deliveries does not, within one external temperature, hold itself to the same number of days, and indeed still less in the cases not usable by us, in which litterless periods alternate with litters (cf. also Kirkham and Burr).

We must first take into consideration whether the rectal temperatures yield at all a correct measure of the inner body temperature, namely of the sex-glands and the germ-cells, or whether these sites concealed deeper in the body might not be still more protected against the external temperature fluctuations.

For the rats themselves I can unfortunately bring no corresponding evidence, but for the rabbit¹ there exist the detailed investigations of Jules Lefèvre (1898, in 1911 p. 539), who established the warmth of individual body regions with the thermoelectric needle in baths of various temperature. Important to me appear the earlier experiments of Congdon (1912, pp. 709—711), namely that the rats approaching sexual maturity, reared at 33° or 10° C, exhibited no difference of the rectal temperature, but at both temperatures a body temperature higher by 0.7° than the older rats reared at 33°. There thus exists a stage in the life of the rats at which a maintenance, going beyond the measure, of a definite—and indeed high—body warmth takes place. This time coincides with sexual maturation (the same is confirmed by Sumner 1913, p. 351 in mice). According to our further temperature measurements on rats (1917, p. 39), which during the measurement became in heat or wild, the rectal temperature likewise rose considerably, to the point of obliterating the influence of the external temperature. The temperature elevation during heat is, moreover, a well-known phenomenon, designated in mammals as "Hitze" ("heat"); it amounts, according to Wunderlich 1870, cited in Lefèvre p. 351, in humans to 0.3° C. On the foetus, repeated measurements of the body warmth have been undertaken after birth (according to Lefèvre, p. 340, probably always in humans), and this was found higher than in the uterus or vagina of the woman giving birth, on the mean of 37 measurements by Rogers (1843 in Lefèvre, p. 340) by 0.3° C. Lefèvre explains this, probably rightly, by the fact that the embryos in the mother's body form organs of strong heat-production, such as the liver. From all these facts the conclusion may now be drawn that the maturing germ-beds, germ-cells and the embryos bring with them a temperature elevation together with a particularly sharply maintained homoiothermy.

This secondary homoiothermy would be the independence of the period and gravidity from the external temperatures. It has its seat not in the heat-regulation apparatus of the embryos, for the fully carried young have at first a still much more imperfect heat-regulation than old animals, as is stated concordantly by all authors, but rather in the higher heat-production of the embryos established calorimetrically by Bohr (1900) in the guinea-pig, which, owing to the relatively only slight temperature surplus over the mother, is given off not by conduction at the surface, but indeed through the placenta and the blood-stream.

## IV. On the Critique of the So-Called "Doctrine of Periods".

The entire registered rat-material permits us to deliver a critique of the so-called "doctrine of periods" (Fließ, Swoboda), which rests not upon an a priori rejection, as had unfortunately hitherto been the case, but upon experimentally established data, and might therefore be suited to make the adherents of this doctrine thoughtful.

To the monthly rule of the human female, with its 28-day period, Fließ (1897) has set alongside a 23-day "masculine" period, to which he is said to have come by clinical experience; the same 23 days Swoboda (1904) claims to have established by a psychological route, in that the recollections [of impressions] advance themselves by an hour [each day], so that after 23 days they fall again upon the same hour¹.

In the reproductive physiology of the human being I have been able to find nowhere any appropriate groundings for such a 23-day period. So far as is known to me, a weekly after-shifting of spermatozoa takes place, whereby four masculine periods would fall upon one feminine. As to the figures, one might point out that the week, so long observed by the culture-human, with its periodically recurring Sunday, could be responsible for the weekly fluctuating sexual activity. In mammals too there can be — as the monthly rule of man indeed shows — strictly maintained periodic processes which in the formation of the precise homoiothermy have become independent of the temperature. That the strict maintenance of constant temperature is really to be held responsible for the independence of pregnancy from the outside temperature is already shown by the conditions in birds. Likewise homoiothermic, they nonetheless do not carry their young to term in their own body, but lay them as eggs, in order then to hatch them out through the body warmth of the parents. With the strictly equal body temperature of the parents, the eggs too will usually undergo an equally long developmental time up to hatching, which for each species comprises a definite number of days, e.g. 21 in the hen. But here one has it in one's power to hatch eggs artificially, without the body warmth of the mother, and in doing so to apply various temperatures. Then, with the degree of the brooding temperature, the developmental time changes in the manner required by the reaction-velocity–temperature rule (as I have calculated from the experiments published by Féré 1894). So when Féré (1906, p. 269) finds laying, brooding and hatching of the young in the ostrich assigned according to his periods, the latter interval may indeed always be the same in nature, but not because of an immutable period, rather because of the always equal brooding temperature. But it is impossible for a maintenance of the developmental periods to hold also in nature for the poikilotherms, which in various places, years and other surroundings are exposed to various temperatures, against which they have no homoiothermy to set. Now Féré (p. 259) finds his periods, and indeed then in their "male" form of 23 days, also in the life course of the tiger snake, namely between conception, egg-laying and molting; further for the development of insects, e.g. of the flea, of the Japanese silkworm, of the bee and of the large pine-pith beetle.

In the agreement of the kindred litters it is not a matter of chance, but of the influence of certain conditions. And these are to be sought not in temperature or other outer factors such as humidity and the like, but in the inner disposition that has become identical through common descent and especially inbreeding. The age at which the first confinement can occur must be hereditarily transmissible to within a few days (or even exactly to one day).

Of the correctness of this conclusion we can convince ourselves by a simple test, which has been brought to presentation in Table M. If we take these averages from the previously communicated age data of the first confinements (Table B), then for the various rat tribes considerably divergent numbers show themselves. If we disregard the few data for *M. dec.* "stall" and *M. rattus* and wild-colored *M. decumanus*, which as a consequence of shorter captivity were perhaps in reality pushed back also by other circumstances, then there remains the comparison between the unrelated albinotic tribes "stall" and "dairy." The mean of 45 confinements of the "stall" rats amounts to 158.2 days, that of 63 confinements of the "dairy" rats to 131.4 days; the difference amounts to 26.8 days. A better picture of the conditions we get on grouping the confinements according to weeks of life. There then shows itself, in the "stall" breed, a maximum of the confinements in the 18th week of life of the mothers, while the "dairy" breed has maxima in the 15th and 17th weeks of life

> *12* By summing across all the broods, one cannot really speak of a maximum within the 15th to 19th week of life.

Unfortunately, only 4 first births from crossings of "Ställe" with "Molkerei" are available; although it was "Ställe" that had supplied the females for the crossing, the distribution of the cases into the 12th to 16th week of life nevertheless seems to indicate an influence of the father, since he always belonged to the "Molkerei" strain with its relatively earlier age at parturition. It therefore remains questionable whether we may neglect the role of the father in the doctrine of periods, as Fließ (1906, p. 157) does in the following words: "Apart from the uncertainty of paternity, the results of our analysis entitle us, for the time being, to see the familial unit only in the mother. Nevertheless, for the sake of completeness, the age of life of the father has likewise been described," and (p. 159): "The role of the father must be a more remote one." In experiments in which the uncertainty of paternity can be avoided, the role of the father could be pursued further. But since Fließ does not take it into account, I too have taken no special regard of it in the presentation hitherto. Our consideration so far has, however, also had a second tacit assumption, namely that in the rats the ovulation period is subsumed once into the gestation period. This assumption was made merely in order to test whether Fließ' considerations on man can be transferred to the rats. Only under this assumption can one find again in the rats any one of his periods at all, even though it would be the "male" one. But there now exist direct observations on rats and mice that the ovulation period amounts to 10 days; this has been stated concordantly by Lataste (1887) for *Mus rattus*, *M. decumanus*, *M. musculus* and others, as well as by Heape (1901) for the two last-named species. Just as in man the menses cease during pregnancy, so in the Murids the ovulation periods are omitted during gravidity. If we take these data into account, then upon division of the time elapsed between the birth of the mother and her first parturition by 10 there would always have to remain a remainder of 3 or 4, which agrees with reality just as little (cf. Table E) as do the calculations carried out under the earlier assumptions. According to Heape and Lataste the parturitions could not be periodically connected with one another at all, since everything would depend entirely on whether pregnancy has occurred or not, so that the next ovulation sets in after 23 or already after 10 days. I do not, however, wish to lay very great weight on this, since, in the absence of my own observations, I do not want to decide whether the number 10 is really to be regarded as established, for its slight shift to 11½ (up to 12) would make two ovulation periods be subsumed into one pregnancy. Heape (p. 26) himself places no value on the fixed magnitude of 10, since in the rabbit, besides 10 days, he had also found 3 weeks, that is, 21 days, and values lying in between, on average 15 days; the gestation period here amounts to 32 days (2 × 16); in the guinea-pig he found a gestation period of 63, which would come very close to 64 = 4 × 16; but here data on the period are lacking. One can indeed, by forming fractions of the gestation period and adding "binding" numbers, obtain some relations or other, but then every certainty of the work is lost. The rat experiments bring no support for the so-called modern "doctrine of periods," which for the cold-blooded animals is impossible from the outset, because here the direct influence of temperature is decisive for the rate of development.

A few words about the influence of the heavenly bodies on the course of life may be appended here.

The presumably "accidental" agreement of the period of the human female with the duration of a lunar revolution has from of old led to manifold speculations about the influence of the moon upon it. Quite rightly Crusius already remarked in his Periodology (1836, pp. 393 and 396) that the monthly course of bleedings cannot be referred to the direct influence of the moon, since not all women menstruate at the same time, but that it could indeed be conceived as a periodic consequence of it. Later, in accordance with the theory of descent, the monthly period was sought to be traced back to the earlier sojourn of the ancestral forms in the sea, which are supposed to have lived in the tidal zone influenced by the moon. But it is incomprehensible to me why precisely man, and not all the other warm-blooded animals as well, should then have retained the same period. A further difficulty of this view lies in the fact that the ancestors living in the tidal zone can hardly be thought of as already homoiothermic. In non-homoiothermic beings, however, the course set for a definite period would fluctuate with temperature as soon as the direct influence fell away, and thus would necessarily produce successive periods of different length in different latitudes. Incidentally, the periods brought about by external factors, and in particular by effects of the moon, already become blurred very rapidly in the individuals of the cold-blooded animals themselves, or in their immediate descendants.

For direct lunar influence, the worms in particular are said to be sensitive, such as the Palolo worm and its relatives (literature cf. Fließ 1906, p. 308, who himself rejects the direct influence), the roundworms (which according to Crusius 1836, p. 238 "at the time of the waning moon appear more restless") and the *Convoluta roscoffensis*. Bohn (1912, p. 34) gives the following account of it: "When the sea withdraws from the shore of Brittany at ebb tide, there appear on the land extended green flecks, which gradually become darker and whose shape constantly changes. These flecks are formed by masses of countless small turbellarian worms, the convolutes.... As soon as the flood comes in, the animals bury themselves in the sand in order to escape the impact of the waves, and rise to the surface again only at ebb tide. The convolutes thus rise synchronously with the movements of ebb and flood, only in the reverse sense. These rhythmic movements continue even in the aquarium, although here the animals are after all completely withdrawn from the influence of the sea movements; in a glass tube filled with moist sand, in which the convolutes are enclosed, the green ring rises and falls and reaches its highest level at ebb, its lowest at flood. But what is still more remarkable, the convolutes in the aquarium even follow the irregularities of the tides: during the neap tide their movements are slowed, whereas during the spring tide they are livelier. This even continues for several more days after their captivity in the aquarium. The movements of the convolutes are conditioned by a periodic change in the sign of geotropism: the animal is alternately attracted to and repelled by the earth.... In my conception, in these processes the changing water content of the tissues forms a decisive factor. At ebb and during the neap tide the tissues of the animals of the sea coast dry out more or less strongly, and correspondingly the chemical transformations in the cells slow down; indeed at times the organism falls directly into a state of rigidity, which Giard has described under the name anhydrobiosis. With the incoming flood and at the spring tide the tissues fill anew with water, and the chemical reactions proceed — at least at first — more rapidly. There exist, however, also tidal rhythms upon which the changing desiccation and moistening apparently has no influence. Thus Anna Drzewina found in the hermit crabs a fourteen-day periodicity, which manifests itself through a change in the sign of phototropism, although the animals always remain under water." Thus far Bohn. It seems to me to accord better with the facts if, in the case of *Convoluta* too, we disregard the action of anhydrobiosis as the causative factor, since the change in water content scarcely comes into consideration in the turbellarian worms, which always remain in the moist sand. Rather, the meaning of their upward and downward movement, alternating with the level of the sea, is directed toward the maintenance of the correct degree of moisture. The sensation for the correct movements can, however, be traced back directly to the lunar influence, which, in an attraction of the water together with the position of the sun, determines the time of the tide and the height of the tide. If the convolutes have a direct sensitivity for the earth-centrifugal water movement at the time of flood, [and] for the earth-centripetal at the time of ebb, then they will sense, even in the aquarium, this level fluctuation — which is admittedly invisible to our eye on account of the small mass of the water — and indeed most strongly at spring tide, and will react to it with the accustomed movement. But as soon as, through the failure of the ebb or rushing flood to appear, they have convinced themselves of the superfluousness of the movements, these too cease already after a few days. By this manner of expression I do not, of course, mean to have asserted anything about the conscious power of judgment of these worms. The extinction of no-longer-necessary reactions in animals in captivity is a fact not to be denied, for which becoming tame itself furnishes ample evidence (e.g. in the Egyptian praying mantis, according to my own experiences).

How rapidly the change of seasons determined by the position of the sun is able to influence the breeding period of cold-blooded animals is shown by the experiments on the acclimatization of the salmonids at the Antipodes (Nicols 1882). The time of egg-laying was, already in the first generation reared from imported eggs, June to August, whereas in Europe it falls in December to January. This is not, say, traceable to the prevention of the imported salmon from seeking out the sea outside the spawning season, for when in Europe one prevents salmon from the sea journey (Day 1885), this changes nothing in the breeding time of their offspring. In the homoiothermic animals too, the annual cycle plays a role only insofar as it can make itself felt through direct action; this is shown very beautifully by the already cited results of the rats in the constant temperatures, where, independently of the degree, they lost the interruption of the breeding periods. That even the period of the human female is not non-displaceable was already known to Crusius, who describes the influence of rarefied air on menstruation, which consists partly in irregularities of the periods in women dwelling in the mountains, partly in the delay of its onset on a journey into the mountains.

Since a certain significance for the periods is also ascribed to the annual cycle, I have in Table N examined the first parturitions of the various rat females as to whether, after 365, 2 × 365, etc. days, accumulations are found within related groups. In not a single case does the repetition of the same birthday in successive years within one and the same rat strain show itself. Accidental, complete agreement among different strains occurs four times, including once between *M. rattus* and *M. decumanus* "Ställe," where the difference of species also excludes any far-fetched familial sameness. Agreement to within one day is found once within the same group ("Ställe"), and besides that three times among different groups; agreement to within two days [is found] three times, equally often among different groups. All these agreements can therefore be regarded only as accidental.

Apart from the annual cycle, in the cold-blooded animals the daily cycle too is essentially determined by the sun, above all by its light. The daily change of the crustacean coloration is immediately reversible when the crayfish are exposed to darkness by day and to constant illumination by night (Megušar 1912).

Among the homoiothermic animals a similar experiment can be carried out with the daily fluctuation of the body temperature. Such a fluctuation is known in birds and, among the mammals, in the horse (Liska; lit. in Lefèvre p. 344) and in the primates (Brown 1909). Now, when monkeys were subjected to an enforced nocturnal mode of life, the body-temperature curve reversed itself (Simpson and Galbraith 1906). For man the data read contradictorily, yet here too the inversion comes about, at least in a number of cases, after a more prolonged reversal of the mode of life (night duty of nurses [female sick-attendants]). (Literature in Lefèvre p. 345.) Here the highest organism, in consequence of its most perfected automatism, still struggles most successfully against the direct influencing by external factors.

Everything points to the fact that precisely this attained protection against the environmental influences makes possible the retention of a definite period in the human organism, and that therefore neither a generalization to the other living beings nor a relation of the 28 days to the "month" can be justified.

## V. Summary.

1. Female rats reared and kept at constant temperatures (*Mus decumanus* and *M. rattus*) exhibit, in the number of days elapsing from litter to litter, no differences that could be ascribed to the external temperatures, although the duration of the periods is very variable.

2. This temperature-independence of the duration of gravidity is traced back to a secondary homoiothermy of the in-heat and gravid animals, which raises the body temperature and defends it against external temperatures better than the primary homoiothermy does.

3. The absence of a temperature quotient corresponding to the other developmental processes is accordingly to be ascribed not to an essential difference of mammalian development, nor to an immutable fixation of the periods, but to the removal of the embryos from direct temperature influences.

4. The gravidity of the female rats, which on average amounts to 24 days, is determined neither from the birth (as Fließ would expect), nor from the first onset of copulation (somewhat in the sense of Swoboda); but close kinship can indeed lead to a simultaneity of the parturitions of siblings, in that the age at which the first parturition takes place tends to be a more similar one within one rat strain than among different strains.

5. The ten-day periods of non-pregnant rat females likewise seem to behave independently of temperature, and otherwise also similarly to the gravidity periods.
