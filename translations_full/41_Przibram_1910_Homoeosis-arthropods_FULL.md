### Introduction.

On one earlier occasion I took the opportunity to point out the importance of teratological findings for the developmental mechanics [Entwicklungsmechanik] of organisms (cf. Grosser and Przibram).

Whereas at that time the variously formed malformations of *one* animal species (*Mustelus laevis*) formed the point of departure, the present communication occupies itself with a particular group of malformations, homoeosis, in various arthropods. By "homoeosis" (Bateson 1894) is to be understood the assumption of the form, or of individual characters, of one member of a meristic series by another member, which latter normally lacks the formation in question.

Several striking homoeotic insects and crustaceans, which have come to my notice in recent years, give me occasion to enter into some regularities that might bring us somewhat closer to an understanding of homoeosis. They give us pointers for the experimental tackling of the problem. It goes without saying that only these planned experiments will have to furnish the proof of the correctness of the hypotheses put forward.

The allocation of the butterfly described as No. 1 came about at the instigation of the physiologist Dr. Ernst Brücke, through the collector Miss Dorette Brücke; Nos. 2 and 3 are in the custody of Prof. Rebel, No. 4 in that of Councillor [Regierungsrat] Ganglbauer, both at the Court Museum [Hofmuseum] in Vienna; No. 5 in that of the Trustees of the College of Surgeons in London; No. 6 was sent to me by my friend J. Ashworth from Edinburgh. To all those named let my heartfelt thanks be expressed for their kind support of my work.

### B. Replacement of the hindwing by the forewing.

No. 2. *Zygaena carniolica* Scop., Hofmuseum Vienna. Label: "Mödling, 30 July 1905. Wagner don." (Plate XIX Fig. 2 and 2a).

Male burnet moth [Widderchen]: Left hindwing replaced by a fully formed forewing, which in size, marking, venation, scaling and outline entirely resembles the left forewing. The colour of the abnormal wing too agrees with that of the rain-faded forewings of the specimen insofar as the abnormal wing, owing to its length, was not covered in the resting position; where, on the other hand, it was covered by the forewing, it shows the fresher red colour of the right, almost normal hindwing, which freshly emerged normal specimens of the species likewise display on their forewings (normal specimen Plate XIX Fig. 2b).

The underside, the wing-base and the black, white-bordered hair-tuft (scapula, tegula) standing above the wing-base on the metathorax (text-fig. 2a) are entirely corresponding to that on the forewing. The catch-bristle [Haftborste, frenulum] normal for the hindwing (text-fig. 2b) is lacking.

The homoeotic burnet moth is otherwise normally formed apart from the absence of the right front leg, doubtless broken off near the base, and a small triangular spot at the margin of the right hindwing appearing flesh-coloured instead of red, and in particular has no other body-appendage homoeotically altered. Its size is the normal one for the species, likewise the coloration, apart from the mentioned defects.

The position of the three legs is such that all come to lie one behind the other in one plane. Their dorsal and ventral surfaces agree with those of the normal legs. Every two adjoining legs are mirror-image symmetrical to one another.

In the sequence from front to back, the first leg possesses the symmetry of a right [leg], the second the symmetry of a left, the third again the symmetry of a right leg.

On superficial examination one can easily be misled into regarding the components as three forelegs, which differ from one another merely by their side-affiliation.

The comparison between the normal left foreleg and the first leg of the abnormality reveals, apart from the somewhat smaller absolute dimensions of the latter, indeed no other difference.

With the normal right foreleg of a comparison specimen it coincides completely.

If we compare it with a normal middle leg, then we must take magnifying glass and dividers to our aid in order to find out the differences with certainty; yet such differences, once found, are also unmistakable to the naked eye.

The same holds for the associated tarsi.

There are therefore, without doubt, a pair of middle legs added to the coxa of the right foreleg. The case belongs, insofar as homoeosis is concerned, to those middle legs which ought to spring from the middle breast finding themselves inserted at the fore-breast.

Yet it is not a matter of a replacement of a foreleg by middle legs, since the right foreleg too has, as third partner, likewise entered into the malformation. In this respect the longicorn beetle [Lederbock] would now belong to the category of "supplementary heteromorphoses" [Zusatzheteromorphosen] distinguished by Wheeler (1896) and by myself (Regeneration 1909). Only it differs in one essential point from the otherwise known cases of this kind, namely the absence of the supplementary structures at their normal place. For in the other supplementary heteromorphoses the supplementary structure is supernumerary, in that, at the normal places, the structures in question are likewise present.

Our beetle therefore forms a new category of heteromorphoses, which might fittingly be called "displacement-heteromorphoses" [Versatzheteromorphosen]: structures appearing at an abnormal place form neither a replacement for the normally standing one, nor a supernumerary addition to the existing parts of the organism, but appear merely as a displacement [Versetzung] from the normal standing-place; this normal standing-place is thereby either entirely lacking, or displaced or transposed to another place.

For supplementary heteromorphosis one uses the customary, but hitherto not sufficiently precise, word: "heterotopy"; one might therefore designate the displacement-heteromorphosis as "heterophory."

From the genus *Prionus* two malformations are known which are of interest in comparison with the one just described:

### D. Replacement of the third maxilliped by a chela

No. 5. *Cancer (Platycarcinus) pagurus* L., ♀, College of Surgeons Museum London, Kat. Nr. 379 A: "Female Crab (*Cancer pagurus*). The left maxilliped has the form of a small chela. The abnormality involves the four distal pieces. A. Johnson, Esq. (96)" (Taf. XX Fig. 5).

Female crab [Taschenkrebs]: Left, third maxilliped partly developed as a chela-leg. Basipodite, epipodite and ischiopodite are still shaped as a maxilliped and formed complete, including the epipodite-flagellum and the setae. The dimensions are the same as in the fully normally developed right maxilliped of the same specimen. The meropodite, on the other hand, is merely half as broad as the right one and bears, at a ventrally turned articulation-facet, three end-segments, which appear as carpopodite, propodite, and dactylopodite of a left chela-leg.

These three segments mean, however, significantly smaller than the corresponding ones of the normal left chela, and relatively too narrow for their size. They stand completely parallel to the corresponding segments of the chela. The left chela (L.) of the crab is normal, except for the dactylopodite, which is shortened and lacks the teeth of the inner cutting-edge. The right chela is lacking, broken off from the autotomy-place [Autotomiestelle] on.

The remaining legs are obtained normal, apart from the break-off of the dactylopodite on the right second leg, likewise the other body-parts.

Carapax-breadth 18 cm, carapax-length 10 cm; the dimensions of the maxillipeds and of the left chela are to be taken from the figure.

The hind-body corresponds completely to that of a normal female, the penultimate segment broad and much longer than the preceding one, the last short, triangular (cf. Heller 1863).

Of the replacement of a third maxilliped by a chela the following cases are already known:

No. 5 *a*. *Cancer pagurus*, ♀, Bateson 1890, p. 580, ♂, Fig. 1; 1894, p. 149, ♂, Fig. 18), College of Surgeons Museum London, Kat. Nr. 379: "Female crab. The normal flattened endopodite of the third maxilliped on the right side is replaced by a small chela. Presented by W. Bateson Esq." (Taf. XX Fig. 5 *a*).

Female crab: Right third maxilliped replaced, from the ischiopodite onward inclusive, by a small chela. To Bateson's description it would still be added that the right chela, located at the normal place, is less pronounced in its dentition than that of the left. The lacking of the epipodite-flagellum of the homoeotically altered maxilliped I attribute decidedly, at least in the present form of the museum-piece [Museumsstück], to a subsequent breaking-off. The inspection in the museum further showed that it is a female provided with a typical abdomen. The designation in the catalogue of the museum is therefore correct, but the designations as male are erroneous.

No. 5 *β*. *Cancer pagurus*, ♀ (Cornish 1884, Bateson 1894, p. 150). Present place of preservation and illustration not known to me. Female crab: Left third maxilliped completely transformed into a chela, whose segments are all distinctly demarcated. This circumstance, as well as the breadth of the carapax indicated as 4 inches (about 10 cm), rule out the suspicion that it could be our own specimen described as No. 5. —

These three cases of the replacement of a maxilliped by a chela form a series with regard to the number of segments which are seized by the homoeosis: in our No. 5 it is merely the three most distal segments, in No. 5 *a* already five, in No. 5 *β* already all (though from the description it cannot be ascertained with certainty whether the coxipodite was altered).

Two further cases of *Cancer pagurus* are adduced (Bateson 1894, p. 150) as homoeosis of similar kind; yet they refer to the replacement of maxillipeds by walking-leg-resembling [schreitbeinähnliche] structures (Richard 1893, p. 99); Text-fig. 5. These I have obtained as transitory regeneration-stages on a series of other crab-species, so that the "walking-leg-resemblance" rests only on the passing-through of a walking-leg-resembling developmental stage (Przibram 1901), but not on the replacement of the maxilliped by a walking-leg. They appear also in long-tailed crabs at the second maxilliped (Przibram 1901) and at the third (Biberhofer 1905). On the other hand there appeared during my regeneration experiments once, in a *Dromia vulgaris*, woolly crab [Wollkrabbe], a resemblance of a regenerate to a chela-leg, without permitting further conclusions.

## Derivation of Rules for Homoeosis

At first glance the table seems to permit the recognition of no regularity of any kind whatsoever. We find both body-parts neighboring the normal limb and more distant ones affected by the heteromorphosis. At one moment the prototypical form of the abnormal [structure] precedes [it] in the normal succession of the metameres, at another it follows after it. Sometimes it is the normal, sometimes the abnormal structure standing at a place that is of lower differentiation.

The picture changes, however, as soon as we take into account the division into substitution-, addition-, and transposition-heteromorphoses.

**Substitution** (Substitution, Homoeosis s. str.) of a limb-structure by one normally belonging to a different segment (see Table: Horizontal rows I, II, IV, V, VI, VII, XI, XII, XIII):

The substituting limb-structure belongs, in this category, throughout among the Crustaceans — and among the insects, insofar as it is *not* a matter of wings — to a segment *following* upon the replaced one. It need not be the *immediately* following segment ¹).

With the wings it is reversed: here the hind-wing is replaced by the fore-wing; the opposite is as yet little known. The replacement of the fore-wing by a leg is doubtful and admits of another interpretation (cf. later: Addition).

> ²) This alleged replacement of wing-covers by a pair of legs would, properly speaking, not be a case of homoeosis even then, if the legs had really stood in place of the wings, in case these had been middle legs; for these belong to the same segment as the wing-covers. Just as little, therefore, do I here cite the *Palloptera ustulata* described by GERCKE, with a wing on the middle of the mesonotum.

The exception in the segment-sequence of the substituting and substituted wings supplements in the loveliest fashion the rule set up by me a little while ago, that always the more differentiated structure is replaced by the less differentiated one. At that time ("Regeneration" 1909, p. 231) I was able to speak only of the sense-functionally higher differentiation, which is replaced by one lower in this respect: "the light-sensitive crustacean-eye [Krustenauge] by a light-insensitive first antenna which moreover lacks the normal otolith; the insect-feeler by the less sensitive [unempfindlicheren] foot; the crab-maxilliped functioning as a palp [Taster] by the chela; the chela of the crayfish functioning as a grasping-organ by the walking-leg [Schreitfuß]!" The hind-wing of the butterflies is not distinguished by a sense-functionally higher development, but indeed by the possession of the hold-bristle [Haftborste], and is therefore to be regarded as the more differentiated one.

Our chamomile-owl [Kamilleneule] (No. 1) furthermore provides us, at the conclusion of the row of sense-functionally variously high differentiations: here the sense-functionally low-organized leg is replaced by a scarcely sense-functionally active hair-tuft [Haarbüschel]. Whether the sense-functionally inactive hind-wing, on the other hand, can also take the place of the leg, appears questionable (XII; cf. further below: Addition).

In every case we can now, with the dropping of the restriction "sense-functionally," set up for the replacement of a limb the principle: in place of a more differentiated structure there steps a less differentiated structure.

Since it has been demonstrated in several cases (Crustaceans), and is to be supposed by analogy in the remaining ones (insects), that the cause of the substitution-homoeosis is to be sought in regeneration-processes, I should like therefore to name it with a more comprehensive term regenerative hypotypy.

This hypotypy can be either a transient one (V, VII), or a persistent one (II, II, IV, VI?). In the former case it is a matter of otherwise caenogenetically suppressed, atavistic stages; in the latter case probably not. The similarity of the former structures to the allegedly homoeotically repeated limbs is merely a superficial one, that of the latter a true homology. That [similarity] of the transient one need not concern a neighboring segment, but rather mostly skips over several segmental-appendages — still provided with particular differentiations, although already lowerorganized; the true, persistent homoeosis, by contrast, always concerns (Crustaceans) the segmental-appendage next-following in the substitution-row, although this (insects) need not belong to the next segment. There are namely lacking in the substitution-row the jaws, so that in the insects the fore-legs follow immediately upon the antennae.

The transient substitution-homoeosis I have already earlier (1901, 1909) traced back to the regenerative appearance of stages which appear caenogenetically suppressed in the ontogenesis of the species in question. The condition for this reappearance is the dropping-out, observed everywhere in regeneration-processes, of particularly adapted larval characters at later stages.

The persistent substitution-homoeosis, by contrast, represents no developmental stage which would have ontogenetically or phylogenetically preceded the replaced limb. The condition is the extinction of the capacity to regenerate the more highly differentiated structure: in analogy with the appearance of the first antenna on the eye-stalk deprived of its ganglion (HERBST), a similar cause may also be conjectured for the other cases. Yet it does not appear beyond all doubt whether the ganglion acts as the mediator of a formative stimulus, or whether at the same time as the removal of the ganglion the rudiment [Anlage] for eye-formation is removed along with it. From the experiments hitherto it does indeed emerge that the eye-ganglion itself is likewise not regenerated — but for its formation the formative stimulus of itself can hardly be decisive? The light-perception is, according to HERBST's own experiments on animals kept in the dark, not decisive, and ZELENY saw, after extirpation of the rudimentary eye of *Cambarus pellucidus*, a heteromorphic antennula arise. Transplantation-experiments could provide definitive elucidation: were the transplanted ganglion-region, without gaining a nerve-connection, to regenerate an eye, then a formative action of the nerve-stimuli would be excluded.

Unfortunately I have hitherto exerted myself in vain to find a suitable transplantation-technique for Crustaceans.

These rules are, however, not valid without exception (cf. III). In particular the appearance of the supernumerary genital apertures in the male *Nephrops* follows the reversed direction.

Also the appearance of epipodites, which normally belong only to the first thoracic leg-pair of *Hippolyte fabricii* (XIV), on the second leg-pair as well, can hardly be regarded as a higher differentiation, because precisely the higher Crustaceans lack these epipodites.

Precisely these exceptions give us a hint that in the addition-homoeosis we have to do with two groups of phenomena.

The variation in the number and position of the genital apertures (XV), just like the variation in the number of epipodite-bearing legs (XIV), occurs comparatively frequently and resembles states which are normally constant in related genera or species. The "additions" need not be regarded as segment-foreign, but merely as characters mostly lacking to the segment, yet still falling within the range of variation of the species. The place of appearance on the limb likewise makes at first glance no abnormal impression: the genital apertures lie on the coxopodite in the same position on the normal as on the abnormal legs, the epipodites spring from the second leg-pair at the analogous place as on the first.

It is quite otherwise with the second group of exceedingly rare addition-homoeosis-cases, known always only in single specimens. The impression is an unnatural one (III, VIII, XVII). Nowhere in the relationship [Verwandtschaft, i.e. among kin] are normal analogies to be found; the structures in question (feeler, leg; thoracic leg, abdomen) belong to a different body-region. Their position in no way corresponds to that which they would take up at their normal segment. They do indeed spring forth in such a way that no increase or decrease in the number of the joints up to the trunk takes place, but they turn to the normal segmental-appendage which they resemble a homologous surface. A geometric symmetry is present, which can awaken the appearance as if the addition-structure had the morphological symmetry of the opposite side, as BETHE (1896) assumed. A more exact comparison of BETHE's figures with the upper and under side of normal crab-legs has, however, convinced me that in the BETHE case it is a matter of a leg with the symmetry of the *correct* side, which, however, implanted with its ventral side on the abdomen, turns its dorsal side toward the observer. It is in this respect referred to the normal *Carcinus maenas* drawn for comparison in the accompanying text-figures 8 *b*–*e*; namely the projecting tooth of the coxopodite (*z*) belongs decidedly to the dorsal side (text-fig. 8 *b* and *c*).

The ventral side presents a quite different picture (Text-fig. 8*d* and *e*).

Wheeler (Text-fig. 7*a*) traces the curvature of the supernumerary feeler on the leg back to the action of the wind. Apart from the fact that the normally-positioned, admittedly substantially smaller feeler exhibits, despite the wind-direction, a curvature opposed to it, we see no fixation occurring, in any way according to the stress, of thread-like appendages fluttering in the wind.

If I behold, in the first group of supernumerary homoeosis, a real, discontinuous variation handed down through inheritance (Bateson), then I should like to give a different explanation for the second group — one which was repeatedly hinted at earlier and which may now, with the aid of the more precise analysis, be treated somewhat more fully: that is, the autotransplantation. I imagine that — as has repeatedly happened in egg-experiments — also in the open, sometimes, owing to mechanical influences, cells or cell-complexes of a particular prospective significance become detached from their place of origin, grow onto another place, and, since their potency is a limited one, there now develop into a supernumerary homoeosis.

Such processes will be able to take place at stages where anlagen later widely separated possess a chance, local neighbourhood. This category differs from the previous one chiefly through the absence of the replacing structure at the normal place. ### Summary.

1) Homoeosis in the widest sense (Bateson 1894), the occurrence of appendages or formations that normally belong to another body-segment, encompasses different groups of phenomena that do not allow themselves, after their separation, to be recognized as regularities under a single rule.

2) The first group, Homoeosis s. str., substitutive (Wheeler) homoeosis, or "replacement-homoeosis," follows the rule that always at the place of a more highly differentiated structure a less differentiated one steps in. In the articulated appendages of the Arthropods this entails, owing to the decrease of the differentiation-height of the limbs from front to backward, that the homoeotically altered limb resembles the normal one of a following segment. In the case of the wings the sequence is reversed.

The cause of the replacement-homoeosis is in some cases certain, in all others most probably Regeneration.

The homoeosis then represents either a transient, and indeed palingenetic (so-called atavistic), regeneration-stage, or a persisting "Heteromorphose."

The condition for the latter phenomenon is perhaps, generally, the loss of the corresponding ganglion?

3) The second group, Heterotopy, adventive (Wheeler) homoeosis, or "supernumerary-homoeosis," and the third group, Heterophory, translative, or "displacement-homoeosis," do not follow the rule set up for the first group.

As causes, in both groups, besides inherited variations, embryonic re-orderings come into consideration. The former are to be assumed where they occur relatively frequently, the latter where it is a matter of isolated cases.

4) Supernumerary structures of Heterotopy, which belong to non-adjacent segments, appear in a dorsoventrally reversed position and attach themselves at such places as had occasion to come into geometric contact, during the embryonic development, with the normal location of the structures.

Their origin would, in the easiest manner, be explained by an autotransplantation as a consequence of a mechanical insult.

5) Supernumerary structures of Heterophory, belonging to neighbouring segments, possess the same dorsoventrality as the rest of the body, and are to be traced back to displacements during the individual development without a preceding complete detachment from their normal location.

6) The distribution of the homoeosis-cases over the various genera is not an accidental one, and gives, on the occasion of this work, cause to choose precisely the affected forms as object for experiments, which alone will be in a position to prove the soundness or untenability of the heuristic hypotheses here put forward.
