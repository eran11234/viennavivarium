## IV. Über das Wachstum der replantierten Augen.
## IV. On the Growth of the Replanted Eyes.

By

### Theodor Koppanyi.

*(From the Biological Experimental Institute of the Academy of Sciences in Vienna [Zoological Department].)*

Alongside the eye-reflexes there was also yet another phenomenon present, which could show us whether our transplantation was a functional one or not. This phenomenon is the growth. The growth of a transplant (»proliferation«) is in general regarded as an objective mark of the continued life of the grafted pieces (independent life, *Vita propria*). We know that the appearance of new blood-vessel anastomoses does not even by itself testify to the continued life of the transplants, since foreign bodies too can become covered over with blood vessels. Thus, for example, we sometimes see that the clear cornea is, as it were, overrun by an invasion of small blood vessels, while at the same time the eye is atrophic. From this it already follows that the proliferation of the capillaries is by no means the same thing as the proper nourishment of the grafted piece corresponding to normal conditions. In any case they [the capillaries] must break through the scleral tissue (the thinner the sclera, the more easily the in-healing succeeds) in order to be able to send an approximately sufficient blood-stream into the new eye. Yet one must be somewhat cautious in the assessment of the growth phenomena, since in many cases it is difficult to judge whether the increase in volume does not simply rest upon a penetration of elements lying in the neighbourhood into the grafted piece, and is a precursor of the certain disintegration. As already mentioned, panophthalmic eyes can enlarge abnormally, buphthalmically, in order then to be able to disintegrate rapidly. Microscopically there are then in such cases frequently wandering cells, polynuclear leukocytes, to be detected.¹

Older experiments too were concerned with the question whether transplanted organs are capable of growth. The old experiments of *Paul Bert* (*Recherches expérimentales* 1866), who transplanted pieces of rat tails under the skin, showed that the tails of young transplant-donors grew, whereas the tails of older individuals, although likewise abundantly vascularized, did not grow further. Besides these older experiments on mammals, later experiments too are known which were concerned with the growth-capability of transplanted organs. Thus *Leo Loeb* found (Arch. f. Entw.-Mech., Bd. XXXI, 1911) that the transplanted uterus can be stimulated to placenta-formation. In the transplanted uterus the placenta forms itself out of the co-transplanted connective tissue, and so the transplant increases considerably. Besides the mammal-experiments, several cases are known from other vertebrates and invertebrates, in which the grafted pieces grew considerably after the grafting, and even regenerated accidentally as well. *Oscar Kurz* transplanted, on newts, extremity-stumps onto arbitrary places of the body, which then restored themselves into whole legs. — The generally known fact that embryonal transplants pass through their developmental stages normally does not really belong to the area of the discussion of transplant-proliferation.

With respect to the growth of the transplanted eyes we must distinguish two possibilities. The homoioplastic union, in which the growth-potencies of the donor and of the recipient agree; and the heteroplastic transplantation, in which this can also be otherwise.

Already with the homoioplastic transplantation we see that the replanted eyes do not grow further in every case. I have already seen cases, namely in rats, in which the over-transplanted eyes were indeed vascularized, motorically and also by means of the trigeminus innervated, but possessed no retina-function. In these cases too no trace of a proliferation sets in; they persist at the same growth-stage at which they had been grafted. The proliferation of the replants therefore seems to be strongly connected with the functional capability of the same. In all those cases in which the physiological demonstration of the full functional capability succeeded, the homoioplastically transplanted cold-blood bulbi grew to a normal degree; and the rats kept on for a long time with transplanted eyes likewise showed no noticeable disproportion of the eye-size. It was also established that in these cases too the normal vascularization had been produced.

With the cold-bloods it is fairly all the same whether we transplant the eyes in the youngest developmental stages (thus in the larval condition).

The transplanted bulbi will grow further quickly enough together with the animal, so as then to be able to undergo the metamorphosis. The relationships lie somewhat more difficult with the rats. We have already indicated that all-too-young specimens are not especially favourable for the purpose of eye-transplantation. The growth-speed of the replants cannot, namely, keep step with that of the host-animal, and so the eye remains behind in growth. Such eyes then occur, in the fully grown animal, as small, shrunken organs. The cause of this fairly puzzling phenomenon is probably this, that the nourishment of the grafted piece indeed suffices to be able to maintain the eye in the functionally capable condition, but is yet no longer enough for the unfolding of the growth phenomena. Even the first period, in which the replants are supplied only osmotically or at first only sparingly with vessels and therefore cannot grow, is of course enough to bring it about that the transplanted eye cannot participate in the uncommonly rapid growth of the young rat and so must naturally remain behind.

Quite other possibilities stand open when the eyes have been exchanged not homoio-, but heteroplastically. To be sure, we have hitherto observed growing heterotransplants only in the cold-bloods. Above all, then, it must be established that the heteroplastic transplantation gives no hindrance to proliferation. As a rule we notice it too, that the functioning heterotransplant grows further. But now there are here two eventualities open. Firstly, the transplant could grow further to the degree of the host-animal; secondly, however, also species-specifically.

Let us take a fire salamander [*Salamandra salamandra*], whose eye-socket harbours a *Molge vulgaris*-eye [common newt, *Lissotriton vulgaris*]. The other eye is the body's own. Although both the donor and the recipient were quite young, freshly metamorphosed animals, the transplanted Triton-eye grew for a long time not with the same speed as the body's own salamander-eye. After 8 months one saw, alongside the much larger salamander-eye, the smaller pond-newt eye, distinguished by a yellow iris-ring (compare the photograph in Part VI, Plate III, Fig. 1). But the smaller pond-newt eye too has grown considerably, only not to the degree of the salamander-bulbus, although at the time of the transplantation both were equally large. Since the bulbus was correctly supplied and, as it later showed in the histological investigation, possessed an optimally preserved retina and was also normally vascularized, the matter cannot rest upon insufficient nourishment. Besides, the transplanted eye has indeed grown from the beginning on. Since the transplanted newt-bulbus never exceeded the size of the maximal pond-newt eye, but did indeed attain it, nothing else remains than to assume
