## I. The Scene of the Observation.

In July 1906 I observed, at St. Margaretenbad (619 m, near Prachatitz, at the foot of the Bohemian Forest mountains), larvae of the blue darner (*Aeschna cyanea* Müller) which were overgrown by filamentous algae (*Oedogonium undulatum* Alex. Braun). My observations and experiments established this occurrence not as a chance event, but as a genuine symbiosis, as a living-together on the basis of mutual advantages, including metabolic exchange; and I resolved, although in many respects I was able to gather only fragmentary data, to publish it, since I can scarcely hope to obtain new material in the foreseeable future. From the locality itself I have, since my departure from there, despite an arranged supply connection, been unable to procure any more; and the phenomenon does not appear to be so widespread that it could readily be found in other regions; at least up to now I have not succeeded, although, as may easily be imagined, I am directing especially eager attention toward the renewed establishment and extension of the observations made at that time.

So that other naturalists who come to the region in question may be in a position to ascertain whether the appearance has been preserved and whether it has undergone any kind of changes, perhaps perfectings, I wish to make the locating of the locality possible by means of an exact description.

If one follows in a descending direction the meadow path which, at St. Margaretenbad, branches off from the carriage road by the Villa Helene and leads fairly straight down to Prachatitz, one reaches, after only a few steps, a place where that path joins with a second footpath that comes down somewhat farther east from the edge of the woods. To the right hand of this crossing of the paths lies, marked out by young willow shrubbery, a small meadow pond.

A thinly trickling little thread of water pours into it on one side and provides for the perpetual supply and removal of fresh water.

Other circumstances, however, cause it to be fairly bare of animal and plant life: for its keeping clean man too, namely, has contributed. The walls of the basin are reinforced and leveled with masonry stones, so that it now cannot silt up so rapidly and, with an area of only about 2 sq. m, has a certain depth (everywhere uniformly 50 to 35 cm), which moreover the washerwomen of St. Margaretenbad use for rinsing laundry, and it is therefore often clouded by soap content, which is admittedly removed again comparatively soon by the outflow, but through its somewhat persisting concentration nevertheless does not exactly [favor] an animal and plant life of the described body of water.

Nevertheless I was able to discover zoologically interesting organisms in it. At some places of the edge branched-moss turfs hang down into the water; *Myosotis*, *Parnassia* and other moisture-loving plants mingle at the shore among the meadow flora proper; but in the water itself there are lacking both the true marsh plants and the submerged water plants (as far as the first glance taught, even including the algae), as well as the otherwise most common animal inhabitants of stagnant waters. We turned [our attention to] tadpoles, water bugs, diving beetles, leeches, and the only thing that came into the dip-net at the first cursory haul were freshwater shrimp (*Gammarus pulex* L.), amber snails (*Succinea putris* L.) and wandering mud snails (*Limnaea peregra* Drap.).

Then suddenly I saw an appearance unfamiliar to me hovering through the water in jerky thrusts, evidently startled up by the unrest which my net-movements had produced. Like a swaying, loosely flapping veil it darted along, obliquely toward the bottom, and disappeared into a crevice of the masonry side-walls.

By partly demolishing the latter, I succeeded in making sure of the mysterious creature dwelling there: I held a dragonfly larva in my hands, whose body was wrapped over and over in a veil of green filamentous algae — which were otherwise lacking in the body of water at this season of the year! At first I believed the larva had merely crept by chance through a mat of algae and had involuntarily dragged this along with it. But under the magnifying glass I saw that the algae grew fast on the body of the larva. I was now able soon to catch a number of well over 100 dragonfly larvae, which had their hiding-places under water at the moreover not deep masonry crevices that granted full admission to the light, crept about leisurely at rest in an upright position on the stones, but when frightened glided through the water and thereby afforded the described, fantastic sight.

Only the larvae belonging to the genus *Aeschna* bear the filamentous-alga tuft. Besides the *Aeschna* larvae, *Anax* larvae too live in that pond. These did not possess those algae, but were instead — in contrast to the former — uniformly olive-green. As I only learned subsequently, Megusar had namely observed normally brown-colored larvae [in which the green pigment of the vegetating algae] reached deep into the interior of their body, [a phenomenon connected with] *Hydrocharis caraboides*; according to this still unpublished observation, which I here reproduce in agreement with its originator, it would not be inconceivable that one would find the green *Anax* larvae associated [with algae], which, however, do not live exosomatically as in *Aeschna*, but endosomatically.

## II. Descriptive Treatment.

With the exception of the mouthparts and eyes, no outer body part of the larvae of *Aeschna cyanea* found at the described locality (cf. the figure!) is entirely free of the *Oedogonium* filaments; yet a certain, regular distribution of the algal lawn is not to be missed, which to some extent recalls the [arrangement] of the skin-covering of [a furry animal]. They are most densely overgrown on the parts namely the thorax and the hindbody; whereas the limbs, whose [more exposed] condition and whose striding or even swimming movements are probably unfavorable to the settling-in, the

algal lawn decreases according to density and length of its components, so as finally in this respect to reach a minimum on the underside, at any rate in causal connection with the lesser amount of light and the friction of the larval body against the substrate. Only in the surroundings of the anus is a stronger accumulation of algal filaments again to be observed, to which the expelled excrements evidently furnish a good fertilizer. On the other hand, this [is favored] from the anus, since the rectum — which, as is known, lies in the form of many transverse gills in an enlargement of the hindgut — is necessarily filled with respiratory water saturated with the oxygen given off by the algae.

Noteworthy is the raised carriage of the wing stumps, otherwise not occurring in dragonfly larvae. In normal larvae they lie close against the thorax and abdomen. This spread-out position is brought about by the algal mass growing beneath them on the meso- and metathorax and on the abdomen, which forms, as it were, a cushion upon which the wing stumps rest. The many air bubbles too (of the oxygen secreted by the algae, chemically pure oxygen¹), with which, when the sun shines upon it, the algal garment constantly appears interwoven as if with strings of pearls, may represent a buoyancy-producing mechanism.

The *Aeschna* larvae, of which both strikingly large, up to 55 mm long (already partially neotenic?) specimens, as well as quite small ones were found, were without exception covered with algae, whether they were young or old stages. The algae were fastened to the chitinous integument by means of their characteristic, lobed rhizoid, and predominantly young filaments at that, but nevertheless some up to 35 mm in length.

In the aquarium I observed that the algae, when the larvae molted, had to be shed along with them as I had expected. For a few days they still cling to the cast-off envelope, then they detach themselves and form small, freely swimming, copiously sexually reproducing mats, as this indeed also corresponds to the otherwise observed developmental course of the Oedogoniaceae. The period between any two molts suffices them exactly to complete the sessile stage; later they then need no substrate any more. The abandoned larval skin succumbs to unusually more rapid decomposition (cf. Experiment 8). But on the freshly molted dragonfly larvae a new algal lawn sprouts up with incredible speed. At times the freshly molted animal was already, as it were, bedewed with a light-green bloom shimmering silkily in direct sunlight, originating from the fine algal down in the process of new formation. In other cases the freshly molted individuals appeared at first algae-free. I placed such ones into boiled water that had then been made air-containing again by shaking; nevertheless the old condition was soon reached again. Microscopic examination taught me that the algae already, when the chitin plates detach themselves from the trunk before molting and leave gaps between themselves, advance through these gaps to the new skin before the old one is cast off; the germs thereof are then demonstrable, and indeed both when the freshly molted animal does not yet show the mentioned green bloom, and also when one prematurely artificially molts the animal still stuck in the worn-out integument (careful cutting open of the chitin plates by means of fine scissors) and now brings a piece of the soft integument coming to light beneath under the microscope. Thus it is therefore provided that the larvae, despite their periodic molts, cannot lose the symbionts which, as we shall see, are valuable to them.

## III. Experimental Treatment.

Since the entire observation period falls upon the month of July, from the 2nd to the 29th of July 1907 (the experiments were continued at the institute building in Vienna until the middle of August), my collecting thereof was at any rate not entirely fruitless, but only a few goals had to lag behind. Nevertheless it succeeded, in the gradual completion, so much that the principial clarity of the important interrelations of the encountered symbiosis-case could be demonstrated.

**Technical remarks:** The dragonfly larvae to be examined were kept in beaker-glasses of 3 to 1 liter content, each of which contained a number of individuals, a glass beneath. The room, whose window, facing the morning sun, received the glasses row-wise. The temperature of the water reached 20° C. in the warm season; during the remaining hours of the day it amounted to 16 to 17°. For feeding there served as a rule the mosquito larvae, which I usually had from the drying out of the pond; when these had become dried out, the predatory dragonfly larvae accustomed themselves to small, worm-shaped cut little pieces of raw beef, which I held out to them impaled on a pointed little wooden stick and thereby moved slightly. After an initial, disconcerted withdrawal the dragonfly larvae finally did decide, after all, to fling their catching-masks toward the morsel, in order to devour it eagerly immediately thereafter.

Before the beginning of the experiments it was necessary first to acclimatize the larvae for a couple of days. This time suffices to make them sufficiently trusting, so that they do not react to every manipulation with violent flight-movements, by virtue of which they partly cast off their algal hanging.

For the purpose of setting up the control experiments, non-algified larvae of the same *Aeschna* species were fetched from a carp pond, not far removed from the meadow pond described in the introductory chapter and filled with rich vegetation (the owner is Mr. Stefan Hall, whom I thank most kindly for his permission to catch animals in the pond!) in Prachatitz, and then kept under the respectively same conditions as the larvae overgrown with algae.

### A. Experiments with the Symbionts Living in Community.

(Mutual Dependence of the Same.)

**1. Experiment: Keeping in carbonated water.**

**a) Soda-water mixture:** A beaker-glass which was stocked with six algified larvae was filled half with ordinary well water, half with soda water, and then quickly closed by means of a greased glass plate. A second glass, which was stocked with six algae-free larvae, was treated likewise.

After 12 hours the non-algified larvae were dead, the algified ones still rather lively. Now I repeated, with that glass which contained the latter, the procedure insofar as I poured off the half of the water that had meanwhile, despite the closure, become poorer in carbonic acid and replaced it with fresh soda water. After a further 12 hours three larvae were only faint, two larvae motionless, one larva dead. The five more or less stupefied larvae recovered, brought back into pure well water, within 10 minutes.

Already from this experiment it emerges that the algae-overgrown larvae tolerate the carbonic-acid content better than the algae-less ones; in the former the carbon-dioxide-assimilating, oxygen-liberating algae parried what to their bearers would have been pernicious would have become. The experimental result is thus in agreement with that of Hadži on the algae-free *Hydra fusca* and on the *Hydra viridis* living in symbiosis with the alga *Zoochlorella conductrix*. Although, far from the laboratory, I had at my disposal neither an air-pump nor a carbon-dioxide bomb, with the help of which I could have carried out the experiment as exactly as Hadži accomplished it on *Hydra*, still the principial agreement of the result ought even without that to be sufficiently clear.

b) Excessive stocking with animals: In the one, only half-filled glass, 12 algae-grown larvae were kept, in the second, in the same quantity of water, 12 algae-less larvae. Through this stocking, far too strong in proportion to the small water-volume (1½ l) under normal circumstances, a carbon-dioxide excess had likewise to arise.

After 3 days, in the control experiment (with algae-free larvae), not one specimen lived any more; of the algae-covered larvae all lived save one specimen, which, however, had been bitten to death by its comrades, and had thus not succumbed to the consequences of the carbon-dioxide atmosphere.

c) Water plants in the control glass: The actual test glass [Versuchsglas] was again treated first in the manner specified under a, then in the manner specified under b; the control animals, however, were provided with various water plants from their dwelling-pond, with *Callitriche*, *Lemna* and *Fontinalis*.

This time, after 3 days, only seven specimens (case a), respectively six specimens (case b), went to the ground; the remaining five, respectively six, algae-free larvae remained alive. Of the algae-grown larvae, however, this time in both cases not a single specimen went to the ground.

d) *Oedogonium capillare* in the control glass: The same experiment was further so arranged that the control larvae, instead of arbitrary water plants, received only algae, and indeed that species which stands nearest to the species living symbiotically on the larvae, *Oedogonium capillare* Ktz. This filamentous alga, which was likewise found abundantly in the Hall fish-ponds, was thrown into the control glass in as nearly as possible the same quantity in which *Oedogonium undulatum* was present in the test glass.

After 3 days, in case a five, in case b likewise five control larvae were dead; one more (two more in case b) died on the fifth day, the rest remained alive. In the test glass [Versuchsglas] all the specimens lived.

The experiment was repeated several times with a result naturally not entirely the same in numerical terms, but qualitatively analogous.

The algae-grown larvae had thus also borne the dirty water more easily than the algae-less ones. Herein lies a certain contrast to a similar experiment by Hadži on *Hydra fusca* and *viridis*, likewise to findings by Engelmann on algae-bearing *Paramecia* and *Vorticellae*: the said "phytozoa" went to the ground more rapidly in dirty water than the genus-relatives not united with algae. The explanation for this divergent behaviour may perhaps lie in this, that the symbiotic algae live intracellularly in the said Protozoa and *Hydra*, but extracellularly in our insect larvae. In both cases, it is true, the animals are, as Engelmann says, "adapted to a larger quantity of oxygen", spoiled in this respect by the dissimilating activity of the algae. There, however, it is the animal body which is first affected by the medium harmful to it and which carries over the effect of the injury sustained, probably, as I have already expressed elsewhere (1907 a), onto the alga, which thereunder likewise suffers and so accelerates the whole process of destruction instead of checking it; here, on the contrary, it is the plant body which first comes into contact with the medium more conducive than harmful to it, and which holds back the injury to the animal body by enabling, through the abundant nourishment, a still more intensive oxygen-secretion. All too high degrees of contamination must here, of course, be left out of account. Nor do I picture it so, that the algal mantle sets up a mechanical obstacle to the advance of the poisons: only the improvement of the water and its impregnation with oxygen, to be expected according to the laws of automatic water-purification and uninterrupted in daylight, which takes place within the range of the algae, can come into consideration.

b) Water spoiled by soap: Both glasses again stocked with six larvae each, the test glass of course with algae-grown ones. Both, however, were this time contaminated with ordinary washing-soap. The soapy water I took at first directly from the pond, just after washing had been done there, in order to have the same concentration to which the larvae were exposed in their free life.

The result was, as compared with experiment 2a, a more favourable one for the dragonfly larvae in so far as there, at the close, after all all the larvae, including the algae-grown ones, had gone to the ground, whereas here, at the end of the experimental period, three animals of the latter were still alive. The experiment was repeated several times with a still higher soap-content in the test glass and with a lesser or no soap-content at all in the control glass, whereby it appears proved that the former — all too high a concentration left aside — did not harm the algae, since it left their assimilation-activity undisturbed and put them in a position to supply the dragonfly larvae with so much oxygen that they for their part were able to defy the contamination. The supposition lies near that, with respect to the advantageous behaviour of the algae, the potash-content of the soap played a part.

This experiment had been undertaken in view of the fact, which I now recall, that the dwelling-water of the algae-grown larvae used to be employed for rinsing laundry and at times to contain much soap. Concerning the supposition, attaching itself to the finding obtained, regarding the origin of our case of symbiosis, the discussion shall come in the following chapter ("Conclusion").

3rd Experiment: Infection with Saprolegniae. Spoiled water and the crowding together of many animal individuals in a small space are uncommonly favourable to the emergence of an epidemic which wreaks great havoc among the most diverse water-dwellers and is caused by a family of the Oomycetes, the water-mould (Saprolegniaceae).

A shallow meadow-puddle near St. Margaretenbad, situated by the road to Pfefferschlag, was on the point of drying out, whereby an animal population found itself crowded en masse into a minimum of space. Nearly all, of whatever class they might belong to, were covered with Saprolegnia-felt: so too the *Aeschna* larvae there.

I resolved to make use of this circumstance for a further experiment, which I best attach here to the experiment with dirty water, since the Saprolegnia-epidemic likewise represents a consequence-phenomenon of contaminated water.

In a non-coloured image, also one not strongly enlarged, algal turf and fungal turf growing on animals could easily be confused with one another; in reality, of course, the latter is distinguished, even for the bare eye, very unfavourably by its dirty greyish-white from the former with its fresh green. The growth-habit, with the growth-centre on the upper side (because of the rubbing-off on the under-side), is, however, quite the same, so that even our illustration can convey an idea both of the one and of the other. I have experienced that persons, to whom I showed the figure and who knew the water-mould from its unfortunately only too great prevalence in our institution, believed without further ado that they recognized a larva attacked by it. But this only in passing.

Three *Aeschna cyanea* larvae afflicted with *Saprolegnia* were placed, in each case, together with three algae-grown larvae of the same kind into the one glass, and with three algae-less larvae into another glass. For a whole week no further spreading of the fungus was to be observed either here or there. On the ninth experiment-morning, however, there suddenly appeared in the control glass (with the algae-less larvae) all six larvae covered with the characteristic white down which the spore-bearers form; these must have shot up overnight to centimetre-height on the larvae that had hitherto — at least outwardly — been clean. The three algae-grown larvae of the test glass remained entirely free of the parasites and were healthy, even after, within 2 weeks, the attacked comrades had departed this life.

This experiment too was repeated several times with varied individual-number of the experimental animals and with a quantitatively divergent, but qualitatively best-agreeing result.

4th Experiment: Keeping in the dark. a) at the otherwise usual experimental temperature and with standing water: The two glasses, the one containing six algae-bearing, the other six algae-less dragonfly larvae, were placed under a dark-cover (a box lined inside and out with black paper).

On the fourth day the algae began to fade and to fall off. They passed into decay, of the consequences of which the larvae were rapidly carried off. On the seventh day not one was alive any more; in the control glass (with the algae-less larvae), however, four larvae were still well and lively (two had been bitten to death by their own kind).

b) at lower temperature and with flowing water: The two glasses, in the same stocking, were not left at their sunny location, as mentioned earlier, but were carried to a woodland brook and placed in the cool water (of 11 to 12° C.) in such a way that a small cascade situated just at the spot in question, chosen for that reason, where the brook fell down from a block of rock, had to bubble directly into the glasses bound shut with organtine [silk gauze], yet not so strongly that a violent eddy arose. It remained, for the experimental set-up, still to darken both glasses as far as possible by enveloping them in black cloth (yet, because of the opening left free at the top, not to the degree as in case a).

Here too the algae went to the ground, since they could not find the light-intensity needed for their assimilation, only 2 days later than in experiment 4a, where they had been kept without throughflow of fresh water and had received no light at all. The larvae, however, here remained alive further, because the continually renewed water, saturated with air in its plunging down, paralysed the decomposition-process of the algae.

c) Repetition of experiment 4a with combination of the harmful agents introduced into the water in experiments 1 and 2: The dark experiment a was repeated several times for the purpose of testing, one after another, with respect to their efficacy under the altered conditions, all the chemical harmful agents applied in the first two experiments, which now here too in the same or weaker measure were admixed to the water.

The relation which in experiments 1 and 2 had turned out in favour of the algae-bearing larvae now reversed itself precisely: now it was always the algae-less larvae which, in the dark plus the water contaminated by carbon-dioxide, faecal matter or soap, went to the ground less rapidly. Admittedly, however, they too were not able, as the algae-grown ones had done in the light, to accustom themselves permanently to moderate degrees of contamination, but fell prey, just like the latter, to a sufficiently rapid death — only in the dark a less rapid one than that which the comrades favoured in the light had had to suffer.

B. Experiments on the separation of the symbionts. (Conditional independence of the same from one another.)

5th Experiment: Liberation of algae-grown dragonfly larvae from their symbionts (pure culture of the larvae). I had hoped to bring along a number of dragonfly larvae from Bohemia to Vienna. Unfortunately, the transport already from St. Margaretenbad to Frauenberg, whither I had betaken myself on the 24th of July, failed, in that the algae detached themselves in consequence of the shakings during the railway journey. Only on a few specimens did the algal vegetation remain preserved; these just sufficed in order now, for their part — after deduction of the specimens still needed for experimental purposes — to serve as control animals in several further experiments which I intended to set up.

I scraped the larvae which had lost their algal cover entirely clean, and kept them, together with a corresponding number, housed in another glass, of larvae which had been algae-free from the outset, in succession (on the going-to-ground of the one series naturally always again with the drawing-in of fresh series, hitherto kept under normal conditions in ordinary water), under all the conditions as indicated by experiment 1, 2 and 3 a.

Of one method of making the larvae algae-clean I must here further make mention, for the sake of its peculiar failure. Incidentally, I have already come to speak of it in the descriptive part: since the scraping-off for the purpose of cleaning seemed to me too crude, I awaited the next moult, at which the green turf is thrown off along with it, and transferred the moulted, apparently algaefree larvae into boiled, then aerated water, in the opinion that they would not here be grown over again. But within a short time a new turf was present, which had come about in this way, that more or less numerous, microscopically small little fragmentary pieces of the old turf grew over onto the new integument, through between the chitinous rings which, just before the entry of the moult, had already detached themselves from the trunk and gaped fairly wide apart at the pleurae.

The course of the experiment now discussed itself hardly requires any detailed description. It suffices when I, summarizing, indicate that the larvae which had been algae-grown and those caught in an algae-free state behaved, over against most influences, in general alike, down to differences lying within the experimental error-limits; that the originally algae-covered larvae had thus, with the loss of their symbionts, also forfeited their greater resistance-capacity.

As regards the carbon-dioxide experiment 1 a and the dirty-water experiment 2 a, there even showed itself, in contrast to hitherto, a not exactly coarse, but nevertheless distinct inferiority of the formerly algae-grown larvae over against the primarily algae-free larvae. Here, then, it came to light that the former had indeed been adapted to a higher oxygen-content than the latter, and therefore now succumbed to the harmful influences almost just as much more rapidly as they had earlier defied them longer.

6th Experiment: Liberation of the algae growing on dragonfly larvae from their symbionts (pure culture of the algae). a) On inorganic and vegetable substrate: In the case of such dragonfly larvae as exhibited, shortly after the moult, a freshly sprouting algal turf, young algal threads were carefully detached from their substrate under the magnifying glass and with the application of a dissecting-needle ground to a lancet-like shape, and their adhesive-disc-like, lobed rhizoid was fastened by pressing-on onto other substrates which, according to experience, are favourable to algal settlement (rough stones, mouldered pieces of wood, stems of fresh swamp-plants). Yet neither on the inorganic nor on the vegetable underlay would they "take root". This could not lie in a mistaken manner of the detachment and re-planting, since, as we shall hear, the transference onto other dragonfly larvae had succeeded quite well.

Algal threads brought in such manner prematurely to free floating showed at first still a progression of the cell-divisions, but

b) **On animal substrate:** The algae held themselves better when I transferred them onto animal nutrient-substrate: on a dead earthworm, on fresh or dry pieces of a freshwater sponge (*Ephydatia fluviatilis* L.), the firm attachment likewise succeeded, which however lasted only an interval of time of 4 to 6 days.

In these results scarcely anything changed to the advantage of the algae when I did not detach them completely, but rather took along a tiny fragment of larval integument and brought the algal filament together with it onto the new nutrient-substrate.

By far the best artificial substrate, however, was furnished by rubbed-up dragonfly-larvae which had previously, in the living state, borne algae. It made no clear difference whether I in doing so worked up the chitinous integument along with [them] or dissected it away beforehand.

These experiments brought out least of all that the scant *Oedogonium undulatum* at the locality in question is dependent, to a high degree, in its existence upon that of the dragonfly-larvae. The adaptation does not indeed go so far as that of the intracellularly living zoochlorellae (Haberlandt, Brandt, Beijerinck, Famintzin, Hadži), but nevertheless far enough to make their pure culture difficult.

7th Experiment: **Transfer of the symbiotic algae onto originally alga-free insect larvae.** a) **Transfer onto the same Species:** When such larvae of *Aeschna*, which had been found alga-free and stemmed from the very same waters as the alga-overgrown ones — the dwelling-water of the latter contained indeed no others — were kept together with alga-bearing exemplars in one and the same receptacle, the former covered themselves likewise within 1 to 2 weeks with a green film, which finally grew up into a lawn. *Anax-*, *Libellula-* and *Calopteryx*-larvae, caged in together with *Aeschna*-larvae, remained alga-free.

Following the already described method, I further detached algal filaments from the body of some *Aeschna*-larvae and transferred them onto the body of other *Aeschna*-larvae which had originally been clean of them. The infection, in spite of the latter being naturally kept separated, proceeded quite well; the algae found a lasting foothold and thriving upon the symbionts forced upon them.

b) **Transfer onto another Species:** The artificial infection succeeded, however, no less well with, or rather in, the other dragonfly genera already named earlier, which then likewise fed onto the algal-hanging [Algenbehang], without a noticeable difference being ascertainable in respect of the local extent of the lawn being present: I was able to set up just this experiment only a single time.

A few exemplars of *Dytiscus* larvae too I infected with the *Oedogonium*: at first it was, upon this underlay, engaged in fairly rapid vegetative propagation; after 8 to 9 days, however, it again came loose.

While the infection of *Hydra fusca* with zoochlorellae from *Hydra viridis* does not succeed for Hadži either, the less specialized adaptation of the *Oedogonium*, as we had proven previously in the pure culture of the algae, expresses itself once more in this, that it allows itself to be transferred onto pre-existing individuals of the same and of other species.

8th Experiment: **Transfer of free-living algae onto originally alga-free insect larvae.** a) **Transfer of the same Species:** The last experiment we could undertake, in order to lay open the conditions of the symbiosis of *Aeschna* and *Oedogonium*, consisted in transferring onto those [larvae] algae that were either swimming, or sitting on another substrate, but not on dragonfly-larvae.

First I tried it with the same algal species, *Oedogonium undulatum*, which grows on the larvae [and] which also occurs freely, mixed among other filamentous algae, in small ponds, namely woodland pools. After searching, I found what I desired in a small pool beside the Pfefferschläger ponds (near St. Margaretenbad). Only the dipping-out was then permitted me, in an alga-poor manner, by the Prachtätzer Fischereiverein. I found both freely-swimming mats [Watten] of slight extent and fixed-attached filaments on stems of *Ranunculus fluitans*.

Applying once more the already described method, I detached the young, still fixed-attached *Oedogonium*-filaments [Fäden] from their underlay and [placed them] onto the abdominal dorsum of the *Aeschna cyanea*-larvae. So that these would not at once, by virtue of their movements kick [them] off, I stunned them beforehand in a weak ether-atmosphere [Ätheratmosphäre], so that they, reckoned from the moment of the planting-on of the algae, lay for about an hour motionless in a shallow, water-filled dissecting-dish, [the larvae] slowly recovering from the narcosis. Several such freely-laid-on and firmly-grown algal mats [Watten] I grasped, and they formed in the course of 10 to 12 days a fairly dense lawn upon the insect. The implantation of such freely-found *Oedogonium undulatum* onto *Anax-*, *Libellula-* and *Dytiscus*-larvae failed.

I must make mention still of a closer method of infecting originally alga-free larvae of *Aeschna cyanea* with *Oedogonium*, which permits [an insight into] the origin-cause of our case-of-symbiosis. It is easy to observe that dragonfly-larvae like to creep through dense algal thickets — first working themselves in, then mostly remaining hidden therein a little while, finally — as a rule on the opposite side — coming forth again. They do this especially, naturally, when, as was the case in the experimental glasses, they have no other hiding-places at their disposal. Often there remain hanging on the larvae that have come to light again some algal flakes, which entangle themselves especially at the extremities easily, and I now wanted to see whether this can lead to a permanent settlement of the algae.

In the one glass, accordingly, firmly-grown [mats], and in a third glass freely-floating mats [Watten] of *Oedogonium undulatum*, were offered to alga-free dragonfly-larvae. Into both forms of the algal lawn the larvae buried themselves willingly; but whereas with the freely-floating ones it forms almost the rule that the dragonfly-larvae load a part of it onto themselves during the winding-through, with the fixed-attached ones this succeeds for them much more seldom, since here the filaments [Fäden], if they are to remain hanging, must self-evidently first be torn out [ausgerissen].

So far as the small material now permitted to be seen, the following behaviour ensued. In the case of the tearing-out and remaining-hanging of still fixed-attached, juvenile algal-filaments, I could sometimes see these adhere with their rhizoid (possibly under first regenerative new-formation of such a one, when the tearing-off occurred) on rough, bumpy places of the chitinous-armour, and in such manner produce a sparse, but from the very outset, in relatively considerable length of their solid-constituents, appearing vegetation. In the case of the loading-on [Aufladens] already of freely-swimming, older algal-stages, these fell off at first, seemingly without leaving a trace [Spur] behind; but many such dragonflies [Libellen], which were isolated, nevertheless received after the elapse [nach Ablauf] of 9 to 10 days a green film, which developed into the same, though from the outset short, lawn [Rasen]. The conclusion to be drawn from these observations should first be discussed in the following chapter. Fleetingly would only the question be touched upon, whether, in the mode of settlement of algae on dragonfly-larvae under discussion — [larvae] that creep through free-living algal thickets — an intentionality of the larvae is to be thought of, such as observations and experiments (cf. the lawns dug up anew [wiederum gegrabenen Rasen] of *Stiassny*) on certain triangular-stages [Dreieckstadien] have taught? I could obtain for myself no certainty about it, but believe I must negate the question raised.

b) **Transfer of another Species:** I repeated experiment 8a, only utilised this time not *Oedogonium undulatum*, but rather other species more nearly or more distantly related to it.

Merely the settlement [Besiedelung] of *Aeschna cyanea* with *Oedogonium capillare* succeeded to a certain degree, yet not yet of long duration, nor did it ever gain the appearance of a luxuriantly proliferating, healthy culture. *Spirogyra*, *Cladophora*, *Vaucheria* on the other hand, also such forms which grow with predilection on animal substrate, on snail- and mussel-shells and crustaceans, neither contrived to hold themselves on *Aeschna-*, nor on other dragonfly- and *Dytiscus*-larvae, but rather separated themselves again at once from the just-so-allotted symbionts.

9th Experiment: **Maceration of alga-overgrown and not alga-overgrown larval-skins and cadavers [Kadaver].** a) **Maceration of whole, dead larvae:** The last, alga-bearing dragonfly-larvae I lost through death in the giant inundation [Riesenüberschwemmung] from Frauenberg to Vienna occurring on the 31. VII. 06, and indeed before they yet [lost] their algae. With their cadavers — they were five in all — I undertook it to compare the decomposition-speed of alga-overgrown and not alga-overgrown larvae, that is, dragonfly-larvae. Self-evidently I had as control-objects an equal number of the latter, which I had carefully stored for this purpose, at my disposal. The cadavers lay in the same element-glasses in which they had also found themselves in the living state; yet the water, which through the decomposition of the larvae took up larger quantities of carbon dioxide and other gases etc. might exhibit, was poured away and replaced through high-spring-water [Hochquellenwasser].

In the alga-free water the bodies [Körper] lay still after 10 days indeed somewhat swollen up, but otherwise of unchanged appearance, although they, as the odour of the water proved, had already passed strongly into decay. In alga-containing water on the contrary the cadavers were, after the elapse of the indicated time, already disintegrated into coarser pieces: namely, the head, the thoracic rings [Thoracalringe] and their appendages, as well as the abdomen [Hinterleib] as a whole, were separated; individual fragments of the extremities lay alongside. All parts were covered by luxuriantly proliferating lawn [Rasen] of the *Oedogonium undulatum*, the rot-odour [Fäulnisgeruch] was a minimal one.

b) **Maceration of shed moults [Häutungen]:** Skins [Häute], of which alga-free and alga-overgrown dragonfly-larvae had occasionally divested themselves at their periodic integument-change, of which the latter still bore the algal lawn, were further observed in the same manner. As already noted in the descriptive chapter, the algae do indeed loosen themselves, a few days after the larval-skin had become ownerless, likewise loose from it and form freely-swimming mats [Watten]; but in spite of that the maceration of the skins which had harboured the algae proceeds incomparably more rapidly. Within five weeks of these only small chitin-shreds [Chitinfetzen] were still present, the former swam completely unchanged in the glass.

About the uptake of organic constituents into algae very little is known. I should not like to imagine to myself that the described result in cases a and b may directly be traced back to the activity of the algae. Rather, the presence of the algae might indirectly have offered a greater quantity of bacteria the conditions-of-existence by virtue of which they could attend more rapidly to the destruction of the died-off animal remains. The microscopic finding does not contradict this assumption. Besides, the richness in oxygen in the alga-glass may have directly strengthened and accelerated the oxidative processes.

## IV. Conclusion [Schlußfolgerung].

We can now, on the basis of the direct observations, which we besides compare with those on other symbiosis-cases, and on the basis of the experiments, determine the individual points of the mutual utility-relationship [Nutzverhältnis] of *Aeschna cyanea* and *Oedogonium undulatum*.

### A. Advantages [Vorteile] which the dragonfly-larvae enjoy from the algae.

1) **Promotion of the animal respiratory activity [Respirationstätigkeit]**, which can thereby also be maintained abundantly even when carbonic acid (soda-water experiment), ammonia and other harmful-things or poisonous admixtures of the water (dirt- and soap-water experiments) would, under ordinary circumstances, make it impossible. Significant therein appears also the circumstance that the mouth-opening-place [Ausmündungsstelle] of the gut [Darmes], in which the tracheal gills [Tracheenkiemen] are hidden and by means of which the pump-movements carried on by the peristalsis stand in connection with the outer water, is surrounded by especially rich algal-hanging [Algenbehang].

An analogous case of this respiration-promotion through the algae we find, after the findings of Hadži, in *Hydra viridis*. I cannot quite believe Knauer when he, reporting on the Hadži experiments, says: »Where the brown and green arm-polyps are abundantly present, these would certainly steer any possible strong carbonic-acid-formation. The observations in the open have yielded that in bad, spoiled water the green arm-polyps die off much more rapidly than the brown ones. There can thus be no talk of a real promotion of the respiration of the green hydras through their green-algae.« To this is to be held in opposition that *Hydra viridis* in our case — there are dragonfly-larva-inhabited waters which, be it from natural grounds (light-deficiency, rock-bottom and the like), be it from artificial causes (the activity of the washerwomen in ours, factory waste-waters and the like in other cases), are devoid of plant-growth. So are admittedly free of submerged plants, on the Sparbach, the waters of the princely *Liechtenstein* animal-park near Sparbach (Lower Austria), where *Hydra viridis* is usually to be found only in masses, but *H. fusca* is absent. Finally is to be pointed out the particular regularity of the polyp-occurrence upon the freely downward-hanging roots of *Lemna* in the water, which *Hydra viridis* is usually wont to harbour in massed number; yet the and the whole broad surface of all *Lemna*-exemplars taken together darkens the water beneath it, so that in it the submerged plants become displaced in measure of the progressing extension of the *Lemna*-raft [Lemna-Flosses]. Only a few algae, especially undemanding in respect of the photochemical climate, can yet eke out their life and exercise in the twilight a modest assimilation-activity, among others most probably also the *Zoochlorella conductrix* resident in the *Hydra*-entoderm. That precisely it is not very light-needing comes out clearly enough from the evidence of Hadži's dark-experiment: the most other algae would, with darkening over a duration of 6 weeks, whether now the water be aerated or not, have perished.

2) **Keeping-off [Abhaltung] of ecto-parasites.** Especially the saprolegnias, but also animal parasites [Schmarotzer] from the classes of the nematodes and flagellates do not tolerate the intense oxygen-atmosphere which the algae spread in their surroundings (cf. also Kammerer, 1904, S. 185). Besides, they mostly befall only such animals as are, from some ground or other, weakened, injured, or otherwise sick. The alga-overgrown dragonfly-larvae, however, are — already for the sake of the other advantages which come to them from their symbionts — always especially resistant individuals.

3) **Masking of the body-form [Körperform] for protection against enemies** and for the better stalking of the prey. It was several times mentioned that the algal mantle completely conceals the form of the insect-larva. On younger stages, on which it is still lacking, it lends to it at least an inconspicuous green colour. This admittedly stands out strongly from the surroundings in the otherwise plant-empty dwelling-water; yet this is by no means a hindrance to its acting protectively and concealingly: if the larva sits still, it counterfeits a bundle of algae growing on a stone; but even when it moves slowly, one believes far rather that the green water-current [Wasserstrom] is driving onward a shred of torn-off algae than that a voluntary locomotion conceals itself thereunder. This is indeed also the reason why one sees other green — and even other, if only not conspicuously coloured — animals so hard to see even in an absurdly green-coloured surrounding. So they must by no means resemble their surroundings, but only some arbitrary object (stones, herbage, piece of bark, etc.) in colour, which one expects to encounter in the surrounding in question. ## B. Advantages which the algae enjoy from the dragonfly larvae.

### 1) Promotion of the plant's assimilatory activity.

Through the intimate connection that symbiosis brings about between plant and animal in cases of mutual interlocking, the well-known reciprocal relationship between plant assimilation and animal respiration rises far above the level of the ordinary turnover of carbon dioxide and oxygen, as is everywhere the case where animals and plants occur side by side. Thus in our case the body covering of the dragonfly larvae and their secretions constitute, at least indirectly, a favorable source of nourishment for the algae, as emerges from the experiments on the maceration of the cast-off, algae-grown skins. Here the circumstance shall once again be especially emphasized that a growth-centre is found in the immediate surroundings of the anus — here even defying the abrasion at the parts of the anal circumference lying ventrally, which otherwise allows no luxuriant turf-growth to come up over the whole underside. The last-mentioned fact does indeed compel one to assume that, through the manuring of the algae by the excrement emerging from the anus, a stimulation of their growth takes place, and that precisely thereby, in immediate reciprocity, the services rendered are able quite particularly to support, at that place, the respiration of the larvae.

Besides this, the algae might also draw advantage from the mobility of the dragonfly larvae. Just as is the case with the "parasite"-actinia attached firmly to the pagurid shell, the dragonfly larvae drag their symbionts, which lack any capacity for voluntary movement, into ever new nutrient medium; they also bring the algae — apart from their own excrement — still thereby valuable manure, in that they tear loose, by their swimming movements, particles of sludge and pieces of bank, etc., from the bottom, and whirl them up.

### 2) Provision of convenient attachment points.

The roughnesses of the chitinous integument, in particular the edges at the boundary of the individual segments, then the lateral spines on the four last abdominal rings, offer the algal rhizoid easier points of attachment than do most bodies otherwise present in fresh waters, and certainly than all bodies present in the home water. As an analogue to this I should like to mention the outgrowths of the carapace of many oxyrrhynchous crabs, of which some are interpreted as special arrangements for the attachment of the algae (Stiasny) — a view to which, for the present case at least, I by no means intend to subscribe.

### 3) Protection against enemies.

Some vegetarians and omnivores of fresh water enjoy with particular relish the sap-green algal turfs. This becomes quite impossible so long as the turf grows on the body of a living dragonfly larva, unless this latter is itself overpowered together with the algae-enemy. But what animal — apart, say, from a strong predatory fish or a very large, greedy water-frog, which only exceptionally (in quite shallow water) comes into the picture — would be capable, or even willing, to attack the well-armed, malicious, armored dragonfly larva? I have repeatedly wished to use dragonfly larvae as fodder, but always with negative success. Many a creature lusting after the palatable algae, e.g. a tadpole, a snail reaching far out of its shell, would thereby itself fall prey to the greedy dragonfly larva. And it may happen that the latter in fact also makes prey in this way through the mediation of the algae, which are in a sense used as bait. So long as the dragonfly larva remains motionless, it would even be conceivable that one or another animal begins to graze on the algal turf, especially in a body of water where, as in our case, there is otherwise no proper aquatic vegetation present. But since I could not myself observe such an occurrence, I have also not mentioned it in the enumeration of the advantages afforded by the algae, but confine myself in a parenthesis to its hypothetical indication. Finally, even granting that an algae-eater whom the dragonfly larva is unable to intimidate should venture near, the alga will still be able to draw benefit from the capacities of its bearer, in that the latter, on the approach of a larger animal, takes flight and, by virtue of its not-to-be-underestimated swiftness, is able, together with its symbiont, certainly often to escape the pursuit. — — The establishment of the fact that the dragonfly larvae like to burrow into detritus-heaps, whereby they manure their algae, leads us to a conjecture as to how the described symbiosis might have come about at all. Already in the introduction I remarked that the algae-grown dragonfly larvae give the impression as though they had merely crept through an algal felt in which they had become entangled and which they now drag about with them for a while against their will. Through experiment 8 b, which demonstrates the possibility of a real algal settlement on the larvae having come about through such getting-caught, it is suggested that in this way at least the first algal settlements on dragonfly larvae take place. The larvae force their way through a tuft of *Oedogonium* sitting fixed on another substrate, tearing loose in the process a number of filaments, some of which are able to attach themselves and multiply on the new substrate, which is for this purpose very suitable owing to its roughnesses. Still more probability is brought along by the assumption that the process occurs more rarely with fixed algae — which are not easily torn loose by the larvae and then for their own part remain adhering only in the minority — than with loose felts of already free-swimming *Oedogonia*, which have reached their final stage and whose spores have found a hold on the new substrate.

The occasion to creep about in sludge and algae is now, in the body of water where I discovered the algae-grown slender young larvae (and where, as indicated at the outset, the first-named hiding opportunity, the sludge, is scarcely present), a particularly abundant one; the disturbance so often repeated there, the stirring up and clouding of the small basin on the occasion of its use by the washerwomen, are pressing compulsions to frequent flight. That in fact the regular, in the truest sense of the word "deep-reaching" effects of the human hand did not leave the animal and plant life of the pond uninfluenced, but on the contrary destroyed it down to scanty remnants, has likewise already been reported in the introduction. It might appear striking that among these remnants there are found, as enumerated in the introduction, also the *Gammarus*, known to be in need of oxygen. As I have however convinced myself, their actual dwelling-water is not the pond itself but its feeder brook, from which they reach the pond by the current and there partly perish in consequence of the unfavorable conditions of life — I found many carcasses! — partly represent the most important, almost the sole source of nourishment of the dragonfly larvae. The other representatives of the pond fauna — so far as I could see, only the *Limnaea peregra* and *Succinea putris* already named in the introduction — have the special peculiarity of withdrawing, by flight onto land, from the harmful substances that temporarily make their way into the water: *Succinea* is in any case more an air- than a water-animal, and the *Limnaeae* creep up on the stones in masses a few centimeters above the water surface, where they remain adhering until the water has again been able to clear itself somewhat.

Thus the conclusion is by no means too bold, that only those slender young larvae were able to maintain themselves there which had procured for themselves an algal turf in good time — in early spring and late autumn, according to the statement of reliable people, algal felts are also said to occur in that pond, otherwise almost algae-free at my observation time, felts which are exposed only in the "season" to more rapid destruction —, and vice versa only those algae remained which were dragged along in good time by dragonfly larvae, so as then, through the hurried swimming movements of their bearers, to escape being scoured and washed off by flight into the crevices of the wall.

This conclusion, justified on the basis of the experiments, affords an insight into the selective force of natural breeding [Naturzüchtung], as it doubtless actually mirrors itself in countless examples: that natural breeding which indeed possesses the power to exterminate the unsuitable, but not to enhance the given suitable, already because it is not always feasible to claim for it those enormous spans of time which biology always had to presuppose for selection as a form-producing motive force.

In the case of the dragonfly larvae, however, the whole process of adaptation to the living-together with the algae must most probably mirror itself anew from year to year. The region is rich in standing waters in which the ready-formed dragonflies disport themselves and into which they lay their eggs; but only a single pool lets larvae arise from the eggs which combine with algae. It is not to be assumed that the dragonflies arising year after year from the symbiotic larvae will always remain faithful to the selfsame pool for egg-laying, especially since one sees them undertake great migrations and predatory expeditions into waterless areas; just as little is it to be assumed that dragonflies which do not descend from other pools and have developed out of algae-free larvae occasionally reach our pool from those waters and choose it for the act of reproduction. The instinct of combining with algae can therefore not be hereditarily fixed in the symbiotic larvae, but must in any case be newly acquired from year to year under the compulsion of the direct and function-altering external factors prevailing in the body of water concerned.

More tenable would be the assumption that the adaptation to the substrate of the larval body is already hereditarily innate in the alga, for which experiment Nr. 6—8 do in fact offer some handles. Thus the dragonfly larva might, on its adaptation-path to be newly traversed each time, experience a support coming to meet it from the alga. As I learned through inquiries, the use of our dragonfly-pond on the part of the laundry dates already from a series of years back — at least one, perhaps even two decades it has already been in use for the said purpose. Thus a span of time which, although still standing in no relation to the periods which, according to the hitherto prevailing conceptions, are required for the carrying-out of a variation, yet, as experience teaches us, fully suffices to bring about, by direct and functional means, an ecological, indeed even a morphological adaptation-phenomenon.

In exactly demonstrated examples of deep-reaching transformations within intervals of few years and less there is today no lack any more: Przibram observed (1902) intra-individual carapax-variations in crabs, and within about a month or a moulting-period the segmentation, armoring, and pigmentation of the abdomen of moulted hermit crabs (1907); I myself produced within 1 to 2 years in captivity the melanistic varieties of various lizards which also occur in the wild (1907 b); in the case of the alpine newts and the midwife toad I succeeded, within 2 to 3 years, in transforming the whole reproductive type and at the same time important physiological processes in the organism, which transformations were inherited by the offspring over a further 3 to 4 years (1907 c).

I should therefore like to set it up as downright highly probable that the symbiosis between dragonfly larvae and algae arose in immediate connection with the laundry-rinsing in the pond concerned. Its cultural use, the associated whipping-up of the water, releases the fear- and flight-movements of the dragonfly larvae; thus these, in their endeavor to take refuge, reach more frequently into the algal thicket, which through settlement of young filaments and germination of dispersed spores has settled on the new nutrient-soil; here for the first time they overgrow the animal at the appropriate time of year, the larva-body otherwise rough and grippable for the alga only at the leading edges. Should the alga in fact combine itself in this way with the larva, it gains in the latter — apart from the advantages discussed above — a further protection in St. Margarethen-bad: it shares the dwelling-water, externally exhibiting no algal cover but a green instead of a blackish-gray *Aeschna*-larva, with the *Anax*-larvae, an association into which the green could be introduced — a possibility which, as already indicated in the introduction, would be founded on the bodily interior — so it would thereby gain a further support concerning the origin of our symbiosis-case. If the assumption is correct, then it follows further from it that the observed symbiosis-phenomenon could enjoy only a slight distribution, indeed remained restricted, perhaps precisely to that single locality where I found it.

Natural science has made us acquainted with a whole series of animal forms which, with a certain regularity, carry about with them an algal turf grown firmly upon them: certain crabs and molluscs one even finds predominantly covered with definite algal species. To my knowledge, up to now only the cases of intracellular living-together of animals and plants have been conceived of as symbiosis, but not the cases of exosomatic algal settlement. For many such occurrences, as e.g. the algal vegetations on turtle-shells, mussel-shells, and snail-shells, the conception here stressed is indeed scarcely justified; here we shall presumably have to establish only a case of dwelling-parasitism [Wohnungsparasitismus], in which the alga represents the parasite, the animal the host, whereby it is striking that constantly, e.g., only certain Vaucheriaceae settle on the shells of the marsh operculate-snail (*Vivipara vera*), others likewise on those of the large plate-snail (*Planorbis corneus*).

The treatment of the symbiosis-case of dragonfly larvae and *Oedogonium* may, however, give occasion for the consideration whether the laying-out and tending of an algal garden, voluntarily practised by many crab species on their carapax (cf. on this the compilation of Stiasny), is not to be registered as genuine symbiosis or at least as mutualism. According to the investigations hitherto, this peculiar association appears as parasitism, wherein, however, it is not, as in the case of the mollusc-shells, the alga, but rather the crab that represents the parasite and the alga the host, on account of the advantages enjoyed by the former. Whether, however, the algae too receive from the crabs benefactions [Guttaten], roughly corresponding to those which the slender young larvae render to their *Oedogonia* — this question would certainly be accessible to, and worth, experimental examination; its answer would perhaps secure for the special case described in the present treatise a more general biological significance.

## V. Summary.

1) A small bricked-up pool which serves for laundry-cleaning and is therefore otherwise quite poor in organic life, harbors larvae of *Aeschna cyanea*, the *Oedogonium undulatum* in symbiosis with them. This *Fadenalge* [filamentous alga] grows principally on the upper side and on the After [anus] of the dragonfly larvae; it leaves the eyes and devouring-tools free and on the whole underside — with the exception of the anal region — sparse (abrasion and lack of light).

2) At the periodic moultings of the dragonfly larvae the algal turf comes off as well; but early replacement is provided for, partly through transfer from other dragonfly larvae, partly by the fact that components of the old turf, shortly before the moult, reach the new integument through the clefts of the integument to be stripped off.

3) Even originally quite algae-free dragonfly larvae of the same species can become infected with algae of the same species through the neighborhood of larvae already grown with algae; furthermore the algal settlement proceeds in such a way that dragonfly larvae, on creeping through (more rarely fixed, mostly free-swimming) algal felts, carry off particles thereof, which become stable upon them.

4) The advantages which the symbionts receive from one another are the following:

a) On the side of the algae: promotion of assimilation (transport into fresh nutrient medium, manure through faeces and burrowing into the sludge); offering of convenient attachment points at the chitin-edges and lateral spines; protection against enemies (through defense and flight).

b) On the side of the dragonfly larvae: promotion of respiration (oxygen-excretion on the part of the algae, especially in the surroundings of the anus, the rectal-gill region), through which the larvae are put into the position to defy strong contaminations (soap- and dirty-water-experiment) and accumulations of carbonic acid (soda-water-experiment); warding-off of animal and plant ectoparasites (saprolegnia-experiment), which cannot exist in the concentrated oxygen-atmosphere of the algal precinct; masking for protection against enemies and for the sake of more productive predatory raids.

5) The stripped-off skins of the dragonfly larvae remain covered with algae for a short time longer and fall victim to an exceedingly rapid maceration, as do likewise any whole dragonfly larvae that may have perished: out of the (immediately, or through bacterial mediation effected?) rapid clearing-away of the carcasses a further advantage may under circumstances accrue to the dragonfly larvae, which are crowded together in masses on a relatively small volume of water. The *Oedogonia* loosening themselves from skins and carcasses form little felts in which lively sexual reproduction takes place, a sign that for them now less favorable conditions prevail than in the stage of their association with the larvae.

6) The advantages procured by the algae for the dragonfly larvae, those of promotion of respiration and combating of parasites, turn into their opposite as soon as not enough daylight is present, in which the algae can assimilate carbon dioxide.

7) But the considerable advantages which as a rule really come to be offered make it appear comprehensible that neither the algae in question maintain themselves as well on other substrates, nor the larvae in question without their algae, as when both are combined. The pure culture of the algae is therefore difficult and succeeds only on an animal, not on an inorganic or vegetable nutrient-soil, and the originally algae-bearing larvae, being adapted to a higher oxygen content, are without their symbionts very feeble.

8) The genesis of the present symbiosis-case is to be understood out of the cultural conditions of the dwelling-water (hitherto the only locality of occurrence): the violent water-movement brought about during the rinsing drives the dragonfly larvae to flight into algal thickets, and indeed already in spring, when such are present at all (other safe hiding-corners do not exist there), out of which algal thickets they tear off portions on coming forth again. This leads to an algal settlement on the larval bodies, which protects the algae from spoilage, because the dragonfly larvae withdraw their symbionts from the mechanical effects which, on the advance of the season and the said use of the water, destroy the rest of the algal vegetation of the basin; and it protects the dragonfly larvae themselves, because the latter are only thereby able to defy the temporary soap-content.
