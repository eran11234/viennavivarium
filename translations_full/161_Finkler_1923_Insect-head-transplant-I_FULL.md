# Head Transplantation in Insects.

## III. The Influence of the Replanted Head on the Colour Dress of Other Body Parts.

By

Walter Finkler.

*(From the Biological Experimental Institute of the Academy of Sciences in Vienna [Zoological Division]¹).)*

*(Received on 13 October 1922.)*

*Archiv für mikroskopische Anatomie und Entwicklungsmechanik*, vol. 99 (1923).

> **Full translation.** A complete English rendering of the running text of “Head Transplantation in Insects (I)” (Finkler, 1923), including all tables, figure and plate legends, and footnotes. Numbers and table cells were transcribed from the page images, not the noisy OCR.

In the first communication the possibility and technique of head transplantation were described. This work shall therefore confine itself solely to the results of the head exchange of differently coloured animals. In order to test the influence of the head, or rather of the eyes, upon the colour dress of the remaining body parts, the transplantation was carried out between differently coloured individuals of one and the same species (homoioplastic transplantation) and also between members of different species (heteroplastic transplantation). The homoioplastic transplantation was almost throughout alleloplastic, because the over-grafting was undertaken between (colour-)races.

The objects of the *homoioplastic* grafting were:

*Notonecta glauca* L., back-swimmer,
*Dixippus morosus* Burm., stick insect,
*Tenebrio molitor* L., meal beetle [mealworm beetle] (larvae),
*Vanessa io* L., peacock butterfly (pupae),
*Vanessa urticae* L., small tortoiseshell (pupae).

For the *heteroplastic* transplantation the following species were used:

*Hydrophilus piceus* Geoff., the pitch-black "water beetle"²) + *Dytiscus marginalis* L., great diving beetle.

*Notonecta glauca* (the unpigmented) back-swimmer + *Notonecta marmorea* (the marbled) back-swimmer.

*Vanessa io*, peacock butterfly + *Vanessa urticae*, small tortoiseshell.

For the presentation of the experiments it is expedient to retain the above division, and here too I shall follow it uniformly in the experiments.

### 1. *Hydrophilus piceus* and *Dytiscus marginalis*.

As the name *piceus* already says, the water beetle is pitch-black over the entire body. An insignificant exception, which moreover does not interest one further here, is formed by certain body appendages, such as feelers, maxillary palps, etc. Seldom does one encounter individuals whose wing-covers opalesce slightly. As simple and constant as the colour dress of the *Hydrophilus* is, so variable is that of the *Dytiscus*. Its chitin is essentially lighter coloured and appears brown in diffuse light, gold-gleaming in sunlight. The pronotum is bordered by a lemon- to ochre-yellow stripe. In some species of *Dytiscus* the lower (acranial) or middle transverse stripe is absent. The bordered diving beetle, *Dytiscus marginalis*, always has all of them. Only it have I used for the experiments. The marginal stripes vary not only in the intensity of the colour but also in their breadth. They also do not remain alike during the life of the single individual. In one large aquarium, into which all the freshly caught beetles came, and whose water became turbid through feed remnants and other contaminations, after months I found only animals whose marginal stripes, though still visible, were bleached and blurred, although the greater number of the animals at the time of insertion into the aquarium showed beautifully yellow stripes clearly set off against the dark chitin. This observation I completed by an experiment, in that I kept the *Dytiscus* under coloured (blue and yellow) bell-jars. Although I made a contrary selection before the experimental arrangement — the strongly bordered ones being placed under the blue and the weakly bordered ones under the yellow bell — I could confirm the above observation: the largest number — admittedly not all — of the beetles located under the yellow bell exhibited an increase in intensity and breadth of the margins, while those under the blue bell paled. In general, after the experiment's conclusion (5 March to 20 June), the occupants of both colour-bells were equally bordered. Since the imago of *Dytiscus* now unexpectedly proved to be colour-inducible, I also tested the influence of blinding. The beetles did not survive the electrocautic destruction of the eyes at all. I achieved better results through extirpation with scissors. Here too, as with head transplantation, contact of the wound with water before its closure is to be prevented. The small percentage of the survivors showed, after about 20 days, a clear change of the overall colouration — I would call it the blinding colour: the marginal stripes bleach out, their boundaries blur, the chitin loses its earlier lustre, but retains the brown colour. The bleached animals were kept in small glass aquaria under medium illumination. The water was always kept clear; the colour change is thus not to be traced back to outer illumination influences.

The head exchange between water beetle and diving beetle was carried out reciprocally. The functional uptake of the head was complete in both cases. The *Dytiscus* head on *Hydrophilus* had no influence whatever on the colour of the transplant-bearer. The animal lived over a month. The pitch-black *Hydrophilus* colour remained unaltered. It was otherwise when the *Hydrophilus* head was grafted onto the yellow-margin-bearer. At an accelerated tempo the above-described blinding colour set in. After a further week the marginal stripes had already completely vanished. Only at the lateral margins did faint traces show themselves. The chitin not only became lustreless, but also took on the deep-black colour of the transplant. This colour state held for 10 days¹). In *Dytiscus* to which blinded *Hydrophilus* heads were replanted, the re-colouration stopped at the blinding colour. Thus the organ activating the colour change is here too, as in the cold-blooded vertebrates, the eye. The keeping of all the animals used in this experimental series was alike: until the wound closure in moist chambers (s. I. communication), thereafter individually in preserving-jars with gravel bottom and pure water. Also all vessels were set up at a uniformly, moderately lit place.

### 2. *Notonecta glauca* and *Notonecta marmorea*.

The head transplantation experiments on the back-swimmer are so organically connected with a colour-induction experiment on the same hemipterans that I must anticipate a brief description of this experiment from a later communication on it, in order to make possible the understanding of the experiments presented in the following. While otherwise all aquatic animals are coloured dark on the back and light on the belly, the back-swimmer, *N. glauca*, forms an exception to this rule. His wings are light and unpigmented, his belly-side is dark. In popular writings, whose authors have a particularly fine nose for all possible and impossible teleological explanations, as well as in zoological textbooks etc., it is always pointed out how expedient this colour distribution is. Before the enemy's eye located above, most aquatic animals are "protectively-

coloured", because their dark back does not stand out from the dark ground. In the back-swimmer, by contrast, it is the belly which, coloured dark, exerts the same protective effect. Seen from below, the light belly-side, or rather back-side, again cannot be distinguished from the bright sky. I will not enter into the disputed question of the expediency, of its origin or of its existence. Only this much may be said: that in fish a re-colouration was produced by reversed illumination, that is, by external influence. Something similar I achieved in *Notonecta glauca*. The experimental animals were kept in a glass tub that was covered light-tight on all sides and on top. Only from below was light reflected through a mirror into the container. After 2 months the wing-covers were pigmented by the constant irradiation and resembled those of *Notonecta marmorea*. En passant be it remarked that I find *Notonecta glauca* predominantly in pools with turbid water or dark, muddy bottom, but *Notonecta marmorea* in clear ponds with a light, reflecting bottom.

The reciprocal exchange of the heads of the two *Notonecta* species remained without influence. The wings retained their colour, no matter which head the body bore. I now transplanted the head of an experimentally pigmented *Notonecta glauca* onto an uninfluenced conspecific. The *glauca* wings became pigmented, although no abnormal illumination was used. Here too the pigmentation resembled that of the *marmorea* wing, only the wing was not completely pigmented. This influence of the transplanted head showed itself without exception in all (12) operated animals.

That the *Notonecta glauca* wing is easily pigmentable is also brought out by the fact that, when placed in tyrosine and tyrosinase, it discolours into a typical *marmorata* wing.

For the understanding of the various reaction of the *glauca* body to a "normal *marmorata*" head and to an "experimentally *marmorata*" head, the following consideration may serve, which, however, is not further [experimentally tested]. We must imagine the role of the eye as activating the colour change. The eye of *Notonecta glauca*, whose wings pigment precisely through illumination, is active. Even transplanted onto another individual (with the rest of the head), it continues to exert its influence for the time. Not so the eye of the *Notonecta marmorea* found in nature. It exerts no influence at all on the body colour, or rather exerts no influence *any more*. Between *Notonecta marmorea* and *Notonecta glauca* there thus seems to exist a similar relationship to that which *Kammerer* gives for *Salamandra maculosa*, *forma typica* and *forma taeniata*. Here too the former spotted form seems to be transformed through illumination influences into the second striped form. Illuminating for the theoretical understanding of the results of the above-described experiments on *Notonecta* are the gonad transplantations on the salamander types. While the ovary of a spotted (*typica*) salamander remains uninfluenced in the body of a [naturally] found striped (*taeniata*) [animal], an experimentally produced *taeniata* female with a *maculosa* ovary also gives birth to striped young. The body influences the gonads only at the time when it is itself influenced, or shortly thereafter, but no longer when the re-tuning, or rather the equal-tuning, of the somatic and germ plasm has already been accomplished, as in the naturally striped salamander.

### 3. *Dixippus morosus*.

The Indian stick insect, *Dixippus morosus*, occurs in various colour modifications, which, according to *Przibram* and *Brecher* (1922), can be produced experimentally through keeping in colour-lined containers. In darkness they become green. This colour they also take on, according to my experience, when they are decapitated¹). We can thus address it as a blinding colour. The results of the head-exchange between the different colour modifications were as follows: a) Green stick insects to which a brown head was replanted retain at first the green (blinding-)colour. After two weeks, by which time the deficiency symptoms²) had gradually disappeared, the body becomes brown first at some places, later everywhere. It takes on the colour of the head, which has remained unchanged. The re-colouration proceeds from the thorax and spreads over the whole abdomen.

b) When heads are transplanted from green onto brown individuals, the body takes on the green blinding colour, which, however, in that case remains preserved.

c) Upon replantation of *brown* heads onto black bodies, the latter first takes on the green blinding colour. After the healing-in period the body becomes brown again and grows ever darker, until the colour of the head is reached.

A change of the head colour was never observed.

The experiments succeed in the manner indicated both with larvae and with imagines. However, I mainly used the latter for this, because the larvae always died of moulting complications.

It is interesting that the animals with transplanted head can still lay eggs. I have, however, not yet examined an eventual influence of the same through the differently coloured head.

### 4. *Tenebrio molitor*.

Quite simply and clearly the results of the head-exchange at the larvae of the meal beetle, the "mealworms", are to be read off. Here there are only two colour-types. Light and dark. The "light" ones are easily distinguished, through the transparent chitin, from the freshly moulted larvae of the dark type, which are also white to yellowish.

As already mentioned, not the head alone, but also some segments are co-extirpated and transplanted.

The "mealworms" too always took on the colour of the head-part. A light head-part grafted onto a brown body produces there, within a few days, a light colouration. A light larva with a brown head always takes on a uniform colouration that corresponds to the colour of the new head-part.

The colour changes must always be observed in living animals, because dead or dying animals become completely darkened and can simulate a false result.

### 5. *Vanessa io*, *Vanessa urticae*.

The colour dress of certain butterfly-pupae is determined by the surrounding colour at the time of pupation and remains unaltered in normal animals. *Brecher* could determine the role of the eye of the caterpillar for the colour of the pupa. *Przibram* described the effect of the decapitation of the caterpillar on the pupal colouration. But it always showed itself that the colour dress of the pupa remains unaltered after the colouring-out and cannot be influenced or changed by any external influences. All the more remarkable are the results of head transplantation on pupae, especially since here we do not know what at the head-part can produce an influence on the rest of the body. As will be explained somewhat later, this cannot be a matter of a free diffusion of dyestuffs from the head into the body¹).

The body takes on the colour of the transplanted head-part. Not entirely; first it is the antennal flagella on which the transplant colour appears. Then the projecting places of the pupal skin are coloured in the sense of the head. As the last stage, the re-colouration of the wing-sheath appears. The remaining parts of the pupa mostly remain unaltered. These results can be achieved with both homoio- and heteroplastic union.

An exception is formed by the gold-pupae of *Vanessa urticae*. Its head can call forth no metallic lustre in matt pupae of the same species. Only a slight lightening occurs.

However, a matt-coloured head can make the metallic lustre of a gold-pupa disappear. In the disappearance of the metallic lustre too, the same sequence is noticed: antennal flagella, projecting places, and wings.

For these results on butterfly-pupae I can as yet give no explanation.

### Summary.

While a functional replantation of the insect eye alone presented great difficulties, the replantation of whole heads by means of the autophoric method enabled me to test the role of the eye for the colouration. As already reported earlier, head transplantation succeeds both between different races and between different species.

The heads were exchanged between:

1. *Hydrophilus piceus* Geoff. and *Dytiscus marginalis* L.
2. *Notonecta glauca* and *marmorea* L.
3. *Dixippus morosus* Burm.
4. *Tenebrio molitor* larvae.
5. *Vanessa io* and 5a. *Vanessa urticae* L.

1. *Dytiscus marginalis* has on the upper surface of the thorax lemon- to yolk-yellow marginal stripes, which blur after extirpation of the eyes. After transplantation of a *Hydrophilus* head the yellow marginal stripe disappears completely. The formerly brown, shining chitin becomes black and matt.

2. *Notonecta marmorea* is distinguished from *Notonecta glauca* by the pigmentation of the wing-covers. The head of *Notonecta marmorea* transplanted onto *Notonecta glauca* exerts no influence on the unpigmented wing-covers of *Notonecta glauca*. But if the head of a *Notonecta glauca*, whose wing-covers had been experimentally pigmented, is transplanted onto a normal *Notonecta glauca*, then the transplant is able to call forth colouration and marking on the unpigmented *Notonecta glauca*.

3. The stick insects occur in various colour modifications, namely in greenish, green, brown and black. Through culture in darkness the animals become green; this colour is also produced by blinding. When heads are transplanted from green onto brown individuals, the body takes on the green blinding colour, which remains permanent. Green stick insects to which a brown head was replanted retain at first the green (blinding-)colour and then become brown after two weeks. Upon replantation of brown heads onto black bodies, the latter takes on at first the green blinding colour for two weeks and then becomes brown.

4. At the larvae of the meal beetle the head was exchanged together with a few segments of the thorax between light-yellow and brown specimens. The larvae always took on a uniform colouration, which corresponded to the colour of the new head-part.

5. The colour dress of certain butterfly-pupae is determined by the surrounding colour at the time of pupation and remains preserved unaltered in normal animals. When head-parts were replanted from light onto dark modifications of the same kind, or conversely, it showed itself that first the distal antennal flagella remaining on the stock, later various other parts of the pupa, took on the colouring of the head. Heads of gold-pupae of *Vanessa urticae* could indeed call forth no metallic lustre at matt pupae of the same kind; however this disappeared at formerly gold-shining pupae through transplantation of a matt-coloured head.
