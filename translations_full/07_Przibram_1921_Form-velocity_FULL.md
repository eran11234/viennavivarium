# Referat.

Karl Przibram, **Form und Geschwindigkeit. Ein Beitrag zur allgemeinen Morphologie.** Die Naturwissenschaften. Heft 6. S. 103. 6 February 1920.

*Archiv für Entwicklungsmechanik der Organismen*, vol. 48 (1921). A review (*Referat*) of a paper in *Die Naturwissenschaften*.

> **Full translation.** A complete English rendering of the one-page review.

The experimental morphology of animals and plants has often taught us a connection between form-variants and the influence of external factors, whereby local and temporal varieties occurring in nature have found a certain elucidation. However, it has in most cases not been possible to discover a cause for the linkage of form and external factor. It is therefore most gratifying for biology that now, on the part of a physicist, my brother Karl Przibram, a general physical rule is demonstrated which, in its application to organic form-formation, in many examples may reveal to us a causal connection between form and external factor. Twenty years ago Karl Przibram had gained from experiments on electrical discharge the impression that the difference of the ray-figures arising at the negative and positive poles rested on the different velocity with which the discharge spreads out from the two poles, which has recently been proven by O. Pederson through direct measurements. To the greater velocity of propagation at the positive pole corresponds a stronger, finely-rayed branching; to the lesser velocity at the negative pole a less branched, more lumpish form. The same rule is found confirmed by K. Przibram for other physical phenomena of propagation, for crystallogenetic and organogenetic processes. In the two latter cases it is the velocity of growth which, when increased, leads to more elongated, more finely branched forms, and, when diminished, to compressed, lumpish forms with little or no branching.

Growth-accelerating factors, even if they are otherwise drawn from different physical domains, therefore yield elongated modifications: thus exclusion of light, in which growth is accelerated and the formation of assimilates is inhibited, yields the well-known etiolation of plants, which however can also be attained through growth-accelerating temperature elevation. Let us also recall the "long-helmeted" summer form of *Hyalodaphnia*, and the long-tailedness of the "heat"-mice and -rats. Wherever branchings occur in the organic world, these too can often likewise be referred to a greater velocity of growth: thus the finely branched leaves of *Polypodium* lose their fine pinnation on dry, poor soil and pass over into simply deeply pinnatifid ones. Probably the water-leaves of *Ranunculus aquaticus* also owe their extremely fine ramification, as against the round-leaved aerial form, to the more rapid velocity of growth in this element. Schmankewitsch's old experiment on *Artemia salina* can now be referred to the velocity of growth that increases with decreasing salt concentration, whereby a tail-formation similar to that of the freshwater crustacean *Branchipus*, with a deeply cleft end-segment and pinnate end-bristles, is brought forth. Great velocity of growth combined with branching we find also in the regeneration of the dorsal papillae of *Tethys leporina*, in the growth of stags' antlers, also in the likewise annually shed horns of the pronghorn buck, further in many tropical ornamental forms, and so on. Hopefully Karl Przibram will succeed in giving us as well the mathematical formulation of his rule and in elevating it to the rank of a law of nature. In any case the comparisons hitherto communicated merely aphoristically offer a valuable starting point for further investigations in all fields of a "general morphology."

Hans **Przibram.**
