## I. Earlier chemical experiments on pigment formation.

From the work of v. Fürth and Schneider cited below, I take the following concerning the history of pigment research:

Bertrand, building on Yoshida's observations, established that an oxidative "laccase" converts the "laccol," which belongs to the aromatic group and is highly oxidizable, into the well-known lacquer varnish in the air. Oxidative ferments which discolor plant juices in the air are in general very widespread in the plant kingdom. To these belongs the tyrosinase, which Bertrand demonstrated in the fungus genus *Russula*, in dahlias and beet-roots [*Beta*], as oxidatively dyeing tyrosine dark alongside the laccase, and whose action he isolated. Other enzymes dye tyrosine cherry-red and blue-black in the beet. So much for the distribution of plant tyrosinase.

In the animal experiment, the authors mention as a predecessor W. Biedermann, on the action of tyrosinase in the gut of mealworms and of *Tyrosin*.

On the hemolymph of insects, dark-coloring sets in after [the blood] leaves the body. Léon Frédéricq traced this back to oxidation, Krukenberg to the action of carbonic acid. In the ensuing controversy there were brought forth as moments favoring the process namely access of air and the introduction of porous bodies, as well as access of water; as inhibiting moments, saturation of the blood with neutral salts, presence of acids and alkalis, warming to 50° to 70°. Concerning this it is to be remarked that already the botanist Bertrand, in the preparation of tyrosinase-free laccase, used the temperature of 50°—70°. The reaction-duration of the normal lymph was, even within one and the same species (*Hydrophilus piceus*), highly variable.

Here v. Fürth and Schneider connected with their experiments to the hemolymph (henceforth designated for short as blood) of butterfly pupae. Standing fluid began within a few minutes to color darkly at the surface; black clod-like masses then appeared. According to Frédéricq and Krukenberg there follows a partial inhibition of the reaction through alcohol and a complete [inhibition] through sulfuric acid. Adding blood to saturated tyrosine solution shows, in contrast to the weak action upon adding to water, a most intensive, dark-violet discoloration with energetic flake-formation that turns the fluid turbid. With watery tyrosine solution, a quite definite quantity of sodium-carbonate solution (alkali!) promoted the process to a high degree, while other quantities worked inhibitingly. Dilute acetic acid always worked unfavorably; a temperature of at least 30° destroyed the active constituents; ice-box temperature did not inhibit the process. The guaiac reaction was, upon longer standing, dirty dark-green; the Spitzer reaction clear. From all this v. Fürth concluded "that the lepidopteran-blood contains an enzyme which is capable of converting tyrosine, as well as a chromogen occurring in the blood itself, in the presence of oxygen into dark-colored products." v. Fürth succeeded in the separation of the enzyme from the chromogen and from the crystalloid substances of the blood with the help of fractionated salt-precipitation. Only upon addition to a tyrosine solution did violet and deep-black flake-formation show itself, and with the appearance of black flakes the process reached its end. Also other readily oxidizable aromatic substances: pyrocatechol, hydroquinone, suprarenin

41* (adrenaline), oxyphenylethylamine [autodigestion of the pancreas] gave reactions, indeed with another coloration. The intact egg-white molecule (casein) was not seized.

The chromogen contained in the lepidopterin-blood now proved itself "neither through phosphotungstic acid, nor through heavy-metal salts, ammoniacal silver solution or bromine-water capable of precipitation, in water-containing alcohol-ether soluble, belonging to the aromatic series, in any case not a substance identical with the tyrosine."

In order to investigate the nature of the conversion-product which arises through the action of the tyrosinase upon tyrosine, this was prepared pure from the end-products (flakes) through extraction of the abundantly contained tyrosine, so that "the thus-obtained product proved itself insoluble in water, alcohol, ether and the customary organic solvents, as well as in dilute alkalis at room-temperature and even in boiling, strong hydrochloric acid." Upon melting of this substance with purest caustic soda, a typical smell of indole and skatole developed.

Schneider had already earlier established that pure tyrosine, melted with caustic potash, gives a product smelling of skatole. The product of the action of the tyrosinase upon tyrosine has the following composition:

| C | 55.44 | percent |
|---|---|---|
| H | 4.45 | - |
| N | 13.74 | - |

The ratio of the number of the atoms is therefore N : H : C = 1 : 4.55 : 4.77.

This corresponds to the characteristic of the melanines after Hofmeister. To these are to be counted the dark pigments of the hair, of the skin, of the chorioidea, of the melanotic tumors, the cuttlefish-black and certain products of protein-cleavage (melanic acid). It is all the more justified to count the conversion-product of tyrosine here in question among these "melanines," since it, by its physical properties, its solution-conditions, and especially by the capacity to deliver skatole-like-smelling substances at the alkali-melt, in every respect attaches itself to this group.

Appendix-wise it is mentioned that Ducceschi, through cautious oxidation of tyrosine with potassium chlorate in hydrochloric-acid solution, succeeded in preparing a melanin-like substance, which at the caustic-potash-melting smells of indole and skatole. Our experimenters emphasize here that the melanin-like product arises through oxidation of tyrosine, which to them appears "not without significance for the assessment of those processes which, at the acid-cleavage and digestion of protein-bodies, lead to the formation, on the one hand of melanines, on the other hand of peculiar indole- or skatole-giving products." With Ducceschi, however, the number-ratio of the atoms (in contrast to above) is N : H : C = 1 : 10.3 : 9.4, which connects to the tyrosine. As explanation of this disproportion the authors assume that, in the fermentative conversion of the tyrosine, at least two tyrosine molecules combine under cleavage of nitrogen-poor or nitrogen-free groups.

But not only to insects is the occurrence of tyrosinases restricted. Also in the blood of crayfish such were demonstrated, albeit only in weak action. As a problem the authors designate the demonstration of the effectiveness of tyrosinases at the formation of the melanin-pigments in the living organism. But the authors at first expected results not from the investigation on the vertebrate, given the protractedness of the formation-process, but rather from the investigation on organs of lower animals with mass-production of the pigment.

The ink sac of *Sepia* came namely into consideration, and here follows the account of Hans Przibram (in 11, p. 241) concerning his successful experiment, undertaken at the instigation of v. Fürth at the zoological station in Triest:

"Two freshly brought-in specimens of *Sepia officinalis* were irritated for so long, and the water changed each time, until no more turbidity of the same set in, which took about half an hour. So then they were killed by a cut into the brain, laid on the back and the mantle on the left side cut through; the exposed ink sac was then detached and pressed out, then also cut open lengthwise and washed out for so long, until no more turbidity of the physiological salt solution used for it set in. The thus-obtained sac-skins were, under addition of quartz sand and physiological salt solution, after preceding chopping with the chopping-knife, ground up in the mortar. The contained pulp, shot through despite all washing-out with dark granules, was filtered twice through baryta-filter and yielded an almost completely clear, very faintly gray-colored fluid.

While [the samples to which physiological salt solution had been added] showed no change (other than perhaps a very faint opalescence), the halves mixed with tyrosine solution took on a saffron-yellow to orange-red, very beautiful coloring, and at last became sepia-brown, setting down a black precipitate."

Since now tyrosine and related substances, after Salkowski (11, p. 242), are in general formed during the autodigestion-process of animal tissue — as Martin Jakoby (1900) demonstrated specially for the liver — it appeared to our authors as possible that, in the formation of all melanin-like pigments, enzymes are active. For the overall-process two kinds of ferments appear to them necessary: 1) an autolytic [ferment] for the cleavage of an aromatic complex from the protein-molecule; 2) a tyrosinase for the formation of the melanin.

This much for the appreciation of the work of v. Fürth and Schneider.

Experiments, in which the press-juice from skins of young higher animals, obtained in the mortar by grinding with kieselguhr, was investigated by Florence M. Durham at the Balfour-laboratory in Cambridge, delivered the following result:

"An extract can be obtained from the skins of certain pigmented animals (rabbits, rats, guinea-pigs and chicks), which possesses the tendency to act upon tyrosine and to deliver a melanin-like substance. This action suggests the thought of the presence of a tyrosinase in the skins of these animals.

The action of this tyrosinase is removed through boiling, does not take place at low temperature, is slowed with time, requires a temperature of about 37° C., and also the presence of a catalyst such as iron-sulfate, in order to set it going. The obtained colored substances are in agreement with the color of the animals used. One obtains black substances if one uses animals with black pigment in the skin, and yellow substance if the skin contains the orange-colored pigment. The colored substances are soluble in alkalis, but insoluble in acids."

## II. Own experiments.

### A. On cephalopods.

### a. Developed animals of *Eledone moschata* Leach.

My experiments on cephalopods I undertook in the summer of 1905 in special connection to the above-mentioned experiment of Hans Przibram.

Foremost there served as my experimental material *Eledone moschata*, of which freshly-killed adult animals were obtained from the k. k. zoological station in Triest. In Vienna the animals were kept in the ice-box until they were each used for the experiment. Pigment shows itself in *Eledone* in the skin and especially intensively in the eyes and in the ink-sac.

#### 1. Skin.

The skin of the *Eledone* shows at the mantle and at the catching-arms (Fangarme), on the upper side, on a white ground an extensive blackish-brown drawing, which rests upon a pigment.

The peeled-off skins of freshly-killed specimens, taken from the mantle and the catching-arms of the animals, I chopped fine with a chopping-knife and tested in a meat-press the snippets mixed with physiological salt solution. After the still-turbid press-juice had been left standing during the precipitation of the larger flakes, I diluted it further, for the prevention of the turbidity, with physiological salt solution and filtered it, whereupon it yielded, without notable turbidity, a somewhat rosy-colored fluid; then I set up the following test-tube series:

**a) Dark, room-temperature:**

1) ½ filtrate, ½ physiological salt solution;
2) ½ &nbsp;-&nbsp; ½ tyrosine solution;
3) ½ &nbsp;-&nbsp; ½ physiological salt solution, 1 mg iron-sulfate as catalyst;
4) ½ filtrate, ½ tyrosine solution, 1 mg iron-sulfate as catalyst;
5) ½ physiological salt solution, ½ tyrosine solution;
6) ½ physiological salt solution, ½ tyrosine solution, 1 mg iron-sulfate.

**b) Brooding-oven, 30° C.**

parallel to a) six samples as above.

The samples — the test-tubes were always stoppered with cotton-wool —, which contained about 15 ccm of fluid, were foremost completely clear, only 1—4 naturally with rosy-red coloring. Already on the next day, at 4, both in the room-temperature and in the brooding-oven, a bottom-standing black precipitate was to be recorded. The next day let a dirty rosy-red precipitate be recognized at 1, 2, 3, which at 3 had discolored to gray; 5 remained waterclear, 6 took on a pure yellowish tint. The higher temperature of the brooding-oven did not essentially modify these processes. On the next day all reactions became still more intensive.

One part of 2, mixed with iron-sulfate, rapidly developed reactions in the sense of 4.

6 showed still later a sulfur-flower-like precipitate; the precipitate of 3 became later dark like that of 4, and the whole fluid too discolored gray, as had already been seen earlier at 4.

Then tyrosine-mixed skin-press-juice of the *Eledone* was set up with 1 mg iron-sulfate for the testing of the combined working of warmth, cold, darkness, light and oxygen-supply in the following combinations:

a) Warmth, darkness; b) Warmth, light;
c) Warmth, darkness, ventilation; d) Warmth, light, ventilation;
e) Cold, darkness; f) Cold, light;
g) Cold, darkness, ventilation; h) Cold, light, ventilation.

The ventilation¹) called forth on the following day in d) and h) a clearly yellower color-tone than in b) and f); incidentally, in the four cases the ventilation mechanically prevented the formation of a precipitate, which otherwise set in promptly as above (on the second day black!); warmth and cold, but especially light and darkness, called forth no essentially different effects.

For the investigation of the action of other catalysts the same press-juice was mixed with tyrosine, at warmth and darkness, with a) platinum-chloride and b) manganese-sulfate; the well-known dark precipitate appeared there too; in an after-experiment with old juice, this, treated with iron-sulfate, delivered a black precipitate and then became completely turbid; with manganese-sulfate only a rosy precipitate appeared, the fluid retained its color.

> ¹ It streamed out of a Spanish tube which was attached to the air-conduit, air in fine distribution.

### 3. Ink sac.

Of the ink sac, too little mass had been available to be able to obtain precipitates and reactions from the analogous samples. Compared with the ink sac of *Sepia*, the development of the *Eledone* ink sac proves indeed to be altogether highly rudimentary, which sufficiently explains the absence of clear results as against the so manifest, above-mentioned findings of PRZIBRAM on *Sepia*.

### 4. Muscle.

Finally, press-juice from the mantle musculature of the *Eledone* was prepared, treated with iron sulfate, half of it together with physiological sodium chloride solution, and half of it together with tyrosine solution; here the juice showed no change at all in the further course. The experiment thus demonstrated clearly that the completely pigment-free, beautifully white-colored muscle masses, comparable to the bled flesh of the hen, do not — as was to be expected — contain the triggering factor of pigment formation (the tyrosinase) at all.

### b. Eggs of *Loligo* Lam.

For the experiments on the eggs I had at my disposal the spawn-masses of the genus *Loligo*, so closely related to *Eledone*, which were likewise procured from the Imperial-Royal Zoological Station at Trieste. The clustered, neatly arranged, whitishly translucent egg-strings were set up in a water vessel, and aeration was arranged for them. They still appeared completely pigment-free, pale yellow.

From the eggs press-juice was obtained, and this product was, as in my very first experiment on the skins of *Eledone* 1 to 4 (see p. 637), treated with the corresponding chemicals. 1 and 2 remained colorless, 3 on the following day still showed white, 4 already black precipitate; in 3 the precipitate also became black in the following days, but less strongly than in 4. The samples at ordinary temperature and in the incubator made no essential difference.

Furthermore, old press-juice of the eggs of *Loligo* was set up with tyrosine and 1 mg iron sulfate, for testing the combined action of warmth and cold, darkness and light, as well as of the oxygen supply, in parallel with the above experiment on the skins of *Eledone*, in the eight combinations a) to h).

The result was completely analogous to the series just mentioned.

## B. Experiments on the Olm (*Proteus anguinus* Laur.).

Following on from the experiments on cephalopod pigment, analogous experiments were set up with olms which originated from the Adelsberg Grottoes and had recently been kept in the cellar rooms of the Biological Experimental Station. These olms had still completely preserved their original pigmentlessness. From the skin press-juice was obtained, which was faintly yellowish in color, and the following mixtures of 20 cc were prepared:

a) ½ juice, ½ tyrosine;  
b) - - - 3 mg iron sulfate;  
c) - - - 3 mg manganese sulfate;  
d) - - - 6 mg platinum chloride.

The test tubes were kept dark. Only b) showed a manifest black precipitate, which proves the iron sulfate to be the strongest catalyst in this case too.

Finally, eight olms of the same provenance, in pairs of two specimens each, were set up — in light with oxygen supply (aeration), light without oxygen supply, dark with aeration, dark without aeration — in small water basins at room temperature, and kept alive under these conditions for a year. One specimen each of the animals reared in the light showed, alongside weak atavistic yellow "salamander" spots, sporadically appearing black dusky-shading over the whole body; in particular at the outermost tail-end there were larger coherent black pigment-patches, yet always the rose coloration, shaded into a more rust-red tone with a light nuance, still came impression-determiningly into the foreground. As we shall see presently, these were probably females.

One specimen each of the illuminated animals further colored itself, in contrast to its more light-remaining companion, deep black, probably a male according to MARIE V. CHAUVIN, who demonstrated the dark coloration in these animals to be the male nuptial dress of the rutting condition in olm-breeding. My animals were unstimulated, as always hitherto under indoor keeping, extraordinarily sluggish, and did not show the further rutting phenomena described by M. V. CHAUVIN. Oxygen supply made no difference in the illuminated animals. The animals set up in the dark all remained completely pale.

## III. Summary.

In the cephalopods and in the olm a tyrosinase can be obtained which converts tyrosine into a melanin. It may therefore well be asserted that here processes are present which form a parallel to the processes uncovered and chemically analyzed by v. FÜRTH and SCHNEIDER in other animals with regard to the formation of dark pigment.

Especially important in the cephalopods (*Loligo*) appears the demonstration of the tyrosinase even in the still pigment-free eggs. This tyrosinase is, significantly, located in the adult cephalopod (*Eledone*) only in skin and eyes, not in the musculature, parallel to the presence or the absence of a pigment formation.

For the cold-blooded experimental animals it is probably characteristic that the thermal factor does not, within wider limits, evoke essentially different effects, whereas in DURHAM's warm-blooded experimental animals the temperature of 37° C. proved to be an indispensable requirement of melanin formation and of the related pigment formations.

Other external factors, not only warmth and cold, but also light or darkness, seem to be of lesser importance in the process in the test tube. By contrast, light proves to be an absolute requirement of pigment formation in the **living** olm. The test tube, to be sure, here too shows melanin formation without the aid of light, though admittedly the strongest catalyst must then be used.

In general, it turned out to be a requirement for the success of the experiments on melanin formation in the test tube, in the case of our relatively weak pigment-formation processes, that inorganic catalysts be present. From the experiments with various catalysts there resulted, with regard to their action, that iron sulfate exerts the strongest action; to this is joined the action of the chemically also closely related manganese sulfate; extremely weak were the actions of the platinum chloride, they differed very little from the results in the absence of any catalyst. (These statements apply, of course, to equal quantities of the chemicals employed.)
