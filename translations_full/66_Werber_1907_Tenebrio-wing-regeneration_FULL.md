In the great gradational scale of the gradual decline of the capacity for regeneration in the animal kingdom — from the protozoa up to the most highly differentiated metazoa — it is, among the invertebrates, chiefly the insects to which (in the metamorphosed state) comparatively the least capacity for regeneration belongs. Whereas the crustaceans are always able to replace amputated limbs, in them this capacity has been retained only in the larval or pupal stage, even if to a lesser degree than in the crustaceans. In the insects, or at least in the coleopterans, this appears to be connected chiefly with the cessation of growth after the metamorphosis from the pupa to the imago has once taken place, since the imagines no longer undergo any moults. This behavior would then be one general among the arthropods altogether: the possibility of moulting is in them an important precondition for the capacity for regeneration (cf. Przibram and Werber, 1907). With the last moult, growth attains its conclusion. In the coleopterans, and thus also in the mealworm beetle, this now occurs at the transformation of the pupa into the beetle. With this conclusion of the capacity for growth there coincides also the cessation of the capacity for regeneration, which is to be regarded as a growth phenomenon (cf. Przibram, 1905). And so it is now readily understandable why insect larvae are able to regenerate individual limbs and even eyes (Werber, 1904), whereas this capacity is no longer present in the imagines. Here the capacity for regeneration is restricted merely to reparative processes of tissue defects (Hope, 1845/46, Verhoeff, 1896). Lost or amputated limbs or larger parts of the body can no longer be replaced in already metamorphosed insects. At least, no such reports have hitherto become known. On the basis of the research results obtained so far concerning regeneration in the insects, the view has — and certainly not unjustly — been asserted that the capacity for regeneration of the fully developed insects is a very slight one, absolutely no greater than, say, that of the higher vertebrates. And concerning the coleopterans among the insects, the assumption has prevailed up to now that they are indeed able to repair tissue defects (such as e.g. a perforated wing), but that they are absolutely incapable of regenerating a limb, an organ, or any part of the body, even if not a very large one. That a beetle should be capable of regenerating, say, a wing — that one would hardly have held to be possible in interested research circles. And I myself would have subscribed to this view, had not chance taught me better.

In the months April to June 1906 I took over the care of a number of isolated praying mantises (*Sphodromantis bioculata*), which served Mr. Hans Przibram for experiments. The care of these animals presents at times, owing to a lack of suitable food, some difficulties, and indeed chiefly with larger larvae, for which the plant-lice otherwise used as food for small larvae prove to be an inadequate nutriment. In fine weather, to remedy this evil, one could readily catch flies, of which the *Sphodromantis* is very fond. In the previous year, however, in the spring, we were compelled, owing to the long-persisting rainy weather, to procure other food for the praying mantises, since often for several days in succession no flies were to be encountered. We thus had to use the mealworm beetles (*Tenebrio molitor*) as food for these animals. For this purpose the wing-covers had to be removed from the mealworm beetles, since the otherwise so voracious and so well-armed praying mantises did indeed catch the beetles, but immediately released them again, when they convinced themselves of the hardness of the beetle, which was entirely clad in hard chitin. Through one small opening of the otherwise excellently tight-closing cages, the beetles robbed of their wing-covers were brought into the cage, which was at once closed again. Now and then I brought into a cage which harbored a somewhat larger larva two and sometimes even three beetles, in which case it not seldom happened that a beetle hid itself in a corner on the floor of the cage and in this way kept the enemy off its body for several days, until the beetle was at last caught after all or perished from hunger. I was, however, always very intent that no winged beetle should, perhaps by mistake, come into the cage. And so I was one day surprised by a fact which I had until then held to be wholly out of the question. I found, in a cage inhabited by a praying mantis, a beetle with regenerated wing-covers. It seemed to me, however, after all quite impossible that this could really be the case. And so I now began already to doubt the "genuineness" of the regenerate I had found, and sought to explain the case to myself in such a way that possibly, after all, through an oversight a beetle had been brought into the cage from which the wing-covers had not previously been removed. Upon closer examination, however, it at once turned out that the doubts I had at first harbored were not justified, since the wing-covers exhibited a much lighter coloration than the rest of the body. The mealworm beetle has, as is well known, after slipping out of the pupa a reddish-brown color, which then becomes ever darker and in about 3 to 4 days turns black. While now the thorax, the head-segment and the entire ventral side were black, the wings alone stood out by their reddish-brown color. So it was now established for me that these "youthfully" colored wing-covers could be nothing other than new formations.

Nonetheless I undertook to test the case experimentally. I set up the experiment in two series, each in one cage. In the first series there were 20 mealworm beetles with extirpated wing-covers, and in the second series 13 beetles with extirpated wing-covers and wings. The animals were left under the same conditions as they are otherwise reared with us. The experiments were set up on 16 October 1906, and on 29 October 1906 I already had — admittedly only in the first series — a definitive positive result to record. What must here be especially emphasized is that comparatively only few mealworm beetles regenerate the wings and wing-covers, and the majority die already after a few days. This may probably be attributable to the slight duration of life of these animals, and therefore any experiments must always be undertaken on a very large number [of individuals] when it is a matter of verifying the correctness or incorrectness [of the result]. — In the first series, where in 20 beetles only the wing-covers had been removed, I obtained regenerates in only a single specimen; all the rest perished after 2 to 8 days. In the second series, where in 13 beetles the wings and wing-covers had been removed, unfortunately all the animals died.

The course of the regeneration process¹ was the following: Two days after the setting-up of the experiment I noticed, in most of the animals from which only the wing-covers had been removed but the wings had been left, that the wings shrank considerably, and indeed in such a way that at the posterior end of the wing a piece

It gave the impression as if these pieces had been lost through breaking off. — After a further 3 days the wings were still shorter; on the 7th day, in one specimen — meanwhile almost all the others had already perished — only a small stump of the wings remained present at the attachment-place, which also disappeared as well. On the 10th day after the extirpation, in the one specimen quite small wing-covers and wings appeared at the attachment-place of the same. These new wing-covers and wings showed a very rapid growth proceeding simultaneously (Fig. 1, 2). In order to have an unobjectionable proof of the genuineness of the regenerate, I did not wait for the complete outgrowth of the wing-covers and wings, but conserved the animal at the stage where they reached almost half the size of the trunk.

It is interesting that the wing-covers do not regenerate at once, but that first the wings disappear, in order then to be regenerated together with the wing-covers. The disappearance of the wings is perhaps to be explained in the way that they, robbed of their protection (the wing-covers), dry out entirely, become very brittle, and then break off through mechanical obstacles. It does not, however, seem out of the question either that the wing-covers are formed only simultaneously and together (from one and the same place), as is possibly also the case in the transformation of the pupa into the beetle.

Worth mentioning, furthermore, would be the following circumstance. After the extirpation of the wings, there is to be noticed at several places of the trunk, chiefly at the attachment-place of the wings, a little membrane (Häutchen) that lifts off easily from the body (Fig. 1, 2, 3). It is now not out of the question that this might be, say, a rudimentary moult, which is triggered by the operation undertaken on the animal. This would then again be a confirmation of the fact that "the complete development of the regenerates in forms which are at all subject to a periodic moulting is tied to this" (Przibram, 1907).

These conditions, here only briefly mentioned but theoretically interesting, as well as other questions arising from the theme, are to receive consideration in a further investigation.
