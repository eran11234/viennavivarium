It is a well-known fact, first scientifically founded by Hecker¹) through statistical investigations, that the later children of a woman exhibit a greater weight and a greater length at birth than the earlier ones. Duncan²) expressed the view that it is not the number of preceding births, as Hecker thought, but the increasing age of the woman that is decisive for the greater weight of the later children. For he pointed out that the children of older primiparae too possess a higher average weight than the children of younger women.

Wernich³), on the basis of statistical investigations, arrived at the establishment of the following propositions:

1) The weight of the newborn increases in a constant manner with the age of the mother up to her 39th year, the length up to the 44th year.

2) Each following child surpasses the preceding one with respect to weight and length.

3) The age of the mother and the number of births influence the growth of the weight and the length of the child, in that each of the two factors makes itself felt through an increase;

the concurrence of a particular pregnancy with the average years exerts an especially favorable influence on the development of the child. For it can indeed be shown, e.g., that the firstborn of thirty-year-old women possess a smaller weight than the children of thirty-year-old women who had already given birth before.

These observations in humans also agree with the statement of animal breeders, who assert that in dog, horse, and cattle the first young ones are unquestionably small and weak in comparison to the later ones, and furthermore that calves from one- to two-year-old cows always turn out smaller than from older ones (Wernich).

The fact that the later children of a woman are heavier could, as Hecker had already done, lead to the assumption that the preceding pregnancies, or rather deliveries and puerperia, are the occasion for the increasing size of the later children. A whole series of theoretical possibilities would exist in order to create a basis for this assumption, and the statistics of Wernich do indeed seem to speak in favor of an influence in this respect being ascribed to the preceding pregnancies. At all events, this part of the question is experimentally difficult to study, since the objection remains that the increasing age of the mother alone is reason enough for the greater weight of the later newborns. This latter fact, which results without further ado from the greater weight of the firstborn of older women, seems, however, to be established so unambiguously that it appeared to me important enough to investigate its cause and to fathom it experimentally, all the more since, to my knowledge, no attempt has so far been made to provide an explanation for this regular behavior.

If we ponder in general about the various possibilities of why the children turn out at their birth at one time larger, at another time smaller, then various moments would come into consideration.

1) Heredity. — The children of large parents as a rule take after them. To my knowledge no investigations exist so far as to how far this already comes to expression in the newborns. For our question this moment is in no case of particular significance, since the children of the same parents show the same progression.

2) Differences in the nutrition of pregnant women are known to be of significance for the weight of the children. It is, however, not to be assumed that older primiparae are nourished better than younger ones.

3) Differences in the manner of life at the time of the pregnancy. — We know that women who take little exercise during pregnancy bring larger children into the world than those who take much exercise (Pinard). This moment too can be neglected in our considerations.

4) It could be assumed that older women carry the fruits longer, so that relative late-births arise. For this too no clue lies at hand, so that we can simply wave aside all these possibilities.

I therefore came more and more to the conviction that external influences can play no role and that the children of older persons have, so to speak, proceeded from differently constituted eggs, from eggs which on whatever ground present the material for a stronger development.

It is here again theoretically possible that already the unfertilized eggs in the ovarium of the woman undergo such an alteration with the increasing age that the cause is to be sought purely on the maternal side, or it could also be that through fertilization a paternal influence comes into play. This latter thought is all the more close at hand, since the fathers too have become older for the later children, and it can also be assumed without constraint that older primiparae frequently also have a husband of correspondingly more advanced age.

As regards now the alterations of the maternal egg, it seemed to me worth examining first whether perhaps the egg develops further, becomes larger, with the advancing age of the woman. The possibility for this seemed to me given without further ado. We know that the egg in the woman enlarges considerably from birth up to puberty. The size of the egg is given for the newborn by Waldeyer as 26 µ, while in the adult, in the state of ripeness, the diameter of the egg according to Kölliker amounts to about 200 µ, that is, the egg enlarges itself by six to eight times. It thus seemed entirely possible that the eggs also enlarge and grow further in the further years after puberty. Investigations in this respect do not, to my knowledge, exist for the egg-cell.

There would, however, result further the question whether actually the larger eggs also correspond to larger fruits. The study of this question is, for understandable reasons, not feasible in humans, or only with the greatest difficulty. For this reason I first set about studying the question in animals. The most suitable for this purpose seemed to me that animal species which produces a large number of macroscopically distinctly visible eggs, as e.g. the various frog species.

I was hereby to some extent misled by the fact that I accidentally found, in a work of Born (Experimentelle Untersuchungen über die Entstehung der Geschlechtsunterschiede. Breslau 1881), the note that in *Rana esculenta* he had established distinct differences in size of the eggs in various specimens, in which it turned out, however, that the older animals produced smaller eggs than the younger ones. In *Rana fusca*, however, this behaved, according to his statement, exactly the reverse. Here distinctly larger eggs corresponded to the older animals.

In a more recent work, Chambers¹) draws attention to differences in egg size in frogs in different clutches, states, however, that no relation exists between the size of the parents and the size of the eggs of their clutch, since he observed small females which laid large eggs, and on the other hand large females which produced small eggs.

Conklin²) found that in the dwarfs of the snail *Crepidula*, which possessed only ¹/₂₅ the volume of giants of the same species, the eggs of the sexually mature dwarfs exhibited the same size as those of the giants, and that they were only fewer in number. This is completely analogous with the epidermis, liver, kidney, and intestinal canal cells, which likewise possessed the same size as in the giants, so that the smallness of the animal was to be explained only through a smaller number of the cells.

When I later carried out, for other purposes, manifold investigations on female frogs, I was able to convince myself that Born's and Chambers' observations must have been erroneous, for I was able to demonstrate with absolute constancy that in *Rana temporaria* and in *Rana esculenta* the larger or older animals produced essentially larger eggs than the younger ones. —now, that the descendants of the large mother animals, which also already produced macroscopically distinctly larger eggs, are larger at hatching than the descendants of small mother animals, whose eggs are smaller.

Experiment 2 and 3 were undertaken in such a way that the eggs of two differently sized females were fertilized with the sperm of the same male. Now while the young of the 9½ cm long female had only an average length of 5 mm, the young of the 12½ cm long female exhibited one of 6 mm, that is, they were about one fifth to one sixth longer than the former.

Thus the size of the eggs is without doubt of decisive influence on the size of the young, and it may well be assumed without constraint that the increasing size of the offspring is dependent on the increasing size of the eggs from which they arose. In other words: older animals produce larger eggs, and from these larger eggs arise larger young.

In Experiment 4, however, it is shown that in the river perch too the size, or probably the age, of the male is decisive for the size of the offspring; for the eggs of a relatively young female (8½ cm long), fertilized with the sperm of a 13 cm long male, yielded very large young, with an average length of 6,4 mm, whereas, for example, the eggs of a 9½ cm long female, fertilized with the sperm of an 8½ cm long male, yielded only young of an average length of 5 mm (Experiment 2). From this, the noteworthy conclusion may be drawn for the river perch that the size of the offspring depends both on the size of the mother and on the size of the father, and that the young fish are the larger, the larger the maternal and the paternal animal are. Since the size of the animal *ceteris paribus* depends on its age, it may likewise be said without constraint that the size of the offspring depends both on the age of the mother and on the age of the father.

The influence of the mother animal is recognizable already in this, that the larger females produce larger eggs, which thus seem to possess the material for larger young. It would be very desirable to undertake analogous measurements on the spermatozoa of the males as well, in order to ascertain whether here too, in accordance with age, analogous increases in size are to be observed. It can now be shown that in *Salamandra maculosa* and *S. atra* as well, the large, i.e. the older, mother animals quite lawfully correspond to larger newborn larvae or earth-salamanders. On this, Tables I and II, pp. 447 and 448 respectively, give information: in each case a series of mother animals was caught simultaneously in a particular region, and both the mother animals and their hatched young were measured at the time of birth.

Still more interesting are Tables III and IV, pp. 449 ff. and p. 452 respectively. Here the mother animals were kept in captivity for up to four years and all their births accurately registered. In this case an exact measurement of the mother animals and of the newborns took place each time. It was shown that the fruits both in *Salamandra maculosa* and in *Salamandra atra* become larger progressively with the successive births. The mother animals themselves also increase in size with advancing age, so that here too an exquisite relation exists between the age and the size of the mother animal and the size of the newborn fruits. This lawfulness could be demonstrated in all the investigations, in altogether thirty cases of *Salamandra maculosa* and in twenty cases of *Salamandra atra*; in Tables III and IV, however, only a few arbitrarily selected examples may be adduced in order to demonstrate in a convincing manner the correctness of our assumptions.

Interesting here is the observation on the female Y of *Salamandra atra*, that the size of the newborns progressively increases up to the ninth birth, but that from then on a constancy in the size of the newborns is to be recorded. This would stand in analogy with the observation on the human female, in whom likewise a certain peak is not exceeded, in that according to Wernich an increase in weight of the newborns on average takes place only up to the 39th, the increase in length up to the 44th year of the mother.

Most striking in this regard, by the way, is also the fact that in *Salamandra maculosa* [*Salamandra salamandra*], with the increasing births, not only does the size of the individual individuals of the newborns increase, but also the total number of born larvae becomes larger from birth to birth. While, for example, in Case A (Table III) the total number of newborn larvae at the first birth amounts to 20, it rises successively to 37 at the seventh birth. Even more striking is this in B, where the number rises from 35 at the first to 62 at the seventh and eighth birth.

Likewise it emerges from the Table that the number of abortive eggs decreases in a quite significant way at the later births. While at the first births half to two-thirds of all eggs perish abortively, that is, become stunted at an early stage, at the later births almost all eggs are developed into normal fruits.

Accordingly, not only does the absolute number of the fruits increase, fewer also perish, and the individual individuals are larger and better developed. Thus in the female, with advancing age, or with the increasing births, the generative power and production capacity rises in a quite striking way.

I would like, supplementarily, only to mention that it holds as a known fact that the eggs of hens too are the larger, the older the hens are, and that the chicks turn out the larger, the larger the eggs were from which they hatched.

From the cited investigations it emerges, then, that

1) in frogs (*Rana esculenta*, *Rana temporaria*), in fishes (*Perca fluviatilis*), in the salamanders (*S. maculosa* and *S. atra*), in turtles (*Emys*, *Clemmys*, *Testudo*), with the advancing age of the mother animals, or with each following birth, the fruits become ever larger.

This stands in full analogy with the facts known to us from the human being.

2) For *Salamandra atra* it appears to emerge that, after the attainment of a certain maximum, a further increase in size does not take place at further births. This would stand in analogy with the observations found by Wernich for the human being. Whether this also holds for the other animal genera cannot be said, since investigations in this regard have not been carried out.

3) It is an established fact that the females, with advancing age, always produce larger eggs (frogs, river perch, turtle), so that thus the increasing size of the fruits stands in closest connection with a progressive increase in the size of the eggs from which they arise.

It is therefore obvious to conclude that also in the human being the increasing size of the newborn children could be traced back to a progressive growth of the eggs in the woman with her advancing age.
