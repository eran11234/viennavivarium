## 1. Definitions and Termini.

The arbitrary transplantation of pieces onto living organisms has long been customary, for practical and theoretical-scientific purposes. In use by gardeners in the grafting of scions, and by physicians for the replacement of lost body parts, many methods have been worked out in grafting, all of which come down to a permanent fastening of the graft-piece onto the substrate. For this securing there serve insertions of the graft-scion into incisions or excisions of the graft-stock, in that the slipping-out, before it comes to a growing-on, is prevented by bast-cords, metal wires, sutures, clamps, adhesives, weighting with little glass plates, embedding in paraffin beds, bandages, or other restraint against displacement. In all these methods, forces foreign to the living being itself are the ones set against the falling-off. These methods of holding-fast by means of organism-foreign forces

For many graft-attempts the pressure alone suffices, before a beginning growing-on of the cut-out tissues and of the graft-pieces incised out of these, to bring about a lasting union. In ocular operations one indeed knows of cases coming from the organism itself, where without any effect that is brought about by injury of the graft-stock, the growing-on of the graft is made possible. Normally there lies a stimulus in the plant-graft as well as in the animal-graft, through which, however, if the graft itself were held fast at its site by external pressure without growing-on first occurring, the growing-on would set in only somewhat retarded. In such cases a foreign skin-layer is indeed wholly switched off, no natural and effective union with the unwounded organism, but rather of the foreign-body kind, occurs. Indeed one would not be able here, at the fastening itself, to distinguish the *allophore* from such a *non-autophore*, since under the *allophore* one might also count an *allochthone*, to be distinguished from one's own similar body-parts held fast by pressure, just as in the graft-scion. This transplantation-method offers, as is known, some advantages over the actual *allophore* one, since with it the damagings fall away which the foreign body would otherwise be able to bring about; on the other hand it can carry decisive influence with regard to the course of the circulation, irritability or poisonous effect, in that severed pieces and parents of the wound-margin enter into mutual contact. On the other side, however, the *homoiophore* transplantation reaches a certain barrier, in that one observes that, in case of fastening that is weaker than with our own flawless method, the growing-on is also set under other conditions. Indeed the *homoiophore* re-union may be brought about through pushing-together of the uppermost skin-layers of the graft-pieces, under whose growing-on the immediate union of the wound-margins makes the uppermost skin-layers durable; that, however, precisely this growing-together of the cut-through nerves and blood-vessels is in no way favorable. Thus pieces cut out of suitable size are to be grown onto the right wound by uninterrupted pushing-in of the wound to the graft-stock. Such tissue cut up after arbitrariness can rarely be led just along the course of the nerves and blood-vessels, which along the substrate, without further insertions and sutures, could lead to disorder of the healing-process. These disadvantages do not allow themselves to be avoided with any transplantation in which, with the removal of certain body-pieces, only the cutting-through occurs, while *Pardo*'s attempts attain the re-union of the cut-through nerve-fibers. Occupied in the last years much with the problems of the color-change, it lay near to me to hold fast the cut-around graft-pieces only by means of one's own forces, which otherwise would hold fast the body part in question on the normal organism.

Such transplantations will thereby be designated as self-holding or »*autophore*« ones. *Autophore* transplantations require thereby indeed no cooperation at the fastening of the graft for the holding-fast of the graft, but rather the natural living-essence itself is present. Only thereby is it to be reckoned where the method is applicable. Such, however, do not require by the nature of the case itself that the components of the union belong to the same kind. One is known to distinguish *autoplastic* transplantations, when the components belong to the same exemplar, then »*homoplastic*«, when the components belong to the same species, finally »*heteroplastic*«, when, however, the various species belong. All these combinations let themselves also be carried out with the autophoric transplantation, when also the homologous organs of suitable size are joined. In the course of our investigation it has come, however, to a desirable union of both components removed from among the same kind, although the term for such union is to be introduced by which the components belong to the same kind. It comes here therefore not just upon designating as *homoplastic* such, of which the partners differ from one another strikingly and externally indeed already in the various kinds of features, namely the color. On the other side the contradiction in the previous use, the union of various races among *heteroplastic*, must be mediated, however, as *heteroplastic*. We propose for the race-combinations the name »*alloplastic*«, in anticipation of the term *allomorphe* of the race-doctrine for the genes distinguishable within one and the same race-feature¹).

## 3. Problems which the transplantation of eyes by means of autophoric transplantation could undertake.

The success of eye replantation in cold-blooded vertebrates by means of the autophoric method gives us at first only the means, ready to hand, of restoring the eye's influence, switched off by the total blinding, upon the path of the nervous union by auto- or homoioplastic application. By control experiments on completely blinded animals of the same kind, in which eyes are inserted at such places as permit no direct connection with the *Thalamus opticus* belonging to them, it must be shown whether the mere presence of the non-seeing eye, by means of any influence upon the color change, is in a position to achieve it, or whether this color change is brought about merely by the light-sensitive eye through the mediation of its normal nervous connection. It is known that the assumption of environmental colors by fishes is in a direct manner dependent on the visual organs, and that the simple severing of the optic nerve, or blinding, leads to a cessation of the normal color change. This phenomenon, treated experimentally by *Pouchet*, has been further pursued in my institute by *Karl v. Frisch*, and in the process the connection of the skin-nerves with the sympathetic nervous system has been clearly laid out. *Frisch*, at that time professor in Munich, then in Rostock, then used the color change of several fishes for conclusions about their visual sense, which *Heß* sharply contradicted, but as newer investigations have shown, on a doubtful basis [these conclusions] have been attacked. When certain fishes upon a yellow ground are not colored as yellow as upon a differently colored one, *Frisch* concluded that the fish has the ability to see this color. Through training experiments it was confirmed that fishes do have a discrimination capacity for yellow and blue. The color change is therefore under certain circumstances a means of establishing in such animals whether their reaction to rays of different wavelength, apart from the differing intensity of these rays otherwise

The fishes take on the environmental color more or less well, in any case they retain the definite color during all the differing illumination intensities. *S. O. Mast* has, through careful experiments on plaice, established that it suffices to hold colored cardboard pieces of small size before the eyes in order to call forth the desired color, e.g. yellow, in the fish's body. It is thus clear that not the total intensity of the light can here be effective, but rather merely the specific wavelength, for the light reflected from the cardboard forms indeed only a small fraction of the quantity of light falling into the eyes, of whose alteration, however, the color change proceeded independently. Since it is thus certain that with the eye of the fish the perception of definite colors is used for the assumption of an analogous body coloration, there now arise, in the case of heteroplastic eye-replantation, several questions whose answering was hitherto altogether impossible. When the eye of one fish is in a position to lend its body a definite color, which then happens through the insertion of the eye of one fish, which normally possesses a different body coloration? Will the success of bilateral transplantation — a thriving cooperation of the peripheral with the species-foreign central parts of the optic nerves and brain with respect to a maintenance of the color-change capacity — take place at all? Blinded fishes take on, as has long been known to me and has been repeatedly confirmed by my pupils, a quite dark coloration. We will be able to expect that auto- or homoioplastic replantation will bring about a renewed brightening and the assumption of the characteristic species color. Which color shall, however, in heteroplastic eye replantation appear, when not both components are able to distinguish the same color from another? In order to isolate the function of the central organ from that of the skin, we will also be able to attempt, with retention of the eyes, to bring a foreign brain autophorically into the head, which, given the fixation of the brain by the skull-capsule and the maintenance of the position of the swimming or resting fish upon the conquest-surface, does not appear impossible.¹

In the amphibians the relations lie still more favorable. If this succeeds, then the role of the central organ and of the peripheral sense organ in the coming-about of the color reaction can be quite clearly established. Further problems of similar kind with respect to picture-seeing could be taken up if suitable material were to be found. It would be conceivable, for example, by transplantation of functionally capable eyes, to restore the visual capacity of animal species with rudimentary eyes. Here, however, the small sizes of the rudimentary organs will make it difficult to find a suitable partner. One would have to take the seeing eyes from very young stages or quite small species, and insert them into older and therefore larger forms with rudiments. It is further a question whether, conversely, the transfer of rudimentary eyes onto seeing animals leads to a further differentiation of the eye. Therewith we come to a further field, in which the autophoric method will permit good work, namely the mutual influencing of graft-scion and graft-stock with respect to lasting properties. Will the transplanted eyes retain their original color, or will they, as is stated for skin-pieces and small puparium-pieces, gradually assume the characters of the graft-stock? And when this happens, does it lie in an immigration or emigration of pigment, or in a real reconstitution of the eye-media? The analysis of this question could best be undertaken in alleloplastic transplantations, in which a race as pigment-free as possible is brought into union with a quite strongly pigmented one.

On bright places of a dark animal, deplanted eyes of a bright race could naturally retain a certain pigment-poverty from the nearest surroundings. If they become pigmented, then it is a matter of a reconstitution of another kind.

*Uhlenhuth* had, on the deplanted eyes of his small Salamander, demonstrated that the larval-yellow iris-ring disappears along with the metamorphosis of the graft-stock, [an iris-ring] which had belonged to the deplanted eye of another developmental stage of the larva. With the success of the heteroplastic amphibian-unions by means of autophoric eye-grafting, the further question will also be decided, whether this influencing of the transformation-time makes itself felt between different developmental stages of one species. In the process yet a further problem comes to the fore, namely whether there are any analogies to the biogenetic fundamental law with respect to the behavior of hetero- and dysplastic unions in comparison with homoplastic ones of differing age? Does a phylogenetically more primitive component behave, in hetero- or dysplastic grafting against a phylogenetically more highly developed one, in any way similarly to an ontogenetically more primitive one against an ontogenetically more highly developed one? This question has proven itself not unjustified. (In parenthesis be it noted that phylogenetically younger would be paralleled with ontogenetically older stage, and phylogenetically older with ontogenetically younger, wherefore the expressions older and younger are to be avoided in this use.) As regards especially the size of cells, it can be investigated whether eyes which, on account of the considerably greater species-size of the eye-receiving species, grow out beyond the normal measure of the eye-donor, undertake a change of cell-size in the direction of an enlargement, or an increase of the cells with unchanged cell-size. That such a growth beyond the normal measure would not be excluded is suggested by the old experiments, which yielded an insertion from smaller animals onto larger transplanted parts (e.g. tails, spurs).

## 4. Further fields of application of autophoric transplantation.

In surprisingly short time, further possibilities of autophoric transplantation have been able to be discovered. *Koppányi* found that in frogs and lizards eye and ear can be exchanged, without any special fixation measures being necessary in the process. This may yield a hitherto unattained means of testing the tenability of the assumption of specific sense-energies. By the auditory transplantation, which is capable of various modifications, mention is also made: besides the exchange of the whole forebrain, the replacement of one half by the analogous brain-half of another species comes into consideration. How will the taxes and the instincts behave themselves there? Or will the assumption of an external behavior come to observation? Yet further can [it] be reported on whole headless heads, on which *Walter Finkler* reports. Other body-parts, which lend themselves to a piecewise (at least partial) replantation, have been investigated among the arthropods (Arthropod-legs). Be it here only indicated of the joint-insertions of *Lezer* and his successors, where, however, no lasting function seemed to result. The replantation of teeth in man led likewise in recent times to no success (*Baumgartner*). Finally, inside the vertebrate-class itself, autophoric formations may be represented by depicting the lens itself as an autophoric formation, which after removal through excentric self-rounding becomes a foreign formation and is replaced.¹

I am nevertheless convinced that with everyone, upon the reading-through of this transplantation program, the question turns up, whether then the successes of autophoric transplantation could not be made usable not merely on the eye for practical-medical purposes. Should the seeing fail forever, one must indeed bear in mind that with successful replantations in remote dimensions, presumably also in apes, the transplantation might under certain circumstances lead to a winning-back of seeing capacity. So now it is indeed important to establish whether the transplanted tissue at the wound-place still remains alive for a longer time than until, through the change of the central-organic spread of nourishment, it dies off. To establish this would be very welcome, in order to be able to draw conclusions on the surviving of human organs upon other animals, and on the surviving of human organs in situ.²

## 5. Summary.

1. »Autophoric« or self-maintaining transplantation shall mean a replantation of body-pieces, in whose retention no special forces of the graft-stock are necessary near union of the living-wax.

2. Such was undertaken twenty years ago for the exchange of the tail-pieces in *Antedon* [a crinoid], used, by means of small tentacle-pieces of the disc itself, and pressed onto the gill-arches. A replantation of organs has been carried out in mammals on the same method undertaken, where the eyeballs through air-pressure and eyelid-closure were maintained in place (*Pardo* for amphibians 1906 calculated lasting).

3. The autophoric transplantations have before all others the advantage that the carried-through blood-vessels, nerves and other tissue can pass into one another, the precondition for rapid joining, [and] verify the changing of the position of the eye through the skull-capsule. The overlap of the method shows itself in the possibility, not merely easy »heteroplastic« unions to bring about, but rather even components of developed animal-classes for the union to bring, as »dysplastic« transplantation be designated mag.

These constituted problems of the sense-physiology to grasp, which itself on the seat of special reaction-differences in peripheral or central parts of the sense-sphere of different animals relate and hitherto in-graspable allowed.

4. The autophoric transplantation as well as theoretically as also practically of problems is capable. Insofar appears it in the application onto the eye of man near the previous successes on animals (over which *Koppányi* recently reported) not hopeless.
