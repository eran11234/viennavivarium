## Critical Remarks on the Value of Stimulus Physiology.

Already through my experiments on the culture of frog-skin epithelium¹) and through a thorough study of the regeneration of frog skin in the organism itself²) I came to the conviction that we gain nothing for the understanding of the processes observed in skin regeneration if we consider them from the standpoint of the wound-stimulus theory or of stimulus physiology in general. On the contrary, it seemed to me as if, in the chain of detail-processes hitherto uncovered through analysis, there was nowhere a place for a stimulus. Admittedly, by no means could all the factors be found that would have been required for a full understanding of some of these detail-processes. Their mode of action, however, which turned out to be a purely mechanical one, seemed to me reason enough to regard these factors themselves as mechanical and therefore, in further research work, as capable of explanation; at the very least this seemed, from an exactly scientific standpoint, far less speculative than if one had set in their place a hypothetical stimulus and thereby given up further analysis. The result of this study was, in brief, the following: Through the removal of the piece of skin no wound-stimulus is exerted, but rather there gathers in the wound an accumulation of a soft-to-fluid medium, readily visible to the eye of the researcher; through the presence of such a medium the cells lying at the wound margin are enabled to migrate out into this medium. Culture experiments with media of various consistency seemed to me to speak in favour of the view that this migration is the consequence of a purely mecha-

This out-migration of the cells from the wound margin into the wound had as its consequence a loosening of the cells in the surroundings of the wound, through which a free lymph circulation between the cells was established. These improved nutritional conditions had to be regarded as the cause that the normally division-capable epithelial cells could again begin to divide mitotically. The purely mechanical character of cell mitosis as well as the fully mechanical relationship of the division-mechanism to the external surroundings appeared, in light of Paul Becquerel's experiments on plant seeds, completely understandable; this researcher showed, namely, that those structures in which the division-mechanism itself is grounded are nothing that would entitle us to the assumption of an autonomy of life, since these structures remain preserved and suitable for the carrying-out of the division-process even after a long period of complete absence of every kind of life in the bearers of these structures, the seed-cells, has elapsed. Through the rapidly proceeding cell-multiplication there was again automatically brought about, in the skin-wound, a condition which contained the originally existing conditions; the cell-proliferation therefore had to cease again of itself.

Already in this process of skin regeneration I caught sight of a striking example of a life-phenomenon which, both as a whole and in its individual phases, appeared completely understandable without the assumption of a stimulus. As long as we remain on the assumption that the cell-proliferation later leading to regeneration is released through a wound-stimulus, the whole process remains for us completely dark; we do not even feel the need to analyse it further. The great advantage that we gain for the analysis of this phenomenon, when we let fall the assumption of a stimulus, consists above all else in this, that we are robbed of the possibility of taking our refuge in a »stimulus-sensitivity« of the living substance, which lets the reaction of the organism appear as something special and assigns the reactions [a status] in which for the specific [character] of life nothing more remains over; the possibility falls away of setting, in the place of those members which are still lacking for the understanding of a definite happening, speculations, and of arbitrarily declaring our research work to be concluded.

The depigmentation of the iris is now, after a successful lens extirpation, a second example, in which the fruitfulness of such a conception comes clearly to expression. We find here again nowhere a place that we could regard as a stimulus-sensitivity of the cells concerned. The direct impetus through which the lens extirpation is given is the mechanical tearing of the connective-tissue sac, which protects the iris cells in the normal way against a direct contact with the fluid medium. We thus produce, exactly there where later the depigmentation occurs, a hole, through which the iris cells of the central iris margin come into direct contact with the fluid humor aqueus. The consequence of such a hole can be studied on the isolated iris cells. The plasma not only of the isolated iris cells, but of every isolated cell is rendered spherical through a fluid medium. Experiments with media of various consistency¹) made it extremely probable that the shape assumed by a cell in a particular medium depends on the relationship of the consistency of the medium to the consistency of the cell plasma, and in such a way, indeed, that the latter always takes on a drop-form when it is surrounded on all sides by a medium whose consistency is lower than that of the cell plasma. Were we willing to explain this phenomenon, running its course according to purely physical regularities, through recourse to the stimulus-theory, then there would remain for us, provisionally at least, far more on the side of the hypothetical, than when we, basing ourselves on our experiences from the unliving nature, conceive of it as a purely mechanical happening.

In the iris cell rendered spherical in this way there appears at the same time the whole pigment at the surface, whereby the largest part of the pigment emerges from the plasma and disperses itself into the medium. Whether this is connected with the change in the surface tension of the cell plasma, or whether other factors bring about this result, cannot be said with certainty. Certain is only that it is here too again a matter of a purely mechanical relationship, namely the relationship between the cell plasma and the consistency of the medium, and that we therefore, if we do not want to leave the ground of fact, must ascribe to the cell a purely passive role. Similarly does this process also play itself out in the eye

I do not by any means conceal to myself that the relationships which exist between the phenomena set forth here by me and the external factors bringing them about are still by no means understandable in all their details. They still require further detailed work. What we have gained, however, is the clear insight that the relationships are in principle no different from those that we observe between simple chemical-physical systems and their surroundings. These relationships are of such a kind, and the resulting process of the giving-off of the pigment is therefore no longer to be regarded as a process in which the iris cells or the leucocytes take an active part. The expulsion of the pigment appears not as an activity or function of the iris cell, but rather merely as the result of the cooperation of the lifeless fluid humor aqueus and of a system of a definite chemical-physical constitution.

Thereby we have gotten beyond Fischel in an essential point. For Fischel believed at the time, as the only way out over against Wolff — who wanted to explain the lens regeneration from a teleological standpoint —, that he had to let the stimulus stand, and he introduced his analytical considerations on lens regeneration¹) with the words: »The question is first this: which is the stimulus by which it²) is released?«

With reference to the answer that Fischel gave to this question, I should like first to point to a character through which the stimuli used in biology differ essentially from those stimuli that are most familiar to the physiologist. Fischel assumed at that time that the lens regeneration is released through a »lesion-stimulus« exerted upon the iris cells. The physiologist, who comprehends under the stimulus-concept such factors as electricity, temperature, pressure, chemical agents, etc., finds himself, at least insofar as his stimuli come into consideration, faced with chemically or physi-

Quite differently does it stand with a lesion- or wound-stimulus — and precisely stimuli of this kind are the most used in biology — ; stimuli such as these are not at all found among the scientifically definable stimuli, and what the essence of the lesion-stimulus is, is at least just as dark as how the reaction comes about through it. We would first have to ask through which moment or through which factor a wound acts as a stimulus. In other words we would, in order to define the essence of the lesion-stimulus, first have to define the lesion itself. But since this already belongs to the change called forth and to be studied in the lesioned object, it follows further that our stimulus-concept also contains elements which belong not at all to the stimulus, but rather to the reaction of the lesioned object, and thus to stimulus-sensitivity. The hope that we, by employing such complex stimulus-concepts, which moreover themselves still require the analysis on the part of the biologist, and which contain in themselves still elements of stimulus-sensitivity, could analyse the stimulus-sensitivity of the object to be studied — this hope seems for this reason from the outset prospectless.

The reasons which have for a long time determined me to turn against the use of the stimulus-concept in biology, as well as the fundamental difference which exists between the manner of thinking of the stimulus-physiologist and the endeavour to conceive of the changes of the organism not as its functions, but rather as a purely passive reaction released through external factors — could perhaps hardly be illustrated better than through a reference to the Herbst stimulus-theory, to which I will here add a few examples. These may show the reader where the boundary in biology stops, up to which we can proceed further with the stimulus-concept.

As a particularly suitable [example] for this there presents itself barymorphosis, that is, those appearances which are released through gravity. As is known, Pflüger found, in the frog-eggs fixed in a forced position, that gravity is responsible for the fact that the direction of the first and second furrowing-plane is vertical, that of the third horizontal. According to the later researches of Roux and Born this means that in the frog-egg substances of various specific weight are present, that the arrangement of these substances in layers lying one above another is responsible for the vertical direction of the first division-plane, and that the arrangement of these substances is determined through gravity. Since the content of the egg is, still before the beginning of the furrowing, displaceable against the membrane of the egg, one can turn this membrane, whose lower pole is normally whitish, in such a way that the whitish part now lies at the side of the egg, while the content, freely mobile against the membrane, is at once again ordered through gravity corresponding to its specific weight, just as it was before the turning. The consequence of this is that now the first division-plane stands indeed again vertical to the horizon, but no longer vertical to the originally white egg-pole. We can express the view of Pflüger in the proposition that in the frog-egg at this time the furrowing is indeed still influenceable through gravity, but not the position of the white egg-pole, since the latter no longer moves downward when it has previously been turned 90° upward. Pflüger has in his experiments seen the proof of the fact that the whole arrangement of the organs arising later is to be regarded as a direct consequence of the action of gravity.

Herbst denies indeed that here a true barymorphosis is present; for here it is simply a matter of a hydrostatic appearance. One can therefore also not say that gravity here is a formative stimulus, since it is responsible for the bringing-about of the form arising thereby.

This example shows us that we like best to speak of a stimulus precisely there where we know nothing about the actual relationship between the happening concerned and the factors calling it forth. For in all those cases with the plants where gravity acts localisingly on the arising of definite organs, the mode of action of gravity is completely clarified; and just here Herbst speaks of a formative stimulus. An experimenter who did not stand under the influence of stimulus-physiology would have proceeded in the opposite way to Herbst. The only barymorphosis that is recognised as such completely fully is the Pflügerian frog-egg; here lies the simplest case, where we in fact see how gravity acts upon the egg precisely as it does in the unliving nature; it consists in this, that the heaviest substances are arranged downward, the lightest upward, provided that they are freely displaceable against one another. Another action of gravity we know absolutely not, and as soon as we want to assert such a one, we remove ourselves from the ground of fact. This fact we do not escape either by the fact that the eggs in a clinostat develop regularly, [that is, are able to develop]. This shows merely that with this experiment either gravity was not switched off or another force acted directingly in the same way as gravity did before. Were this not so, then these eggs could not have developed normally, since indeed Born showed that with a turning of the eggs by 180°, as a consequence of a mixing of the substances, the eggs die off. Gravity is thus necessary for the development of the egg; it localises actually the arising of the organs of the frog and determines thus the form of the finished organism, in that it arranges the substances necessary for these organs, at the arising of the egg, in a definite way. It is presumably responsible not merely for the maintenance of this arrangement, but indeed already for the formation of this arrangement in the developing egg. From this ground it is also quite mistaken to compare the frog-egg without further ado with a »tumbler« [Stehauf], as Herbst has done. Such a comparison would only fit if the egg, like a tumbler, were so constructed by a higher intelligence that it only secondarily and as a consequence of this construction were brought into relation with gravity; to assert that, we would only be entitled if we had established that the arrangement of these substances in the egg is initially not brought about through gravity, but rather through other forces. As long, however, as this has not been established, I must perceive in gravity that localising force which from the very beginning compels the egg into its characteristic substance-arrangement and thus into the entire organ-arrangement of the frog.

It is to me downright incomprehensible that this instructive example, instead of stimulating to the far-reaching investigation of the remaining barymorphoses, was for the sake of the stimulus-concept laid aside unused. Were this not to have happened, then it would probably already have turned out that a whole number of other barymorphoses rest exactly on this same action of gravity; indeed, it is clear from the outset that they rest on this and only on this, since a gravity bringing about another arrangement than such a one is unimaginable and has hitherto never been observed. And even more, were it not the heaviness through which the substances enter into relation to this force, then we would precisely not call it »gravity« [Schwerkraft]. This fact could indeed then also not escape Herbst, however much he also strove to make plausible another kind of gravitational action for the plant barymorphoses. Herbst refutes, namely, with the following words a hydrostatic action upon the *Marsilia*-spores¹): »Firstly, namely

For we see precisely from this example, where the sifting of facts has been undertaken according to their usefulness for the stimulus-concept, what is to be the final fate of the entire stimulus-physiology. In the same moment in which a Barymorphosis is really understood, it will occur to no person any longer to exclude, in the interest of a stimulus-physiology, gravity, which one wishes to regard as a stimulus, from the genuine Barymorphoses, and, conversely, to let the stimulus-physiology fall in the interest of the genuine Barymorphoses. Exactly the same would naturally have to hold also for all other stimuli. For just as we understand the lesion brought about at the iris and its action, so we should have to refrain entirely from speaking of a lesion-stimulus; for in fact through the lesion no stimulus at all is exerted, but rather the iris-cells are brought into contact with the *humor aqueus*.

To be sure, one can object thereto that this is here a mere quibble over words, and that one could in the end, even when one had analyzed all the individual phases of an occurrence into chemico-physical expressions, still retain the stimulus-concept, simply in order to comprise with this word all the welcome collective names for the movement of the organs that are of significance for the living world, or whose action upon the organisms is just about to be studied. With this one could perhaps declare oneself in agreement; for when one assumes, e.g., that a cell takes on spherical form in a liquid medium, let that be the consequence of a stimulus, namely of a change of surface tension — whereby one would have to keep before one's eyes constantly that this same stimulus has exactly the same action upon an oil-droplet. If it is already difficult to picture to oneself a "stimulated oil-droplet," there comes in addition the fact that at least at present no such clear conception is bound up with the stimulus-concept, and that we are always already, through the bare word "stimulus," led into the temptation to obscure the actually existing relations. One will indeed deny this when one refers, e.g., to a sentence of Herbst, which immediately betrays the intention to penetrate more deeply into the "mechanism" of the tactual phenomena. Here it says namely (note 1): "Hereby it must, above all things, be attended to whether we are, in the case of the layering-together of individual cells with one another, dealing with a stimulus-phenomenon or with a capillary action, which indeed in most cases will be decidable without difficulty." Here shows itself the gulf, which lies between the view represented here and a stimulus-physiology. For according to our opinion there would be between these two actions no difference; we must make clear to ourselves thereover that a capillary action, if one speaks at all of stimulus-phenomena, would under all circumstances be such a one, and that all stimulus-phenomena are nothing other than such purely mechanical phenomena. This is proven without further ado by the experiments with isolated cells, whose form, e.g., is determined through such forces as adhesion and surface tension. Such a conception is by no means compatible with the customary stimulus-concept; on the contrary, the stimulus-physiology becomes for us downright an obstacle, when we wish to understand the mechanism of a life-phenomenon,

That such a separation is not merely the consequence of the necessity of isolating the objects of biology in a particular manner during biological research, and presenting them to our attention especially handily, results from this: that we, in physics and chemistry, by no means bring the inanimate bodies that here present the object of our research into a sharp opposition to their environment. This separation in biology is rather the outflow of our own anthropomorphic thinking, which, further condensed by certain philosophical schools, has led to a complete separation of our I from the objects of the external world, and which we now artificially carry into our biological researches, while yet in fact an intimate concatenation and interpenetration of every organism with the inanimate external world takes place.

To be sure, several mechanists of the present have endeavored to set the stimulus-doctrine right for a purely mechanistic research-method, without however having achieved the desired success. Among them Roux occupies the first place. Although built up with comprehensive detail-knowledge and acuity, the thought of the functional irritability cannot, in this new version either, escape the purposiveness-idea. For the action of the functional stimulus is by no means thought of in such a way that the cells thereby play a purely passive role, as it is thought of from a really mechanistic standpoint and as it also presents itself to a prejudice-free research as the actually existing form of organic occurrence; but rather, in the functional adaptation everything comes back to the irritability of the cell, and thus to the active cell-activity, whereby I here refrain from the fact that that which Roux designates as functional adaptation has provisionally not yet been demonstrated as a general trait of the living substance, but rather, on the contrary, the existence of such a one for the Amphibians is contradicted at least in one example (note 1).

Exactly the same is to be said of R. Thoma, who, although he does not seem to know this, has in essentials taken over the Rouxian theory of the functional adaptation and merely worked it further under the name "Histomechanics." Although I wish here neither to express myself in detail over Roux nor over Thoma's Histomechanics, I should nevertheless like, with one example, to elucidate the unfamiliar opposition between a mechanistic mode of view and the functional adaptation, and indeed I shall make use for this of Thoma's first histomechanical fundamental law, according to which the width of the vessel-lumen depends directly upon the rapidity of the blood streaming in the vessel. The more quickly the blood streams, the wider does the vessel-lumen become, and conversely. Thoma grasps the influence of the blood-stream upon the width of the vessel-wall in a purely mechanical way, wherein lies the particular distinction of his own view and that of Roux. He says thereover (note 2): "Whether, however, the peripheral layer of the blood-stream glides off along the vessel-wall or adheres immovably, in all cases, admittedly, a streaming liquid exerts a pull at the tube-wall. This presents a purely physical phenomenon ..." So far we could fully agree with Thoma, although we therein in principle would see not the least essential distinction over against Roux, since the latter has indeed analyzed his functional stimulus as well and has employed it as such merely as well-defined purely chemico-physical factors. Now, however, comes the essential thing in the Thomian treatise, and therein his Histomechanics likewise again becomes entirely Rouxian functional adaptation. The reaction is therefore not the direct continuation of the external disturbance, but rather it represents a genuine, vital counter-performance. It is perfectly clear that Thoma has by no means, as he erroneously believes, gone beyond Roux. Stimulus and irritability are an indispensable auxiliary concept for both, and the specific reaction-quality, which Thoma categorically rejects, he has unconsciously taken over wholesale from Roux. Indeed, we even find here, undiminished, the concept of the "trophic stimulus" again.¹ And in addition to this there is the further point that, in a recently published work by Stockard,² it was clearly shown that in fish embryos at least the vessel-width does not depend on the velocity of flow, whereby the whole first histomechanical fundamental law, and thus a principal support of Thoma's, appears greatly threatened, while on the other hand this result makes my doubts entertained above against the correctness of Thoma's fundamental law appear very well justified. In this way we arrive at a demand that is precisely opposed to the standpoint of Virchow. For we must require that biology analyze the processes of life so far that they present themselves as a direct continuation of the chemical or physical factors acting from outside. A real mechanism is only that which regards the performances proceeding from organisms as such performances as simply belong to the passive disturbance initiated from outside. By ascribing to the epithelium or to the iris a wound-irritability, we break off the analysis at a point where it ought just to be beginning.

In the preceding sections I have shown that the physiology of stimulation, contrary to the opinion of some mechanists, could be fitted much better into a vitalistic than into a mechanistic system. When in doing so I spoke repeatedly of vitalism and mechanism, I did not, as I should like to emphasize here, by any means have in view the meaning of philosophical systems usually associated with the use of these two words. I recall on this occasion a saying of my former teacher, Professor Hans Przibram, whose fundamental thought was roughly this: that for us it is, in the first place, not a matter of deciding whether what happens in the world proceeds according to vitalistic or mechanistic principles, but rather more a matter of investigating those facts which constitute this happening as completely and exhaustively as possible. Building upon this view, I have here not wished to subject the speculative side of vitalism and of mechanism to a critique, but rather have had in view their practical value for research work. If one reckons it as given that both views actually exist in science, and that they must therefore influence the development of our science, then these two concepts lose the character of speculative systems and present themselves as research methods.

Against this procedure it might perhaps be objected that a method, as Driesch¹ pointed out in an article on the value of the morphological and experimental method, is something that belongs entirely to the domain of logic and, being for that reason not subject to criticism, must not be confused with mere views such as vitalism and mechanism are. This opinion may theoretically have its correctness, but in practice it is not confirmed; for how else would it be possible that Driesch was compelled to write several polemical works in defense of the experimental method, and that, despite all this, individual researchers still hold fast to a purely morphological method because, according to their subjective discretion, it presents itself as superior to the experimental one! We therefore cannot deny that already in the choice of our working method we approach research work in an extremely subjective manner.

And exactly the same standpoint can also be transferred to all our theoretical views. It is indeed a widely held opinion that the researcher ought to approach the investigation of the facts entirely free of preconceptions. But this view is only partly correct. For quite apart from the fact that nature offers us problems only when we ourselves create these problems, building upon a definite general theoretical basis, it must above all be admitted that a research that is conducted without a previously chosen method is nothing other than an aimless groping about. It is indeed true that in this way too many facts can be found, but in any case this would mean delivering oneself over entirely to chance and conducting our research with a downright unconscionable squandering of time and energy; this assertion cannot be overturned even by the fact that a number of the most significant biological discoveries were actually made merely by chance.

> ¹ Driesch, Biol. Zentralbl. Vol. 19. p. 33.

From this point of view, taken from practice, it therefore seems not only possible but indeed imperative once to divest the physiology of stimulation, recognized as vitalistic, and mechanism of the character of a theory, that is, to consider them independently of the relations they bear to the facts already found, and to present them in their relations to the facts that are yet to be found. Here the superiority of mechanism over the physiology of stimulation at once becomes apparent. For the former alone is able to indicate to us precisely that point up to which we have to carry our analysis if we wish to regard a process in biology as completely understood. With the assumption of an irritability of living substance we can declare the analysis exhausted at almost any arbitrary point. Whatever we lack in facts is simply charged to the account of irritability. Thus, for instance, from several quarters there has been a desire to explain not only the cell proliferation that appears after the removal of a tissue-portion, but even the whole regeneration that attaches itself thereto, as a consequence of wound-irritability, and lens regeneration was regarded by Fischel as a consequence of the stimulation, exerted through the lesion, of the iris cells. At that time, from many quarters, lens regeneration too was regarded as completely explained, whereas it now turns out that we are precisely at the beginning of the analysis. Still an enormous mass of detailed work remains to be accomplished if we wish at first merely to understand the conditions necessary for the proliferation of the iris cells.

The only method that leaves no doubt as to the point at which we have solved the task of biological research is mechanism; for from its standpoint "to understand a biological process" means, under the conditions at present prevailing, to have resolved it into such individual occurrences as are the direct function of one or several such factors as are also known to us from the inorganic sciences. At the same time, this relation between an individual occurrence and the factor releasing it must be exactly the same as that which obtains between the individual occurrences appearing in lifeless nature and the factors bringing them about; it must be a genuine reaction and must therefore also be expressible in the chemical-physical notation customary for such reactions.

Against this it might perhaps be objected that even then we should not have gained much for the understanding of life-processes, even if we really succeeded in reducing biology completely to chemical-physical regularities, since we have for the present still not attained an understanding of the "essence" of these latter themselves. To establish to what extent chemistry and physics have grasped the essence of their object is not the task of biology, and it may remain undecided for us how much truth or untruth there is in this assertion. But this much is certain, that this objection by no means touches what is essential in our demand stated above, in so far as this demand merely requires that in the biological sciences we arrive at the same kind of understanding as in chemistry and physics. If this succeeds, then we have in any case thereby achieved a significant simplification, and have thereby satisfied an essential requirement of our thinking.

Finally, it might still be objected that we could not attain this task even in the restriction last made, or at least that we are so manifestly far removed from the attainment of this goal that such demands are still much too premature, and that we must therefore, for the time being, hold fast after all to the concept of a living substance with its known fundamental properties, among them irritability. But this objection too would be out of place where we regard the physiology of stimulation and mechanism as working methods. What is at issue is the fact that we have at the very least to set up this demand in principle, since by any other path we cannot arrive at a usable working method. For, as already mentioned, no other method can give us information as to when we have in fact to regard the analysis of a process as concluded. Every other method permits us to break off the analysis at an arbitrary point and to put, in place of that knowledge of the actual which we might possibly attain by our above-required method, a system of speculations. But since our striving in science is for a clarification and not a veiling of the truth, the path we must take follows of itself.

Doubtless the increasing superficiality, which is spreading more and more in the doctrinaire physiology that today still almost always clings exclusively to the various forms of irritability, furnishes us with the best sample of the slight value of the stimulus-concept as against our modern research method. I therefore cannot agree with Verworn¹ when he says: "But what have I demonstrated when I have demonstrated in the stimulus the cause of the twitch? The history of physiology shows that the development in practice has long since

> ¹ M. Verworn, Erregung und Lähmung. 1914. p. 25.

gone beyond the stage of this contented explanation." On the contrary, the state of our present-day physiology shows clearly, as we saw, that things by no means stand as well as Verworn thinks. For either the physiologist still stands at that stage at which by the word stimulus nothing further is to be established than that in animate nature too definite causes have definite effects — whereby, of course, as Verworn rightly says, nothing is achieved. But those who have gone beyond the mere establishment of this fact have always, as soon as they held fast to the stimulus-concept, inevitably gotten into the blind alley of irritability.

If we do not wish to fall victim to the same fate, then we must rouse ourselves in good time and give up not only irritability, but all the trains of thought connected with it, as foundations of our science. Although the great majority of biologists and physiologists have not yet been able to resolve upon this step, we may nevertheless say with satisfaction that precisely some of the most successful and most significant representatives of biology recognized this necessity already long ago, emphatically warned against the use of the stimulus-concept, and emphasized that the whole doctrine of irritability is nothing other than an old-fashioned relic with which we can no longer make our way in biology.

Most effectively, I perhaps close this section with the words with which J. Loeb in the year 1909, in his "Chemical Developmental Stimulation of the Animal Egg,"¹ characterized the value of the doctrine of stimulation for the understanding of fertilization: "Now it happened that fifty years ago the most influential physiologists, Helmholtz, Ludwig, and Du Bois, had pursued the analysis of life-phenomena only on the physical side; as soon as the thread of investigation led into the chemical side, they let it drop or cut it off with the explanation that here a 'stimulation' takes place. Thus the students learn that the muscle, the nerve, the retina, the glands are electrically, thermally, mechanically, and chemically 'irritable.' But what actually goes on in the muscle and the retina under electrical, mechanical, thermal stimulation was, until the most recent time, not investigated, although that is precisely the most essential task of physiology. Now the developmental stimulation of the egg by a spermatozoon or by chemical interventions has likewise been designated a 'stimulation' of the egg. One must beware of believing that anything is gained by this designation. The essential task remains thereby untouched, namely to investigate the chemical

character of the fertilization process. Physiology would in general gain if one were willing to resolve to let the word stimulation, temporarily at least, drop out of its vocabulary and to investigate the phenomena of life quite generally, as though there were only chemistry and physics, but no 'stimulation.'"¹
