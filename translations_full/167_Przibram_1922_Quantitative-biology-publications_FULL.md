# Publications on Quantitative Biology 1916–1919 in the English Language

## Reviews

### by

### Hans Przibram

The reviewer has on several occasions (Roux's Vorträge u. Aufsätze, Heft III: Anwendung elementarer Mathematik auf biologische Probleme [Application of Elementary Mathematics to Biological Problems], 1908; "Die biologische Versuchsanstalt," Zeitschrift f. biolog. Technik u. Methodik, III, 1913) advocated the development, called for by Roux, of experimental biology in a quantitative direction, and has since occupied himself experimentally and theoretically with the problems of quantity (cf. Experimentalzoologie IV, Vitalität, 1913, as well as his own works and those of his collaborators from the zoological department of the Biological Experimental Institute in the Archiv für Entwicklungsmechanik). With the lifting of the scientific blockade, various books and periodical publications have reached him from North America and England which expressly profess a quantitative biology, but which regard it, depending on the author, in a peculiar way. It therefore seemed advisable to the reviewer, in the interest of the German public, to publish his gleanings in the form of short reviews in the appropriate journal, the Archiv für Entwicklungsmechanik, since, owing to the long isolation, knowledge of the English-language publications of recent years has been able to spread only little among us.

1. **Jacques Loeb** (The Organism as a Whole from a physico-chemical Viewpoint, New York and London, G. P. Putnam's Sons, The Knickerbocker Press, 1916; with 51 Illustrations; 369 pages) declares the ultimate aim of the physical sciences to be the consideration of all phenomena by means of groupings and displacements of ultimate particles, and since there is no discontinuity between the matter which constitutes the living and that which constitutes the lifeless world, the aim of biology can also be expressed in the same way (p. 1). With regard to this we are better off concerning our knowledge of the instincts than concerning form-formation, since by the former we can show that the apparent instincts in many cases follow physico-chemical laws with almost mathematical accuracy (p. 10). One of these examples is the relations of animals to light, since these are gradually being traced back to the law of Bunsen and Roscoe. This law states that the chemical effect of light equals the product of intensity and duration of illumination. Loeb then asks, where would the theory of heredity be without numbers? "Biology will be scientific only to the degree to which it succeeds in tracing the processes of life back to quantitative laws" (p. 11). After rejecting a far-reaching analogy between crystal and living being, the production of artificial organisms is discussed, and this problem is held not to be similar to that of the perpetuum mobile, "since the idea of a perpetuum mobile contradicts the first law of thermodynamics, whereas the production of living matter may be impossible although it contradicts no law of nature" (p. 38). "The overwhelming difficulties of imagining such a possibility consist not only on the chemical side — i.e., the production of proteins from CO₂ and N —, but also on the physical side, as soon as the necessity of a definite cell structure is taken into consideration." "The imitation of cells by colloidal precipitates is only of significance for the study of the origin of the structures in the living thing," but "they are otherwise no imitation of the living, since the characteristic synthetic chemical processes are lacking to them" (p. 39). Loeb regards the cytoplasm of the egg as the seat of the species characters, while in the chromosomes only the race- and individual marks are localized (p. 70), wherein he apparently follows an idea of Raymond Pearl (p. VII). This is the explanation for the predominance of the maternal characters in fertilized, enucleated eggs. The spermatozoon there acts merely as the exciter of parthenogenesis (which the reviewer, in the similar case of the praying mantises, has designated as pseudogamy, Arch. f. Entw.-Mech. XXXVIII, 561, 1909). Beside p. 124 there is shown the photograph of a male frog reared until after the metamorphosis, brought to development by simple pricking of the egg, which was followed by two further males (later, however, Loeb has reported that female frogs too can arise parthenogenetically). In regeneration the following are taken into consideration (p. 176): "1. the flow of substances in preformed channels; 2. the existence of general or specific substances necessary for the growing or regenerating organ" (e.g. p. 156 iodized amino acids for the growth of the frog's legs, after Morse, Journal Biological Chemistry, XIX, 421, 1914). "To this a third element is to be added: namely the 'suction effect' upon these substances by means of a developing organ" (e.g. p. 176 the phenomenon described by the reviewer as compensatory hypertypy, of the development of a small crab-claw into the large one after severance of the latter in *Alpheus*, Arch. f. Entw.-Mech. XI, 321, 1901). Loeb raises the question why a growing organ in general produces such a suction effect, and holds the explanation by means of the consumption of just these substances by the growing organ to be possible, which leads to a low osmotic pressure of these substances in the growing region, so that this fall of the osmotic potential acts as a cause of a further inflow (p. 177; cf. on this Przibram, Exper. Zool. 2. Regeneration, 1909, p. 222). Now the numerical relations of the chromosomes in the [two] sexes and the chemical nature of the sex-differences are discussed (Chap. IX). Instincts and tropisms find only brief mention (Chap. X), since they are discussed in detail in other publications by the same author (cf. below, No. 2). Then the influence of the surroundings and adaptation is treated. Loeb takes the standpoint that there is really no other adaptation to the surroundings than that which glasses too display, which burst upon sudden temperature-increase, while they withstand a slow one (p. 336). It is not possible, by means of long-lasting action of an external factor, to attain a higher degree of resistance than the object in question possesses anyway, if only one does not let the degree of action to be attained set in suddenly, but rather leaves the organism a short time for the displacement of its juices and for the repair of small damages. This is elucidated by means of several tables for heat- and salt-concentration (Chap. XI). Whereas Loeb up to this chapter rejects the efficacy of a natural selection as an explanatory factor for the form-building and the behavior of living beings, he now joins the view represented by Eigenmann and others, that the cave-animals had already, before their entrance into cave-life, been deprived of their eyes and of other adaptations suited only to the light, and would have maintained themselves owing to the lacking competition in the cave, while outside they were threatened with extinction (p. 324). To the reviewer this "Preadaptation" (Cuénot, La Génèse des espèces animales, Paris 1911) seems to stand in contradiction with the results of experimental cave-zoology, since the blind and pale cave-animals were often brought into the light and inherited eyes and colors (cf. Exper.-Zoologie, III, 1910, and V, 1914), whereas, conversely, in the case of transplantations into the darkness several forms lost eyes and colors. Loeb writes off briefly merely the one case of the cave-olm, in which Kammerer (Arch. f. Entw.-Mech. XXXIII, 349, 1912) obtained at all six young reared under suitable light conditions (not merely 2 specimens, as Loeb inadvertently states) with large eyes. The improbability of tracing this back to the "Mutation of the Olm with large eyes," which Loeb earlier (Biol. Bull. XXIX, 50, 1915) once attempted, he himself seems to have come to recognize, for he now admits the possibility that *Proteus* actually responds to light-influence with eye-enlargement, just as Loeb himself observed light as a form-building factor for polyp-regeneration in the polyp *Eudendrium*. No other of the investigated polyps showed this relation. To wish to set the case of the olm parallel to this likewise as an exception — for this the knowledge of the sense-organs in the polyps is lacking to us (has perhaps *Eudendrium* a special sense-physiological relation to light?). In regard to the inheritance of acquired characters Loeb does not behave in a fundamentally rejecting manner, "he only feels that more work ought to be done" before one

could let the data of Ehrlich (p. 332 on trypanosomes) and Kammerer (on *Alytes* p. 338 and *Salamandra* p. 339) find use for this problem. Finally, experiments are adduced on the delimitation of life, namely its shortening by means of lasting action of temperature-increase, and the death, traced back not to a fundamental property of the living, but rather brought about by gradual poisoning, which by itself might maintain itself through the continuing capacity for division, in the way of autokatalysis, in equilibrium (Chap. XIV).

2. **Jacques Loeb** (Forced Movements, Tropisms and Animal Conduct; in: Monographs on Experimental Biology edited by J. Loeb, T. H. Morgan and W. J. V. Osterhout; Philadelphia a. London, Lippincott, 1918. Vol. I. 205 pages, 42 illustrations) brings out, in association with two other experimental biologists, a series of monographs on experimental biology. "Biology, which for a long time was purely descriptive and speculative, has begun to adopt the methods of the exact sciences, in the conviction that the knowledge necessary for lasting progress should be not merely experiments, but rather that these should be of quantitative character" (Editor's Announcement). The purpose of this series of monographs is announced as being to promote this further development of biology as much as possible. The present first volume contains in the first four chapters a very precise representation of the theory of forced movements (Chap. I), recently known from Loeb's earlier publications. The relations of orientation to light, previously set forth in symmetrical animals, are here extended to artificially symmetrical animals or animals made artificially asymmetrical by one-sided blinding, whereby above all the experiments of Holmes on *Ranatra* and those of Garrey on predatory flies find mention. A detailed literature-survey permits not only the finding of the works cited in the text, but also brings, besides, all the other publications suited to a quantitative conception of animal movements and instincts, comprising 554 numbers. In Chap. VI a machine constructed by J. H. Hammond and B. F. Miessner (Electrical Experimenter Sept. 1915, p. 202) is described, which is made light-sensitive by means of selenium-cells and, upon illumination, sets a motor running, in that the selenium-cell struck by the light switches on a motor. When the selenium-cell, so to speak the "eye" of the one side, comes to lie in the shadow, so an electric current is thereby actuated in such a way that it again turns toward the light, and as soon as both selenium-cells are again struck by equal light-intensity, the machine moves toward the light-source. This machine is compared by its inventors with Loeb's explanation of the flight of the moths toward the flame. Loeb here writes: "the actual construction of a heliotropic machine does not merely support the mechanical conception of the will- and instinct-actions of animals, but the theory of the heliotropisms has served as the basis for the construction of the machine. We can therefore say with certainty that no ground exists for ascribing to the heliotropic reactions of lower animals any form of sensation, i.e. of brightness or of color or of pleasure or of curiosity, as the heliotropic reactions of Hammond's machine just as little admit perceptions" (p. 69). An impermissible anthropomorphism Loeb calls the comparison of the movement of a lower animal toward the light with that of the lost wanderer in the wood toward the lighted hut. "As Bohn demonstrated, the final path along which a positively heliotropic animal moves under the influence of two lights shows that the anthropomorphic interpretation in this case is just as false as in any other case. A human being would go to one of two illuminated houses and not to a point between them, which would be determined by the relative intensity of these two lights" (p. 82). The determination of the path of the lower animals, when unequally struck by light, takes place on the ground of the parallelogram of forces (Chap. VIII), as is taken to be valid for the Bunsen-Roscoe law (Chap. IX). "This leaves no doubt that the heliotropic reactions of animals and plants are conditioned by the speed of the photochemical processes" (p. 100). The well-known views of Hess on the total color-blindness of the invertebrate animals and fishes are rejected, since an analogizing of the sensations constated in human beings — "which are heliotropic" (p. 110) — with the orientation of lower organisms is inadmissible, especially as the maximum of the light-action with many lower animals and plants coincides precisely with that of the color-blind human beings. Light, just like gravity (Chap. XIII), acts not as a stimulus, but rather by means of a quantitative mass-displacement of the movements or of the growth-curvatures, whereby a simplification in the explanation of helio- and geotropism is made possible.

Rheotropism, and probably also anemotropism (Chap. XIV), rest upon the fixing of the movement by means of the apparently distant retinal-image of the bank and lead straightway to a movement of the animal in a direction opposite to that of the medium. A particularly good object for the study of the reactions to moving retinal images Loeb found in the Californian lizard *Phrynosoma blainvilli*, which in the moving railway-train, looking toward the window, shows its nystagmus of the head, in that the head, slowly moving in a direction opposite to that of the moving train, wishes to hold the eyes fixed upon objects lying outside — telegraph poles, trees, etc. The head moves, until it is maximally bent, whereupon it is brought back by a quick, jerky movement into a new normal position, and then there follows again the apparent movement of the outer objects, and so forth (p. 129). The chapters (XV) on stereotropism, (XVI) on chemotropism and (XVII) on thermotropism bring nothing essentially new. In the penultimate chapter (XVIII) more complicated instincts are traced back to the influence of special hormones upon tropisms, namely the transformation of the sexual drive by means of circulating substances, as Steinach above all has taught us. In the concluding chapter (XIX) the memory-images to be presupposed in higher animals are mentioned only quite briefly, above all Pawlow's quantitative method for investigating the associations in the dog by means of measuring the flow of saliva. "It is not intended to give the details of Pawlow's results ..., but rather to show that the influence of an associative memory-image is just as exactly measurable as, for example, the direct illumination of the eye; and rather that what we call a memory-image is not a spiritual but a physical agent" (p. 167).

"The production of heliotropism by means of CO₂ in *Daphnia* and the production of the definite courtship of the male A for the female B are similar phenomena, differing merely in the nature of the hormones and the supervening tropistic effects of certain memory-images in the case of courtship."

"Our assumption of the existence of a 'free will' in human beings rests upon the fact that our knowledge is oftentimes not sufficiently complete to render account of the orienting forces, especially when we carry out a previously deliberated act, or when we perform an action which gives us pain or leads to our destruction, and [upon] our incomplete knowledge of the wellnigh endless number of possible combinations and mutual inhibitions of the orienting influence of individual memory-images" (p. 172).

While the reviewer agrees from the heart with the clear experimental results and the interpretation of heliotropic movements of lower animals on the basis of Loeb's theory of symmetrically struck eye-spots, as well as with the rejection of the Hess views, he cannot suppress a misgiving about Loeb's discussions insofar as they extend to man and his psyche. At the beginning of Loeb's book one receives the impression that he rejects a comparison of the causes of animal movements with those of man, because in the latter he regards the sensations as movement-exciting, but does not wish to transfer such [sensations] to lower animals as "anthropomorphism." At the end of the same book, however, even in man every action is placed on the same, purely physico-chemical level as in the rest of the animals. But then can the allegedly different behavior of man from these [animals] not be claimed as a special proof of their tracing-back to physico-chemical processes? The reviewer many years ago (Einleitung in die experimentelle Morphologie [Introduction to Experimental Morphology], Wien, Deuticke, 1904) pointed to the occurrence in man also of the tropisms described by Loeb in lower animals, and his pupil Szymanski (Pflügers Archiv, CXLIII, 25, 1911) was able to demonstrate by experiments the subjection of human orientation-movements to the same quantitative laws as in the lower animals. In particular, the keeping to a diagonal under the action of two movement-stimuli operating at an angle to one another could be clearly shown; Bohn's example therefore does not hold good.¹ By dropping Bohn's thesis Loeb could easily remove every inconsistency.

> ¹ In fact, on beholding two equally large lights, a wavering would set in in the movement of man, which documents his inability to decide freely between the two demanded directions. As long as possible a decision will [be deferred] by the simultaneous keeping-in-eye of both lights, hence movement in the resultant will take place; finally some small difference in the light-action or in the visual acuity, [or] a habit or a memory-image, will turn the scale.

3. **Jacques Loeb and W. J. V. Osterhout** (The Journal of General Physiology, published bimonthly, at 2419–21 Greenmount Ave., Baltimore, Md. by the Rockefeller Institute for Medical Research) have, furthermore, since September 1918 been publishing a journal for general physiology, which was intended to serve the need, arising among North American researchers through the cutting-off of communication with the European continent, to publish experimental-biological works on animals and plants. According to Loeb and Osterhout, experimental biology and general physiology are to be regarded as one and the same, since they are identical in method, namely quantitative experiments, and in aim, namely the explanation of life from the physico-chemical standpoint. It is intended to report continuously in the Archiv für Entwicklungsmechanik on the issues of the new journal as they appear. It is therefore, in order to avoid repetitions, first refrained from discussing the individual works, and only mentioned that Loeb's contribution to the first issue of the first volume (p. 81) deals with the law that determines the amount of regeneration in the stem of *Bryophyllum calycinum*. Just as in some earlier treatises by the same author (Science XLV, 436, 1917; Botanical Gazette LXV, 150, 1918; Annales Institut Pasteur XXXII, 1, 1918), the "investigation of this phenomenon by merely qualitative methods" is rejected as leading to mere idle talk, and a quantitatively demonstrable mass-reaction is set over against Noll's "morphaesthesia," Driesch's "entelechy," the regeneration explained by Weismann's "selection," and finally the "wound-stimulus" of many authors, as the decisive [factor] for the quantity of newly growing tissue produced. (The reviewer published a quantitative growth-theory of regeneration in the Physiolog. Zentralblatt IX, No. 18, 1905, and explained the quantitative course of the regeneration-curve in Arch. f. Entw.-Mech. XLV, 1, 1919, and is pleased at the agreement in the whole manner of consideration.)

4. **D'Arcy Wentworth Thompson** (On Growth and Form, Cambridge, University Press, 1917; 793 pages, 408 illustrations) wrote his book "as an easy introduction to the study of organic form, by methods which are the commonplaces of physical science, in no way new in their application to natural history, which nevertheless naturalists are little accustomed to apply" (Prefatory Note). "So soon as we venture forth on the paths of the physicists, we learn to weigh and to measure, to deal with time and space and mass and their dependent quantities, and more and more to see our knowledge expressed and our needs satisfied through the concept of number, as in the dreams and visions of Plato and Pythagoras; for modern chemistry would have gladdened the hearts of those great philosophical dreamers. But the zoologist or morphologist has hesitated to call to his aid the physical and mathematical sciences, to which the physiologist was eagerly ready [to turn]." "The zoologist has scarcely begun to dream of defining in mathematical language even the simpler organic forms. When he finds a simple geometrical construction, e.g. in the bee-cell, he would rather trace it back to psychic instinct or design than to the operation of physical forces; when he sees in snail or Nautilus or tiny shell of the Foraminifera or Radiolaria a close approximation to the perfect sphere or spiral, then he is, out of old habit, inclined to believe that after all it is something more than a spiral or a sphere, and that in this 'something more' there lies that which neither physics nor mathematics is able to explain" (p. 2). "How far mathematics will suffice to describe the structure of the body, and physics to explain it, no man can foresee. It may be that all the laws of energy and all the properties of matter and all the chemistry of all the colloids are as powerless to explain the body as they are incapable of teaching us to understand the soul" (p. 8). For his part Thompson declares that he does not share this opinion. How it is that the soul informs the body, of this physical science teaches him nothing: consciousness is not explained to him by all the nerve-paths and neurons of the physiologist, "but in the construction and the growth and the working of the body, as in all that is of the earth earthy, physical science is," in his view, "our only teacher and guide" (p. 9). From this standpoint Thompson now subjects that which in organisms is bodily to a mathematical and physical investigation. Beginning with the change of working-capacity under proportional enlargement of a form (Chap. II, following the reviewer's Anw. el. Math. [Application of Elementary Mathematics], p. 10), growth and regeneration are represented in curves and their significance discussed (Chap. III): "Growth-velocity is subject to definite laws, and the velocities in different directions tend to maintain a definite ratio, which is more or less constant for each specific organism; and to this regularity is due the fact that the form of the organism is in general regular and constant." "The phenomenon of regeneration is [bound up] with a strong temporary increase in the velocity of growth (or 'acceleration' of growth) of the injured surface; in other respects regenerative growth is the same as ordinary growth in all its essential phenomena" (p. 154). There now follow the most important chapters of the book, in which, for a great series of forms occurring in living nature, complete analogues are demonstrated in the physics of lifeless formations, [analogues] which here as there can be traced back to surface-phenomena or other force-fields. Let there be mentioned: the cell-division figures (Chap. IV); the outer form of individual cells, namely unicellular ones (Chap. V), and their tracing-back to surface-tensions; conjugation of unicellulars (Chap. VI) and adsorption; cell-aggregates (Chap. VII), cell-divisions (Chap. VIII), bubble-arrangement; concretions, skeletal-needles (Chap. IX) and Liesegang's rings. Whereas Thompson, up to this chapter, was able to ascribe the considered forms not merely to a mathematical rule but also to one of the force-complexes occurring in physics, he is, at the end of this chapter, compelled to concede that for the explanation of the polar arrangement of rays, e.g. in the skeleton of many Radiolaria, alongside the physical forces demonstrable for the secretion and the joining-together of the skeletal parts, an inherent polarity must still be sought: "To say that the arrangement rests on a specific polarization of the cell means simply to state the problem in other words and to set it aside for later solution. But it is possible that we may be able to learn something about the lines along which we have to seek such a solution, if we take into consideration Lehmann's 'liquid crystals' and the light which they throw upon the phenomena of molecular aggregation." "For Lehmann seems indeed, in non-living, chemical bodies, to have shown the existence of precisely such a determinant, of precisely such a 'formative force,' as would be of infinite help to us, such as physics and mathematics might postulate for the explanation of our axial symmetry (e.g.) in the Radiolaria" (p. 486). "But it is otherwise clear that the symmetry of crystallization, whether liquid or solid, offers no exact parallel, but merely a series of analogies in the protoplasmatic symmetry of the living cell" (p. 487).

The remaining chapters deal chiefly with the mathematical representability of spiral forms, such as the fibers in plants (Chap. X), the shells of molluscs (Chap. XI) and foraminifera (Chap. XII), the horns and antlers of mammals (Chap. XIII), the arrangement of leaves or "phyllotaxis" (Chap. XIV), without thereby grasping the essence of the phenomena causally. This part of the book is thus exclusively "descriptive-lawful," but not "causal-lawful" in the sense of Roux's earlier designation; it treats solely the "regular occurrence" in place and time and the co-occurrence of various events, not the causes of the occurrence. In Chapter XV the modeling of the form of eggs, sea urchins (? Ref.) and blood vessels by pressure forces is explained; in the following chapter (XVI) the well-known adaptation of the bones of mammals to mechanical stress is taken as the basis for a technical comparison of the whole skeleton with a bridge.

The last chapter (XVII) is perhaps the most original of all and has as its content a theory of organic transformations which Thompson had published in 1915 (Transactions Royal Society Edinburgh, L., pp. 857–95). "The organic forms, which we can more or less precisely define in mathematical expressions and afterwards strive to explain and trace back in expressions of force, are manifold, as we have seen; but nevertheless they are few in number in comparison with Nature's almost infinite multiplicity" (p. 722). "For many reasons, therefore, there is a great quantity of organic forms which we are able neither to grasp nor to define in mathematical expressions." "But in a very large part of morphology our chief task lies rather in the comparison of related forms than in the precise definition of each one; and the deformation of a complicated figure may be an easily understood phenomenon, although the figure itself had to be left unanalyzed and undefined. This process of comparison, of recognizing in one form a definite permutation or deformation of another, quite apart from a precise and adequate understanding of the original 'type' or measure of comparison, lies within the immediate province of mathematics and finds its solution in the elementary use of a certain method of the mathematician. This is the method of coordinates, on which the theory of transformations is founded" (p. 723). Thompson now shows by various examples that related animal species can, according to their outline or to the outline of individual parts, be transformed into one another on paper by a transformation of the coordinate system in such a way that, once the mode of transformation has been established, point corresponds to point in the transfer, without this having been taken into account beforehand. Thompson then uses this method for the reconstruction of the fossil intermediate stages between two known extremes by gradual uniform transformation of the coordinate system, and as proof of its applicability figures, among others, the intermediate links to be obtained in this way between *Hyracotherium* and *Equus*, beside which he figures the well-matching intermediate species known from the succession of earth strata (pp. 766 and 767). The author hopes by this means to free himself from speculation in phylogenetic reconstructions; he is naturally no friend of selectionist and Lamarckian explanations; thus he rejects (p. 671) any adaptation to the color of the surroundings.

5. **Julius MacLeod** (The Quantitative Method in Biology, in: Publications of the University of Manchester, Biological Series No. II; with 27 figures, 228 pages; Manchester, University Press; New York, London, Bombay, Longmans, Green & Co., 1919) sets forth in the preface: "In physics, chemistry and mineralogy the properties of objects are measured and expressed by numbers, called constants. A constant is independent of any theory whatever; it is the exact expression of a fact. The constants are in truth the building stones by means of which theories are built up, the explanation of the observed facts is found, and the way is opened to new discoveries. In botany and zoology, on the contrary, the properties (characters) of living things are usually described by means of words. We say that a given object is long or short, broad or narrow, oblong or oval, etc. Much progress would be made possible if such designations, as long, broad, short, etc., were replaced by numbers. ... Variability has hitherto been the great obstacle which has made the general use of quantitative data (figures) in the biological sciences impossible. The purpose of the present book is to describe a method by which biological constants can be established ..." (Preface I). "It may be noted that the present book is not in truth a treatise on mathematical biology: mathematical knowledge (Part VI) is only used in order to find out the primordia and the method of measuring them" (Part VII). Since the species concept is a chemical one (Part I), since the living mixture of every kind appears plastic even under the influence of external factors, we would have to establish the possibilities of all the reactions belonging to a kind of organism, in order to be able to include variability in the calculation of the constants (Part II). Since, however, in consequence of deficient knowledge of biological chemistry such a description cannot yet be given, we must for the present content ourselves with other quantities that we can measure. Hitherto the quantitative method has been applied in biology: 1. in the description and classification of species (Linnaeus, Latreille, etc., cf. § 31), 2. in what is in particular called biometry (Quetelet, Galton, Pearson, Weldon, etc., cf. § 32), 3. in the study of crossing (Mendel, cf. § 33), 4. in phyllotaxis, including the flower diagrams (cf. § 40), 5. in the measurement of certain physiological functions and corresponding properties (cf. § 41), for which examples are given (Part III). By a primordium are understood the simple properties of every living being. The primordia of one and the same kind, or even of one and the same individual, do not reach at the same time the final state of equilibrium, at which, in the absence of any further play of forces, the last-measured state continues to persist. Then this particular primordium is grown up (adult). The adult primordium is in every species variable, because the final equilibrium will be reached at another time or in another degree when the external factors differ: this particular equilibrium is the individual adaptation or accommodation (Part IV). "The individual is adapted to its surroundings, not for the purpose of attaining certain physiological advantages, but because it cannot be otherwise" (p. 52). "Strictly speaking, every state of equilibrium ought to be given by an equation in expressions of the forces. In the present state of biology it is impossible to enumerate and to measure the forces which are at play. Therefore we must take refuge in an indirect method, by measuring the material parts which are held in equilibrium by the acting forces. We may count the similar parts (organs, segments, etc.) which are in equilibrium, measure their dimensions, their form (and many are able to be given in expressions of the dimensions), their relative position (angle of deviation, etc. ...), their physical properties [elasticity, etc.], etc." "An important question is to determine at what moment" of growth "the measurements are to be carried out. Here two methods (which the embryologists follow) might be adopted: 1. We are able to divide the time (hours, days, etc.) between the beginning and the end of development into a definite number of intervals, measuring the properties at the end of each such interval." On account of the different developmental velocity of eggs, sometimes even of the same clutch, however, developmental stage and age are not often comparable, on which account this method seldom promises good results. 2. "According to the second method a property is adopted as a measure of comparison or leading property, the development of the others being investigated in comparison with this measure. The choice of this leading property is naturally arbitrary and depends in each individual case on the object investigated. In the development of a fish, a leaf, a petal, etc., the total length of the object is usually a suitable leading property" (p. 62; Part V). As variation is investigated by the measurement of different individuals, so the gradation of the chemical material in the different axes of an organism can be subjected to measurement (Part VIII). By this gradation the body of the living being is distinguished from crystals. The previous comparisons of crystal and living being are based merely on the outer, superficial form and are therefore to be rejected, whereas in reality an analogy exists merely in the inner states of equilibrium. "The value for any arbitrary property of a crystal is invariable along each of its axes" (p. 198). In the organism "we likewise distinguish axes, which are lines of stability." "In a plant or an animal the value of a given property, measured successively at different places along a given axis, is variable, instead of being constant as in crystals. Its variation is regular, i.e. governed by a geometric law, which is to be discovered by measurement in each individual case and may be represented by a curve of gradation" (p. 199). The last part of the book (Part IX) once more recapitulates the fields of application of quantitative biology and gives a botanical example of the method employed by the author of setting up and measuring primordia for the purpose of identifying species. "Identification tables are constructed in the following way, with limitation in one direction or the other." The author "takes, e.g., all the species of a genus, or the species of a family, which are native in a given region or boundary, etc. For each primordium a table is drawn up, in which the minimal and maximal values for each species are given." In the illustrated example four primordia, one ratio, and six kinds of grasses are used. As primordia the lengths of the culms and ears are taken. Concerning zoological examples, reference is made to MacLeod's work on *Carabus*, the publication of which is imminent. Finally attention is drawn to the fact that in the "Bertillonage," as it is used in criminalistics for the purpose of identifying individual men, here too, with the measurement of a sufficient number of characters, the belonging to definite races, or, with application to other animal species or plants, also the species is contained therein, so that such "Bertillonages" are able to serve not merely for the distinction of individuals, but also for the placing of the same into a system.

In the now-following classification of the primordia (Part V) these are, following Bateson, divided into meristic and substantive variations (p. 69). The mode of origin must not be taken up into the classification, since, e.g., the assertion of a convergent adaptation presupposes a great quantity of hypotheses and "appears more suited to conceal the problems for which one is to search than to solve them." Similar forms are rather expressions of similar equilibrium positions, and therefore a precise description of the mutual position of the particles is required. MacLeod "therefore prefers to adopt the method of the mineralogists. Two different substances, which both crystallize in the form of a regular octahedron or of a pentagonal dodecahedron, find themselves in one and the same state of equilibrium. ... Likewise the forelegs of *Gryllotalpa* and *Talpa*, etc., are systems of material particles which stand in equilibrium, for a very simple reason: were they not, then they would be modified (further developed) for so long until a state of equilibrium were reached." "We can expect," until more work were done, "to find in the collected data (numbers!) material for a morphology of the states of equilibrium, which, in a certain sense, could be a crystallography of organisms, quite independent of any theory about their origin and genealogy" (p. 82). "The application of the quantitative method to the study of animals and plants has been founded almost exclusively on the theory of probability (theory of chance)." "In the works in which the theory of probability is set forth, we find a great quantity of mathematical knowledge, whereas facts and realities are mentioned only with few words and regarded as of secondary importance." "The path followed by the theory of probability is slippery ground. From the biological standpoint the theory of probability" (frequency) "ought to be built up by continual connection of observation, measurement, comparison, experiment, and mathematical deduction" (p. 115). How probability and chance are to be made use of quantitatively therefore requires further discussions (Part VI). In contrast to the Gaussian error curve, usually given predominant consideration on the part of variation research, many variations proceed in a definite distribution in the variation groups, not following a simple probability, in that definite groups, "variation steps," are favored in frequency, probably because they represent more stable equilibria (Part VII).

The reviewer hopes that he has succeeded, in the present reviews, which make use to a large extent of the authors' own words, in presenting how these differ essentially not merely in the method and in the field of application of quantitative biology, but also in the purpose of its use. Jacques Loeb, an enthusiastic monist, sees in the quantitative experiment a welcome ally in the struggle for a physico-chemical conception of the biological processes, including those otherwise ascribed to a psyche; D'Arcy Thompson seeks a grasp of the organic form of the animal and plant body by tracing it back to the laws of geometric limitation, as physics teaches us to know them, but renounces the inclusion of psychic processes; MacLeod, finally, wishes to confer new fruitfulness upon the dry soil of an unsatisfying systematics by grafting on a quantitative mode of consideration, whereby he wishes to hold himself aloof from every hypothesis and from every other purpose of research than that of the simple collection of facts. If hereby all three deny a special adaptation of living beings and on the other hand again reject the crystal analogies hitherto advanced, the reviewer would like to remark that this apparently concordant behavior has in each author a different ground and also cannot be justified as a consequence of their other views. Loeb would rather not have a special adaptation, because such a one would make the (physico-)chemical explanation of the phenomenon of life more difficult, and no crystal analogy, because he fears that he would then have to acknowledge, in place of purely chemical mode of reaction in the organism, yet another "formative force." To D'Arcy Thompson adaptation is unsatisfying, because it cannot be grasped in a mathematically-geometrically precise manner, and the crystal analogy, because the forces geometrically determining the crystal cannot without further ado be applied to similar organic forms.

MacLeod, for the avoidance of hypotheses, may regard the adaptations merely as an existing, but not as a state of equilibrium that has come into being, and rejects the previous crystal analogies (but were they all known to him?), because they have compared merely such primordia as would not suffice for the characterization of the species in living beings. For further pursuit of these problems reference is made to the reviewer's "Physiology of Adaptation," which is to appear in Asher-Spiro's Ergebnisse der Physiologie, and to the continuation of his crystal analogies in the further parts of the Archiv für Entwicklungsmechanik. The further development of quantitative biology he conceives to himself as a compilation of formulae which are genetically connected with one another and grant us, after the manner of physics, a causal insight. A larger publication on "Form and Formula in the Animal Kingdom" is in preparation and is to bring the progress hitherto achieved on this path since the publication of the application of elementary mathematics to biological problems (Leipzig and Vienna, Franz Deuticke, 1922).
