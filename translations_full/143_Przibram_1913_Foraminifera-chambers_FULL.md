# The Chamber-Progression of the Foraminifera as a Parallel to the Moulting-Progression of the Mantids.

By

Hans Przibram.

(From the Biologische Versuchsanstalt in Vienna.)

Received on 8 December 1912.

*Archiv für Entwicklungsmechanik der Organismen*, vol. 36 (1913).

> **Full translation.** A complete English rendering of the running text of “The Chamber-Progression of the Foraminifera” (Przibram, 1913), including all tables, figure and plate legends, and footnotes. Numbers and table cells were transcribed from the page images, not the noisy OCR.

In a recently published series of experiments (Przibram and Megušar 1912), *Sphodromantis bioculata* Burm. had as a rule doubled its weight from moult to moult. The measurement of the pronotum length in the same praying mantises yielded a progression with the quotient 1.26. This number is equal to the cube root of 2, and will therefore always appear in measurements of any arbitrary length whenever the enlarging volume retains a similar form and exhibits a progression with the quotient 2. Since in this case there is also the greatest probability that the specific gravity does not change in the course of growth, the doubling of the mass, as an expression of the doubling of the volume, stands in the finest agreement with the increase of the length by the cube root of 2.

When for other purposes I was studying the magnificent work on the Foraminifera by Rhumbler (1909–1911), I unexpectedly found, in the section: The size-progression of the chambers and the mathematical constructibility of the chamber- and shell-form (l. c. p. 175), an almost complete parallel to the moulting-progression of the praying mantises. The Foraminifera grow, namely, out of a spherical "embryonic chamber" in such a way that the sarcode, flowing forth periodically after a resting pause and complete emptying of the faecal matter (cf. Winter 1907), surrounds itself with a new chamber. Both Rhumbler and van Iterson (1907) assume that in this process the whole quantity of the sarcode takes part through swelling and flowing forth, so that the successive size-progression of the chambers is conditioned in its magnitude by the growth-progression of the sarcode. If now one also assumes the size-progression of the chambers to be a regular geometric series, this can, in agreement with the regular periodic increase of the plasma-body, be regarded as the immediate expression of the latter.

Since the later chambers, in contrast to the embryonic chamber, are not spherical, a direct determination of the volume is hardly possible, and here too a separate weighing in the case of the smallness of the objects is excluded, all the more so as for concrete measurements only lengths in the section, or under the microscope on thin-sections, have been able to be observed.

If now the chambers are to follow one another in approximately similar fashion, as tends to be the case with the exception of the embryonic chamber, then a regular geometric progression of any comparable length must yield an equally regular geometric progression of the volume of the successive chambers, by which the increase-quotients of the measured lengths raised to the third power must yield the corresponding quotient for the volume.

As van Iterson already showed by one example (*Triloculina rotunda* d'Orb.) after the figure of Schlumberger (1891), it turns out that the same geometric progression results not only here but also for other Foraminifera through measurements of the chamber-diameter, whether one takes the length or breadth of the chamber, with little deviating quotients.

In the following table the cases adduced by Rhumbler are compiled tabularly for the sake of brevity, just as for the further explanations.

It now turns out, in this compilation, that the average of the diameters agrees rather exactly with the quotient 2. We may add for the cube root of 2, in agreement with the sentence previously put forward in further development of the one established by Iterson and Rhumbler (l. c. p. 177): "The size-progression of the chambers, that is, the increase of the chamber-diameter with the ordinal number of the chambers, will follow a geometric progression" — that this progression will on average yield the quotient cube root of 2 in respect of a length, hence the quotient 2 in respect of the volume.

Now as regards the accuracy of this statement, it must first of all be pointed out that the deviations of the average quotients given by Rhumbler for the individual species (specimens?) from the average number out of all quotients, namely 1.267, mostly deviate less than the quotients declared by the authors to be constant for the successive chambers of one animal (cf. the column: Limiting values of the progression-quotient in our table).

Furthermore, our general average is based on 58 numbers (the sum of the differences out of the ordinal numbers of the successive chambers given in the rubric "chambers taken into account"), whereas each individual average quotient is accorded only 3 to 14 measurements; the number of usable figures, however, as is well known, raises the probability for the significance of the average. Furthermore, the admission must be taken into account that van Iterson already makes with the following words in regard to the applicability of the constant ratio between two corresponding distances in successive chambers: "If one now tests this conclusion on the shells, it turns out that with shells of uniform chamber-arrangement it is correct to a very rough approximation, although quite considerable deviations are not seldom. But consider that the similarity of the chambers is certainly not exactly realized and that one must therefore already be content if the ratio does not vary too greatly."

Rhumbler did not find the constancy of the chamber-progression-quotient confirmed in all Foraminifera-shells. In some species, namely, there changes, in the course of the accretion of ever new chambers, the form of the latter in quite definite ordinal numbers: thus *Biloculina sarsi* Schlumberger (1891; Rhumbler 1903, p. 247) shows from the 15th to the 17th chamber a far more elongated form than before ordinal number 15 and after ordinal number 17. The length-quotient for the 14th to the 15th chamber amounts to 1.7, likewise that for the 15th to 16th and 16th to 17th; that for the 17th to 18th chamber 1.5. The average of the abnormal quotients therefore amounts to 1.65; whereas the average of the chambers 5–14 and 18–21 is almost exactly equal to the cube root of 2; namely 1.25 (instead of 1.26).

Possibly it is the case that in these deviatingly-formed chambers too the quotient for the volume-increase is constant and equal to 2; then the quotients for other linear dimensions than those of the measured lengths would have to exhibit a correspondingly smaller progression. Unfortunately the figures lying before me allow no measurement of the breadths and still less the comparison of the heights of the chambers of *B. sarsi* to be carried through.

It is moreover by no means excluded that here too there comes into play within the form-changing volume-increase a constant unit, whether equal to the cube-root-of-2 unit, or because at the places of the volume-quotient 2 occasionally the quotient 4 is found.

In what manner complete intermediate stages of the form-changing volume-enlargement from moulting to moulting occasionally occur, in order to make comprehensible similar ones in some Foraminifera-forms, is shown by the example of the measurements of Winter (1907) on *Peneroplis pertusus* Forskål, which has quite gently increasing small increase-quotients in length, while the breadth could not be set; here it would be a question of being able to verify in detail, when the quotients lie close to 1, whether some quite small chambers come in between, in which case the dimensions named by the said author, measured at parts, would not agree with their consecutive ones.

According to the figure given by Rhumbler (1909–1911, p. 229) of a more strongly elongated form, the breadth of the chambers XII to XXIX (the earlier ones already yield small absolute values) shows the breadth-increase confirmed at the breadth-quotient 1.43, while one would believe oneself entitled, by means of the average quotients 1.04 and 1.38 occurring there, to the progression-quotient. This average too lies lower than the individual values of the species compiled in the above table; it would however, by its inclusion in the overall average, depress the actually existing constant which lies well above 1.267 to 1.254.

Likewise comparable with the van Iterson and Rhumbler measurements are the winding-quotients which Möller (1878) for various Foraminifera of the Russian coal-limestone gives. These quotients refer in part to the geometric increase-conditions of the snail-like shell-figure, in part to its individual chambers. To this is added that the chambers of many of the species examined fall, through incomplete partitions, into a larger number of subdivisions. Thereby the actually given values become smaller, but the geometric progression need not on that account be torn apart.

A small magnitude of the quotient for the breadth-increase is shown by the first 9 chambers of the so-called B-form, e.g. of *Biloculina bradyi* Schlumb., while the A-form of the same species yields from the beginning the quotient 1.28, which was then also measured for the further chambers of the B-form. The B-form thus turns out to begin with a smaller embryonic chamber, to which the steeper size-progression connects, while the A-form, on the other hand, joins a likewise larger, or also perforated embryonic chamber, gently increasing, so that the chamber is indeed broadened, but the volume not enlarged, when the form is reached that arises with the A-form (Rhumbler 1909–11, p. 186–187, Fig. XLV and curve XLVI).

In the "microspherical" variety the volume increases more strongly than in the "macrospherical" variety, less in the size of the variously sized chambers replacing one another.

For these embryonic chambers an analysis according to the rule of the progression of linear dimensions in the cube root of 2 is not directly possible; rather it could be that the small embryonic chamber begins, gently increasing, with the chamber indeed broadened, but the volume not enlarged. Whereas the "microspherical" embryonic chamber yields a progression with the quotient cube-root-of-2.

While here the rule valid for the progression of the mantid-moultings, of the chamber-progression of the Foraminifera, here the chamber-progression-quotient of the Foraminifera, appears constant, the "microspherical" first chambers of every inclusion of this gradation are to be derived.

Only thus does there lie close here a very interesting parallel. In the description of the spherical Gestalt of the microspheres Rhumbler makes (1909–11, p. 188), which I cannot give better than in his own words:

"As it turns out here, the protoplasm of the microspheres becomes originally less anomalously differentiated, just as in the megaspheres there are interwoven inheritance-plasma; was therefore the sexual origin of the microsphere not unimportant, so here in the analogy then also the difference of the metazoa with their entirely undifferentiated spherical condition of the egg from the differentiated condition of the later embryonic formations rises up. The macrospheres, on the other hand, bring as mere division-products of the mother-animal the more or less ready plasma-differentiation of the mother more or less ready along with them, just as also the spherical differentiation-height of the metazoa from the dividing-condition comes along during their reproduction."

That the microspherical forms of the Foraminifera owe their origin to a two-sexed begetting, against it the macrospherical forms of the same species their origin to the division of individual mother-parts — this is not even a hypothesis, but a fact observed by all other researchers who have likewise occupied themselves with it (Schaudinn 1903, Winter 1907 e.a.).

But if we have the right to compare the microsphere-formation with the embryogenesis, then we may straightforwardly expect a smaller increase-quotient than for the metagenetic growth, for during the cleavage-stages at least the eggs of the metazoa too, so far as we know, increase in volume, and of this the mantids will form no exception, even though we have unfortunately not yet been in a position to investigate the embryonic stages quantitatively.

The cause for the deviation of the embryogenesis from the rule of volume-doubling from stage to stage valid for the later growth (and indeed not in equal times!) can be found by us in this, that in the later growth it is a matter of a proportional enlargement of an already differentiated form, whereas in the embryogenesis it is a matter of the moulding of a form-difference developing itself ever further from stage to stage.

While here the new-additions of one chamber, and on the other hand the casting-off of the skin, designate distinctly stepwise-separated stages of the growth, and in both processes similar quantitative rules have been remarked, it lies close to investigate whether these processes are really "qualitatively so different from one another" as it should appear at first glance.

In the first place it is in both cases a matter of an envelope, secreted from the surface of the animal, becoming too small for its further-growing owner, and of one's having to undertake this enlargement of the all-too-narrow home. But here we at once strike upon a difference: while the insect simply abandons its old skin and contents itself with a rapidly hardening new skin, the new shell of the Foraminifera remains with the old chambers in connection, and the animal inhabits not merely the last chamber, but withdraws itself also again into earlier ones. At least this is the usual process — but not the only occurring one! For in an earlier treatise Rhumbler (1905) had established for the genus *Hormosina* that at each new chamber-formation the sarcode quite leaves the old shell and beside it builds up a new, spherical one. Mostly this new building remains in connection with the old, but the sarcode inhabits only the respective end-chamber. Here therefore it is a matter of a process essentially similar to the insect-moulting, which, in those specimens in which the new chamber separates itself from the old, runs parallel down to the detail.

As unusable for the measurements were regarded thicknesses that did not exceed half a millimetre; further the embryonal chamber of the A-forms, and the chambers of the central part of the B-forms which correspond to it and are shaped differently from the later chambers.

In Table 2 are compiled all the carried-out thickness measurements, as well as the progression-quotients yielding therefrom for the thickness-progression.

If now from all 146 obtained individual quotients the average is drawn, one obtains the number 1.287; if the average is first taken for each measured figure, and then the sum of these averages is divided by the number 35 of the measured figures, one obtains the number 1.285; we are therefore in any case entitled to assume as the general average "at most" 1.29. This general average is thus removed from the presumed third root of 2 = 1.26 by only 0.03 or 2.4 %, and even if we raise the 1.29 to the third power, then this = 2.15 deviates by only 0.15 or 7.5 % from the demanded doubling.

Probably, however, the progression of the shell-mass deviates in reality far less from the doubling than the calculated values yield. In some cases, in which the following [chamber] exhibits, in place of a progression, a regression, or, otherwise expressed, the quotient is a proper fraction, it can be shown that this decrease of the thickness is by no means likely to be bound up with a decrease of the mass. Such quotients, namely, which are smaller than 1, occur precisely then, when the chamber with the higher ordinal number has undergone, relative to the preceding one, such a change of its form that the surface has become larger in proportion to the volume. In such cases the shell-mass must spread out over a relatively greater stretch than otherwise, and therefore, precisely when it attains the same increase-quotient, will yield a lesser thickness.

These cases are: the chamber IV of *Biloculina depressa* A, much more strongly stretched relative to chamber III (quotient 0.89); the chamber V of *B. Fischeri* A, likewise strongly stretched relative to chamber IV (quotient 0.94); the chambers XV and XVI of *B. globulus* B, more strongly drawn out at the vertex than their predecessors (quotients 0.96 and 0.97); and the chamber V of *B. Sarsi* A, more arched at the vertex than chamber IV — although less so than in the foregoing example — (quotient 0.94).

If we find for the quotients moving below 1 sufficient explanations, the question now is why in general a somewhat too high quotient prevails. For this too a motivation can be brought forward from the geometrical relations: as a rule the following chamber encompasses the preceding one somewhat less, so that the shell-mass made available distributes itself over a somewhat smaller circle-segment and can therefore attain a somewhat greater thickness. That we, with this conception, draw near to the truth, those rarer cases show again, in which precisely each later chamber encompasses the earlier one more strongly, as in *Biloculina comata*, and then lower quotients (XXII : XXI = 1.11; XXIII : XXII = 1.12) appear. This latter phenomenon reaches its clearest expression in *Biloculina lucernula* B, whose chambers encompass several preceding ones and whose quotients fall out still lower (XVII : XVI = 1.03; XVIII : XVII = 1.05).

Not only the detailed agreement of the views developed above, the exceptions being explicable, speaks for the significance of the ratio 1.26.

The measurements of one and the same figure, but of different chamber-numbers, namely, yield against one another a stronger deviation than the average of the quotients of a figure deviates from the general average.

The size-increase with the linear quotient "third root of 2" is thus in the average more exactly realised than the constant progression within the examples at all.

The cause of the frequent occurrence of the doubling from moult to moult in the Mantids was assumed: »if every cell of the body has once divided and has grown back up to the original cell-size in each of its division-products, without otherwise having experienced any change in its differentiation, then we shall be able to expect, as the result of this process, a doubling of the weight. If now, precisely after the carrying-out of such a 'division-step', the cuticula that has become too small is cast off, then we obtain precisely with each moult a doubling of the weight.« (Przibram and Megušar 1912, p. 706.)

In contrast to the Mantids, the Thalamophora are to be reckoned among the unicellular [organisms]. As cause for the chamber-progressions, therefore, the splitting into more cells and the growing-up of the newly-formed cells to the old size does **not** come into consideration.

But this difference too does not fall all too heavily into the balance, if we again go back to the more deeply lying cause of the fixed cell-size. This rests indeed upon Richard Hertwig's »nucleo-plasma-relation«, which states that every nucleus of a definite size is capable of gathering around itself only a definite quantity of plasma. With the increase in size the ratio between nuclear-growth and growth of the cell-body always shifts to the disadvantage of the former, so that a continual disturbance of the relation takes place, which finally, at a definite value, leads to decay.

If we apply the same consideration to unicellular beings, then we shall have to expect either a complete division, a propagation-process, as soon as the shifting of the relation has reached this value, or else a fragmentation of the nucleus and a scattering of the part-pieces into the plasma-mass, without division of the latter, will be able to take place.

This latter alternative seems in the Foraminifera to be realised. Already Schaudinn (1903) remarked that in the five-chambered Polystomella crispa the nucleus had repeatedly divided, and that already earlier multiple nuclei are demonstrable. Winter (1907, p. 94) states for Peneroplis pertusus that the chambers are laid out comparatively more rapidly than the advancing of the chromatin proceeds. This could be explained by the fact that only after the cell-body has grown up to a definite size (namely, almost regularly, about the double) does the bursting of the old fetter take place, and only then does the fragmentation and re-distanced arrangement of the nuclei take place.

In the incomplete isolation of the influence-sphere belonging to each nucleus within the sarcode could further lie that factor which effects a deviation of the progression-quotient downward from the doubling, and precisely in this species. It is a conclusion following logically from the conception of the nucleo-plasma-relation, that with incomplete isolation the shifting of the relation decisive for it, to the disadvantage of the nucleus, would have to enter earlier than with completely isolated influence-spheres. Perhaps, therefore, even to those differences in the progression-quotients that are tied to the species a significance could be demonstrated, if we have exact investigations concerning the nuclear-division relations.

For periodic fluctuations of the progression-quotients of chamber-breadths or -lengths of individual abnormally appearing exemplars Rhumbler (1909—11, p. 211) has uncovered the cause in the fluctuating salt-content and the therewith fluctuating osmotic pressures at their finding-places. These are Aestuaria-forms of Polystomellae and Nonioninae: »This explicable deviation only makes the approximation to the course of an exponential curve in the other progression-curves stand out all the more drastically; it argues against a chance-ness in the similarity of constructed and empirical curves« (l. c. p. 212).

### Summary

1) The progressive size-increase of the chambers built up one after another by the Foraminifer yields, for the average of analogous linear dimensions, 1.267, a number which comes very close to the third root of 2 (= 1.26).

2) From this it may be concluded, under the presupposition of the retention of similar form and the participation of the whole Sarcode in each chamber-building, that the volume-increase and, with it, the mass-increase of the animal, from the building of one chamber to the building of the next, takes place in the third power of this number, thus almost exactly by doubling.

3) Measurements of the shell-thicknesses of successive shells yield an average of 1.29, which therefore likewise deviates only little from the third root of 2.

4) From this the conclusion may again be drawn that the mass of the deposited shell experiences a doubling from chamber to chamber.

5) In all these points we find a parallel to the growth-quotients of the praying mantis *Sphodromantis bioculata* from moult to moult and from skin to skin.

6) Differences lie in this, that

a) in the Foraminifera the newly-built-on shells remain in connection with the old ones, and mostly (but not always! — *Hormosina*) the animal also inhabits the old ones, whereas the Mantids cast off the skin entirely with each moult;

b) the Foraminifera, as unicellular beings, cannot carry out the doubling of the volume through division of each cell with the formation of cell-boundaries and subsequent growing-on of each cell to the original size (yet before the chamber-additions a fragmentation of the nuclei does take place! — *Orbitolites* etc.).

7) The explanation for the periodic entry of physiological states, which with necessity bring about a new building-up of double size, can in both cases be found in the nucleo-plasma-relation.

This [relation] must, with increasing growth, shift to the disadvantage of the nucleus, and at a definite size-ratio between the nucleus and the plasma-region governed by it leads to division; now each time the ratio for a new division will be reached again, when the same quantity of plasma is again at the disposal of each nucleus; for two nuclei this will thus be the double mass, so that the doubling of the mass always concludes a new division-step (presupposed that the division of all nuclei takes place synchronously).
