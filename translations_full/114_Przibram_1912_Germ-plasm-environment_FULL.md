# The Environment of the Germ-Plasm.

## I. Programme of Work.

By

### Hans Przibram.

The question of the inheritance of characters acquired in the course of life has entered a new stage.

Nowadays it is hardly necessary to discuss whether characters which have arisen through the influence of the external world become visible in the descendants, or whether they cannot rather be traced back to the earlier hereditary constitution of the still-undifferentiated young.

That this was answered in the affirmative sense for by far the larger groups of animals and plants, hardly anyone any longer maintains; rather the question is now left open as to in what way the alteration is carried over to the descendants.

I have, on the occasion of the compilation of the cases experimentally tested in the animal kingdom (Experimental-Zoology. 3. Phylogenesis. 1910. p. 241), formulated three possibilities in the following sentences:

»α) New¹) hereditary characters arise, without reference to somatic alterations, in the germ-cells, when these can be struck by external factors (germinal variation, germinogenic inheritance).

β) New hereditary characters can, under the influence of external factors, arise first in the soma and then be transferred from the soma to the germ-cells, although the latter

> ¹) By »new« is meant only the contrast with the »old« characters openly present in the parents; whether these new characters are, in the sense of reversions, »old« or not, can mostly not be exactly demonstrated.

need not be struck by the external factors (somatogenic inheritance, or somatic induction).

γ) New hereditary characters arise in the soma and in the germ-cells in adequate manner, as soon as the latter have been reached by the same factors as the former (parallel-induction).«

At the same place a brief critique of the arguments speaking for and against each of these possibilities was attempted.

A decision could not be reached, chiefly because we possess all too scanty knowledge of the »environment of the germ-cells« — whether as such there come into consideration merely the body of the parent-animals or the outer world in the wider sense.

Indeed it is downright striking how little one has striven to establish under what physico-chemical conditions the germ-glands stand, although such far-reaching conclusions as their complete independence from the soma (Weismann) or the impossibility of their being reached by the external factors which alter the soma (Semon) are represented with all sharpness.

To fill this gap seemed to me all the more important, as I perceive in the exact knowledge of the »environment of the germ-cells« a conditioned presupposition for it, that no a priori incorrect positions can be taken up in our attempts to explain the inheritance of acquired characters.

It would be regrettable if we, freed by the experiment from one speculation, should now once again fall straightway into a second, which up to now at least lacks the real basis in equal measure.

The programme of work for the investigation of the »environment of the germ-cells« comprises two larger complexes of questions: first, the establishment of the physical conditions under which the germ-glands normally stand in the body; second, the alterations which these conditions undergo upon alteration of the environment (external factors and soma); and third, the reciprocal relations between the germ-glands and the rest of the body, between their inner world and their »environment« in the narrower sense.

As methods for the establishment of the physical conditions, direct measurement of the degree of action of the factors coming into consideration is first of all to be brought to bear immediately upon the germ-glands.

This is not always easily possible; it may then at first suffice to bring registering-substances in place of the germ-glands, which permit the efficacy of the factor to be established upon removal.

Where the penetrability of the soma for an external factor would presumably not be altered by death or by the removal of body-sections, the procedure will be further simplified, in that one simply uses somatic substances through which the external factors could reach the germ-glands by the shortest path. The same methods, combined with the arbitrary alteration of the strength of each external factor and with the investigation of artificially altered animals, will also contribute to the solution of the second complex of questions.

More difficult does the third part of the investigation present itself, in that it will at first be a matter of establishing whether differences relative to their environment occur in the germ-cells, even granted a uniform surrounding soma. For it would namely be precarious, even if we had proven the possibility of a penetration of the external factors as far as the germ-cells, still to exclude that the germ-cells could defend themselves against this influence — that is, to trace their possible alteration not, after all, back to the direct efficacy of these factors.

The germ-cells themselves, then — the inner world of the germ-glands — must also be investigated with respect to their physical properties. For the most part this will have to be done on the spot (an Ort und Stelle); of the lesser part it may also be permitted to be tested after its removal.

In particular, the methods will take shape variously according to the object and especially according to the external factor to be tested. If we take the eight external factors adduced by Davenport, namely 1) Chemical, 2) Moisture, 3) Density, 4) Mechanical, 5) Gravity, 6) Electrical, 7) Radiant, and 8) Heat and Energy, then it is asked which of these we should test on which objects.

In many cases there would be open doors to be forced — that is, if we first wanted laboriously to investigate whether the factor is able to penetrate as far as the germ-cells; in many cases experiments are already known which settle the question for certain classes of animals. It comes chiefly down to subjecting to the test those objects with a definite external factor for which the greatest difficulty of the penetration of their soma is to be expected; for if we choose those, we shall be all the more entitled to conclude to a general mode of action.

For the purpose of selecting the objects, it is therefore advisable to consider the groups of the animal kingdom with respect to their probable penetrability for each individual one of the eight external factors.

### 1. The Penetrability of the Soma for Chemical Agents.

Although nutritive substances of very various composition can be taken up by one and the same animal species, hitherto little influence of the chemical composition of the nourishment upon the variability of the marks has been observable. This phenomenon has its cause chiefly in the breaking-down of the nourishment which takes place by digestion, and through which finally the same chemical compounds come to the body-cells as building-stones.

Two groups of exceptions can, however, be observed, in which the chemical composition of the nourishment is not without influence upon the appearance of those nourished by it. The one group is formed by such substances as undergo no decomposition during digestion, or at least penetrate into the body-tissues before their complete breaking-down; the second group is formed by such substances as yield, for the body, poisonous waste-products which are not smoothly excreted before they could exert their poisonous action.

Of the first group there may be adduced the various fats, which are resorbed unaltered by the intestinal wall of the warm-blooded animals and then can be found again in the fat-deposits of the body with their characteristic marks, as Pfaundler jun. and other physicians have found. It is not known to me whether these various kinds of fat are able to penetrate as far as the germ-cells, but it is probable that they are at least deposited in a certain quantity in the fat-tissue surrounding the germ-cells.

Already at the transition of fat-soluble dye-stuffs into the germ-cells, this we know from Stowski in moths, Gage and Riddle have shown it in guinea-pigs and chickens, while the embryo of the sucklings left the Pyrrol-blue in Goldmann's experiments harmlessly behind, whereby the embryonic-water, however, was tinged. In all these examples it is naturally not a matter of an inheritance of acquired characters, but of a purely mechanical passage of the dye-stuff into those cells of the body which, being fat-containing, form a solvent for it. For our question, however, the interest lies in the fact that the chemical substance penetrates the body and enters as far as the germ-sites, or even into the germ-cells. Were it therefore able to call forth some alteration of marks, it could do this both in the soma and in the germ-plasm; indeed it would even come by preference precisely to the analogous tissue of the germs. It now appears, to be sure, not easy to find compounds which satisfy both requirements, namely the unaltered passage through the nutritive wall and the calling-forth of definite marks. The direct alteration of the colour itself cannot, as said, be regarded as the acquisition of a new mark. The quantity of the dye-stuff thereby remains unaltered.

More practical interest is therefore claimed by the second group, of substances resisting species-specific assimilation, namely those with poisonous action. Hither belong Houssay's experiments with meat-feeding in fowls, which in several successive generations led to a progressive hypertrophy of the horn-substances, and finally to degeneration. Here, in the intensification, an action of inheritance shows itself; the influencing of the germ-cells could very well come about through the direct action of the poisonous excretion-products upon the sperm- and egg-cells in the course of development.

The difficulty, in this case, of distinguishing between the direct action of the poisonous products and the eventual influence of the altered somatic cells upon the germ-glands, makes this object appear little suited, although perhaps by transplantation the sex-glands could be withdrawn from the influence of the far-altered soma. On the other hand, a suitable object might be found in the cyclopean malformations obtained by Stockard in fishes through treatment of the embryos with magnesium solutions. If magnesium were able to penetrate through the fish-body as far as the germ-cells, it could also call forth in them alterations which again yield cyclopes. By using live-bearing fishes, the first generation of cyclopes could be obtained by breeding from normal two-eyed parents in magnesium, and thus the independence of the germ-cell affection from its presence in the parent-soma could be demonstrated. The experiments on the penetrability of the soma for chemical agents shall therefore set in here, and it shall first be tested whether the magnesium-mixture in question can be found within the fish-body, in the surroundings of the germ-cells; then whether it has access to the germ-cells themselves; finally, whether it would be able to set up analogous alterations upon action on the still-immature and on the fully developed germ-products.

In lower, invertebrate animals the penetration of magnesium or other salts into the body-interior undoubtedly takes place, according to all that we know of the permeability of their body-walls for salt solutions. It will chiefly be a question of whether any protective measures against penetration into the germ-cells are present, and whether examples can be found of alterations which, called forth by such a salt in the soma, would again come to appearance in the germ at its otherwise uninfluenced development.

Important for the further analysis of the hereditary path of chemicals would be the examination of the quantitative distribution of a substance dissolved in the body-fluids or otherwise penetrated, upon the various tissues of the soma on the one hand, upon the various zones or other hereditary anlagen of the germs on the other. This problem already touches the inner world of the germs. When a chemical substance passes over to the germ, it could at first be quite diffusely dispersed in the undifferentiated germ; but as soon as the differentiation into the various tissues sets in, it would distribute itself, quite analogously as in the parents, in various proportional ratio upon the various tissues, and thereby could call forth the analogous alterations as in the parent-soma. In this way the localization of alterations upon their transition to the descendants could be brought nearer to our understanding.

### 2. The Penetrability of the Soma for Moisture.

Repeatedly has there been reported on the influencing of the soma by altered moisture-content and on the retention of the alteration through the descendants put back: let one be reminded of the butterflies of Pictet, of the beetles of Towers, of the salamanders of Kammerer. Since the germ-products lie more or less embedded in a fluid medium, it appears at first almost impossible that an alteration in the water-content of the surroundings of the soma could make a difference in the moisture surrounding the germ. Yet observations by Bataillon on the germ-products of the lamprey have already shown that at certain times of the spawning-period such a thickening of the content of the sex-glands takes place that ordinary river-water acts upon them like a dilute solution, namely calls forth blastotomy and thereby double-formations. It is therefore from the outset also not to be excluded that an altered moisture-content within the soma alters the moisture-content of the germ-sites. The question must rather first proceed thus: whether the alteration of the moisture-degree of the environment of the soma, when it alters marks of the latter, alters the moisture-content of the soma.

This is indeed by no means necessary; the emerging alterations could rest merely upon a stimulus-action of quite another kind released by the external moisture-degree. But even if it should turn out that to the alteration of the mark there actually corresponds an alteration of the somatic moisture-content, it would thereby not without further ado be certain that the action exerted by the somatic moisture-fluctuation upon the germ would have to come to expression in an immediate moisture-fluctuation of the same. For the moisture could very well alter the germ in such a way that it would later show the alteration parallel to the parental soma, also as regards its own moisture-content. But this need not come to expression in the moisture-content at the first stages of development, just as little as characters of the altered parent-imago can be recognized in the young before the completion of their metamorphosis. The absence of a moisture-fluctuation in the germ-cells could therefore not be regarded as an argument against the direct action of altered moisture upon

the germ-cells.

The procedure for examining the possibility of a penetration of the moisture-alteration as far as the germ-cells will therefore first have to take its beginning in the determination of the moisture-content of the unaltered and of the altered soma, as moist environment of the germ-glands. Then it will be examined whether the germ-glands themselves are so affected by this fluctuation are touched, so that they themselves exhibit recognizable changes in their water-content. The absence of this change must not deter us from searching for other actions of the moist environment upon the germ-glands, which could demonstrate the reaching of the germ-cells by the altered moisture.

### 3. The Penetrability of the Soma for Altered Density.

In the case of animal species living exclusively in water, no change in the moisture of the surroundings can indeed take place, but a change in the density of the water can. Changes of the forms upon altered salt-concentration were, as is known, already described long ago by Schmankewitsch in *Artemia*, a lower crustacean species; unfortunately his experiments, despite the repeated occupation of other researchers with the same genus, have not yet been repeated in the proper manner, so that above all it still appears unclear whether a transmission of the changes to the descendants takes place at all upon their being put back into the original conditions.

Experiments would therefore really have to be set up more for the establishment of this transmission than for the establishment of whether the altered osmotic pressure is able to penetrate as far as the germ-cells. For as much as we know up to now about the osmotic conditions of the lower animals, these are namely permeable to salts, at least to the degree that to a heightened external concentration there corresponds a heightening of the inner salt-concentration, although no perfect permeability need prevail. In the higher animals, in part also in the amphibious lower animals, the inner pressure becomes ever more independent of the surroundings¹); but I know of no examples of the alteration of such animals through altered concentration of the medium.

> ¹) Interesting are the conclusions which E. Quinton (l'Eau de Mer milieu organique. Paris, Masson, 1904) has drawn from this with regard to the retention of the original osmotic pressure of the seas even in the vertebrates.

### 4. The Penetrability of the Soma for Mechanical Agents.

The transmissibility of mechanical injuries, insofar as they are not accompanied by pathological phenomena and by phenomena touching upon the change of the chemism, is still to be regarded as an open question. Vigorously combated, namely by Weismann, on the ground that we could not see by what path the germ-cells were supposed to be reached, its complete rejection nevertheless leaves with many an uneasy feeling, because in the literature a great number of cases are known which would then have to be ascribed to a mere chance in the coincidence of the same or of a similar mutilation in parent and child. The question is whether we have any right at all to deny the possibility of a process because we are unable to state how it could have come about. If we wished to apply this principle to the inorganic world, then it would presumably be ill-served, for example with the actions of magnetism, indeed with all apparent »action-at-a-distance.« But the argument is not even sound in this respect, that a conception of the possibility of an action of the injury of the parent-animal upon the germs would be excluded. If we think of the germ-cells as not closed off against the changes affecting their environment — and this we must in any case do, in the face of the facts of the transmissibility of characters newly evoked by other factors — then an exchange between the substances of the soma and of the germ-cells is possible. It is not necessary to fall back upon Darwin's pangenesis, contradicted by many experiments and facts, in order to derive an adequate material change of soma and germ-cells. For if we think of the equilibrium-condition suggested by the regeneration- and compensation-experiments, then in the case of an injury the substances usable for the repair will arrive at this place. If now, with strong consumption, no halt is made even at the germ-cells, then we can see that an impoverishment of the germ-cells precisely in those substances which were removed by the mutilation can take place.

The impoverishment in particular substances can of course only be adduced for defects of the organs or body-regions concerned, but not for the precisely localized reappearance. In fact, however, even this precision leaves very much to be desired. Many circumstances will always stand opposed to the transmission of defects, above all the power of regeneration, which belongs precisely to the germ-cells and embryos in a high degree (on which I, in my Introduction to the experimental morphology of animals, 1904, page 123, have already pointed out). But also the termination of the wound-healing-process or of the regeneration at the parent will, even when no complete regenerate has come about, make possible a recovery of the germ-cells. Therefore, in all experiments hitherto set up on warm-blooded animals concerning the inheritability of mutilations, the injury just after birth was perhaps a moment especially unfavorable for positive results. The cases of apparent transmission of scars and other consequences of mutilation that have most often become accidentally known refer, on the other hand, to descendants one of whose parents had suffered the loss shortly before or during sexual maturity. Perhaps, by variation of the time at which mutilations are set, we shall yet arrive at conditions under which a transmission is at least regularly simulated¹).

Also the transmission of consequences of mechanical claiming-upon through use or non-use could, as an altered production-quantity of the form-building substances in question, in general likewise, in the corresponding distribution-quantity, impart to the germs an altered stamp. But faultless cases of such transmissions are also still outstanding¹). From these considerations the adherents of the influenceability of the germs by the soma will derive a support for their view; this much can well be granted, that an inheritance of scars and consequences of use, if it occurs, cannot be referred to a reaching of the germ by the mechanical insults or claiming-upon itself. Only in the case of pressure-action could it in general come to an analogous deformation of the germ-cells; air-pressure would here, on account of its uniform distribution, be the most fitting. Perhaps the alteration can be achieved through precisely this, or the transmission then tested (Alpine flora?).

About the actions of force-exertions (among which sound-waves too are to be reckoned) and about the transmissibility of properties acquired in such a way, nothing at all is known.

### 5. The Penetrability of the Soma for Altered Gravity.

It is not to be doubted that all parts of the animal body are subject to gravitation, and that it is only with difficulty possible to

alter the gravitational conditions of the soma without also putting the germ-cells into an analogous environment. Yet changes of the normal force of gravity are seldom of significant effect upon the development of eggs, and even developed animals react for the most part merely with changes of their orientation or with pathological tearings-apart, when they are withdrawn from the normal force of gravity by the centrifuge. Those substances which are separated out by the centrifuging in the egg — no matter whether the latter is exposed with or without the ovary — remain without influence upon the normal development (experiments on *Cumingia* among others, cf. T. H. Morgan, Cytological Studies on Centrifuged Eggs. Journal of Exper. Zoölogy. IX. No. 3. 1910. p. 611ff.). We have therefore here found no example which would be suited for our problem. A pointer to how easily an inheritance of a property acquired through centrifuging, namely of keeping oneself in equilibrium on the centrifuge, could be simulated, is shown by the experiments of Stanislaus von Stein (Die Wirkung des kontinuierlichen Zentrifugierens auf die Entwicklung von Eiern, Kücken, Fischen und Meerschweinchen. Leipzig, O. Leiner, 1910). He himself writes (p. 33): »On a rotating disc 2 m in diameter (12 to 13 revolutions in the minute) chicks that had slipped from the egg on the centrifuge and had lived there, ran and kept their equilibrium well, while on the floor they either stood still or went about slowly and feebly, and easily stumbled. The quite opposite appearance was shown by those hatched by the hen: on the rotating disc they squatted, stretching out one leg toward the periphery in order to support themselves.«

Thus, under the influence of the rotation, the special ability is worked out, at greater velocities than the rotation of the earth, of preserving equilibrium and moving freely. — Had Stein also obtained the eggs, lying on the centrifuge, of hens that had themselves already lived on the centrifuge and had acquired this property of equilibrium-preservation, then one could easily believe that the chicks had already inherited this property, since they could keep themselves in equilibrium right at hatching; but according to Stein's experiment this was to be ascribed to the direct influence of the centrifuging upon the embryos. The centrifuged chicks of Stein remained strongly behind in growth, even when a few were definitively taken down from the centrifuge. Stein obtains descendants of these too and holds in prospect a further publication about their growth. It would be of interest if he would here also make experiments concerning their behavior on the floor and on the centrifuge, in order to test whether the young had received, from the centrifuged parents, the equilibrium-preservation on the centrifuge.

### 6. The Penetrability of the Soma for Electrical Processes.

Experiments on the inheritability of properties altered by electricity (or magnetism) have not hitherto been set up, at least not in the zoological domain.

It would probably not be difficult to inform oneself about the electrical behavior of the body-interior surrounding the germ-cells, yet under the given circumstances this investigation would be of no immediate value for our problem. That the body-interior could, despite the relatively poor conductivity of most tissues, become an electrical field, is to be foreseen. Experiments would best be set in with medium-strong currents, since high-frequency currents are wont to be conducted off along the surface of the body.

### 7. The Penetrability of the Soma for Radiant Energies.

The almost complete penetrability of all living tissue for certain rays is at present so well known that there is hardly need to name them as Röntgen-, Becquerel-, Radium-rays etc. For the questions which now occupy us, these kinds of radiation are therefore of only subordinate significance, because they do not belong to those factors which in nature could have played a part in the transformation of the species. From the literature on their influence upon living beings, moreover, scarcely any data are to be found which would let any inheritability of the marks altered by them be recognized. To be adduced would be at most the property of the Röntgen-rays of so altering the spermatozoa (e.g. of the toad, cf. Bardeen, Abnormal development of toad ova fertilized by spermatozoa exposed to the Röntgen-rays. Journ. of Exp. Zoöl. IV. 1907. No. 1. p. 1—44) that the eggs fertilized with them become malformed embryos. What is known is that the Röntgen-rays act upon the testicle and ovaries to a high degree, can even call forth sterility; so the result might be reached too, if not the sperm itself, but rather the father were exposed to the Röntgen-rays.

A far greater interest than the named rays is, however, claimed by the light-rays. The differently bright and differently colored surroundings of the animals is indeed in a position to bring forth great change in the coloration and other marks.

Unlike the Röntgen-rays, the light-rays do not penetrate into a body according to the degree of the specific density of a body, but follow quite other laws, which have to do with the chemical composition and the physical structure. As is known, such systems as let the light through are called transparent. Many animals are just as transparent as glass; with these it is self-evident that the light is able to penetrate even up to the germ-sites. To be tested are therefore namely strongly pigmented forms, and among these we shall have to select such whose soma is demonstrably altered by different illumination, whereby then this change reappears in the young set back under the original illumination-conditions. Such cases have namely been described by P. Kammerer (Zeitschrift f. induktive Abstammungslehre. IV. p. 280. 1911).

In some animal groups which possess a coloration influenceable by light (cf. Kammerer, Umschau. X. p. 133. 1911) we meet with a very deeply pigmented peritoneum, which would be suited to protect the germ-sites against too strong penetration of light. Possibly this is a device for lowering the influence of illumination, that is, therefore, for making the bearers more independent of illumination. The descendants of illumination-altered lizards obtained so far show that this pigment-mantle does not in any case wholly prevent the transmission of the light-change.

For the testing of the penetrability for light the fire-salamanders are very suitable, since they correspond to all requirements: they are influenced by the illumination in a transmissible way, have such a size that with them it is good to operate, are very strongly pigmented and equipped with two strongly mutually divergent colors.

### 8. The Penetrability of the Soma for Altered Temperature.

In regard to the reachability of the body-interior through altered temperature of the outer world, the animals fall into two great groups: the variable-temperature or poikilothermic, and the warm-blooded or stenothermic. The former alter the temperature of their body-interior parallel to the outer fluctuations, and it is to be assumed that the germ-cells too are affected by this fluctuation to almost the same degree. Transmissible characters which have been evoked by temperature-change in poikilotherms can therefore, like the temperature-varieties of insects, without further ado be traced back to a direct parallel influencing of the germ-cells, which indeed also finds its confirmation in the fact that e.g. in Towers' experiments on beetles a change of the descendants takes place even when the already transformed parents, incapable of further change, were exposed during the maturation of the germ-cells to the temperature-fluctuation in question. It is not excluded that altered descendants could also be obtained from butterflies that are no longer themselves changed but are exposed during the last pupal period to altered temperature — an experiment which the previous observers (Standfuss, Fischer, Schröder) have not yet carried out. Quite different at first glance appear the conditions to lie with the stenotherms: birds and mammals maintain, with few exceptions (winter-sleepers), a constant inner temperature, and that even in the face of very great fluctuations of the outer temperature. I have therefore myself believed that here we could very easily decide whether the altered soma exerts an influence upon the germ-cells which could not also be traced back to the direct action of the warmth (cf. Transmissions of acquired properties in mammals: Experiments with heat-rats. Verhandlungen der Gesellschaft deutscher Naturforscher und Ärzte. 81. Vers., Salzburg. II. 1. Leipzig, Vogel, 1910. page 179). In a discussion of the experiments brought forward by me at the Salzburg Congress of Naturalists, Professor Loewit (Innsbruck) drew attention to whether perhaps, after all, a temperature-rise might not have been present in the interior of the parent that was exposed to a very high temperature (35° C.), since in little-ventilated rooms such an overheating of the body, reminiscent of fever, occurs. Although now, in our experiment-cages, a sufficient ventilation was present, it nevertheless seemed to me worth the trouble to test once again how far the inner temperature of the warm-blooded is really uninfluenced by the outer temperature. Looking into the literature likewise led to pointers that the independence does not need to hold wholly even within normally occurring outer temperatures (cf. recently: Jules Lefèvre, Chaleur animal et Bioénergétique. Paris, Masson, 1911. 1107 pages); thus for example the body-temperature of man in the tropics is about 0.3° C. higher than in Europe, and indeed independently of race, and at an outer temperature of 47° C. the body-temperature in California rose 0.5° C. above the normal. As slight as these fluctuations are, they nevertheless give one to think.

That the warm-blooded too do not preserve a wholly constant temperature even upon temperature-lowerings has been demonstrated namely through experiments with baths. Lefèvre thereby found the interesting fact of a step-wise falling of the body-temperature, whereby the animal strove, at each step, to keep the now lowered temperature constant again.

For us it is namely a matter of whether, in those cases in which a transmission of a mark-change evoked by altered temperature has taken place, a fluctuation in the body-temperature was to be ascertained; such an example is afforded by Sumner's measurements on mice that had been kept at different temperatures, and likewise by my heat-rats. The low temperatures of Sumner exclude the influence of an overheating.

Our problem demands, for the reaching of the germ-cells through warmth-fluctuation, the following answerings: How far does the inner temperature of the body fluctuate with the outer temperature? Do the germ-cells enjoy a special isolation against possible fluctuations? Are the temperature-fluctuations which might be able to penetrate as far as the germ-cells strong enough to be able to influence the germs in parallel manner with the soma? Without wishing in any way to anticipate the answer thereto, I should like to point out that evidently the body-tissues too (skin excepted to a certain degree) cannot be struck by the full force of the outer temperature in the stenotherms.

The preceding pages may suffice, in order to make our program precise. It remains left to the individual workers, who carry out the experiments, to discuss the methodics and literature in detail; therefore I refrain from a particular bibliography. Up to now there have taken part in the works of the program in our institute: for chemical agents — A. Walther; for moisture — S. Morgulis; for light — S. Šećerov; for warmth — E. Congdon. The experiments will, each according to the reaching of a more or less concluding result on some partial question, be published as continuations of this essay, but under the name of the experimenter in question.
