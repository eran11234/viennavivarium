The present communication supplements the one that preceded it under the same title²⁾ in two respects: the investigation is extended to somewhat more highly organized animals, namely the rotifers, and in addition, on the movement of the paramecia, the relation derived by Einstein for the Brownian movement, but valid for any disordered movement, between the mean square of displacement λ̄² and the diffusion coefficient D, λ̄² = 2Dt, is tested.

The observation of the rotifers (*Hydatina*) was carried out exactly as with the infusoria, except that now the very convenient telescope-magnifier of Zeiss (objective +8.3) was used to advantage. The observation interval was again 4 seconds.

I would like here right away to forestall the misunderstanding as if far-reaching conclusions about the psychic behavior of the animals could be drawn from this investigation, say in the sense of the absence of a conscious will. The only inference that has emerged so far is merely that the animals, under the conditions described, are subjected to stimuli that change purely at random in time and place; as such there come into consideration in the first place: the proximity of the cover glass and the slide, the presence of other animals, irregularity in the distribution of food substances, etc. It might most readily be possible, perhaps, to find from a determination of the lower limit for the time interval, for which the movement is still disordered, in various animal species a relative measure for the persistence of an impulse of will. The movement studied here also differs from the Brownian in that the complication of the path does not, as with the latter, continually increase with decreasing time interval. For if one considers, say, the drawing of the path of a little animal reproduced in the previous communication and imagines the points entered always after a fraction of a second instead of from 4 to 4 sec., then as a rule a strong change of direction of the path will occur only after the passing of a series of points, and over such a stretch the relation λ̄² prop. t will of course not be fulfilled.

This difference in the character of the movement also justifies the introduction of a mean path velocity, which with the Brownian movement, as is well known, is not feasible. It may be noted further that from this mean velocity at least the order of magnitude of the per-second work performance of the little animals in the movement in the horizontal plane can be calculated. If one imagines a spherical little animal of radius 10⁻² cm moving in a straight line with a uniform velocity of 0.07 cm/sec¹⁾ and applies the Stokes resistance formula K = 6 π μ a v (K force,

As to the diffusion coefficients, let me first recall that in general, as is well known, the quantity of a stoff [substance] diffusing through a cross-section is defined by the concentration gradient of the size of this stoff per unit cross-section, this passing through in the opposite sense, whereby it is immaterial whether the quantity of the stoff is ordinarily moved by the weight, or as in the suspensions in a new application of the previous experiments by the particle numbers, presupposing that the same establishment of the concentration gradient is met.

The determination of the diffusion coefficients of the *Paramaecium* followed a modification of the method of Schuhmeister²⁾. The apparatus consists essentially of a glass tube of wide bore, whose cross-section is just so large that two of the two abutting tube pieces are inserted. With closed cock the volume of one tube equals that of the other plus the tube neck (height above this tube neck of cylindrical spaces 5 cm, height of the neck 1.2 cm). The two free upright spaces can be closed off by means of glass plates, the apparatus pressed together, sealed. The apparatus is held vertical with the short cock-tube at the bottom and a plate placed underneath, and is filled through an open cock with the liquid with the little animals in it. If one now computes D according to the Einstein formula D = λ̄²/2t from the λ̄²/t = 0.001 given for these paramecia at 15° in the earlier communication, then D = 0.000125 results, hence of another order of magnitude! The agreement is better than was to be expected with the small number of observations and their fluctuating nature.

A more precise investigation with various animal species and under various conditions would for this reason be of interest, because from the degree of agreement a decision could be reached whether the spreading of the animals over a space placed at their disposal takes place through mere diffusion, or whether they additionally have a tendency to move themselves out of the crowd. In the example communicated here the diffusion in consequence of the disordered movement alone seems sufficient for the explanation. The λ̄²/t emerged for this *Paramaecium* culture from four different measurement series on 10 to 15 animals each in wide vessels at 15° as 0.043, 0.060, 0.060, 0.065; and from this D = λ̄²/8 equal to 0.0054, 0.0075, 0.0075, 0.0081, on average 0.0071, in sufficient agreement with the lowest value, 0.0084, found directly for the smallest concentrations. At the higher concentrations there could therefore, besides the disordered movement, also be a tendency of the animals to move away from one another at work. With the large fluctuations of the individual values this cannot yet be taken as proven; but in a more precise investigation this possibility will have to be attended to.
