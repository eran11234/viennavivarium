# Agreement of Positive and Negative Dopa-Reactions on Frozen Sections with those on Extracts

### (At the same time: Causes of animal coloration. XI.)

By

**Leonore Brecher and Ferdinand Winkler.**

(From the Biological Experimental Institute of the Academy of Sciences in Vienna [Zoological Division].)¹

*(Received 2 August 1924.)*

*Archiv für mikroskopische Anatomie und Entwicklungsmechanik*, vol. 104 (1925).

In a comprehensive review of the present state of the doctrine of pigments, Bloch (1923), with reference to the work of Przibram, with the collaboration of Dembowski and Brecher (1921): Action of tyrosinase on dopa, also raised the objection that the experiments had indeed been carried out on extracts, a method which is less exact than the one used by Bloch himself, namely that of conducting reactions on tissue sections.

Since the same objection could possibly be raised against the experiments of Sato and Brecher (1923 and this issue), on account of the negative outcome of the reactions characteristic of dopa (green coloration after addition of ferric chloride, then, upon addition of sodium carbonate, a change to purple-red), which were carried out on extracts, we have conducted these same reactions for the demonstration of dioxyphenylalanine in melanin-forming tissues microscopically also on frozen sections, of the kind that Bloch uses in his investigations, and indeed in particular on the same objects that Bloch too used.

**Method:** The solutions used for the microchemical color reactions were: a very strongly diluted, only faintly yellowish-colored ferric chloride solution (prepared by 100-fold dilution of a concentrated solution); a concentrated sodium carbonate solution and an ammoniacal silver nitrate solution (1 drop of concentrated ammonia to 10 cm³ of a 2% silver nitrate solution).

We embedded the fresh skin pieces, cut out from the living animal, in agar according to the method indicated by Bloch and then prepared frozen sections of between 10–30 µ thickness. The sections were placed immediately on slides and observed under the microscope upon addition of a drop of the appropriate reagents.

In a few cases listed in the protocol that follows here, the sections were prepared from the skin of fresh corpses, and in two cases we used sections of objects preserved in formol.

Since the silver nitrate reaction is actually not a reaction specific for dopa, as pigments too give it, it is, for the positive demonstration of dopa (strictly speaking, of an orthophenylalanine), only the green coloration upon addition of ferric chloride and the change to purple-red upon subsequent addition of sodium carbonate that is decisive.

On the whole we have, as is described in more detail in the protocol that follows, attempted the microchemical demonstration of dopa by means of the ferric chloride and ferric chloride–sodium carbonate color reactions on the black and white skin of quite young, 7-day-old rats and of older rats, on pigmented and unpigmented eyes of quite young, 7-day-old rats, on the skin of wild-colored rabbits, on the scalp and the external genital organs of a human embryo, on the scalp of fresh human corpses. As a control to this, the same microchemical reactions were carried out at the same time on sections of the seed coat of *Vicia faba* as well as of the leaves and stalks of this plant, from whose seeds, as is well known, dopa is obtained, further on the butterfly pupa cocoons of *Saturnia pavonia* and *Eriogaster lanestris*, in which Przibram (1922) had demonstrated dopa in extracts of the cocoons. Further, the reactions were likewise carried out, as a control, on sections of the cocoon of *Bombyx mori*, whose extract gives a negative dopa-reaction, and on *Vanessa* pupae.

The investigations were carried out partly in the clinic of Professor Kermauner, partly in the Histological Division of the Physiological Institute in Vienna directed by Professor Walter Kolmer, partly in the Zoological Division of the Biological Experimental Institute directed by Professor Przibram, for which we here too express our best thanks to the named directors.

### Experimental results.

*Protocol excerpt:*

**Plant parts.** *Vicia faba*: Seeds. Fairly thick sections prepared with the razor. Addition of ferric chloride and acetic acid gave a very distinct positive reaction. Still more distinct is the red coloration upon addition of sodium carbonate. The reaction upon addition of silver nitrate is likewise positive. These color reactions all occur in the light-brown coat of *Vicia faba*; the inner part gives no reaction.

*Vicia faba*: Leaves, both from etiolated plants kept in the dark and from green plants kept in the light, as well as from the lower white portions of the leaf-stalks. Very thin razor sections. Positive reaction with ferric chloride: green coloration. Upon addition of sodium carbonate, immediate change to reddish violet. The reaction with silver nitrate likewise positive.

*Brassica oleracea*, cabbage leaf. Negative reaction.

**Butterfly cocoons and pupae.** *Saturnia pavonia* and *Eriogaster lanestris*, cocoons, 10 µ thick frozen sections. Upon addition of ferric chloride the characteristic green coloration arises, which is further intensified upon addition of acetic acid. Upon subsequent addition of sodium carbonate a change to red occurs. (The red color is much weaker than in macroscopic experiments.) The section treated with ammoniacal silver nitrate solution first becomes reddish violet, then it is especially one layer of the cocoon that appears completely blackened. All these microscopic color reactions are weaker in comparison with the macrochemical treatment of a piece of cocoon, which gives very intense color reactions.

*Bombyx mori*. All reactions, microchemically on sections and macroscopically on pieces of cocoons, negative.

*Vanessa urticae* pupae (living pupae). 24 µ thick sections:

a) light pupa,
b) middle pupa,
c) dark pupa,
d) golden pupa.

After addition of ferric chloride + acetic acid the green coloration characteristic of dopa does not appear. Upon subsequent addition of sodium carbonate the characteristic purple-red coloration does not appear. Upon addition of silver nitrate all sections show blackening.

**Mammals, skin.** *Migratory rat:* Piebald rat, 7 days old. Black skin from the middle of the back. 20 µ thick sections. Addition of ferric chloride + acetic acid: reaction negative. Ferric chloride with subsequent addition of sodium carbonate likewise negative. Addition of silver nitrate: the bulbs become very distinct and the darker marginal part of the skin is blackened.

White skin spot, to the right of the previously taken black spot: Reaction with ferric chloride and ferric chloride + sodium carbonate negative. Reaction with silver nitrate also negative.

Pigmented eye of the piebald rat: Reaction with ferric chloride and ferric chloride + sodium carbonate negative.

White rat from the same litter, thus 7 days old: Analogous spot of the skin like the black skin spot of the piebald rat. Color reactions negative.

Albinotic eye of the white rat. Reactions negative.

Small piebald rat: Black-white piece of skin from the back. 15 µ thick sections. Ferric chloride and ferric chloride + sodium carbonate reaction negative.

Snout skin from the white snout: Reactions negative.

Large black rat: Back skin: Ferric chloride and ferric chloride + sodium carbonate reaction negative.

Large white rat: Back skin: Reactions negative.

Rabbit, wild-colored, belly skin: 15 µ thick sections. Negative outcome of the color reactions characteristic of dopa.

**Human.** Female human embryo from the 5th month of pregnancy, frozen sections prepared 4 hours after birth, with careful addition of water without agar. Sections 15 µ thick, scalp and skin of the external genital organs. Reactions with ferric chloride and ferric chloride + sodium carbonate negative. Reaction after addition of silver nitrate positive.

Belly skin of a 25-year-old brunette woman, who had been laparotomized on account of an ovarian disease and from whom, in the region of the skin incision conducted along the linea alba of the belly, a piece of skin was taken and immediately cut up into 15 µ thick frozen sections. The ferric chloride and ferric chloride + Na₂CO₃ reactions turned out negative.

Scalp of fresh corpses: 51-year-old woman, dark-blond, and 28-year-old man, black-haired: 20 µ thick sections. The ferric chloride and ferric chloride + sodium carbonate reactions negative.

Preserved sections, 10 µ thickness, from a 6-month-old human embryo which had lain one month in formalin. Sections from the clitoris. Ferric chloride and ferric chloride + sodium carbonate reaction negative. In the silver nitrate reaction the basal part of the epidermis was most strongly colored, while the remaining portions of the epidermis showed only a browning, which diminished more and more toward the surface.

Belly skin of the same embryo. Ferric chloride and ferric chloride + sodium carbonate reaction negative. Silver nitrate reaction strongest in the basal portion of the epidermis, while the remaining parts also show a certain browning.

We thus see that on the objects in which dopa had been demonstrated in the extract (*Vicia faba* — Guggenheim, cocoons of *Eriogaster lanestris* and *Saturnia pavonia* — Przibram), the microchemical demonstration of dopa on sections also turns out positive, whereas on objects in which the microchemical demonstration turned out negative (*Bombyx mori*), the reaction was negative on extracts too.

The detectability of dopa is thus the same whether one conducts the reactions macrochemically on extracts or microchemically on frozen sections.

Neither the experiments of Sato and Brecher on extracts on the one hand, nor the reactions conducted on tissue sections in the present communication on the other hand, have been able to demonstrate the presence of dopa in those melanin-forming tissues in which such a presence had been assumed by Bloch.

### Summary.

Whereas Chodat (1922), Meirowsky (1923) and Przibram (1921) reject a specific dopa-oxidase, since according to the latter's findings the 3,4-dioxyphenylalanine blackens both spontaneously and upon addition of alkali or tyrosinase, Bloch holds firmly to the specificity of the "dopase" and to its demonstration through addition of dopa. As against the alleged experiments to isolate the dopase, he appeals to the difference of the methods used. This objection he could also raise against the experiments of Sato and Brecher (1923 and this issue), on account of the likewise negative outcome of the reactions characteristic of dopa (green coloration with ferric chloride, then, upon addition of sodium carbonate, change to purple-red, etc.) in the extracts of the melanin-forming tissues. Following his demand for application of the frozen-section method, only such objects — in particular the same ones that he had used — were investigated microchemically for dopa. Neither the eyes nor the black or white spots of a 7-day-old or older rat, nor the scalp of humans, showed the mentioned green or red coloration. Equally negative turned out the analogous tests on pupae of various color types of *Vanessa urticae*, further on the cocoon of *Bombyx mori*.

On the other hand, the frozen sections of the cocoons of *Saturnia pavonia* and *Eriogaster lanestris*, in which Przibram (1922) had achieved the demonstration of dopa in the test tube by means of extraction with water, showed a distinctly positive outcome.

There is accordingly no occasion to assume a difference in the detectability of dopa, according to whether one investigates a frozen section or an extract.
