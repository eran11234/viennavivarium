The capacity for regeneration of the caudal horn in the mulberry silkworm (*Bombyx mori* L.) has already been examined by Kellogg¹ incidentally to his experiments on the legs of the same animal. His experiments, however, yielded a negative result with respect to that organ. This circumstance led Kellogg — adhering to the power of selection that produces and destroys regeneration capacities — to express the view that the caudal horn, having become useless in the course of time and therefore lacking any biological significance for the animal, had forfeited its capacity for regeneration by way of the negative selection process.

To what extent the effect of selection had made itself felt, and whether rudimentary organs, or organs held to be such, lack the capacity for regeneration, my following experiment was at least to decide for the present case; I undertook it to test his experimental results, in the supposition that Kellogg had experimented with stages that were too far advanced. The silkworm caterpillar as such passes through four moults, or, as Kellogg puts it, four periods. After the fifth moult it becomes a pupa. The execution of the operation on

animals of the first and second period seemed to Kellogg technically difficult, and he therefore operated exclusively on animals of the third and fourth period. According to his findings, the replacement of the amputated legs did not occur with the first moult after the operation had been performed, but always only with the second moult. A regeneration of the caudal horn, however, was in any case hardly to be expected in such old animals, since the operated caterpillars had only one or two more moults to complete before pupation. If, for example, he operated on animals after the third moult, then between the time of the operation and that of the formation of the pupa there lay only one larval moult; the following moult already yielded the pupa, which, however, no longer possesses the caudal horn as a strictly larval organ. If, on the other hand, he operated on animals that still had two moults to undergo before pupation, then a complete regeneration is inconceivable, on account of the slight supply of blood to the said organ, hand in hand with this on account of its slight nourishment and slight growth, and finally also on account of the rapid pace of the moults still outstanding.

My experiments were carried out on animals which I reared from eggs; these I had sent to me from the Imperial-Royal Agricultural-Chemical Experimental Station in Görz, for which I should like here once more to express my warmest thanks to the said institution.

On 29 IV 05 I operated on 20 little caterpillars which had hatched 3 days previously, by cutting off, under the magnifying glass and by means of fine scissors, the still inconspicuous caudal horn close to the body. From the wound thus produced only a little greenish blood-fluid welled out, which soon afterwards congealed into a dark wound-scab. The operated animals were kept in a small glass vessel until the wound had closed. Thereupon I transferred them by means of a brush into an insectarium (a rectangular wooden frame, the two long walls glazed, the front one of these arranged as a sliding door, the lid covered with wire mesh).

The feeding proved very difficult, since at that time, when I already had to set up the experiments as a result of the premature hatching in the warm rooms, the mulberry leaves in this region were not yet developed. For a fairly long time I was obliged to place a larger number of young, merely budding twigs — which, by virtue of a gratefully acknowledged permission of the Imperial-Royal Prater Inspectorate, I was allowed to have fetched from the Prater avenues — in the warm glasshouses of the Biologische Versuchsanstalt, in order to bring them to early unfolding by means of high warmth and humidity. The leaves and larger buds thus obtained were, as long as the caterpillars were still small, offered in a finely chopped state on the white filter paper, which I had previously moistened a little. Later I placed for them small twigs, stuck into medicine bottles filled with water, in the container. The bottles in question I wrapped with a rough paper, in order to make it easier for any larvae that had possibly fallen down to reach the twigs again.

In the rooms where the experimental animals were located there prevailed approximately a constant temperature of 35° C.

animals no longer possessed any wound-scab. Strangely enough, I was never able to find a shed larval skin. Perhaps the skins, as many animals regularly do, are devoured by the caterpillars. On closer examination I was able to notice, at the place where the caudal horn had previously been inserted, a light-colored skin-spot. Of a regenerate there was as yet no trace. On 6 V 05 an epidemic broke out in the insectarium which carried off all my experimental animals except three specimens. The survivors I therefore transferred into another, larger insectarium and supplied them abundantly with food. On 9 V 07 I noticed, in place of the light skin-spot, a small knobby outgrowth without any of the hairiness that is to be seen under the magnifying glass on the normal caudal horn. On 17 V 07 the knob seemed to me to have grown considerably and to be similar in shape to the normal caudal horn. Now there also appeared again, in attenuated degree, the bristling of the primary horn. On 25 V 07 I found two specimens of these collapsed and morbidly colored brown. When I took them in my hand, a foul-smelling fluid flowed out. The last animal that I still possessed had shortened to half its previous length and was preparing for pupation; I conserved it immediately thereafter.

The regenerate obtained (Fig. 2) is, measured from the root, about 1 mm long, tapering distally and finely bristled, whereas the normal caudal horn (Fig. 1) of this stage exhibits a length of approximately 1½ mm, is slightly flattened at the tip, and shows a stronger bristling.
