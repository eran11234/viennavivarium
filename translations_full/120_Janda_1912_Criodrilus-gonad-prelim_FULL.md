# The Regeneration of the Sexual Organs in Criodrilus lacuum Hoffm. I.

By

Dr. Viktor Janda.

*(From the Biological Experimental Institute in Vienna.)*

With Plate XX.

Received on 13 September 1911.

*Archiv für Entwicklungsmechanik der Organismen*, vol. 33 (1912).

> **Full translation.** A complete English rendering of the running text of “The Regeneration of the Sexual Organs in Criodrilus lacuum Hoffm. I (Preliminary Communication)” (Janda, 1912), including all tables, figure and plate legends, and footnotes. Numbers and table cells were transcribed from the page images, not the noisy OCR.

At the Biological Experimental Institute in Vienna I had the welcome opportunity to obtain a larger quantity of *Criodrilus lacuum*. On account of its eminent capacity for regeneration and its tenacity of life, this form seemed to me very well suited for the continuation of my experiments on the regeneration of the sexual organs, which I had already carried out 2 years ago on *Rhynchelmis limosella*, all the more so as it turned out that it is capable of reproducing a fairly large number of segments (well over 20). *Criodrilus* can also be kept comparatively very well in mud.

In order to test the regenerative capacity of the sexual organs of this animal, I operated on about 300 mostly medium-sized and adult specimens (of which some still showed distinct external marks of sexual maturity) in September and October (27 IX, 6 X, and 9 X) of last year, in such a way that I cut off 17–30 segments at their anterior end, that is, the entire sexual region. The severed segments were preserved in concentrated aqueous sublimate solution and counted once more. Since, with a certain degree of care, the mortality of the operated animals was rather low, it was no great difficulty to keep a sufficient number of them alive. Already within 2 months considerable conical regenerates developed. A part of the animals with regenerates was, after a lapse of about 5 months (on 3 III), preserved for the purpose of histological examination; the other part was kept alive. On 26 VI and 7 VII 1911 one could already observe fairly distinct traces of beginning sexual maturity on the regenerates. In one animal one could even establish well-developed ♂ pores. I subjected a few animals at once to a thorough microscopic examination. The animals anaesthetised with ether (25 specimens in all) were cut in two under the magnifying glass precisely at the boundary between the regenerate and the rest of the body, and both parts were isolated and preserved in sublimate. The unobjectionable isolation of the regenerates from the old body parts was thereby greatly facilitated, in that the boundary between the two parts was still very well preserved and could already be clearly recognised with the naked eye.

The scrutiny of the series of sagittal sections has now revealed the presence of well-developed gonads, ciliated funnels with efferent ducts, and seminal vesicles. In some animals I even established the ovaries in six and seven consecutive segments, namely in the 8th, 9th, 10th, 11th, 12th, and 13th (Fig. 2), and in the 8th, 9th, 10th, 11th, 12th, 13th, and 14th (Fig. 1). (The mouth segment included.) I also observed the ovaries in the 6th and 7th segment. — According to Rosa (3) and Collin (1) the ovaries and oviducts in normal Criodrili occur only in a single pair and lie in the 13th segment.

The regenerated ovaries possess a typical, mostly pear-shaped form and in some animals are so large that they almost fill the entire free segmental space (Fig. 1). The large yolk-rich eggs with round vesicular nuclei and distinct nucleoli are confined chiefly to the distal periphery of the ovaries, while the interior and the lower stalk-like tapered parts of the same consist of young, much smaller, and densely crowded egg cells with numerous mitoses. The ovaries are attached with their stalk-shaped base closely against the dissepiment wall and are traversed by branched blood vessels which penetrate inward through the constricted base of the ovary. At the lower end of the dissepiments, opposite the ovaries, the intensely stained ciliated tubes with oviducts can also be very clearly recognised on longitudinal sections (Fig. 1 and 2).

The testes, sperm funnels, and — in one animal also in the body cavity — floating groups of sperm cells, I found in the 8th, 9th, and 10th segment. Normally, according to Rosa (3) and Collin (1), the two pairs of testes and two pairs of sperm funnels are said to be restricted to the 10th and 11th segment.

The anlagen of the seminal vesicles present themselves as evaginations of the dissepiment wall composed of several folds (Fig. 3). In my previous examinations I found these fairly well developed, besides in the 9th, 10th, 11th, and 12th, also in the 7th, 8th, and 13th segment, sometimes on the anterior, sometimes on the posterior dissepiments. But I must reserve more precise data on the position and connections of the seminal sacs with the segmental cavities for further examinations. In one animal, in which I was able to demonstrate external male sexual pores, the two anterior seminal sacs fill the greater part of the segmental space and contain numerous sperm cells. In normal animals the seminal sacs, according to the concordant data of Rosa and Collin, occur in four pairs in the 9th, 10th, 11th, and 12th segment.

In one animal, from which 17 segments had been removed and which replaced 16, I observed on the regenerate external ♂ sexual pores (Fig. 5a and 5b). These lie on both sides of the 13th segment (in normal animals, on the other hand, on the 15th or 16th(?) segment) and are surrounded by yellowish, translucent glandular swellings.

Abnormal displacement and development of the male sexual pores has also been described by Prof. A. Mrázek (2) in normal sexually mature specimens of *Lumbriculus variegatus*, and he has illustrated 20 different types, from which it emerges that the openings of the male sexual apparatus in *Lumbriculus* can be shifted from the 7th and 8th to the 6th, 9th, 10th, and 11th segment.

The presence of the gonads in the regenerate can, in my opinion, only be explained by the fact that the somatic cells can, under certain circumstances, transform into sexual cells.

I cannot refrain from drawing attention even now to an important remark which I take from the above-mentioned work of Professor Mrázek (2), and which receives full confirmation through the data just discussed. There it reads (p. 454) as follows: »But perhaps the great variability of the sexual organs of *Lumbriculus* might find its explanation in yet another way. It would be possible that we are here dealing with regeneration phenomena. In the case of the individuals it would also be a matter of posterior fragments of the asexual generations, which first had to regenerate the anterior body part with the sexual organs.« ...... »The many cited ‚abnormal‘ developments of the sexual apparatus we could regard as cases in which it is a matter of regeneration.«

On 10 VII of this year I collected in the Danube near Vienna an enormous quantity of freshly deposited and older *Criodrilus* cocoons with partly hatching young, and 3 days later I found at Klosterneuburg about 100 completely sexually mature animals with spermatophores. Yet at this time the external sexual characters in the normal animals are much more distinctly developed than in the operated ones.

A more detailed discussion and supplementation of the facts communicated above, and their theoretical utilisation, shall remain reserved for a further communication.
