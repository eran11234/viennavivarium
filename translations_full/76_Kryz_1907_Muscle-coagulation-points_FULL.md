## I.

H. Přibram, in the *Beiträge zur chemischen Physiologie und Pathologie* [Contributions to Chemical Physiology and Pathology], edited by Franz Hofmeister, Vol. II, Issues 1 to 3, 1902, published an experiment on the chemical characterization of several classes of animals on the basis of their muscle plasma, in which he followed a method according to v. Fürth for obtaining and testing the muscle plasma, which the author also employed in his experiments relating thereto. This method shall be set out here in a few words.

The muscles of the freshly killed animal, as exsanguinated as possible, are comminuted, ground up with quartz sand and with the addition of physiological saline solution, strained, and thereupon the muscle-plasma juice obtained—if necessary with the aid of a meat press—is filtered until clear. The muscle-plasma juice is carefully coagulated stepwise at various temperatures, with filtering-off of the coagulum obtained in each case, and thereupon the individual fractions are tested with specific salt solutions for their precipitability. The solutions should react just acid at the time the reactions are set up. H. Přibram, in his work cited above, included a table in which he set out the results of his investigations in a clear synopsis. The works of the author were presented in the following table according to the same scheme, and for the most part animals were drawn upon that had not previously been investigated.

The gaps in the table are caused partly by the quantity of fluid being insufficient for all the reactions, as in the case of smaller animals, partly by the impossibility of obtaining clear filtrates in the case of some species.

Concerning the selection of the animal species investigated, the following may be noted.

Of the river lamprey, which was investigated by H. Přibram in its larval form, the author took the full-grown animal. The coagulation points of the two muscle plasmas were found to be identical. In the case of the sterlet, no clear filterable differences could be obtained on account of the impossibility of obtaining a clear muscle-plasma juice, free of myogen fibrin, so that it could not be decided with certainty whether myogen fibrin was present or not. The desert monitor, however, only became available to the author when it was being kept at high temperatures; perhaps the coagulation temperatures of the muscle-plasma protein bodies were thereby shifted somewhat, since they came out somewhat higher.

The land and pond tortoises, like the bats and the ground squirrel, were taken during winter sleep [hibernation], in order to be able to investigate the muscle chemism of the muscles in winter sleep at a later time, and to undertake comparative experiments concerning the temperatures of the muscle plasmas of the same animals described above.

## II.

H. Přibram obtained, in two pond tortoises that were killed during winter sleep, no myosin coagulation of the muscle plasma, and on the basis of this finding considered the possibility of the disappearance of the myosin during the winter sleep. To elucidate this question, the author investigated, in the month of March, animals still sleeping, which had been put into winter quarters in the preceding autumn, with respect to their myosin coagulation, and found that, both in two specimens of the pond tortoise and also in the land tortoise and the common bat, the myosin and all the other muscle-protein bodies were still present almost up to the end of their winter sleep, as emerged from the coagulation values obtained, which agreed with those given in the table.

The bat had been put into winter quarters in a space whose temperature lay only a few degrees above the freezing point, so that the coagulation values obtained, which did not differ from those yielded by the bats investigated in summer, permit the conclusion that even through low temperatures the coagulation points of the muscle plasma show no shifts.

The ground squirrel could be investigated only in summer, since that animal, which had been put into winter quarters, awoke at a time during which the author was away on a journey, and therefore the investigation of the animal still in winter sleep could no longer be undertaken.

## III.

Several animal species were kept for a longer time at higher temperatures, in order to ascertain whether, perhaps as a consequence thereof, the muscle-protein body that coagulates at the lowest temperature would suffer an upward shift of its coagulation point, or whether some other indications would result from the coagulation findings, on the basis of which one might infer a chemical muscle alteration.

A preliminary experiment showed that tree frogs from Corfu did not tolerate well a constant air temperature held at 30° C., since 50% of the experimental animals already perished after 4 days. Fire salamanders showed themselves to be more resistant, since they tolerated the constant air temperature of 30° C. for 10 to 12 days before they perished. Furthermore, it was found that earth toads and water frogs are best suited to such heat-acclimatization experiments, since they endured the temperature of 30° C. without harm during the 14-day duration of the preliminary experiment.

The actual experiments were begun on 23 IV 1906, and indeed 10 specimens each of the species *Rana esculenta*, *Bufo vulgaris* and *Salamandra maculosa* were brought into spaces of a constant 25° C. temperature, where they remained until 8 V. On 26 and 30 IV one water frog each perished, both of which were replaced, as also happened with one fire salamander that died on 8 V.

On 8 V all the animals were transferred into spaces that had a constant 37° C., and from this day on the dead animals were no longer replaced. On 9 and 18 V two each perished, on 15 and 20 V one fire salamander each.

The water frogs and toads remained alive at the temperature amounting to 37° C. On 23 V, that is, just after one month since the beginning of the experiment’s duration, two of the fire salamanders kept at these higher temperatures were killed and the coagulation values of their muscle-plasma protein bodies investigated. The following coagulation temperatures resulted for both animals:

| | Myogen fibrin | Myosin | Myogen | (4th) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| | 36—**42**¹⁾ | 45—**51** | 54—**61**¹⁾ | 69—**80**¹⁾ |
| | 37—**43**¹⁾ | 45—**51**½ | 54—**60**¹⁾ | 70—**80**¹⁾ |

If one compares these values with those given in the table, which were found in a fire salamander kept at normal temperature, one sees that only differences falling within the experimental-error limit are present in the temperature figures.

The two fire salamanders still alive, and all the earth toads and water frogs, were then left at 37° C. until 19 VI, and thereupon all were transferred into spaces whose air temperature amounted to a constant approximately 40° C.

Already on 21 VI both fire salamanders perished, so that the conclusion is probably justified that the fire salamander can indeed be acclimatized to temperatures of 30—37° C., but that it endures a temperature of 40° C. only for a very short time.

The water frogs and earth toads, too, which had become well acclimatized to the temperature of 37° C., did so no longer at the temperature raised to 40°. On 24 VI six perished, on 25 VI two water frogs, so that on 30 VI one of the two still-remaining water frogs could be killed and investigated. The following coagulation temperatures resulted:

| Myogen fibrin | Myosin | Myogen |
|---|---|---|
| 39—**42** | 46—**51**½ | 53½—**65** |

A comparison with the normal values given in the table for the muscle-plasma-coagulation temperatures of *Rana esculenta* likewise lets no greater differences be recognized here. The last water frog still remaining alive was left further at 40° C. and perished on 1 VII, so that the conclusion can be drawn that the water frog is indeed somewhat more resistant than the fire salamander against higher temperatures, but that it too cannot be acclimatized to lasting temperatures of about 40° C.

The earth toads endured the temperature of 40° C. relatively the best, although the time during which they endured this temperature is indeed too short for one to be able to speak of an actual acclimatization to it. Some earth toads perished quickly at this temperature of 40° C., others kept themselves alive relatively long.

In all the fire salamanders, water frogs and earth toads investigated, it had shown itself that, through the keeping of the animals at higher than the normal temperatures (up to the maximal temperature) over longer periods of time, no shift whatever of the coagulation points of the muscle-protein bodies appears to set in.
