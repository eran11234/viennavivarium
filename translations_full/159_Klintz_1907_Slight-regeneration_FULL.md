## I. Arrangement and Method of the Experiments.

In order to obtain some clarity regarding the known slight regenerative capacity of the Cyclopidae, I undertook relevant experiments on them with the intention of examining them precisely on the basis of the various age-stages, of which none of the researchers hitherto occupied with the same theme makes mention. In this way the existing gaps were, if possible, either to be filled, or the actually slight regenerative capacity of this family was to be confirmed. With regard to the just-mentioned obscurities which prevail in the literature concerning the operated stages, I set up my experiments in the following manner.

1. Group: Cyclopid breeding from ten sexually mature females (source material for the juvenile stages).
2. Group: Regeneration experiments on the antennae in sexually mature animals.
3. Group: Regeneration experiments on the furca in sexually mature animals.
4. Group: Regeneration experiments on the antennae in juvenile stages.
5. Group: Regeneration experiments on the furca in juvenile stages.
6. Group: Control breeding of the sexually mature animals.
7. Group: Control breeding of the juvenile stages.

Here I must mention that I could not confine myself to a particular kind of *Cyclops*, but rather took the *Cyclops* specimens needed for my experiments, of necessity, indiscriminately from an aquarium whose contents had been scooped up in the vicinity of Vienna (Moosbrunn). The following species were represented therein: *Cyclops quadricornis* Müll., *Cyclops viridis* Jur., and *Cyclops coronatus* Cls.

Both the operated and the non-operated animals were kept, singly or several together, in drinking glasses or glass dishes which were filled with pure high-mountain spring water. The water was changed every 8 days; in addition I put into each vessel either duckweed (*Lemna minor*) or waterweed (*Elodea canadensis*), with which plants the necessary food-animals were at the same time introduced. All the experimental glasses were set up at a uniformly illuminated place and kept at a regularly equal high temperature (in the early spring 16 to 17°, in summer 18 to 20° C.).

The animals destined for the operation I operated upon, when they were sexually mature, on the slide (object-carrier), by means of a thin scalpel. In the juvenile stages the organs in question were amputated (pressed off) by means of a small coverslip, which, on account of its transparency — one can see at any time which part is being struck — deserves preference over metal instruments. I succeeded in operating juvenile stages already after the first moult, thus still as Nauplii, yet the animals regularly perished as a result of too great a loss of body mass. This too Przibram experienced in his experiments. After the second moult the animal assumes the *Cyclops*-form, which places by far fewer difficulties in the way of the operation.

## II. Presentation of the Experiments.

### 1. Cyclopid breeding from ten sexually mature females (source material for the juvenile stages).

For the rearing of juvenile stages, which were later to afford me full certainty regarding the age of the experimental objects in the regeneration experiments, I took ten sexually mature *Cyclops* females and isolated them in the above-mentioned manner each into one drinking glass. Each glass was designated with an Arabic numeral. So that I might be able to follow the perfect development and to obtain the precise time of the appearance of the egg-sacs and a precise overview, to this end I kept a record, in which I entered the dates of the appearance of the egg-sacs, of the egg-laying, and those of the moults up to complete development. Each female I kept so long until it had completed four layings, and in this way I arrived at a sufficiently large number of young animals. After each laying I removed the female and designated the glass, in which the laid eggs now lay, with the Arabic numeral of the female, and each laying of one and the same female with a Roman numeral. In this way I avoided confusions. Not always, however, did I obtain juvenile stages in the glass dishes, since in some cases the egg-sacs were stripped off and the eggs perished.

For the sake of a precise overview I append the above-named records to the text; I have, however, restricted the dates of the individual moults to 100 animals.

The time in which the embryonic development proceeds varies quite considerably; in general, however, one may assume with a certain certainty that it is completed within at most 8 days. That in summer an acceleration can set in as a result of the high temperature, as Claus (cited after Bronn) has observed, I can confirm with my experiments set up in July 1907: both the embryonic and the post-embryonic development now proceeded, in July and August, within half the time otherwise required for this. Jurine, in his notes, brought out something similar. The longest interval between two layings lasted 10 days, the shortest 4 days. The post-embryonic development behaves exactly according to the data given by Claus in Bronn's "Tierreich" [Animal Kingdom].

The duration from the hatching of the young up to sexual maturity is just as little bound to a definite period as are the individual moults occurring in the meantime. From my notes it emerged that the animals which first became sexually mature were exclusively females, and these required, in the months of March up to the beginning of July, the time of 50 to 70 days. The males have a longer post-embryonic development, and their sexual maturity is to be recognized only by the fully grown (telometric) condition. After the onset of sexual maturity the animals moulted no more.

### 2. Regeneration experiments on the antennae in sexually mature animals.

For this experiment I operated upon 50 animals. After the amputation, which the animals tolerated without further ado, a small bleeding occurred; the blood soon congealed and over time coloured itself blackish. The antennae were amputated not at a definite spot, but rather at various ones, according to where the scalpel happened to strike. Experiments were thus arranged in all possible cut-positions, yet all the animals still showed, even after 3 months — within which time they perished — the amputated spots closed only with a clot of congealed blood. Moults I could here never observe, and I conclude from this that sexually mature *Cyclops* are capable of moulting neither in the uninjured nor in the naturally or artificially injured condition. From the onset of sexual maturity no moult could any longer be called forth by any artificial intervention.

Hübner set up the same experiments and found pretty much the same, only with the difference that with him the congealment-clot was cast off in the course of 1–3 weeks and in its place a chitin-deposit appeared, which was convex toward the outside. Casting off of the congealment-clot I could never observe in sexually mature animals. On the other hand I can confirm Hübner's statement that during the experimental period egg-sacs formed on already sexually mature operated animals. Przibram and Ost likewise obtained no regeneration of the antennae, although Ost, as I too in many cases, removed only the last segment of the antenna. Jurine made the same experiments on the antennae in *Cyclops* females and writes on p. 40: With regard to the animals operated upon at the antennae, it is to be noted that one female twice in 5 weeks laid eggs, whereupon it appeared to become diseased, moulted once more, and emerged from the skin with two equally long antennae.

It is very strange that Jurine got to see moults in sexually mature animals. It would be natural here to object that these moults possibly came about as a consequence of the diseased condition of the animals. This objection is, however, excluded, since Jurine himself writes on p. 37: Usually every egg-laying is preceded by a moult. Perhaps Jurine kept several age-stages together in one glass, so that it was impossible for him to distinguish precisely the sexually mature from the non-sexually-mature animals. At least I cannot interpret

### 3. Regeneration experiments on the furca in sexually mature animals.

The loss of the furca the animals tolerated much more poorly than that of the antenna, and I was compelled, in order to obtain results as reliable as possible, to operate upon over 100 animals.

The cut-positions turned out variously on account of the slight size of the animals. At first I observed that animals which had been operated upon right at the end of the body or at the first or second tail-segment died after a few hours. Even animals operated upon at the next-to-last tail-segment perished after some time, at all events because the anus is located there and consequently in the operation a piece of the gut is injured as well. But if I amputated only the terminal bristles together with their basal pieces, the animals remained alive for up to over 2 months. After the operation a closure again formed out of a congealed blood-drop, which remained visible up to the end of the animals' lives. In this experiment too I could observe neither a regeneration-bud nor a real regeneration.

Ost likewise carries out the same experiments here and says, also, that newly furca-deprived animals perished on him. But from nine other animals he obtained regenerates of the tail-threads and says that the regeneration went hand in hand with the moult; from this I conclude that Ost operated upon juvenile stages, but not sexually mature *Cyclops*, in which a moult after the operation is certainly not to be expected.

Hübner obtained no results with the tail-threads, since his animals were able to live only 8–14 days. The same is said also by Jurine (p. 73) of *Diaptomus castor*, which died with a scarring-over of the wound, without moulting.

### 4. Regeneration experiments on the antennae in juvenile stages.

After attaining the conviction that in sexually mature Cyclopidae the regenerative capacity of the lost paired organs is excluded, I tested the regenerative capacity of the juvenile stages. There, in accordance with Przibram's remark (p. 173), one could probably expect favourable results from the Nauplii. Strange as it may seem, however, the experiment, to amputate the individual coloured limbs, did not succeed for me — not a single animal could I keep alive up to the next moult, for all perished, as already mentioned, after a short time. Since, then, my endeavour was here unsuccessful, I had to await the second moult, after which the antennae and the furca are clearly to be distinguished from the pear-shaped body, and whose amputation does not exactly have an injury of the body as a necessary consequence. Here I succeeded in squeezing off the antennae by means of a small coverslip. To the operation I subjected over 50 animals, which lost the antennae entirely or only partially. Here I must remark that I shifted my experiments entirely onto the second, longer pair of feelers, and that the first remained completely uninjured in all the operated specimens.

The first experiments made in this way I set up in March, and the animals lived until the beginning of July.

Immediately after the operation the congealed blood-drop appeared, which later fell off and after a few days made room for a convex regeneration-bud. Of the 50 specimens operated upon partly on 30. III., partly on 12. IV., 48 animals perished at the first moult following upon the amputation (in the life of the animal the third) and showed only the above-mentioned regeneration-bud. Two animals, which on 16. IV. 07 had emerged alive from the old skin and from which one third of the right antenna (Fig. 1) had been amputated, showed at the amputation-spot in each case two new segments, which, however, in size remained far behind the normal ones (Fig. 2). Probably the animals would, after the next moult, have emerged with an antenna completely newly formed as regards the number of segments; yet the animals perished after 2 days, and I am compelled to leave this question open.

In this case (in the juvenile stages), therefore, regeneration has shown itself, which in the next group will emerge still more clearly.

## 5. Regeneration experiments on the furca in juvenile stages.

Here, with animals after the first moult, the same technical difficulties arose as in the preceding experiments. I therefore had to use, as with the earlier ones, animals at the same stage, that is, ones after the second moult. Already in the sexually mature forms the loss of the furca represents a severe injury; it was even more difficult, in the juvenile stages, to overcome the bodily damage thereby produced. I operated on more than 100 animals and only 20 remained alive. The cuts fell on various furcal segments; in one specimen only on the setae.

The animals operated on at the furcal segments did not regenerate. The animals moulted two days after the operation. After 2 to 3 days a wound closure formed, which remained the same without any further scarring until the end of life.

The one animal, from which both setal bundles together with a part of the attachment piece had been cut off (Fig. 3), I had operated on on 8 April 07. On 14 April 07 it moulted and regenerated the two long and the two short caudal setae on both sides (Fig. 4). On taking it out of the vessel the left ones broke off, and the animal was reared further, in order perhaps to make a renewed regeneration possible. But it died on 19 April 07 without having moulted again.

In the very latest period I took up once more the experiments just described. For this I used a brood of my own from three females. After the second moult, which the young of these females had completed on 31 July 07, I operated on over 20 of them. The animals in which the furca was amputated above the anus or abutting on it, died after a short time; those, however, in which I amputated only the setae, remained alive. The operation, which I carried out on 2 August 07, was followed on the next day by the formation of a clotting plug (Fig. 5). On 4 August 07 the regeneration bud already appeared, provided with the small caudal setae (Fig. 6). On 5 August 07 the animal moulted and emerged from the old skin with the fully regenerated, and what is especially to be noted, the normal caudal setae standing at equal length (Fig. 7). On 7 August 07 the animal moulted once more. This regeneration, accelerated as compared with the earlier experiments, is to be attributed solely to the circumstance that the animals were exposed to direct sunlight and thereby to a high temperature, which on the one hand produced a great speed of growth, but on the other hand proved harmful in such a way that many animals perished as a result of the strong evaporation of the water and the drying-out of some of the glass dishes.

I have thus succeeded in demonstrating regeneration in the juvenile stages of the Cyclopidae; nevertheless this is restricted only to a few body parts, or rather to small portions of them. As to the accompanying phenomena that were to be observed in the period from the day of amputation until the attainment of sexual maturity, and as to the manner in which the animals became sexually mature, I reserve the right to report shortly.

Where OST has achieved regeneration of the caudal setae, he too must have used juvenile stages for the operation.

## 6. and 7.: Control culture of the sexually mature animals and of the juvenile stages.

As a control, I placed in a glass 20 sexually mature specimens of the Cyclops species enumerated at the outset (entirely females) and reared them under exactly the same living conditions as the other experimental animals, only with the difference that the control animals were all together in one glass, while the other experimental animals were isolated. The former lived from 13 March to 18 June 07, that is, over 3 months, under my observation. Their absolute age was, however, decidedly higher, since females already sexually mature and all provided with egg-sacs had been caught for this purpose. During the experimental period they produced an enormous mass of new individuals. The nauplii already hatched out, which had been set up as controls, carried out their postembryonic development properly, and after they had become sexually mature, frequently laid eggs. They lived until their natural death, over 3 months, in the experimental glasses.

## III. Summary.

1) The normal postembryonic development of *Cyclops* proceeds with six moults; after the sixth moult the animals are sexually mature.

2) Sexually mature Cyclopidae moult neither in the natural nor in an operated condition.

3) *Cyclops* shows only in its juvenile stages the capacity to regenerate lost organs (antennal portions and furcal setae).

4) In sexually mature, operated animals not even a regeneration bud formed, but only a clotting plug.

5) The reproduction of the animals suffers no disturbance through the operation on the antennae and caudal setae.
