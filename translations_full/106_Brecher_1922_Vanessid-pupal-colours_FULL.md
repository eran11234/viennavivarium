## First Part: Description of the polymorphism.

### Setting up of four main colour types.

As the pupae of *Pieris brassicae* (cf. Brecher 1917, first part), so too the pupae of many Vanessa species show — as has already been described by other authors: Wood (1867), Poulton (1887—1892), Kathariner (1905), Menzel (1913) — a different colouration according to the surroundings on which they pupate.

Thus those found in the open on black-painted laths are dark blackish; on white limestone walls bright whitish; on grey stone of medium grey-brown colouration; while the pupae hanging on the leaves of the food-plant are gold-lustrous, with green emerging variously distinctly in the individual Vanessa species.

These various colourations of the pupae are characterised by the different development of the following colouration-elements:

a) a black-brown colouring substance, which is deposited in the chitin envelope in more or less distinct flecks and in a distribution appearing diffuse to the free eye, and which, according to its development, yields the darker or lighter tone of the ground colour;

b) a rose or white opacity of the envelope, which constitutes the ground colour;

c) a green colouring substance, deposited in the deeper-lying tissue layers, which can shimmer through the chitin envelope more or less;

d) a gold lustre of the envelope, which appears when the black pigment in the envelope is lacking.

Silvery or shimmering like mother-of-pearl when the rose to white opacity of the underlay is present, the lustre becomes deceptively gold-like there where the opacity of the envelope is also lacking.

According to the stronger or weaker development of these four various colouration-elements, the pupal colourations occurring within each of the here-treated Vanessa species *Vanessa Io, urticae, Pyrameis cardui* and *P. atalanta* can be divided, similarly as with *Pieris brassicae*, into four main colour types, namely dark, medium, light and gold pupae.

The dark ones are distinguished by the strongest development of the black colouring substance, which covers the rose ground colour again almost wholly and lends the pupa a brown to blackish colouration. Through the opaque envelope no green shimmers through, and the gold lustre is not developed at all or only extremely weakly.

The medium ones show a lesser development of the black colouring substance, so that the light ground colour comes more strongly to the fore. The gold lustre is restricted to a few circumscribed places.

With the light pupae the black colouring substance steps wholly back and the strongly developed opaque rose to white ground colour comes forth almost wholly pure. The gold shimmer is, in comparison to the previous type, more strongly spread out.

The gold pupae have no black colouring substance, but also no opacity of the envelope like the light ones; over the whole pupa a strong metallic gold lustre is spread out.

Concerning the shimmering-through of green, there exists between the Vanessa species a difference, which comes to expression most strongly precisely with the last-named type, in which the pigmentation of the cuticula is lacking. Thus the gold pupae of *Io* let the green of the deeper tissues shimmer through completely; they therefore here have a decidedly green colouration with a transition from green to gold. With the other three species no green shimmers through, unless the greenish reflexes of the gold pupae with *atalanta* and *urticae* were not merely of an optical nature but were to be traced back to a weak shimmering-through of the green colouring substance. With *cardui* these greenish reflexes are also lacking. The wholly fresh pupae, just slipping out of the caterpillar skin, however, are green in all colour types and in all species, before it comes to the development of the pigments in the envelope and to the hardening of the chitin.

Upon what it may rest that the gold pupae, which after all show the very least pigment-deposition in the envelope, retain the green colouration even after the hardening only with *Io* and not also with the others of the named Vanessa species, still requires investigation. It could here be a matter of a development of the green colouring substance itself that is differently strong in the various species [perhaps in connection with the different colour of the caterpillars], or a differently great light-permeability of the chitin envelope in the various species. The latter could be caused either by the various thickness of the cuticula or by a differently strong development of the gold lustre, in that, with a very strong development of the same, all light would already be reflected at the surface and none more could reach the deeper green tissues.

A further difference within this type of the gold pupae concerns the rose to white intercalation, in that with *atalanta* the type of the gold pupae still shows the opaque intercalation in places.

## Localization of the Colour-Factors in the Pupal Envelope
### (under microscopic examination).

### Vanessa Io.

If one places a little piece of envelope cut out of a living pupa under the microscope and examines it at a suitable magnification (the conditions described here refer to an observation with Zeiss ocular 1 and objective 6), one sees, similarly as in *Pieris brassicae* (Brecher 1917), a network of anastomosing canals, which may be more or less filled with dark-brown pigment and at the pigment-free places appear translucent, brilliantly light-yellow.

At the crossing-points of these canals one sees little white spots light up, in the middle of which is a black point, which, on raising the tube, reveals itself to be a hair. These are the pore-canalicules that pass vertically through the chitin and end in a hair-process, as they have been described for the pupal envelope of *Pieris brassicae* by Leydig (1855), Semper (1857), Petersen (1891), Brecher (1917). In contrast to *Pieris brassicae*, in which the hair-process is long, thin and hook-shaped curved, in *Vanessa Io* it is shorter and at the end thickened club-shaped.

On examination of an *Io* pupal envelope under the microscope, what strikes one above all are circumscribed dark-brown or yellow-brown spots — the pore-canalicules surrounded by dark pigment —, from which the mutually anastomosing horizontal canalicules issue. One clearly sees that the deposition of the dark pigment proceeds out from the pore-canalicules. The pigmentation through these is the prominent feature in the envelope of *Pieris brassicae*. In *Vanessa Io*, what strikes us at first glance is rather the dense network, and the pore-canalicules must first be sought out.

The meshes or bars between the network of anastomosing canals show, with the deposition of black pigment and at the various colour types, the seat of various colour-phenomena, in connection with the colour-factors that appear macroscopically as a more strongly or more weakly developed pink-to-white ground-colour or as gold-lustre. On the drying of the envelope in air, all these colour-phenomena and -differences in the bars disappear; one sees the bars gradually fill themselves with air and become darker, grey, finally wholly opaque.

On the addition of a drop of water, they lighten up again and show once more the phenomena characteristic for each colour type.

Already at this magnification, but still more distinctly with immersion, one can at times recognize a hexagonal areolation of the chitin. Especially favourable for this observation are places of the canalicule-system not too densely filled with the dark-brown pigment, where it comes to expression in the granulation of the dark pigment. For these dark granules show namely a very regular arrangement and are separated from one another by equal intervening spaces. On closer examination one recognizes that these lighter intervening spaces around the dark points abut on one another through regular edges. One gains the impression of a hexagonal areolation of the chitin, which also meets us at other places that are free of black pigment, hence also in the meshes of the network, and as one can also perceive it on the envelope of *Pieris brassicae* (Brecher 1917, p. 91) (cf. also Biedermann, Chitin-structures, in Winterstein's Handbook of comparative Physiology 1913). When the dark pigment is deposited very densely, one no longer recognizes the individual fields; such places show a uniform dark-brown colouration and are therefore unsuitable for the recognition of this cellular structure of the chitin.

There, where no dark pigment is deposited at all, or where it is developed only in a light-yellow degree, one can likewise perceive this areolation more poorly, so too around pigment-free pore-canalicules, in that the space situated around the pore-canalicule stands out lighter from the bordering areolated chitin.

It would also be worth mentioning here that with a certain focusing one notices streaks and clefts, which perhaps stand in relation to the characteristic colour-phenomena in the Vanessids.

We can thus recognize, on the pupal envelope of *Vanessa Io*, similarly as on the envelope of *Pieris brassicae*, pore-canalicules that pass vertically through the chitin-envelope and end in a hair; issuing from these, horizontal canals that anastomose with one another and form a network; and furthermore a hexagonal areolation of the chitin.

The deposition of the dark pigment is, in *Io* too, similarly as in *Pieris brassicae*, bound to the surroundings of the pore-canalicules and to the horizontal canalicule-system.

Concerning the superposition of the various pigments and remaining colour-elements, which we have just considered in their surface-arrangement, and concerning the finer structure of the chitin-envelope in the Vanessids, only exact histological and histogenetic investigations on cross-sections of the envelope, separation of the layers by maceration procedures, and other methods suitable for these purposes can give information; these are to follow only later.

### Varieties among the four Main Colour-Types.

The differences between the colour types consist in the various formative quantity and spreading of the black pigment in the canalicules and in the colours which appear in the bars between the canalicules.

To illustrate these varieties, a coloured plate ought to have been appended, which would present the surface-image, seen at the previously mentioned magnification, of one homologous little piece each out of the envelope of a typically dark, middle, light and gold-pupa; but in order not to make the publication of the present work more difficult, the inclusion of coloured plates had to be dispensed with. This plate, however, just like the one depicting the pupal colour-types, will be supplied elsewhere in a comprehensive account of the colour-adaptation of the pupae.

### Very dark Type (Type 1).

The network of anastomosing canals is filled almost entirely with black-brown pigment, out of which the pore-canalicules light up as white little points. Between the broad canals one sees only individual narrow light-yellow coloured cross-anastomoses, which likewise represent parts of the network in which no more pigment was deposited.

The gaps of the network appear in blue-grey colour.

### Middle Type (Type 4).

The network of horizontal canals is filled with the black-brown pigment only in part. At other places it is only the pore-canalicules that are surrounded by a dark pigment-halo, others by a lighter one.

The bars between the canals appear in greenish-grey colour.

### Light Type (Type 6).

The dark pigment is not developed at all. Both the network of horizontal canalicules and the surroundings of the pore-canalicules are free of pigment. The latter appear at the crossing-points of the horizontal canalicules as brownish points, which issue out of the middle of a sharply contoured white circle.

The bars between the canals appear whitish-grey. Only at some places, which show a metallic lustre macroscopically, do they appear in weak interference-colours and indeed reddish-bordered; toward the inside follows blue, thereupon greenish, finally grey.

### Gold-lustred Type (Type 8).

Here no dark pigment is developed at all. The horizontal canals are quite transparent, without pigment. Also the pore-canalicules, which one sees at some places of the horizontal canals as grey points surrounded by a light white halo, show no deposition of pigment in their surroundings.

The bars between the horizontal canalicules shine in various lively interference-colours, which causes the gold-shimmer at the macroscopic examination.

A remark may here be added concerning the strength of the horizontal canalicules in comparison to the bars: At the dark pupae the canalicules filled with black pigment are much broader and the bars lying between them very inconspicuous; whereas at the gold-type the canals represent only quite narrow translucent intervening spaces between the mighty bars shining in lively interference-colours.

Since the black pigment forms still before the hardening of the chitin in the pupal envelope, it appears comprehensible how, in the still plastic substrate, the stronger pigment-formation makes the canalicules broader, while at the absence of pigment-formation they remain thin. But it could perhaps also be the reverse that applies, namely that the remaining-broad of the canalicules is the cause of the black pigmentation (?).

This difference in the breadth of the canalicules, according as they carry pigment or not, recalls the difference described in the chromatophores (Amphibians — Schmidt 1920) in thickness and massiveness between pigment-filled and pigment-free processes. Indeed, the whole arrangement of pore-canalicule as centre, with the horizontal canalicules issuing from it, strongly recalls the picture of chromatophores — in chromatophores too, unifications into a network have been described, Fischel (1919). To what extent this structure and spreading of the pigment, fixed in the pupae through the hardening of the chitin, has a protoplasmic basis that approaches the concept of the chromatophores, could be clarified only by histogenetic investigations.

For the rest, the nature of the chromatophores and their mechanism, as they have been described in fishes, amphibians, etc., is actually still entirely unclear, and the question is perhaps not entirely unjustified whether there too certain structures of the skin, relations of the oxygen-supply, do not locally determine the chromatophores, and whether it might not perhaps also be good to investigate the mechanism of the chromatophores once also from the standpoint of pigment-formation. It does not appear improbable to me that here the extension of the processes might be the primary thing, and that an increased oxygen-supply is connected with this, which would promote pigment-formation. (See also Kudó, this issue.)

## Second Part: Examination of the Influence of Light.

## I. Analysis of the External Light-Effect.

### 1. Influence of coloured surroundings upon the pupal colouration.

#### Experimental arrangement.

The experimental influenceability of the pupal colouration of the Vanessids through various surrounding-colours, already known to earlier authors (Poulton 1887–1892, Kathariner 1905, Menzel 1913), was repeated also in my own experiments as starting-point for the further analysis.

The influence of the following surrounding-colours upon the pupal colouration was here examined: black, grey, white, darkness; red, yellow, yellow-green, blue; furthermore the gold-lustred surroundings decisive, according to the Poultonian view, for the gold-lustre of the Vanessid pupae, and as a control to this a silver-lustred as well as also otherwise-coloured metal-lustred surroundings such as metallic red, gold-green, metallic blue-green and metallic violet.

As experimental containers served the prismatic wooden boxes lined coloured, described and there illustrated in the *Pieris*-work 1917, p. 106, with two-sided light-entry and inclined attachment for the strengthening of the reflection; furthermore in other experimental series small wooden boxes with only upper-side light-entry (see Brecher, *Pieris* 1919, p. 293). The effect of the colours was examined one after another upon *Vanessa urticae*, *V. Io*, *Pyrameis cardui* and *P. atalanta*, and indeed for the two former in all named colours and with very abundant material; for the two latter, of which less material was available, only in four colours each (*cardui* in black, white, yellow, gold, darkness; *atalanta* in black, white, grey, yellow, darkness).

The caterpillars, mostly collected in the field in the half-grown state, then reared until the entry of pupation-ripeness in terraria on nettles (in the case of *cardui* on thistles), were, in these and the other experiments where nothing else is mentioned, as soon as they became pupation-ripe and wandered away from the food, brought into the coloured conditions without the food-plant. Mostly for each one experimental series caterpillars of one rearing were used, or at least from a narrowly delimited locality.

#### Experimental results.

These experiments have, in confirmation of the earlier statements (Poulton 1887–1892), shown the experimental influenceability of the pupal colouration of the Vanessids through various surrounding-colours.

With regard to Poulton's conception that the gold lustre of the Vanessids in a metallically lustrous surrounding stands in a selectionistic connection, we wish first to discuss the influence of the metallic-lustre colours. We see that gold and gold-green too have brought forth the gold pupae, but that the other metallically lustrous surfaces have brought forth no gold pupae, but rather silver and metallic blue-green medium ones without gold and without green, metallic red and metallic violet dark pupae with very much black pigment, likewise entirely without gold.

On the other hand we see, out of the row of the matt colours in yellow and gold-green, the beautiful gold pupae arise, just as in gold. It is therefore — as had already earlier been correctly recognized — the yellow rays which bring forth the gold pupae. It is these rays which, in a gold-lustrous surrounding, cause the gold-lustrous pupae to arise, and not the metallic lustre as such, since indeed other metallically lustrous surfaces, which reflect no yellow, do not have this effect. Under the influence of the yellow rays the development of the black pigment and of the opacity in the shell is hindered, whereby the gold lustre — conditioned no doubt by a definite structure of the shell — comes to the fore. In *Io* the green also still shimmers through the transparent shell, which yields the green gold-lustrous pupae in this species. Likewise it is, in *Pieris brassicae*, in a gold-lustrous surrounding just as on yellow, the green pupae that have arisen. Thus the gold type of the Vanessids corresponds in fact to the green *Pieris* type, since, as we see, they are also called forth by the same conditions. In the Vanessids there comes in addition yet a new factor, namely the gold lustre of the shell, which develops when the shell is transparent and pigment-free, hence comes about everywhere where the development of the pigments in the shell is hindered, thus just as well in yellow and on green leaves of the fodder-plant as also on yellow-reflecting metallically lustrous surfaces, hence also on gold colour.

As regards the other metallic-lustre colours, they too influence the pupal colour through the colour reflected by them.

Now since we can thus rule out metallic lustre as a factor upon the pupal colouration, we wish henceforth to consider in summary the action of all the surrounding colours employed: we see that black, red, violet, blue bring forth quite dark pupae, through increase of the black pigment; yellow and yellow-green bring forth the gold pupae through hindrance of the development of the black pigment and of the white and emergence of the gold lustre, whereby the matt yellow-green paper employed, similar as in *Pieris*, had not entirely uniform results, hence not so strong an action in the direction toward the bringingforth of the green gold-lustrous pupae in *Io* as the gold lustre in urticae had in the latter in yellow. Alongside transparent typical *urticae* pupae there also appeared, with white surroundings, some of pigmented gold pupae [—] (with green leaves, however, there arise always typical gold pupae). White brings forth the brightest pupae, through very slight development of the black pigment and strong emergence of the white opacity. Here *Io* appears less sensitive to white than *urticae* and *Pieris*, in that only with particularly favourable illumination did the brightest pupae arise, but otherwise medium ones. Finally blue-green, as a neutral grey, brings forth medium pupae through a medium development of all the colour factors in the pupal colouration.

The question whether, in the action of coloured surroundings on the pupal colouration, it is a matter of the action of various degrees of intensity or of the action of the colour quality, has been decided in *Pieris*, through numerous varied experimental arrangements (see Second Part 1917, Fourth Part 1919, Fifth Part 1921, Eighth Part, in preparation), in the latter sense.

In the Vanessids too we see, after these experiments, a similar action of the coloured rays as in *Pieris*, namely that of the yellow rays in the hindrance of the development of the pigments in the shell, that of the blue and violet rays in the strong development of the black pigment in the shell.

Where all the active rays are reflected in equal measure, hence in grey or otherwise neutrally acting surfaces, or where no rays at all are present, as in darkness, there follows a medium development of all the colour factors, hence medium pupae.

If we now pass over from the consideration of the action of grey to the two ends of the tone-less row, to white, which reflects all the visible rays, and to black, which absorbs all the visible rays, then at first there seemed to exist a parallel between the increase of the black pigment and the decrease of the light intensity. Poulton, who ascribes to light indeed only one — and that a negative, pigment-hindering — action (namely that of the yellow rays), but assumed no positive action upon the increase of the black pigment, since "the black pigment forms in the pupae indeed also in darkness," wondered, however, about how it comes about that black in strong light brings forth darker pupae than complete darkness. This problem, which had already arisen in *Pieris* (Brecher 1919), has led to the conclusion that the positive action of the black upon the dark colouration of the pupae is to be traced back to the ultraviolet rays reflected by black, hence likewise represents a specific ray-action, just as it could likewise be traced back in *Pieris* with the action of the white surroundings upon the white formation to the action of the ultraviolet rays 1919, Fifth Part 1921.

In the following section the experiments carried out on the Vanessids concerning the role of the ultraviolet rays in the black colouration of the pupae (exclusion through quinine sulphate) and the role of the infrared rays in the white colouration of the pupae will be communicated.

What concerns the action of coloured surfaces and the arising of dark pupae, on this we shall enter more closely in a later section (I, 3).

Provisionally let some words now be devoted to the action of the coloured surroundings on the arising of the gold pupae in *cardui*. With the short experimental series for the verification of the suitability for influencing through the preposing of the surrounding colours of *P. cardui*, there resulted the actual fact characteristic of the influenceability, that in all the boxes employed predominantly gold pupae arose. Some days later there showed itself in fact that the largest part of the pupae of all boxes harboured parasites (an ichneumonid: *Amblytetes camelinus* Wesm.), which let themselves be recognized through the bursting of the pupal shell already at this departure. Now there were precisely those pupae in all surrounding colours that had been afflicted by parasites the gold ones, while the few that had not been afflicted let the action of the surrounding colours be recognized, so in black dark, in white pale ones; and not in yellow and gold were also the not-afflicted pupae likewise gold-lustrous. This one could secondly determine also on the empty pupal shells already gold-lustrous. One could in the pupae, in which still the essential of the others' appearance was held, [determine] — i.e., according to whether it was the butterfly or whether only a parasite has slipped out of the pupa. In the former case the departure takes place through the median dorsal slit; in the second case there slips out of the gold and white again only the bare shell; the rest of the opening-mode then is again another, in that the front shell of the pupae afflicted by parasites is raised in the form of a kind of flap.

Thus one could in *cardui* determine the same mode of action of the surroundings on the pupal colouration as in the other forms, where the empty ones, already gold-lustrous, [could be] determined. We learn in *cardui* to know a second possible cause for the arising of the gold pupae, namely a state of weakness manifestly called forth through the harbouring of a parasite, or other change, which hinders the pigment formation independently of the acting-in surrounding colours, whereby the gold lustre comes to appearance.

Although the other Vanessid pupae, *Io*, *urticae*, etc., also harbour parasites (such as the maggots that emerge and then harden into puparia [*Tönnchen*], — *Tachinen* —), this has nevertheless not exerted an influence on the characteristic action of the surrounding colour. They can indeed be afflicted by parasites just as well dark as medium, pale and gold pupae. Also here no reddening of the pupa follows from the departure of the dark pupae. It means therefore with these forms no error, when in the registration the pierced pupae too have been registered.

A further observation concerns *Pyrameis atalanta*: *Vanessa atalanta* occurs in two caterpillar-formations, one black with white spots and one pale greyish-green. It results, then, that these two different caterpillar colourations are unimportant for the pupal colouration and for the influenceability of the same. Furthermore is to be remarked regarding this, that even the yellow rays cannot hinder the opacity.

If we now go over to the question of how, when rays reflect in like manner, hence in grey or in neutral-grey-acting surroundings, the pupal colour turns out, then it will be more closely entered upon in a later section (I, 3).

## 2. Action of invisible rays.

### Vanessa urticae.

### a) Experiments on the role of the ultraviolet rays (in the action of black surroundings).

#### Exclusion through quinine sulphate.

In order to investigate the role of the ultraviolet rays for the positive action of black surroundings on the black colouration of the Vanessid pupae, the method of the exclusion of the ultraviolet rays from the black surroundings through preposing of quinine-sulphate solution employed for *Pieris* was used. There came hereby black-clothed little boxes with double-glazed light-access (description and figure in Brecher, Fourth Part 1919, p. 297) to be employed for the verification, and indeed:

1. one-sidedly with preposing of quinine sulphate, exclusion of the ultraviolet rays,

2. one control-experiment with full light-access,

3. one control-experiment with weak lowering of the general light-intensity through covering with white tracing paper [*Pauspapier*].

The measurement of the light-strength with photographic paper yielded with the preposing of quinine sulphate the same weak lowering of the intensity as the lowering of the general light-intensity through tracing-paper covering. In both latter conditions the blackening was the same.

#### Course of the experiment (cf. Table A).

Black with full light-access yielded throughout very dark and dark pupae (Type 1 and 2).

Black with exclusion of the ultraviolet rays through quinine sulphate yielded a not insignificant lightening of the pupae in comparison to black normal. There arose here predominantly pale pupae (Type 5), similar as in the white-experiments.

Black with lowering of the general intensity through tracing-paper covering on the contrary yielded dark pupae just as black with full light-access.

#### Results of the experiment.

Preposing of quinine sulphate let pale pupae arise also in black, while the equally effective lowering of the general light-intensity through use of photographic paper (ordinary celloidin paper) did not have this effect.

These experiments confirm in striking manner also for *urticae* what had already been ascertained for the experiments on *Pieris brassicae* (Brecher, Fourth Part, 1919), namely that the positive action of black surroundings just as the dark colouration of the pupae depends on the action of the ultraviolet rays reflected from black.

### b) Experiments on the role of the infrared rays (in the action of white surroundings).

#### Experimental arrangement.

For verification or repetition I wish to refer here to the experimental arrangement described more closely for this purpose in the *Pieris*-work, Fifth Part 1921, p. 6 ff., and there depicted in the text-figure. There were employed, just as in white-clothed round glasses, the following experimental conditions.

White at normal Temperature (room temperature 23°):

1. with exclusion of the ultraviolet rays through preposing of iron-vitriol-rhodankalium solution,

2. with full light-access,

3. with lowering of the general light-intensity through covering with a layer of tracing paper.

(Measurement with photographic paper yielded with this latter experimental condition a somewhat stronger lowering of the photographically effective light-intensity than the preposing of rhodankalium.)

White at heightened Temperature (34½°) through ice-jacket solution [*Eismantellösung*], temperature near entry of the ice on the floor of the container 4°, at upper edge 9°, however did not stay constant, but rose with melting to 14°): 1. with exclusion of the ultraviolet rays through preposing of iron-vitriol-rhodankalium,

2. with full light-access,

3. with lowering of the general light-intensity through covering with tracing paper.

White at heightened Temperature (34½°, see *Pieris* V, 1921, p. 8) with full light-access.

As controls to these experiments served caterpillars of the same rearing, simultaneously pupated in black and grey boxes and on green leaves of the fodder-plant in the terrarium.

The experiments White at normal temperature were repeated.

#### Course and results of the experiment (cf. hereto Table B).

For comparison of the results were chiefly decisive those pupae which had pupated immediately on the white surface at the illuminated places, thus were actually exposed to the influence of the white surroundings, that is, those pupated under the upper edge (see *Pieris*, V, 1921).

What concerns the experiments at normal temperature, it shows itself that White with full light-access just as in black and white with somewhat lowered intensity, pale pupae with whitish-shimmering gold lustre (Type 5) arose.

In contrast, the preposing of iron-vitriol-rhodankalium had in consequence that pale pupae of a whitish lustre (Type 5) arose, similar as in black with full light-access without the preposing of quinine sulphate.

These experiment-results do not stand in any contradiction with the somewhat yellowish-green colour of the solution with the preposing of iron-vitriol-rhodankalium, since otherwise these pupae would have had to arise, corresponding to the action of the yellow rays, just as without lowering of the intensity, with an even stronger gold lustre (no difference compared to the normal ones).

With the experiments at lowered temperature there shows itself between the preposings no difference compared to the normal temperature.

Manifestly no noticeable lowering of the temperature through the ice-jacket was attained at this height. On the other hand it is namely to be considered that the differences existing through the various preposings in the content of ultraviolet rays were with the thermometer just barely measurable and thus yielded no clear differences in the pupal colouration, whereas through the ice-jacket itself at the upper edge a certain temperature-lowering compared to the room temperature would have to have been reached, and yet the pupae show themselves to be different independently of this, according to the pre-screening, just as do those at normal temperature.

At deeper places in the ice-enveloped experimental containers, where therefore an actual marked lowering of the temperature must have taken place, there shows itself, on the other hand, clearly the darkening action of the cold, in that here the white-effect appears entirely abolished and fairly darkly pigmented pupae, Type 1 and 2, arose.

White with raised temperature had the opposite-sense extreme effect, in that here the whitest pupae of all arose. They are throughout white pupae looking like mother-of-pearl, in which no black pigment at all is developed, but on the other hand a strong gold lustre and a very strong development of a white opacity. The combination of gold lustre and white underlay calls forth precisely the impression of mother-of-pearl. They are the white-shimmering pupae such as arise in White at normal temperature, but the prevention of the black pigment and the strong development of the white carried to the extreme. From the gold-pupae arising in Yellow they are entirely different. For this let reference be made to the pupa of *urticae* figured by v. Linden (1905), which she likewise obtained at raised temperature.

Standfuss too had brought *urticae*- and *cardui*-caterpillars to pupation in White and other colours: blue, yellow, red at 40° resp. 37°, and obtained white pupae only with a white background, whereas with the other colours a raising of the temperature did not have this effect. Likewise he too obtained, even in White at normal temperature, no white pupae.

These experiments thus show clearly, just as for *Pieris*, the influence of the heat-rays and of warmth in general upon the prevention of the black pigment and the favouring of the white opacity. The latter effect is with *urticae* even still much more clearly evident than with *Pieris brassicae*, since with *urticae* the other types have no white, but rather a pink opacity in the shell.

These experiments were thus able to confirm in White the connection found with *Pieris* between the ultrared rays and the white pupae, and to show that the action of a white surroundings upon the arising of white pupae is to be traced back, also with *V. urticae*, to the action of the ultrared rays.

### Vanessa Io (cf. Table B).

Experiments set up with *V. io* for the same purpose, with the pre-screening of quinine sulphate resp. iron-vitriol–rhodankalium [potassium thiocyanate] in front of black, red and white surroundings, unfortunately yielded no very clear differences according to the pre-screenings.

The cause for this is presumably to be sought in the poor lighting conditions that prevailed in these experiments.

In consequence, there arose in Black no quite dark pupae, and since between the pupae from Black on the one hand and those arising in darkness — that is, the normal Type 2 — as well as those in neutral surroundings on the other hand, there exists only a weak gradual difference, it is clear that the pre-screening of quinine sulphate could have no clear lightening as its result. It is otherwise with *urticae* and *Pieris brassicae*, where between darkness-pupae and black-pupae very clear differences exist.

Likewise the evidently lesser sensitivity of *Io* to White is to blame for the failure of the experiments with rhodankalium pre-screening, since already in consequence of the unfavourable light conditions White without pre-screening did not yield the quite light pupae.

By contrast, the experiment White with raised temperature had a result of the same sense as the corresponding experiments on *Pieris* and *urticae*, which thus is a further support of the conclusions drawn there concerning the action of the heat-rays. There arose here, namely, pupae entirely without black pigment and without green, with slight gold lustre and strong development of white opacity in the shell, whereby the pupa appears of a whitish-yellowish colour.

(In general one gains from all the experiments the impression that *Io* is less sensitive to a white surroundings than *urticae* and *P. brassicae*; likewise that the difference between the pupae from Black and from darkness is smaller than with the named species, whereas the sensitivity to yellow rays is even greater than with the named species.)

#### Experimental results.

These experiments show that coloured light transmitted through a filter also has an influence upon the pupal colouration just as the reflected coloured light does:

Yellow transmitted light brings about, just as yellow-reflecting surfaces do, the arising of the pupae without black pigment and without opacity, but with strong gold lustre of the shell, which with *Io* still lets the green shimmer through. Both transmitted and reflected yellow light thus yields with *Io* the gold-green, with *urticae* the gold-lustred type.

Blue transmitted light yields, just as blue-reflecting surfaces do, dark pupae with much black pigment and without gold lustre.

A difference in the action of light transmitted through a filter and light reflected from surfaces in white daylight shows itself, in agreement with the data of Poulton (1892) and Kathariner (1905), with Red: red surfaces in white light bring forth, just as Black does, quite dark pupae, whereas the red light transmitted through the filter has the same effect as yellow light, in that it brings forth typical gold-green resp. gold-pupae.

Through the investigations carried out on the occasion of the similar experiments on *Pieris brassicae* (Seventh Part, this issue 1922) concerning the content of ultraviolet rays in the various coloured surfaces and under filters, by means of a paper of paraphenylenediamine sensitive only to ultraviolet rays, we can explain this difference in the action of the Red by the fact that red surfaces in white light, in consequence of the ultraviolet rays reflected from these surfaces to a high degree just as from Black, bring forth dark pupae with strong development of the black colouring-matter; but that under the red filters used the ultraviolet component is lacking in the transmitted light, and therefore pupae without black pigment arise.

The action of the transmitted red light must, however, in addition be a positive one, for otherwise under red filters just such pupae would have to arise as in darkness. This is not the case, since in darkness with Vanessids dark pupae arise, whereas under the red filters gold-green resp. golden pupae arise, hence similar ones as in Yellow.

For this distinction of the action of the Red from the action of darkness, the experiments on Vanessids are even much more favourable than those on *Pieris brassicae*.

The action of the red filters is thus a similar one to that of the yellow light.

Since the subsequent spectroscopic investigation — admittedly under extremely strong arc light — which I owe to the kindness of Herr Prof. Karl Przibram (Radium Institute), showed that through the red gelatine some orange too is still transmitted, it must after these experiments still remain undecided whether the green resp. gold-pupae in the transmitted red light are to be traced back to an admixture of orange and somewhat yellow rays in the light transmitted by the filter, or to a yellow-like action of the red rays themselves. Only upon the use of absolutely monochromatic red light will it become possible to become acquainted with the specific action of the red rays. The fact that with *Pieris* and *urticae* under the red lithium-carmine bell jar the pupae that arose are somewhat whiter, more opaque, than those characteristic of Yellow, could also speak for the possibility that perhaps the action of the red rays stands close to the action of the ultrared rays.

In view of the fact that the influencing of the pupal colouration through the colour of the surroundings takes place through the eye (see the next chapter), I should like here to point to the interesting parallel which appears to exist between the specific actions of the colours upon the colouration and the distinction of the colours in insects (bees, v. Frisch [1914], *Bombylius fuliginosus* Knoll [1921], butterflies Knoll [in press]). According to v. Frisch (1914), the bees distinguish Yellow and Blue, but they confuse a Red with Black, blue-green with grey; also they are, within the warm and cold colours, not capable of distinguishing the colour-gradations: they confuse, on the one hand, orange-red with yellow and green, and on the other hand blue with violet and purple-red. »The behaviour of the bees thus recalls the symptoms that are characteristic of red-green-blind persons.« Likewise we see (if we disregard the action of the ultrared rays) two specific actions of the spectral regions upon the pupal colouration, namely that of the yellow and that of the blue to ultraviolet rays; whereas to the red and green part no specific action belongs, and they act, according to the admixture of ultraviolet resp. blue rays, like Black resp. like Grey, and in the absence of these and the probable admixture of orange or yellow, similarly to the latter.

## II. Path of the Light-Influence.

*Vanessa Io, V. urticae.*

In order to decide the question whether the colour-influence upon the caterpillar takes place by way of a perception through the eyes, Poulton (1887) had painted over the eyes of the caterpillars (*V. urticae*) with black lacquer, whereupon it showed itself that the lacquered ones too let the same colour-influence be recognized as the non-lacquered ones. From this he concluded that the colour-action upon the caterpillar takes place independently of the eye.

These experiments of Poulton's were repeated by me. I brought caterpillars of *Io*, whose eyes I had painted over with an oil-paint, bone-black, into coloured boxes (black, yellow), into diffuse daylight as well as into darkness.

There arose in Yellow gold-green ones, in Black dark ones (yet not quite so dark as from non-lacquered caterpillars), in darkness dark and more greenish ones (cf. Table D). These experiments had thus, in confirmation of Poulton's experiments, yielded that the lacquering of the eyes does not abolish the influence of the colours upon the pupal colouration. It seemed hereby that the colour-influence is not mediated through a visual sensation. The fact that lacquered caterpillars behave, in the evening after darkness has set in, on the approach of an incandescent lamp, differently from non-lacquered ones, in that they carry out no searching-movements, spoke for the [conclusion] that the lacquered caterpillars could not have seen.

Experiments with electrocautery blinding, which Herr Prof. Przibram carried out on caterpillars of *Pieris* (cf. in Brecher 1919) and *Io* (cf. Table D, Series B), yielded the fact, surprising to me, that the colour-action was abolished: there arose in Yellow, Black, as well as in darkness, the same and indeed middling pupae, a few more greenish ones however these too in all surroundings. Although through this kind of operation, which is accompanied by a certain loss of blood, only a small percentage attains to pupation, still the results of *Io* sum up with those of *Pieris* and show that here it cannot be a matter of chance occurrences. The removal of the eye through electrocautery blinding thus has the abolition of the colour-action as a consequence.

Control experiments, which I carried out with caterpillars that had been bled by the cutting-off of a leg, showed that with these caterpillars the characteristic colour-action (Yellow and Black were tested) was not abolished.

From this it emerged that it is not the injury as such with the electrocautery-blinded ones that has the abolition of the colour-action as a consequence, but rather that it must be the absence of the eye which abolishes the colour-influence.

In order to meet an objection of Dürken's (Naturwiss. 1918), whether perhaps in the electrocautery blinding the heat-factor might play a role, Przibram sought in 1920 for a more favourable and more unobjectionable method for the removal of the eyes with the caterpillars. He found the following very favourable method: by means of a silken noose the whole head is tied off and thereupon the eyes or the whole head are cut off¹). The previous tying-off has the purpose of preventing the bleeding completely. The headless caterpillars so operated upon pupate very well and yield quite beautiful pupae. With this method one obtains a much greater percentage of pupations than with the electrocautery blinding.

With this operation-technique too it showed itself very clearly that the absence of the eyes had the abolition of the colour-action as a consequence: there arose everywhere, that is, in particular also in Yellow, the same pupae as in darkness. (Tested with *urticae* too.)

It thus emerges from these experiments with removal of the eyes, beyond doubt, that for the colour-action the eye is unconditionally necessary. Wherein, however, the role of the eye consists could not yet emerge from these experiments.

Is the role of the eye of a purely chemical nature (cf. e.g. Przibram, Causes of Animal Colour-Clothing II: The eye functions as a tyrosinase-organ), bound, independently of a colour-sensation, only to the presence of the eye?

Or is the colour-influence mediated through the visual sensation? Perhaps after all some light-rays had penetrated into the eye even with the lacquered caterpillars and had thus brought about the specific colour-action.

These considerations made it appear necessary for me to occupy myself again with lacquering experiments. Should the results of my earlier lacquering experiments, like those of Poulton too, actually be traced back to [the fact] that rays had penetrated through the lacquer-layer into the eye, then the following experimental arrangement ought to decide the question whether the colour-action is mediated through the eye: with decreasing light-intensity the colour-action upon the lacquered caterpillars ought to cease earlier than with the non-lacquered ones, since after all the black coating over the eyes must in any case weaken the light-intensity. In particular this ought to show itself very clearly with the action of Yellow.

## III. Colour-Sensitivity in the Various Stages up to Pupation.

Poulton (1887) had, as is known, established that not, as one previously believed, the fresh pupa, but rather the caterpillar is colour-sensitive, and he ascertained by means of his »transference experiments« the time-point of the colour-sensitivity, the »sensible period« of the caterpillar.

Poulton distinguishes three stages before pupation:

Stage I: The caterpillar ceases to feed and usually leaves the food-plant and begins to wander about.

Stage II: The caterpillar remains quiet at the pupation-place chosen by it and spins, at the end of this stage, between the hind-feet a silken web, by which it is fastened to the under-surface.

Stage III: The pupa of the Vanessids has hung itself up on the silken web and hangs with the forebody directed downward, the head curved somewhat upward. This stage lasts up to the stripping-off of the caterpillar-skin, that is, up to the pupation.

Concerning the duration of the various stages Poulton made exact observations.

According to Poulton the sensible stage falls in Stage II and the beginning of Stage III and ceases some hours before the pupation. Of these Stage II is said to be the more sensitive (for *urticae* and *Pieris brassicae*). With *io* Stage II is very short, 5–9 hours, Stage III very long, 25–32 hours, and Poulton therefore assumed that with *io* the colour-sensitivity lasts a much longer time still during Stage III, indeed that even the most important part is shifted onto Stage III.

In view of the assessment of the results of Poulton's »conflicting colours experiments« for the ascertainment of the role of the eyes for the colouration of the pupa, in which he had already used hanging caterpillars, as well as of such of my own, and in order to win points of reference about the stage to be used in the setting-up of further similar experiments for the decision of the question whether the light-influence is mediated through a visual sensation, it seemed to me necessary to test once more for myself the colour-sensitivity of the various stages in my own experiments.

In yet another connection too the setting-up of such observations seemed to me of interest, namely in view of the effectiveness of the tyrosinase (cf. the section Chemism of the colour adaptation), which consists in this, that yellow rays first increase the effectiveness of the tyrosinase and then exhaust it — a fact which we have already adduced for the explanation of the appearance of black alongside green stick-insects [Stabhaarschrecken] in yellow surroundings (Przibram-Brecher 1922) —, black on the other hand first inhibits it, then favours it. It would be possible that, upon bringing the caterpillars into the colour-conditions at a later developmental stage, the shorter effect-duration of the colours would have dark pupae in Yellow, on the other hand light ones in Black, as a consequence. Admittedly *io* is for this purpose, in consequence of the slight differences between the effect of black and darkness, less suited than *Pieris brassicae* would be. And in close connection these observations also yield a parallel to the difference, to be treated in the third part, of the chemism of these stages, with which the various colour-sensitivity of the same stands in connection.

In consequence of the slight gradual difference between the effect of black and darkness with *io*, mainly the experiments with yellow surroundings come into consideration for the establishment of whether an influencing has still taken place.

Poulton's Stage I: After a shorter or longer time of the wandering.

Poulton's Stage II: Caterpillars that, with contracted segments, rest quietly at a single place.

Stage IIa: Quiet, already attached caterpillars.

### Experimental results.

As Poulton already assumed, and as the experiences in my own experiments have taught me, no influencing takes place upon caterpillars in the wandering stage after Stage III; otherwise it would have to make a difference in the experimental results whether the caterpillars had previously wandered for a shorter or longer time under other conditions. That, however, is not the case in my experiments either.

The critical stage for colour-influencing is the quiet stage, and when the caterpillars are brought into the colour-conditions in this stage at an early hour of the day, so that the light can act upon this stage straight away, then one obtains the characteristic influencing, that is, in yellow the pupae characteristic of yellow. But if the caterpillars are brought in at a later hour of the day, the result is no longer so certain. Those which began to wander again and then still fixated gave, in yellow, partly the typical golden-green [pupae], partly however deviating types. Perhaps this difference is to be explained by the fact that the former still wandered the whole night and only fixated towards morning, so that the influencing in the morning light could still take place, [whereas] the latter hung themselves up straight away in the evening and thus the time of colour-sensitivity already lay [behind them] for the time of darkness. Those that could no longer wander and fixate show no influencing at all any longer.

As regards the colour-sensitivity during the hanging stage, it must be noted, in order to assess the results without doubt, that the influencing during the hanging stage could take place in the way that the brought-in caterpillars avoided the differently coloured underlay, settled on the upperside, that is, again on the fixated caterpillars of the new surroundings, and that in this way a falsification of the normal course of the stages occurred, if otherwise one ought to have exposed the hanging caterpillars to the early times of day in coloured surroundings.

And likewise one must regard Poulton's "Conflicting colours experiments", which he carried out with already hanging caterpillars, somewhat sceptically.

It would in future be best to carry out such experiments for the determination of the role of the colour-sensation of the caterpillar for the resulting pupal colouration with caterpillars that find themselves in the quiet stage or in an advanced wandering stage.

## IV. Is the similarity of the pupal colour to the surroundings to be traced back to a choosing-ability of the caterpillars ripe for pupation?

### Experimental arrangement.

The view advocated by Doflein (1907), that the colour-adaptation of the animals in the surroundings rests on a choosing-ability of these animals, namely [their ability] to seek out for their abode that surroundings which resembled their own colouration, was sought to be confirmed for the Vanessa caterpillars also by a student, Hedwig Menzel (1913). This work gave occasion for the experiments described in the following.

For this purpose the large, two-part, coloured-lined wooden boxes (Brecher 1917, p. 106) used [elsewhere] were adapted in such a way that the two compartments of a box were lined with two different colours acting oppositely upon the pupal colouration; namely, in one box one compartment was lined with silver-paper, the other with yellow; in another box one compartment with black, the other with green-yellow. The glass wall separating the two compartments was removed, then a larger number of wandering caterpillars was placed in each double-box and the boxes were set up in daylight. The caterpillars wandered about in the box for 1–2 hours over the two colours, in order finally to come to rest somewhere in the one or the other compartment. The box was thereupon at once placed in darkness, in order to withdraw the caterpillars from the colour-action. In the sense of the Doflein view, there must after all lie in the caterpillars themselves the tendency to form either green or dark pupae, which would have induced them to seek out the surroundings coloured agreeably to them. The caterpillars that wandered into the yellow or green compartment must thus already beforehand have been predestined to a green pupal type, and consequently green pupae must arise from these caterpillars, even if the yellow rays did not act at all and the whole sensitive stage elapsed in darkness. On the contrary, among the caterpillars that had wandered towards black or silver-grey, which came [to rest] in the darkness, no green pupae whatever ought to be found.

### Course of the experiment (see Table F).

On removal of the pupae from the boxes kept in darkness, the following distribution of the pupae according to number and colour over the compartments showed itself:

In the yellow-silver box, of the 40 caterpillars introduced, 14 had gone into the silver compartment and 26 into the yellow compartment and had pupated here. Among all these pupae only a single green one arose, and indeed precisely in the silver compartment. All the other caterpillars, those wandered towards yellow as well as those towards silver, gave dark pupae corresponding to the darkness.

In the black-green box, of 40 caterpillars 36, that is, the most, wandered into the green compartment and only 4 into the black compartment. In this experiment no green pupae whatever arose, but rather dark and middle ones, such as always arise in darkness. It follows therefore from these experiments too, as indeed all the other experiments have already shown, that it is the direct light-action that is authoritative for the pupal colouration.

## Third part: Chemism of the colour-adaptation.

We come now to the question, in what way the light of different wavelength conditions the pupal colourations of the Vanessids, that is, which constituent colour-elements of the pupal colouration it influences.

What the colour-elements concerns, the following:

1. The melanin of the pore-channels colours more or less strongly, in the network of the horizontal channels of the chitin, the as yet colourless chitin black; the black pigment is melanin, whose formation is conditioned through the oxidation of a blood-dye, as has likewise been able to be confirmed [for] the Vanessids (cf. Fürth and Schneider, 1912). The oxidative origin of the melanin is characterized by the fact that one finds it everywhere at the localizations of these pigments at the pore-channels, where it appears even more clearly in blue pupae through the stronger oxygen supply.

2. The rose colour lies in the envelope of the Vanessa pupae as stored opacity; it is by its nature not yet exactly known and later investigations should occupy themselves with it.

3. The green, which in the case of a transparent envelope can shimmer through more or less, is a yellow-green, ether- and alcohol-soluble dye, the so-called "animal green" [Tiergrün] (Przibram 1906) widespread among insects, a lipochrome.

4. The golden lustre, which appears in the absence of a deposition of pigment in the envelope, is probably, as is also assumed by Poulton, Biedermann (Winterstein's Handbook: Colour and Marking of Insects, 1914), Lord Rayleigh (1920), a structural colour, which rests on an interference phenomenon produced by a particular structure of the chitin envelope. (The golden lustre disappears upon the drying-out of the envelope and reappears upon moistening; it cannot be extracted by extraction agents.) There are as yet no more exact investigations of the golden lustre of the Vanessa pupae available, such as have been made e.g. for the iridescent colours in other insects (Biedermann 1904, Kapzow 1911, etc.). Investigations on this are planned by me for later.

It is clear that the differing development of the dark dye in the first place determines the pupal-colour type. It was therefore above all the task (Pieris, Brecher 1917, 1921) to follow the connection between the development of the black dye in the envelope and the different surrounding-colours. It became evident from these investigations, and especially also further advanced through investigations on Halimasch-tyrosinase and animal tyrosinases (Przibram-Brecher 1919, Przibram-Dembowski 1919), that the action of the colours upon the development of the melanin in the envelope is to be traced back: 1. to the colour-sensitivity of the tyrosinase, 2. a reaction-sensitivity of the tyrosinase, 3. a particular mode of action of the colours of different wavelength upon the reaction-state (Przibram, Theory 1919) and 4. particular changes of the chemism during the stages before pupation, with which probably also changes of the reaction-state go hand in hand, and which explain the sensitivity to light precisely in this stage, further also the adaptive colour-action (Pieris, Brecher 1921).

The first three factors are of more general nature. The tyrosinase is strongly widespread in the animal and plant kingdom; nevertheless it does not everywhere come to an adaptation, and the butterfly pupae of the different species also behave differently in this. Thus it is actually necessary, for the explanation of the adaptive action of the light upon the pupal colouration of the species with light-variable pupae, as also for the obtaining of points of reference about the origin of this adaptive capacity, to carry out as numerous as possible investigations in different species, both light-variable ones as well as such as show no light-variability. These [investigations] must devote particular attention, among the named factors, to the chemism in the stages before pupation. Further, the influenceability of the tyrosinase by light in the different stages must be followed.

First of all it is naturally important to carry out such comparative investigations on the species, [comparing] the light-variable species.

There were therefore carried out for the Vanessids the following investigations:

I. Testing of the reaction-state of the different stages up to after pupation.

II. Influencing of the tyrosinase through light of different wavelength during the transformation from caterpillar to pupa.

These investigations have for their subject the dependence of the melanin-formation on the light of different wavelength.

What the dependence of the individual colour-elements upon light concerns, they are not treated in the present work.

Regarding the green, a connection with the yellow rays is to be assumed according to the findings on *Pieris*. Since the Vanessids show, precisely with regard to the green, such great differences between the individual species, it would also be interesting to carry out investigations regarding the causes underlying this difference. Investigations on the green dye are in progress, which will perhaps give information about the nature of the same and its genesis.

What the whole deposition in the envelope concerns, there exists probably a direct connection with the light, and indeed in the sense of a prevention through the yellow rays and a promotion through the ultra-red [rays]; but investigations on the manner [of this] can first be carried out when the nature of this colour-element will be known. (On this too I have investigations in progress.)

Finally, with the golden lustre it is a matter not so much of a body-colour, but rather of a structural colour; investigations on this therefore do not belong within the framework of this part dealing with the chemism of the colour-adaptation. Investigations on the same will only be able to be carried out later. In any case, with the appearance of the golden lustre under particular conditions a direct connection with the light is little probable; rather the prevention [of the deposition] of the pigments in the envelope might here be the primary [factor].

## V. Chemism of the different stages in the transformation from caterpillar to pupa (in neutral surroundings).

These investigations yield a different behaviour of the various developmental stages in the transformation from caterpillar to pupa, both in *Io* and in *urticae*.

According to the earlier investigations (Przibram-Brecher 1919), the falling-out of the melanin indicates a more strongly acid degree. According to this criterion, both in *Io* and in *urticae* the falling-out of the melanin in the samples with the tyrosinase of pupation-ripe caterpillars of the various stages up to the hanging-up indicates, [and] the remaining-suspended of the melanin with the tyrosinase of the stages after the hanging-up indicates, a more acid reaction of the tyrosinase of the caterpillar from the time when, near pupation-ripeness, it still sits on the plant and still feeds, up to the hanging-up, and [a shift] toward a less acid reaction during the hanging and the pupal stages.

This reaction-reversal from a more acid to a less acid state takes place around the time between fixation and hanging-up, as appears to follow from the divided behaviour of the melanin samples with the tyrosinase of these stages. In *Io*, of the samples prepared with the tyrosinase of the »quiet-to-fixed caterpillars« 2 have fallen out strongly, 2 very slightly, and 4 not at all. In *urticae* the samples treated with the tyrosinase of freshly hung-up caterpillars gave a similar divided result, of partly fallen, partly suspended-remaining samples (only 2 fallen, the others not fallen), which indeed points approximately to the same moment of the reversal. I should like to recall here once more that, according to the investigations of Poulton (1887) and to my own experiences, the light-sensitive stage lasts from the time of wandering (according to my experiences, of »red«-wandering) until quite a short time after the hanging-up, and indeed falls especially into the time of the quiet and fixed stage, and it is precisely this time in which, as we see, the reaction-reversal takes place.

This method of working-up, however, gives us no information about the more precise reaction course up to the reversal and after it; for that, other methods of investigation must be applied.

A difference with respect to the reaction-change of the stages between the Vanessids and *Pieris* (cf. VI, 1921) consists in this, that in *Pieris* the large feeding caterpillars are less acid and give no falling-out of the melanin, and even the red-defecating wandering *Pieris brassicae* (corresponding to the red-wandering ones of the Vanessids) give a tyrosinase which, on immediate setting-up of the samples, causes no falling-out of the melanin, and only the longer-standing tyrosinase of this stage, or tyrosinase from caterpillars in a more advanced stage before fixation, causes a rapid falling-out of the melanin, whereas in the Vanessids the caterpillars already in the feeding stage give a strongly falling-out melanin. With respect to the moment of the reversal from the strongly acid into a less acid state, which in the Vanessids was established as occurring around the time of the hanging-up, no comparisons can yet be drawn according to the investigations on *Pieris* hitherto (VI, 1921), because the Pierids do not hang themselves up but remain fastened to the substrate by a girdle, and because hitherto I had not yet made any distinctions in *Pieris* corresponding to the fixed and the hanging stage of the Vanessids. In any case it agrees in general with the behaviour of the Vanessids that in *Pieris* the tyrosinase of the fixed ones gave a falling, [and] the tyrosinase of the pupae a suspended-remaining, melanin formation. Only the quite evident strong acidification in *Pieris* during the red-wandering, that is at the beginning of the sensitive stage, is not clearly recognizable in the Vanessids, since already the caterpillars still on the plant are acid and gave a falling melanin formation.

Another difference between the Vanessids and *Pieris* concerns the angel colour [Angelfarbe]. In *Pieris* the tyrosinase of the feeding caterpillars produces a rosa [pink] angel colour, that of the red-wandering ones a red to deep cherry-red, quite similar to that which the tyrosinase of *Halimasch* [honey fungus] shows. Only in the later stage, after the tyrosinase has passed through the red phase in the body itself, do normally only violet tones appear as angel colours. In the Vanessids, although under continuous observation, rosa tones as angel colour of the samples were never noticed, or only extremely weakly and transiently. Violet or blue-violet tones appeared at once. To be sure, a reddish colouration of the filter on which residue of Vanessid blood precipitated by ammonium sulphate had lain did show — and indeed regularly in the corresponding stages, wandering green, wandering red, and quietly fixed — that here too the red phase occurs, but quite transiently, during the working-up.

The tones of the angel colours are connected, as Przibram (in Przibram-Brecher 1919) has found, with the reaction state: red colour-tones indicate a more alkaline, blue ones a more acid reaction; hence the angel colour of the *Halimasch* tyrosinase is red, that of the animal tyrosinases in general blue-violet. The discovery of a red angel colour in *Pieris* (Brecher VI, 1921) in the feeding and red-wandering caterpillar stage agreed with the discovery that these stages gave no falling-out melanin formation, and pointed to a less acid state in these stages. The fact that in the Vanessids no such red phase is to be noticed, but rather that the samples turn violet directly, agrees perfectly with the other phenomenon, that already the samples with tyrosinase of large feeding caterpillars give a falling melanin formation, and speaks for an already more strongly acid state in this pre-pupation-ripe stage.

With respect to the activity-curve of the tyrosinase in the course of the development from caterpillar to pupa in the Vanessids, it appears that the activity in smaller caterpillars is very weak, then in the adult ones is very strong and steadily increases; at fixation it reaches its peak (1 minute angel-time for the ¹/₁ tyrosinase); during the hanging stage and in the just-emerging still-green pupae, in which no melanin formation has yet taken place, it is likewise very strong. The slight decrease in activity of these two stages compared with the fixed one, as appears from the registered angel-times of the samples, is probably only an apparent one, in that precisely in this stage of the hanging caterpillars and of those engaged in pupation the blackening of the blood is so extraordinarily lively, compared with the earlier caterpillar stage, that already during the working-up into tyrosinase a large part of it is exhausted through melanin formation, so that the dissolved tyrosinase already contains fewer active particles. But already during the hanging stage, too, blackening processes evidently take place in the living caterpillar, since it has often happened that caterpillars of this stage, on cutting into the leg for bleeding, let out at once a brownish-green blood. Other hanging caterpillars have, like the caterpillars from earlier stages, yellow-green (in *Io*), others, which are further advanced, pure-green (*Io*) blood without a yellowish tinge. These differences, which were noticed only by chance and could not yet be pursued further, allow one to infer a blackening process in the blood during the hanging stage. At its close the yellow-brown component disappears in the blood, and the same is now pure green.

As soon as the melanin is formed in the pupal envelope, the tyrosinase activity sinks to a minimum, in order then later, during the pupal stage, to recover again. In general this activity-curve agrees with that found in *Pieris*. (Cf. also Dewitz 1905.)

It is also of interest here to take into consideration the difference in the precipitation of the blood by ammonium sulphate in the various stages; not in all stages, however, is the activity of the tyrosinase dependent on the amount of the residue, but in some stages it is after all: the smaller caterpillars give extremely little precipitate with ammonium sulphate, their tyrosinase is very weak; the large feeding, wandering, fixed ones, up to after the hanging-up, give a strong precipitation with ammonium sulphate, their tyrosinase is well active; in the hanging stage and in the fresh pupae the precipitation is less, and yet here the activity of the tyrosinase is a very great one. In the freshly coloured-out pupae again it agrees with the exhaustion of the tyrosinase that almost no precipitate at all forms in the blood on addition of ammonium sulphate.

Precisely from the different amount of the residue in the various stages, however, a very weighty objection could be raised against the correctness of what was said above about the reaction course of the stages. For there was always, without regard to the amount of the formed residue in the precipitation of the blood with ammonium sulphate, used the same amount of n/80 NaOH — equal to the original blood quantity — for the dissolution of the residue. It is therefore clear that the tyrosinase solutions which contained more residue in n/80 NaOH were more acid than those which contained, in the same amount of alkali, a much smaller amount of the animal, somewhat acid residue, and the difference in the behaviour of melanin formation under the influence of the stages before and after the hanging-up would therefore not be sought at all in a difference in the reaction state of the stages themselves, but would have to be traced back to the different acid degree of the tyrosinases — [arising] precisely from the different amount of the dissolved residue in the same amount and concentration of alkali. This is to be sure a very weighty circumstance, and therefore in a later experiment this experimental error was avoided by use of amounts of the solvent adequate to the amount of residue. The following three stages of *Vanessa urticae* were used: caterpillars just entered into the quiet stage, not yet fastened down; hanging caterpillars; and fresh, still-soft, partly already coloured-out pupae. From each of these stages the same amount of blood, 1.5 cm³, was obtained, for which from the quiet ones 21, from the hanging ones 25, and [from] pupae 21 were necessary. The residue precipitated by ammonium sulphate amounted for the quiet caterpillars to 0.2795 g, for the hanging ones to 0.177 g, and for the pupae to 0.053 g. These residues were dissolved in n/80 NaOH, and indeed, corresponding to their weight, that of the quiet ones in 2.8 cm³, of the hanging ones in 1.7 cm³, and of the pupae in 0.5 cm³, and from these starting tyrosinases ¹/₁ further dilutions were made with n/80 NaOH up to eightfold dilution, that is, strengths ½, ¼, ⅛. The working-up was carried out simultaneously for the quiet and the hanging ones, and for the pupae one day later; otherwise the treatment was a completely analogous one. The samples set up with the tyrosinases showed the same differences between the stages as in the earlier experiments, where no regard had been taken to the weight. Thus, for example, if we consider the series of samples with tyrosinase of strength ½, those of the quiet ones [show], as usual, a strong precipitation, those of the hanging ones are opaque black, not fallen; likewise those of the pupae too are not fallen. These differences remained preserved later as well. Although, then, the solutions of the hanging ones and the pupae were in the same concentration as those of the quiet ones, the samples set up with them nevertheless did not fall out as is the case with the quiet ones. The indicated change in reaction state in the sensitive stage thus receives a support through these latter experiments. The fact thus remains beyond doubt, that between the quiet and the hanging stage a change in the chemistry takes place; precisely this difference in the amount of precipitate in the precipitation of the blood with ammonium sulphate, which is to be traced back to a different amount of the blood protein, represents one of the differences, further [there is] the difference in the activity of the tyrosinase. In general, stages too which gave much residue (see Table G, *Io* quiet-fixed, *urticae*: hanging a short time) gave for the greater part no falling-out of the melanin, and thus behaved in this respect more similarly to the succeeding stages with much less blood precipitate than to the preceding stages with strong precipitation by ammonium sulphate. Further, in *Pieris* the feeding pre-pupation-ripe and the wandering red-defecating caterpillars had behaved alike with respect to the strength of the precipitation on addition of ammonium sulphate, and yet between the two stages there is a great difference in melanin formation, in that with the samples of the former it did not fall out, while with the latter it fell out at once with strong melanin formation (cf. VI, 1921, Table A, Ord.-Nos. 12 and 16).

### Experimental technique.

In order to be able to follow more exactly the reaction course in the transformation from caterpillar to pupa, other methods had to be sought, which would, firstly, in general confirm the conclusions drawn with the method described in the preceding section regarding an acid state before the hanging-up [and] a decrease of the acid degree at the time of hanging-up, [and] secondly would permit further differentiations with respect to the reaction state, namely the establishment of whether, in the course of the stages from wandering up to fixation, a further acidification still takes place, whether the reversal into the less acid state at the hanging-up is a sudden or a gradual one, [and] how great the difference of the reaction state is; further, whether at pupation a further decrease of the acid degree, or a remaining-stationary, or again an increase takes place. All these questions seem very important for the understanding of the influence of colour on the pupal colour, and finally it would also be interesting to be able to establish differences in the reaction state under the influence of the colours.

Moreover, in order to avoid as far as possible any alteration of the condition during processing, it is necessary, as has already been mentioned, to find a method that requires a shorter manipulation and a smaller number of caterpillars.

The direct titrations of the blood of the various stages reported in the immediately following section were later abandoned again, since they too lack the desired accuracy. Prof. Pauli had the kindness to draw my attention to a work by Sörrensen (1912) and the indicator method contained therein. By this method, direct acidity determinations of the blood (determination of the hydrogen-ion concentration) could be carried out by adding a few drops from solutions of certain substances — the indicators — which have the property of showing, at a certain degree of acidity or alkalinity — hydrogen-ion concentration —, a sharp colour change. The hydrogen-ion concentration of the fluid to be investigated is determined thereby in that the tone of the colour change which this fluid has taken on upon addition of the indicator is compared with a scale of comparison solutions whose hydrogen-ion concentration is known and to which likewise the same quantity of indicator was added. Unfortunately, however, this method too could provisionally not be used for the acidity determination of the caterpillar blood, on account of its yellow-green proper colour and the rapid blackening of the same, to which is added the further circumstance that the indicator coming into consideration for the acidity degree of this blood (weakly acid: reddens blue litmus paper), namely paranitrophenol itself, has a yellow-green changeover colour.

However, I was able to use the method indicated there in a quite different way, for assessing the reaction course of the caterpillar blood during the transformation.

Earlier experiments by dripping the blood into various concentrations of caustic soda (see *Pieris*, VI 1921) had already shown that, corresponding to the reaction-sensitivity of the tyrosinase, the drops taken from these solutions do not behave the same upon addition to tyrosin, but rather show an optimum of effectiveness (according to Algulhon 1907 this lies on the weakly alkaline side of phenolphthalein-neutrality) toward the limit of effectiveness — on the alkaline side and one on the acid side —, and further an earlier precipitation of the formed melanin at acid degrees.

From the shifting of the precipitation boundary for the melanin as well as of the optimum of effectiveness, the reaction course of the stages could be ascertained.

Since, however, I was advised by Herren Prof. Fürth and Pauli against the use of caustic soda for determining the degree of acidity of egg-white-containing solutions, I used the method with other solutions, which constitute a mixture of caustic soda or hydrochloric acid with another solution — the so-called buffer solutions —, which are supposed not to have the disadvantages that made the use of caustic soda alone appear impractical.

For this, the standard or comparison solutions indicated by Sörrensen for the indicator method, whose hydrogen-ion concentration is given in a table of Sörrensen's work, were prepared according to Sörrensen's recipe and the mixing-proportions prescribed there. I first used phosphate mixtures (primary potassium phosphate + secondary sodium phosphate). But since these proved too acid for our purposes, in that they all gave precipitation of the melanin, I subsequently used only borate mixtures, consisting of an alkaline boric-acid solution (prepared by dissolving 0.2 g-Mol boric acid (12.404 g) in 100 cm³ normal caustic soda and dilution with water up to 1 litre), which were mixed with 0.1 normal caustic soda or 0.1 normal hydrochloric acid in the various proportion-quantities given in the table (cf. Sörrensen, 1912, p. 438).

I must remark that, since at first this was a matter of preliminary experiments, I did not "standardize" the solutions used by me, so that their hydrogen-ion concentration is certainly not identical with that of Sörrensen for the same mixing-proportions. I tested the solutions used by me for their reaction only by means of phenolphthalein and was able likewise to establish the changeover point from colourless to pink, which according to Sörrensen's tables is supposed to lie between pH = 8 and pH = 9, in my solutions in the same region. For all these details I refer to the appended Tables I and J.

Of these mixtures of various alkaline degree, 1 cm³ each was given into a test-tube, and thus a rising series of solutions was set up, into each of which 2 drops of blood of the caterpillar stage under investigation (mixed together from several caterpillars of the same stage) were dropped. After thorough shaking, 4 drops from each of these blood-solutions were dropped into 1 cm³ of tyrosin each — and indeed two samples were set up from each concentration —, then ignition-time, ignition-colour, effectiveness of the tyrosinase, and the precipitation or remaining-in-suspension of the melanin after a certain time, were noted at the various alkaline degrees, as is evident from the appended tables.

In this way the externally distinguished stages before, during, and after pupation were examined, both in *urticae* and in *Io*, and for each stage the degree up to which the melanin still precipitates after an analogous time was established.

#### Results of the experiment.

It has been shown that, in an equal time from the setting-up, in the caterpillar stages before hanging the melanin in the tyrosin samples still precipitates at a more alkaline degree of the blood-solution at which, with the blood of the hanging caterpillars, it remains in suspension. The tyrosinases of feeding, wandering, and hanging caterpillars, as well as of fresh pupae, were exposed to pre-irradiation and their colour-sensitivity tested. (On the experimental method, see the corresponding section in Brecher, *Pieris*, VI 1921.)

#### Course of the experiment (cf. Table K).

#### Tyrosinase of feeding caterpillars.

Of the tyrosinases set up openly in the colours, after 2 days that from yellow went first, then those from darkness and black, and last that from white; at the second taking-out after 4 days of pre-irradiation nothing went any more.

The air-free-melted-in one gave no differences at the first taking-out after 2 days; upon renewed melting-in and a further 2-day pre-irradiation, at the taking-out of drops and setting-up of samples, the samples which had been charged with tyrosinase from white, yellow, and darkness went first, those from black later. At a third taking-out after 7 days of pre-irradiation, no samples went any more.

#### Tyrosinase of wandering caterpillars.

Openly pre-irradiated: After 2 days of pre-irradiation, yellow- and darkness-tyrosinase let the samples go first, then those from black, and last those from white. After 4 days, likewise those from yellow went first, then those from darkness, last those from white and black.

The melted-in one shows no differences after 2 days; after 4 days yellow goes first, then white and darkness, last black. The same order of succession shows itself also at the third taking-out of tyrosinase drops after 7 days of pre-irradiation.

#### Tyrosinase of hanging caterpillars.

1. Experiment. Of the openly pre-irradiated ones, after 2 days of pre-irradiation those from yellow and darkness went first, then white, last black. After 4 days of pre-irradiation an inverted behaviour shows itself: the samples charged with the tyrosinases from white and from black go first, and those with tyrosinase from yellow as well as those from darkness later.

In the air-free-melted-in ones, no differences appeared after 2 days; after 4 days those from yellow first, then those from white and darkness, last those from black.

2. Experiment. The openly pre-irradiated ones showed no differences after 2 days; after 4 days, however, nothing went any more. The air-free-melted-in ones showed no differences after 2 and 4 days of pre-irradiation.

3. Experiment. The openly pre-irradiated one showed no differences after 4 days; after 10 days yellow, green, blue went first, then darkness and red, later black, last grey and white. After 12 days: yellow, green–blue–black–darkness, red, grey–white. The air-free-melted-in ones were opened for the first time only on the 11th day of pre-irradiation. Of the samples charged with them, those with the tyrosinase from red went first, then yellow and darkness, white and green, grey, blue, last black. After renewed sealing and opening on the next day, that is, on the 12th day of pre-irradiation, yellow went first, then red, white, darkness and grey, blue, green, last black. After renewed sealing and 14-day pre-irradiation in all, yellow went first, later blue and white went a trace; the tyrosinases from the other colours have become inactive. At an even later taking-out after 38 days, only that from yellow went any more. All the others had become inactive. The openly set-up tyrosinase had become inactive much earlier.

#### Tyrosinase of fresh pupae.

Of the openly set-up ones, after 2 days of pre-irradiation those from darkness, then those from black went, while those from white and yellow are inactive. After 4 days of pre-irradiation none went.

Of the air-free-melted-in ones, after 2 days of pre-irradiation those from yellow went first, then darkness and white follow, and last that from black went. After 4 days of pre-irradiation nothing more went.

#### Results of the experiment.

These experiments clearly show the colour-sensitivity of the Vanessid tyrosinase, in that already a short pre-irradiation is sufficient to bring about differences in the effectiveness of the tyrosinase depending on the kinds of light acting upon it. In general, with the pre-irradiation of tyrosinases in little tubes that were accessible to oxygen access, there showed itself already after a short pre-irradiation (2 days) the acceleration or promotion of the effectiveness by the yellow irradiation, the inhibition by the black surroundings. The result thus corresponds to what we have designated, in *Halimasch* (Przibram-Brecher, 1919) and other butterfly species like *Pieris* (Brecher 1921), as the first phase of the yellow-effect, which corresponds to the shorter pre-irradiation, as direct colour-effect. The "reversal", as it had been obtained so beautifully in *Pieris* and in the tyrosinase of the red-wandering stage, with strongest going of the black-irradiated and weakest going of the yellow-irradiated tyrosinase, which corresponds to the formation-degree of the melanin of the pupae in these colours, was obtained in the Vanessa experiments once, with the tyrosinase of the hanging caterpillars. Of the openly pre-irradiated ones, after 2 days yellow and darkness went first, black last; after 4 days of pre-irradiation the relationship reversed itself, in that white and black went first, yellow and darkness later. Furthermore, with the tyrosinase of the fresh pupae, already after 2 days of the openly pre-irradiated ones, black went before yellow. With the tyrosinase of the wandering caterpillars, unfortunately no reversal could be achieved in the Vanessa experiments, perhaps because in the Vanessa experiments precisely the critical stage, which would correspond to the red-defecating stage in *Pieris*, is absent. On account of the dark skin of the caterpillars of *Io*, the gut contents do not shine through, and at the time of these experiments I had not yet found the criterion by which I could also distinguish the *Io* caterpillars of this stage, namely to determine the colour of the gut contents through the mouth. However, I then did not continue these experiments any further, because I have pursued the influence of the colours on the tyrosinase of the various stages in another way, namely by irradiation in vitro.

In any case, however, these experiments have already been able to prove the colour-sensitivity also for the tyrosinase of *Io*, even though, owing to the imperfections of the experimental arrangement (absence of the critical stadium in the experimental series, and not sufficiently long pre-irradiation or, on the contrary, missing of the correct moment for the removal of droplets), probably very often only the direct effect of the rays — that is, acceleration by yellow, retardation by black — and only in some cases also the »reversal« corresponding to the colour-adaptation, with strongest effectiveness of the tyrosinase from black and weaker effectiveness of the tyrosinase from yellow, came to light.

As regards the airtight enclosed tyrosinases, their setting-up in the colours had at first, upon the first removal after 2 days' pre-irradiation, no differences in effectiveness as a consequence. Only after the renewed contact with oxygen and renewed enclosure did differences show themselves upon the second removal, namely the more rapid onset of the samples with the yellow-irradiated tyrosinase and the weaker onset of the samples with the tyrosinase from black, which corresponds to the direct colour effect. These differences also remained later, upon repeated removal and re-enclosure, and further pre-irradiation of the tyrosinases.

Furthermore, these experiments show, in confirmation of the experiments on *Halimasch* [honey fungus] by **Przibram** and **Dembowski** (1919), that exclusion of oxygen protects the tyrosinase and permits its longer preservation. While the openly preserved ones are already inactive after 4 days, the air-free fused-in [sealed] ones retain, in some cases (hanging ones, especially in yellow), their reactivity even after a month or somewhat longer. This preservation of the butterfly tyrosinase by exclusion of air is, however, in any case — corresponding to the more acid state of this tyrosinase — not so unlimited as in *Halimasch* (cf. **Przibram-Dembowski**), where after 3 months the fused-in tyrosinase still showed undiminished effectiveness.

Therefore, also, in contrast to the *Halimasch*-tyrosinase, which under air-exclusion shows no colour-sensitivity, with the butterfly tyrosinases precisely the air-exclusion is a method by which one can follow for a longer time the differences in tyrosinase effectiveness called forth by the colours, since the openly set-up ones are destroyed much too rapidly, whereas with the fused-in butterfly tyrosinases the enclosed oxygen already suffices to convey differences of the colour-action to the tyrosinase. For the reversal, after all, there was probably not enough oxygen present, and the stronger effectiveness of the yellow-irradiated ones was retained after longer pre-irradiation, so that after a month of pre-irradiation finally only yellow still reacted.

### 2. By irradiation of the tyrosinase in vivo.

#### *Vanessa Io.*

##### Experimental technique.

The caterpillars, brought in the red-wandering stadium into coloured boxes, were taken out at particular stages and bled for the obtaining of tyrosinase. The following stages were tested: hanging caterpillars, quite fresh still-green pupae, already coloured-out, still-soft pupae, and further-advanced pupae with hardened envelope, and indeed in the various colour conditions always simultaneously and with the caterpillars or pupae being in the same stadium.

##### Course of the experiment.

###### Hanging caterpillars. (Cf. Table L.)

In one experiment no differences appeared between the effectiveness of the tyrosinase from yellow and from black; in two experiments the tyrosinase of the caterpillars from yellow is the strongest, that from black the weakest; in two experiments that of the caterpillars from yellow is the weakest, that from black the strongest.

As regards the tyrosinase of the caterpillars from white, neutral and darkness: in one experiment, where yellow is the strongest [and] black the weakest, darkness = yellow is the strongest, white weaker. In the experiments with yellow the weakest [and] black the strongest, in one case darkness = neutral [is] between the two, and white had so little tyrosinase residue that it did not come to the setting-up of samples at all. In the second experiment darkness is likewise between yellow and black, and indeed somewhat stronger than yellow.

Finally, in one experiment in which simultaneously only caterpillars from white and darkness were processed, »white« is somewhat stronger than »darkness«.

Thus, in most experiments distinct differences appeared.

###### Pupae. (Cf. Table M.)

Of the pupae just slipping out of their caterpillar skin, still quite green [and] soft, those from yellow have a much weaker tyrosinase than those from black. But both have, in any case, a well-effective tyrosinase.

In likewise still-fresh, 1-day-old pupae, in which the colouring-out process has already taken place — which are therefore gold-green in yellow, [and] dark in black — the same difference shows itself, namely a much weaker tyrosinase in the pupae from yellow.

The tyrosinase of such pupae after the colouring-out that has taken place is exhausted, it is very little effective. Upon precipitation of the blood with ammonium sulphate there arises so weak a turbidity that it settles into a usable precipitate only after longer standing.

In already hardened, more advanced pupae, likewise that of the green pupae from yellow is weaker than that of the dark ones from black. In this stadium, again, an increase in the tyrosinase effectiveness, that is, a recovery, is to be ascertained.

Also, the differences between the types which come to expression in the degree of colouring of the tyrosine samples upon addition of tyrosinase are, in this stadium, no longer lasting; the differences in the colour-intensity of the samples cancel themselves out after longer standing.

##### Experimental results.

In agreement with the melanin formation of the gold-green pupae without black marking in yellow and of the dark pupae with much black marking in black, there is also to be noted a difference in the tyrosinase effectiveness, in that the pupae arising in black possess a more effective tyrosinase.

As proof that the differences in the colouration (degree of development of the melanin) of the pupae under the influence of the various surrounding colours actually rest upon the influencing of the effectiveness of the tyrosinase by light of various wavelength, and not upon other causes, there already shows itself — as was to be expected — in the still quite fresh, still-green pupae in both surroundings, yellow as well as black, which therefore externally do not yet at all let their origin from yellow or black be recognized, the same difference in tyrosinase effectiveness, that is, in accordance with their provenance a very weak tyrosinase in the pupae from yellow and a much more effective one in the pupae from black.

This difference in tyrosinase effectiveness under the influence of the various surrounding colours we encounter even in still earlier stages, in the hanging ones. Not always, however, is the same difference as in the pupae to be encountered in the hanging caterpillars, that is, a more weakly effective tyrosinase in the hanging ones from yellow, a more strongly effective tyrosinase in the hanging ones from black. In two experiments precisely the opposite, namely a more strongly effective tyrosinase in the hanging ones from yellow, has been found, and in one experiment, in which the caterpillars were processed quite shortly after their hanging-up, no differences at all came to light. Obviously the various outcome of the experiments is connected with the fact that various, externally indistinguishable stages during the hanging came to processing in the various experimental series. That emerges clearly from the experiment in which the caterpillars came to processing a short time after the observed hanging-up, and which let no differences in effectiveness be recognized. It is possible that in this case the differences of the colour-influence in the tyrosinase effectiveness had not yet come to expression.

For the hanging caterpillars used in the other experiments, the point in time of the hanging-up — and thus the stage in which they were at the working-up — is not known, since in the evening they were still wandering, but in the early morning [they were] already hung up. There would, however, result a wonderfully beautiful parallel to the behaviour of the tyrosinase upon irradiation in vitro (see the corresponding works), if it were to emerge from further experiments, with exact knowledge of the stage in which the hanging ones find themselves, that the caterpillars with more strongly effective tyrosinase in yellow and more weakly effective [tyrosinase] from black correspond to an earlier stage of the hanging than those which, similar to the fresh pupae, show the weaker effectiveness of the tyrosinase from yellow. For upon irradiation in vitro it had indeed been shown that the yellow rays at first act promotingly upon the tyrosinase effectiveness, while the black, ultraviolet ones at first inhibit; upon longer mode of action of the colours this relationship reverses itself (**Przibram** and **Brecher** 1919, *Pieris*, **Brecher** 1921). In the pupal colouration, however, always this second effect of the rays, corresponding to the longer duration of action, comes to expression. Perhaps a stage can be found in the caterpillar in which precisely the 1st phase of the influence comes to expression, in the strongly effective tyrosinase from yellow.

With the establishment of the colour-sensitivity of the tyrosinase in *Vanessa* too — with the establishment of the reaction-course of the caterpillars up to pupation on the one hand, in so far as the methods used here guarantee an accuracy, and with the assumption known from the literature (see **Przibram**, Theory 1919), that the action of rays of various wavelength is that of a definitely directed influence upon the reaction state, whereby to the yellow rays an acidifying, to the ultraviolet ones an alkalescing action is ascribed (see also *Pieris* VI, 1921), on the other hand — there results the following scheme for the influence of the colours upon the pupal colouration of *Vanessa*:

| Caterpillars up to fixation (sensitive stadium): acid. | Upon hanging-up (towards the end of the sensitive stadium): | Upon pupation: increase of the acid degree, that is, direction toward acidification. |
|---|---|---|
| **Effect of yellow rays** (yellow and green surroundings): acidifying »–» | Reversal into less acid, that is, direction toward alkalescence + Acidification = Neutralization (optimum of effectiveness) — Phase: I. | + Acidification = Exhaustion of the tyrosinase (pupae without melanin) — Phase: II. |
| **ultraviolet rays** (black surroundings): alkalescing »–» | Alkalization + Alkalescence = Inhibition of the tyrosinase effectiveness — Phase: I. | Acidification + Alkalization = Neutralization — optimum of the tyrosinase effectiveness (dark pupae) — Phase: II. |

The process would accordingly thus be that the yellow surroundings acting upon the caterpillars located in the sensitive stadium have as their consequence first an increase of the effectiveness of the tyrosinase, the black surroundings an inhibition of the tyrosinase effectiveness. This phase does not come to expression in the pupal colour, and then passes over into the second phase, in which yellow has very greatly weakened the tyrosinase and black has increased its effectiveness. This condition, which exists precisely at pupation, has as its consequence the colour-adaptation of the pupae to their surroundings.

An inconsistency arises in this explanation of the colour-influence upon the pupal colouration, when we keep before our eyes the fact — already undoubtedly established by the experiments mentioned in this work as well as in the *Pieris*-work — that a role pertains to the eyes of the caterpillars for the colour-adaptation of the pupae. Since the experiments for the clarification of what kind this role of the eye may be have not yet reached a conclusion, the decision of this question must be left to a later point in time. We may, however, already on the basis of the experiments mentioned in this treatise as well as in the *Pieris*-work (IV, 1919), not close ourselves off from the fact that the influence of the colours upon the pupal colouration, that is, the arising of green pupae in yellow, of darker ones in black, etc., takes place only in the presence of the eyes, [and] that on the contrary the caterpillars deprived of their eyes give in all surroundings the same middle pupae, similar to the pupae from darkness, that is, no longer let the characteristic effect of the yellow rays upon the green-colouring of the pupae be recognized.

It seems to me important, already now — without thereby wishing to anticipate the further investigations into the role of the eye upon the pupal colouration — only in order to justify the chemical explanation of the colour-influence upon the pupal colouration given above as well as in the *Pieris*-work, which rests upon so many observational facts, and to bring it into agreement with the role of the eye, to put forward some considerations about this, which for the time being can have only the value of a working hypothesis.

To this purpose I should like to set the following facts against one another:

The pupation-ripe caterpillars are influenced in the characteristic manner by the surrounding colours with the participation of the eyes.

This influence consists, as we have seen, in an influence of the colours upon the effectiveness of the tyrosinase.

The pupation-ripe caterpillars without eyes let no influence of the colours upon their pupal colouration be recognized; there arise everywhere similar pupae as in darkness.

We thus have, upon irradiation of living caterpillars in various colours, a colour-sensitivity of the tyrosinase only with the participation of the eyes. Without eyes, on the other hand, no colour-sensitivity of the tyrosinase shows itself in the living animal.

Against this, we see upon pre-irradiation of tyrosinase in the test tube an analogous influencing of the effectiveness of the tyrosinase under the influence of various colours, as in the caterpillars with eyes — but the solution has no eyes! And that in the living animal it can nevertheless be a matter of the same process of colour-influence upon the tyrosinase effectiveness as in the test tube, and as of other processes regulated by the eye in the colouring-out process itself, is shown to us by the investigations described in this section, which have yielded a different effectiveness of the tyrosinase already in the hanging stage and in the still-green pupae of the animals taken from black and yellow conditions.

We have, however, upon irradiation in vitro too, an analogous case of the colour-insensitivity of the tyrosinase, as in the caterpillars without eyes, namely when the tyrosinases were set up in the colours completely sealed off from access of oxygen (and the enclosed oxygen no longer sufficed to call forth differences) (see **Przibram** and **Dembowski** 1919, experiments with *Halimasch*-tyrosinase). **Przibram** derived from this the explanation that, in the action of the colours upon the tyrosinase effectiveness — which has a parallel in the photobromination process of toluene (**Brunner** in **Przibram-Dembowski** 1919) — it is a matter of a different mediation of oxygen atoms by the various colours.

On the other hand, according to the investigations of Moleschott and **Fubini** (1879), the various colours have an influence upon the gas-exchange in the respiration of the animals. Blinded animals show here no differences in the various colours.

It would therefore be conceivable that a role in respiration pertains to the eyes, and [that] the various colours determine the gas-exchange by means of the eyes, and that, in the influence of the colours upon the tyrosinase — which takes place by means of the mediation of oxygen — the eyes bring about this different mediation of oxygen to the tyrosinase in the various colours. The absence of the eyes would cancel the difference of the gas-exchange in the various colours, and would have the same consequence as the air-free fusing-shut of the tyrosinase in vitro, that is, a cancellation of the colour-sensitivity of the tyrosinase.

This role of the eye, which I permit myself to introduce only as an auxiliary hypothesis into the scheme about the chemism of the colour-adaptation, is in any case accessible to experiment, since the gas-exchange in the various colours could be measured in seeing as well as in blinded caterpillars.

## VII. On the question of the colour-adaptation of the *Vanessa* pupae to their surroundings.

At the conclusion I should still like, with a few words, to touch upon the question of the colour-adaptation of *Vanessa* to its surroundings.

We can in general say that the influencing of the pupal colour through the surrounding colour leads to a certain agreement of the colouration with the natural surroundings, which one might also designate — in another sense than the selectionist one — as colour-adaptation. There arise, namely, on black surfaces, in consequence of an increase of the melanin through the ultraviolet rays reflected by black, blackish pupae; on white surfaces, in consequence of inhibition of the melanin and promotion of the white through the infrared rays reflected by white, whitish pupae; on grey surfaces, through the middle development of the melanin brought about by uniform reflection of all rays, pupae of a more grey-brown, middle colouration. Now, as regards the fourth type, that of the gold-pupae, these arise on the green leaves of the food-plant, where the reflection of the yellow rays prevents the development of the pigment in the envelope and lets the gold-lustre come to appearance. Now, the gold-lustre would indeed be something to be regarded as conspicuous for the vertebrate eye, and one can here speak less of an agreement between this type and the natural undersurface causing it, the green leaves. In fact, however, we see that in this type too, in *Io*, the green of the lower tissues shines completely through; they are green gold-shining pupae which arise on the leaves, [and] therefore do indeed show an agreement with the colour of the under-surface, just as in *Pieris* and the other types in *Vanessa*. And the gold-pupae of *urticae* too, which indeed have no pronounced green, are with their greenish reflections nonetheless better adapted to the green leaves than one of the other three types would be. On gold-shining surfaces, as we have seen in the experiments, the adaptation of the gold-pupae to the substrate is a striking one.

On gold-shining surfaces, as we have seen it in the experiments, the adaptation of the gold pupae to the substrate is striking.

On the part of the adherents of the selection theory there is ascribed, on the one hand (Poulton 1890), to the gold pupa the significance of a protective colour. Poulton has for this constructed the following hypothesis: He assumes for the Vanessas an earlier home with a dry climate and sparse vegetation in a region of glimmer-rich rocks. In these glistening surroundings the capacity to produce gold pupae would have arisen in the Vanessas by selectionistic means. When later the vegetation became richer, and the caterpillars began to pupate on the plant, then a secondary adaptation to the changed conditions would have taken place, and indeed with the different Vanessa species in entirely different ways: with *Io* through the acquisition of the green form; with *urticae* and *cardui*, which have no green and whose gold pupae would therefore stand out from the leaf-substrate, by the fact that almost all wander away from the plant near the pupation-readiness (the gold pupae encountered on the plant are ones stung by ichneumon wasps); with *atalanta* through the acquisition of a remarkable instinct: the caterpillar conceals itself for pupation either between two leaves which it spins together, or it bites somewhat through the stem of the leaf on which it is hung up, so that the leaf dries out and through its appearance now becomes similar to the pupa that has formed upon it.

The question whether the pupae agreeing with their surroundings in colouration actually enjoy a protection in pursuit by enemies—which is indeed the presupposition for the selectionistic origin of the adaptation—was examined by Poulton and Saunders (1898), in that pupae were exposed in the open on substrates of various colours. Those pupae disappeared which did not agree in their colouration with the colour of the substrate.

An opposed view, on the part of other selection-theorists, would have the gold-lustre of pupae regarded as a warning-colour, which is supposed to indicate bad taste (Fritz Müller, cited after Poulton). Bateson (1892) too is of the opinion that one might regard the gold pupa of *urticae* rather as a warning-colour than as a protective colour, and herein turns against Poulton's view. Poulton, in reply to this, carried out experiments in order to see whether the gold pupae would be spurned as food by animals. He states that the gold pupae were eaten with great relish by the others, by marmosets.

Piepers (1903, S. 188) holds that the origin of the metallic-shining pupae could stand in causal connection with the very strong reflection, in the tropics, of the strong sunlight from the leaf-surface, in the temperate zones with the mirroring of dew or raindrops upon the leaves shone upon by the sun. But for the origin of the adaptation he assumes in general a higher nervous activity, a conscious imitation.

From the present investigations it could be shown how the light of various wavelength stands in connection with the resulting pupal colouration. It is the result of the external light-action and of a whole chain of factors in the caterpillar, which together constitute the sensibility to light: a colour- and reaction-sensitive tyrosinase, a definite change of the reaction-state in the transformation from caterpillar to pupa, and further a definite function of the eye. The action of the light is a chemical one and rests upon changes of the reaction-state. Of the factors composing the sensibility of the caterpillar, the first, the colour- and reaction-sensitive tyrosinase, is widely distributed in the animal and plant kingdom. Changes in the reaction-state may rest upon the appearance of nucleic acid in such stages as are distinguished by simultaneous cell-divisions. There must therefore still be added a factor, a special sensitisation, which brings about the light-variability of the pupae and their adaptation in the same sense as the surrounding colour. It must be connected with the eye and bring about a quite definite course of the reaction-state; it is still unclear. As to the phylogenetic origin of the sensibility in the light-variable pupae, perhaps exact investigations and experiments on species which normally show no light-variability, and a closer penetration into the question of heredity, could give information.

As concerns the significance of the colour-adaptation of the pupa, I have, in the work on *Pieris* (VI, 1921), described it as a protection against light [Lichtschutz]. This assumption could indeed also be tested as regards its validity.

As I have subsequently learned, there is in algae (Gaidukow 1903) such a "chromatic adaptation", but in the positive sense: the acting colour calls forth in the algae the complementary colour, before-

To this phenomenon, known in plants, my conception of the significance of the pupal adaptation as a protection against light would form a very plausible counterpart and support this surmise. With the pupae the acting colour indeed brings forth the same colouration, whereby these rays are again reflected from the pupal surface. This would therefore represent a negative chromatic adaptation.

## VIII. Summary

The pupae of many Vanessas show, like those of *Pieris*, a different colouration according to the surroundings on which they pupate. One can divide them into four main colour-types: dark, middle, light and gold pupae, which are characterised by the different development of the following colour-elements: of a black (melanin) and green pigment, of a rose to white opacity, and of a gold-lustre of the shell. The types correspond, in the development of the pigment, to the four main colour-types of *Pieris brassicae*, whereby the gold-type, which in *Io* has a green ground-tone, corresponds to the green *Pieris*-type, from which it is distinguished only by the gold-lustre.

Experimentally, the darkest pupae of *Vanessa Io, V. urticae, Pyrameis cardui, P. atalanta* arise on black, the lightest on white, the gold pupae on yellow-reflecting, the middle ones on neutral-reflecting surfaces and in darkness.

Metallic-shining surfaces too act upon the pupal colouration only through the colour-qualities reflected by them (not through the polarisation-state of the light); only the yellow-reflecting ones among them (gold, gold-green) therefore let the gold pupae arise.

The action of black surfaces rests, as in *Pieris brassicae*, upon the ultraviolet rays reflected by black, that of white surfaces upon the ultrared rays.

Not only the light reflected from coloured surfaces, but also light let through by coloured filters, has the same specific efficacy upon the pupal colouration. However, on red surfaces dark pupae arise, under red filters the gold pupae. This difference finds its explanation in the absorption of the ultraviolet rays by the filters used. A similar difference concerns the blue-green.

The pupal colourations of the Vanessas can therefore, analogously to those of the Pierids, be traced back to specific actions of the light-rays: ultrared inhibits the development of the black pigment and promotes the white; red (?), orange, yellow, yellow-green prevent the formation of the black pigment and of the white opacity, and thereby let the gold-lustre come forth; blue-green (?), blue, violet, ultraviolet promote the black pigment.

For the action of the colours upon the pupal colouration the eye is necessary. Removal of the eyes abolishes the colour-adaptation entirely; overpainting of the eyes with black colour does indeed permit it, yet the specific ray-action is extinguished already at an intensity of the light which, with non-overpainted eyes, brings forth adaptation.

When tyrosinase was obtained from the successive stages of caterpillars and pupae kept under neutral light-conditions, dropped into tyrosin, and the reaction-state tested according to the greater or lesser precipitability of the melanin, then there showed itself an increase of the acid-grade upon reaching the pupation-ready stage, thereupon a decrease of the acid-grade in the hung-up caterpillars, that is, at the end of the colour-sensitive stage. Other methods too gave agreeing results: when haemolymph was dropped into solutions of various alkali-grade and afterwards tyrosin was charged with drops of these solutions, then the precipitability of the melanin decreased with rising alkali-grade of the solutions. The grade at which the melanin still precipitated was, in the stages before the hanging-up, of higher alkalinity than in the hanging caterpillars. The titrations, difficult to carry out free of objection, likewise point in the same direction.

The testing of the tyrosinase from hanging caterpillars or pupae out of a yellow surrounding showed, corresponding to the definitive pupal colouration, a weaker efficacy than that out of a black surrounding.

When tyrosinase was enclosed in little tubes and exposed to various colours, then the tyrosinase kept in yellow blackened tyrosin more strongly than that kept in black. These differences of efficacy no longer change after different lengths of pre-irradiation time, whereas with open tyrosinase the reversal described earlier in *Pieris* takes place.

The state of the tyrosinase therefore remains permanently preserved under exclusion of air, which may form an analogy to the extinction of the colour-adaptation of living caterpillars after removal of the eyes; for old experiments by Moleschott and Fubini have already had as their subject a dependence of the oxygen-uptake upon light in seeing animals.

If caterpillars of *Vanessa Io* are given the choice between differently-coloured substrate and are, after their decision, brought into darkness for pupation, then there exists no difference whatever between the colourations of the pupae, according to the [ground]colour at which the caterpillars had come to rest. The colour-adaptation in Vanessa pupae therefore does not rest upon caterpillar-races whose instinct, in agreement with the pupal colour proper to them, would compel them to seek out a surrounding coloured similarly to this.
