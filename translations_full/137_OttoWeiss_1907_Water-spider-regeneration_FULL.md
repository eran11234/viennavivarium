# Regeneration and Autotomy in the Water Spider
## (Argyroneta aquatica Cl.)

By

**Otto Weiß.**

(From the Biologische Versuchsanstalt in Vienna.)

With 2 Figures in the Text.

Received on 23 February 1907.

*Archiv für Entwicklungsmechanik der Organismen*, vol. 23 (1907).

Among the so-called exceptions, to which the capacities for regeneration and autotomy are supposed to be lacking, the water spider (*Argyroneta aquatica* Cl.) was also counted by Friedrich¹). I have now, prompted by Privatdozent Dr. H. Przibram, undertaken to investigate the water spider with respect to its regenerative capacity, and I have indeed succeeded in demonstrating in it both autotomy and regeneration.

Experiments, which were carried out in the months of April to September 1906, were conducted in such a way that, in animals which were caught in water ditches near St. Andrä-Wördern close to Vienna and then kept separated in preserving jars²) [Einsiedegläser] and fed with *Corethra* larvae and flies, I cut off in each case one hind leg at the place corresponding to the preformation site for autotomy, at the trochanter-coxa joint.

Since the animals had already undergone several moults, my experiments were at first unsuccessful. But I then succeeded in bringing several pairs to mate, and in obtaining eggs. After 21–23 days the young hatched, which I immediately separated and used for experiments. At first the animals were placed, after the operation, in the kind of water in which they ordinarily live. But here the animals always perished.

> ¹) Paul Friedrich, Regeneration der Beine und Autotomie bei Spinnen [Regeneration of the legs and autotomy in spiders]. Archiv f. Entw.-Mech. Vol. XX. pp. 469–506. 1906.

> ²) The separation is necessary, since the animals are large predators and attack one another.

From the wound site, which was heavily mildewed [verpilzt], an infection had set in. I now applied the greatest caution in the operation, in that I performed it only with previously well-annealed [durchgeglüht] instruments, and brought the operated animals into quite clean water of the Wiener Hochquellenwasserleitung [Vienna High-Spring Water Pipeline], which draws the water—which contains no harmful germs whatsoever—from a height of 2000 m. Here, to be sure, the animals did not perish, but neither did they regenerate.

Only when the animals were placed, immediately after the operation, into preserving jars whose bottom was covered with a piece of filter paper slightly moistened with a little water did regeneration set in, in some specimens (Fig. 1). In animals which, kept out of the water, already exhibited regeneration buds, the regenerative capacity ceased when they were brought into water. In operated animals which had been kept in water, regeneration buds appeared, when they were brought into dry preserving jars, between the 2nd and 3rd moult (Fig. 2).

Regeneration set in approximately 4 weeks after the operation, which interval corresponds to the time that lies between two moults. Animals which had more than two moults behind them no longer regenerated. To be sure, the water spider, in contrast to the other spiders, in which 8–10 moults occur, moults only about four times, which makes the fact of the rapid extinction of the regenerative power more understandable.

The regenerated leg showed a normal segmentation and differs from the normal legs only through its small size (Fig. 1).

Autotomy I was able to establish in several water spiders. I injured an extremity of an animal with a needle and brought it into a vessel with water. After some time the animal crept up the wall of the vessel until it was a little above the surface of the water. Here it remained almost motionless. Only after the lapse of some hours did the autotomy take place.

Friedrich wishes to interpret his results in the sense of the Weismannian theory, in that he attempts to demonstrate that regeneration and autotomy in the land spiders are adaptational phenomena [Anpassungserscheinungen], and that such an adaptation would be purposeless in the water spider, since the latter builds its dwelling-web very hidden among submerged water plants, and therefore is not exposed to the loss of limbs. But if it is attacked, then it is swallowed whole [in toto] by its pursuers—as such he designates fishes. As regards this latter statement, I was likewise able to establish its incorrectness. The fishes (*Leuciscus phoxinus* L.), to which I presented water spiders, always disdained them. On the other hand, I repeatedly had occasion to observe that the water spiders, in the fights which they carried out among themselves, lost extremities. Zernecke¹) also states that the water spider is attacked by backswimmers [Rückenschwimmer] and water scorpions [Wasserskorpione], which after all cannot swallow the spiders whole [in toto].

### Summary.

The water spider (*Argyroneta aquatica* Cl.) regenerated the hind legs, which were cut off at the trochanter-coxa joint, and also showed, some hours after injury of a leg, autotomy. The regeneration appears to take place only when the animals are kept out of water.

> ¹) Zernecke, Leitfaden für Aquarien- und Terrarienfreunde [Guide for friends of aquaria and terraria]. 2nd edition. 1904. p. 208.
