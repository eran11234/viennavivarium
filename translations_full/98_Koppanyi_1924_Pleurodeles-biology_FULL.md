## Experiments on the Biology of the Ribbed Newt Pleurodeles Waltli Michah.

### II.¹) Independence of the yellow lateral spots from rib-pressure.

By

**Theodor Koppányi.**

(From the Zoological Department of the Biological Experimental Institute of the Academy of Sciences and the Physiological Institute of the University of Vienna.)

Among the morphological peculiarities of the ribbed newt (*Pleurodeles Waltli* Michah.) [*Pleurodeles waltl*], it is above all the unusually large number of vertebrae that strikes us. The vertebral column of the ribbed newt consists of 56 vertebrae. And yet the whole animal is no longer than about 10–15 cm. Even if we examine the animal only superficially, we must direct our attention to the broad back of the animal, which owes its breadth to the powerfully developed pairs of ribs. The first cervical vertebra bears no ribs; the following 14 vertebrae, however, are equipped with what are probably the best-developed ribs in the realm of the Urodeles. They attach to the vertebral processes, with which they connect by means of two little heads (Capitulum costae). The ribs are about 8 mm long in the adult animal; in freshly metamorphosed animals they are still scarcely perceptible. The 16th vertebra has a difficult task: it bears the bony framework of the pelvis. The forefeet have four, the hind extremities five free toes.

The genus name *Pleurodeles* already shows us that the overall habitus of our animal is characterized by the conspicuous development of the ribs. Those places of the granular, gland-rich skin where the rib-ends abut also show a particular change. These places, namely, which correspond to the rib-endings, are marked by the presence of larger horny tubercles. Moreover — and this is what shall at first exclusively interest us — at these places the skin color deviates from the general coloration of the animal; it is, namely, in sharp contrast to the brownish-green color of the back, dirty-yellow.

*Leydig* has already stated that the pointed rib-ends can pierce the skin during strong lateral curvatures of the animal; in his opinion we are here dealing with an autotomy. This statement was often doubted, in particular by *Knauer*, the former director of the Vienna Vivarium, who expressed his conviction that the skin-parts pierced by the rib-ends are to be found only in spirit-preserved specimens, [and] that the supposed "self-mutilation" of *Leydig* is only a consequence of the alcohol-preservation. The later observers, above all *von Bedriaga*, completely verified *Leydig*'s statements that the *Pleurodeles* ribs pierce the skin quite frequently and at times end free, outside the skin. In the terrarium of the Vienna Biological Experimental Institute I often kept the ribbed newts and could quite frequently observe that the rib-tips pierce through the yellow skin-spots and protrude at a length of ¹/₄–1 mm. Incidentally, anyone can convince himself of this, that during violent curvatures of the animal [the rib-tips] emerge from the skin. This may perhaps also occur without a defensive movement; then one must have proceeded carelessly, [whereby] the sharp rib-tips pierce not only the *Pleurodeles* skin but also our own skin, and we soon feel a sharp pain. It is not impossible that, with the rib-prick, a skin-gland secretion also penetrates into our body, and that we are here dealing with a doubled protective device.

Already from the fact that the ribs can pierce relatively easily through the skin reaching up to them, it follows necessarily that they exert pressure, that the integument therefore stands under mechanical pressure. As the places where the ribs abut do indeed correspond to those that are how-greatly stretched, indeed how strongly the skin is brought into a state of pressure, it is therefore to be presumed that the deposition of the yellow pigment (probably Lipochrome) and the strong pressure stand in a causal connection. It is therefore comprehensible that the supposition arises that the yellow pigment-spots are deposited under strong pressure. Just as one likes to assume that those places of the skin of the toe-tips of the Urodeles (*Triton* and Salamander species) which are placed under the mechanical pressure of the phalanges appear colored yellow.

The justification of this assumption was tested experimentally. I started from the presupposition: if the yellow skin-spots, after the removal of the corresponding ribs, lose their color and gradually take on a color that agrees with the general greenish-brown back-color, then it would thereby be proven that the deposition of the yellow pigment is to be ascribed to the influence of the constant mechanical pressure. assume the opposite and believe that the two processes are completely independent of one another and that the yellow pigmentation develops autonomously.

In Experiment Series I, four ribs each were removed from fully grown specimens of *Pleurodeles Waltli*. The operation technique of the excision is quite simple. The animal, narcotized by means of ether, is stretched out in the dorsal position, and the skin-muscle layer is opened at the height of the second cervical vertebra (Vertebra colli) at a distance of 2–3 mm from it with a sharp scalpel. The cut is enlarged corresponding to the extent of four vertebrae. In the further course of the operation the skin-edges are grasped with forceps, and by means of a scalpel and a pair of scissors the skin and the musculature along the four ribs is carefully prepared away. Now the ribs lie free, and by means of a smaller pair of bone-forceps the ribs are nipped off at their gable-shaped little heads from the vertebral processes. The eventual rather strong bleeding that may arise is stanched, and the skin-edges are closed with a few sutures. This kind of operation has a double advantage: 1. the yellow spots themselves are spared (the opening of the skin is indeed carried out at a distance of 6–7 mm from them); 2. since the cut is placed so that the skin-wound lies almost in the middle of the back, it is not exposed to any rubbing against the vessel-wall. In this way the danger of infection is reduced and the favorable course of wound-healing is guaranteed.

The care of the operated animals is very simple. They were housed individually in round glass vessels; gravel served as substrate. In the first 3 weeks post-op, the operated animals were bathed daily in a tub filled with potassium-permanganate solution and carefully checked with regard to their postoperative molting. Supported by the spring weather and the summer that followed, the wound-healing proceeded smoothly and rapidly, and already after 8 weeks the animals presented a normal appearance; nothing more was to be seen of the wound. The appearance of the dirty-yellow spots was now observed daily. But no change occurred. Even after 5 months the yellow spots did not lose their color, although the ribs had so far shown no trace of regeneration, and there was thus no longer any question of mechanical pressure from the rib-ends. It must therefore be established that the yellow spots, even after the removal of the ribs — that is, after the cessation of the mechanical pressure — retain their earlier color-tone, and it must be concluded that the persistence of the yellow areas is not to be ascribed to the influence of the rib-pressure, but stands in no causal connection with it and occurs autonomously.

Much weightier, however, would be the objection that this experiment is therefore not conclusive, because it here concerns a fully grown animal, which is indeed completely differentiated and little changeable, and one cannot well demand that at this age of the animal a de-differentiation, respectively a color-change, should occur — and that it is nonetheless possible that in the juvenile animal the rib-pressure calls forth the yellow color.

But in order to exclude such objections from the outset, a second experiment series was set up at the same time, which in my opinion can serve in this question virtually as an *experimentum crucis*. The operation was varied in this second experiment series in such a way that now no longer the ribs, but together with them also the simultaneously prepared-away skin and musculature, were removed. Self-evidently — for that was the very purpose of this experiment — the so-called yellow spots were also removed, namely four spots each, corresponding to the removed ribs. The animals survive this operation too with good care. If now, in the operation described in Experiment Series I, [the procedure] miscarries and the prepared-away skin-muscle layer becomes necrotic and is then entirely newly formed, we achieve — without the now-described operation — the same effect: then too the yellow spots "cleanly" perish. The dying-off of the prepared-away but not

removed skin can be induced artificially, and indeed rather easily, by applying no sutures at all, or insufficient sutures, or by conducting the postoperative care in such a way that the smooth course of wound-healing is thwarted. The line of thought that prompted me to this Experiment II was the following: if it succeeds in removing the yellow pigment together with its whole surroundings and the removed skin regenerates, then one must surely be able to establish whether or not the yellow spots form again in the regenerated skin. If they do not form, then the evidential force of Experiment I is indeed thereby called into question, since in this case one could well maintain that the yellow spots — despite the regeneration tendencies of their whole surroundings — do not appear as a consequence of the absence of the rib-pressure. That too would admittedly be a negative result, but it would furnish an argument against the autonomy of the pigment-formation.

The experiment, however, proceeded throughout in accordance with my expectations. The pulled-off skin of the test animal regenerated fairly quickly, and although it was now no longer irritated by ribs, yellow spots formed in it. The complete regeneration of the skin took 3 months; already at this time clear traces of the yellow color showed; the yellow coloration of the initially light-brown skin became ever more conspicuous; at the end of the 4th month yellow spots were already present. Also the large, horny tubercles, which otherwise lie in the middle of these yellow spots, I could not find.

We thus obtained in Series II a result that is positive and unobjectionable, for here a direct formation of the yellow dyestuff could indeed be established despite the absence of the rib-pressure. It thus follows from this finding: A causal nexus between yellow pigment and mechanical pressure does not at present exist; the emergence of the yellow coloration proceeds autonomously.
