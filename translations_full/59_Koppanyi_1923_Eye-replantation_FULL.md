## 1. The autophoric operative technique.

The present report, which presents only the fruits of one year's experiments in the field of the autophoric transplantation method (cf. Przibram, this work, first part) and of eye plastic surgery, makes no claim — already on account of the brevity of the time — to be regarded as a detailed special investigation. Rather, it gives a connected overview of the results so far and of the possibilities for further investigations. When in the spring of 1920 I occupied myself with the colour change of the frog amphibians (Anura), I deplanted eyes of the fire-bellied toad (*Bombinator igneus*) and, in the green toad (*Bufo viridis*), eyes into the most varied locations of the dorsal region. With Eduard Uhlenhuth's method I did not have much luck, since in the adult animal, together with the eyeball, only the upper eyelid can be removed, and the extirpation of all the surrounding skin areas is a sheer impossibility.

Nevertheless, I have not rejected Uhlenhuth's method outright, but rather established that one must produce the dorsal pocket in the neck or back region by means of a small muscle incision. From the edge of the wound in the back region, I detached the underlying skin pieces, removed them from there, and formed out of these a skin pocket (Bursa integumenti) or, respectively, a skin cylinder, into which the enucleated eye was inserted, so that it was protected from the auxiliary apparatuses. The edges of the bursa can be united into a circle (skin cylinder). In a spherical triangle around the cornea an auxiliary apparatus is formed, in which in case of need the lids can be replaced artificially. The operated animals were placed on wet filter paper, a small glass bell was put over them, and they were kept for two days in the dark. Under such circumstances the animals remained calm and the deplantate was not lost. After a course of about 48 hours the animals can now remain quietly in the terrarium, without further dangers arising.

In the replantations of the vertebrate eye, which I subsequently took up after the deplantations, I proceeded independently of the previous plastic surgery. In eye transplantations an »alloplast« is hard to conceive of, since one transplants the eye itself. The fundamental principle was therefore in the case of the autophoric operations: *to insert the eye (without injury to the same) in its normal position* in the orbit, *so that the implantate may be held without plastic aids.* In order to achieve this, however, in the several types of animals various measures had to be taken into account.

It is three basic types of the autophoric eye transplantation that I should like to mention. 1. Operation of the animals with developed lids, 2. Operation of the animals with eyelids, 3. Operation of the animals with the lid and nictitating membrane (Membrana nictitans).

To the first type belong the fishes and amphibian larvae. One places the slightly curved scissors at the lower half of the orbit, inserts the bulb here, and cuts away from this stump (approximately 1—2 mm) along with the extirpated eyeball remaining. Then one cuts off from the outside both the inner and the outer corner of the eye with the scissors, and one peels the bulb out of the orbit. After the severing of the optic nerve this is an easy, light work. Once one has freed the bulb, one must not let it lie too long in the orbit, but rather undertake the operation on the opposite side beforehand. Then one dips the eyes anew, takes them with little spoons out of the eye sockets, and sets them into the other orbit. Finally one must bring the eyes into the correct position with forceps. After this the operated animals are placed for 2 minutes on wet filter paper, then one can place them again in the water. The ectomy is almost without loss of blood feasible. In the whole ophthalmectomy one must, on account of the danger of spore-germination, always pay attention to the strictest asepsis. We do well to close the aquarium off as sterilely as possible, so that the water is protected from spore-infection. The success of the transplantation is naturally also dependent on whether we have hit upon the eye sizes matching one another.

In the tadpoles of the most varied Anura, which likewise belong to this eye type, one must, still before the beginning of the enucleation, remove or displace the skin layer protecting the eye. Such similar protective skins are also possessed, e.g., among the fishes, by the mud-loaches (*Cobitis fossilis*).

The second type of the replanted eyes comprises the mammals, which in the case of the murids here possess a tiny little nictitating membrane. Before the operation (carried out on murids), one must follow the strictest asepsis prophylaxis. The two animals are stretched out onto »boards« [Brettchen], the surroundings of the eyes are washed with iodine tincture (not shaved, only the large whiskers are cut off). With the thumb and forefinger we grasp the two eyelids of the one animal, draw them strongly apart, so that the bulbus springs out of the orbit. Now one cuts the eye all around with curved scissors and lifts the eyeball out of the conjunctival sac. In the severing of the eye muscles one comes quickly to the optic nerve. There the eye must be cut off close under the optic nerve. In the cutting through of the optic nerve a few drops of blood run down, which are to be washed off with iron-chloride cotton wool. The second animal is subjected to the same treatment, then one dips the eyes over. If the eye is now freely movable and is seen as quickly as possible — when the new eye is already inside the orbit, one must place it into the correct position, the lids again together. The last traces of blood are also to be carefully removed; otherwise the lids stick together and the replantate goes to ruin. In the case of old animals one can pierce the lids with a sterile pin (but so that the lids do not come together), which one removes 2—3 hours later; then [the animals are placed] again in the warmth source. After 4—5 days I placed the experimental animals again into the cages.

The first type of the visual apparatus is shown, among my experimental animals, by the transformed amphibians. Considered from the technical standpoint, the enucleation, respectively the replantation, of the Urodele or Anuran eyes differs only through the very deviating sizes of the bulbi from one another. The experimental technique is most difficult in these groups. The animals, especially the Anura, have namely the property, on the touching of the head, of closing the eyes so tightly that one can with difficulty get the lids apart again. One must, with a pressure exerted on the outer corner of the lower eyelid, bring the eye artificially to opening, then quickly insert the scissors into the inner corner of the lower lid (pressing aside the lid itself and the nictitating membrane, and above both of these) and reach the optic nerve with a sure cut. The eye we can remove with and without the upper eyelid; the former has, especially in the heteroplastic unions, a great demonstrative value. In the introduction of the bulbus into the eye socket we proceed further in such a way that, grasping the lower lid and the nictitating membrane with forceps, we raise the same and with them cover the just-inserted eye. This is a very important moment. By it we prevent the difficulties which would otherwise arise in the wound-healing and blood-supply. When we have removed the eyeball with the upper lid, then we must take care that the latter matches the surrounding skin areas. The healing-in of the transplanted upper lid occurs also in heteroplastic transplantations. In the dysplastic transplantations, for which I have chosen the two components from the representatives of the first and third type, one must transplant the bulbus of the amphibian eye onto fishes without lids; in the reverse case the upper eyelid of the amphibian should, if possible, not be removed — it is drawn over the fish eye in order to fasten the replantate.

The autophoric operative technique is not restricted merely to eye transplantations and not merely to vertebrates. The principle is always the same: the stump is so constructed that it holds fast the fitting replantate without aids. I have also occupied myself with the exchange of the eye and the ear in the Anura and reptiles. Above all I must mention the interesting fact that the eye and ear cavity in one and the same animal species is as a rule of equal form. In the lizards they are oval, in the frogs circular. The exposure of the ears is to be undertaken proceeding from the maxillary side, whereby one must always hold to the macroscopically visible ear boundaries.

## 2. Selection of the material, observations before the experiments.

Ed. *Uhlenhuth*, formerly assistant of Prof. Dr. *Przibram*, now Professor at the *Rockefeller* Institute, used for his fine eye-deplantation experiments, as experimental objects, the larvae of our native Urodeles (Triton and Salamander). These were very welcome to him, both in technical and in scientific respect. The eyes heal in quickly, and he was able to show that the nerves can grow out of the perception organ into the spinal ganglia, when they had just found an outlet to these. This fact later brought us to the idea of transposing my deplantation experiments on the same objects into the field of eye replantations. In theoretically scientific respect the Urodele larvae were such suitable experimental objects because they permitted a deeper insight into the metamorphosis problems. He was able to establish that the deplanted salamander-larva eyes, when their age does not differ much from that of the stock [host], undergo the metamorphosis simultaneously (synchronously) with the body's own functioning eyes of the animal. The special metamorphosis of the fire-salamander eye consists in this, that the yellow, larval iris ring of the larva disappears, i.e., it is substituted by the immigration of black pigment (iris pigmentation). These problems shall occupy us still later.

Proceeding from the thought that the regenerative capacity of the fish eye is perhaps still greater than that of the amphibian eye, I have also drawn the fishes into the series of my experimental objects. Since I intended to subject the eye not only as a sense organ but also as a factor playing a role in the colour change to a closer investigation, I would have used the minnows (*Phoxinus laevis*), which we could not obtain at that time (difficulties stemming from material procurement often disturbed me in the work. Yet now more favourable conditions seem already to prevail, which will probably make it possible to procure these animals — the lack of which crippled my activity in certain directions [minnows, axolotls, olms] — larger amounts of the reptiles and mammals.) But also the bleak (*Alburnus lucidus* Heck) and the crucian carps (*Carassius vulgaris* Nilss) have proved very usable experimental objects. We shall still have occasion to set forth that these animals are not too unsuitable for colour-change experiments. Besides, technical reasons too speak in favour of them. In my practice I have by preference undertaken heteroplastic unions between the two fish species, because the eyes of these animals deviate strongly from one another, and therefore the mutual grafting-on is very demonstrative. Especially striking are the differently coloured iris rings ›*Alburnus* — silver, *Carassius* — gold‹, and in addition the fact that the pupil (»seeing-hole«) in the bleak does not stand in the middle of the eye. For dysplastic combinations the mud-loach (*Cobitis fossilis* L.) was very usable. They have a narrow yellow iris ring, similar to the fire-salamander larvae.

When we now pass over to another animal group, to the tail-amphibians (*Urodela*), then we know already that they furnished Uhlenhuth very good experimental objects. I have chosen larvae and adult animals for my transplantation experiments, and indeed almost all the Lower-Austrian Urodeles. The fire-salamander (*Salamandra maculosa* Laur. forma typica Boul.), the water-newt (*Triton cristatus* Laur.), the pond-newt (*Triton vulgaris*) served in the homoio-, hetero- and dysplastic unions. The eye types, in particular the iris colours, are very different (larva of the fire-salamander iris ring narrow and orange, *Triton cristatus* — broader, orange, that of the *Triton vulgaris* — broad lemon-yellow), and the animals have also a very great regenerative capacity; nevertheless some newts showed at certain seasons (e.g. *Triton vulgaris* in high summer) no sufficient resistance capacity.

From the group of the frog amphibians (Anura) I have likewise experimented on tadpoles and on adult animals. The representatives of the Ranidae were the water-frog (*Rana esculenta* L.) and the grass-frog (*Rana temporaria* Gaupp not Linné). Although in these the eyes are already somewhat similar, one can nevertheless, in the heteroplastic unions, distinguish them from the original eye of the host animal by the co-transplanted upper eyelid. The Bufonidae too were represented in my experiments by two members of the family: the common toad (*Bufo vulgaris* Laur.) and the green toad (*Bufo viridis* Laur.). Of the (partly neotenic) tadpoles of the Pelobatidae, especially the spadefoot-toad (*Pelobates fuscus* Wagl.), I received some examples, which I used for heteroplastic transplantations.

In a larger quantity I was able to collect fire-bellied toads (Unken), my most excellent experimental animals. The eyes of the fire-bellied toad (*Bombinator igneus* Rösel) were the most used components for homoio-, hetero- and dysplastic transfers. Not only through the eye colour but also through the characteristic triangular pupil was this animal a very instructive object for the hetero- and dysplastic unions.

It is already an old observation that the cold-blooded animals react to operative interventions with moulting. On account of the moulting of these animals I have, with respect to the mortality after the operation, made no bad experiences; on the contrary, I was able to observe that the salamander larvae too show this specific postoperative moulting. I have then also noticed moultings in normal larvae, which are thin and delicate — no wonder that they seem hitherto still not to have been described.

After I, on account of lack of material and time, had only little opportunity to carry out experiments on reptiles and birds, the mammals (*Mammalia*) came in turn. Although I knew that the murids, namely the brown rat (*Epinys norvegicus* Erxl.) and the house-mouse (*Mus musculus* L.), are no good »seeing-animals«, I was nevertheless compelled, on account of easy procurement possibilities, to use these for the eye transplantations.

Yes, the murids are even pronounced »scent-animals« [»Witterungstiere«]; they seek out the food and the sexes by means of their tactile and olfactory sensations. After longer observation, however, »vision tests« can be made out.

In several respects, however, murids are recommendable. They are easy to observe, to rear, and in captivity to bring to reproduction. We have even in the experimental institute in December 1920 and January 1921, that is, in those months which mostly bring with themselves a pause of the reproduction of the murids, obtained several litters. The rats and mice were also, on account of the occurrence of albinotic and pigmented eyes, very demonstrative for the alleloplastic transplantations. In the exchange of such eyes only this perhaps makes difficulty, that the albinotic eyes, at equal animal size, are smaller than the pigmented ones. At least this was the case with my material. There must therefore, for the alleloplastic eye transplantations, smaller *wild-grey* or *spotted* and larger *albinotic* animals be taken. (From a written communication of *Donaldson* to *Przibram* I gather that in America the albinotic eyes possess a greater weight than the pigmented ones.) In the case of the rats, as is to be seen, experiments were made with all three colour races, with wild-grey, spotted and with albinotic animals. The extension of the experiments to other mammals shall follow in the nearest future¹.

## 3. Eye transplantations in Anamnia.

Before I report somewhat more in detail on my eye transplantations, I should like only to send ahead that the following experimental descriptions were composed not according to the temporal sequence of the experiments, but rather according to a systematic grouping of the selected material.

The fish eye is a very tender, fine organ, with which one must deal with the greatest care. When one places the fish, after the grafting-on of a foreign eye, again in the water, one sees that the animal swims about restlessly, often strikes the snout against the wall of the container (static disturbances are only exceptionally observed in operated animals), without its losing the eye. What force can here come into consideration that holds the replantate fast? Air- or water-pressure plays here certainly a role.

Eyelid closure, the holding-fast of the implant by clotted blood (in the operations there is usually no bleeding at all) cannot come into consideration; a gluing-together of the body fluids, however, certainly can. Blinded and normal control animals were placed alongside the experimental animals for the later testing of visual acuity.

When we enucleate the eye, it is self-evident that all the eye muscles, all the motor nerves (oculomotorius, trochlearis, abducens) and the sensory nerves (opticus, ramus ophthalmicus of the trigeminus) must be severed. Transplantations in crucian carp and bleak are less interesting than the heteroplastic transplantation of these fish eyes, when one then inserts the new eye into the orbit. I cannot give space here to a recapitulation of the comparative morphology of these fish eyes, but it is important to repeat that the *Carassius* eyes [crucian carp] are more compact and smaller than the *Alburnus* bulbi [bleak] of the same size; the pupil is in the middle of the eye, the eye color (apart from the pupil) is not uniform, little silver, the dominant tone is gold. By contrast the *Alburnus* eye is flatter, the pupil displaced toward the oval side of the eye, the color is uniformly silver-gray.

If we exchange these eyes, we observe that the transplanted eyes do not change. Lens opacities may indeed occur here and there, but these later regress. The formation of a stable cataract was rarely observed. I must recall the fact that blinded fish (among my experimental animals especially the crucian carp) darken rapidly. Here I wish merely to anticipate, as a morphological fact, that as a consequence of a homoio- or heteroplastic eye transplantation the body color, which had already become quite dark, soon gradually lightened again.

From the experimental protocols: On 31 December three crucian carp were placed in a container next to a window. A normal animal, a bilaterally blinded one, and a fish with bleak eyes. The blinded animal already became strongly dark on the second day (1 January), and attained its completely dark coloration on 5 January. The fins are very dark, their terminal portions almost black (especially the pinna caudalis). The ventral side strongly pigmented in front. The crucian carp with the bleak eyes at first became dark just like the blinded one; on 6 January it began to lighten, the fins likewise became lighter, the ventral pigmentation disappears. On 16 January the lightening is still more distinct, but the body color no longer resembles the golden-yellow crucian-carp type, it became silver-gray (the fins too).

The experiments have now been set up on a larger scale; the macroscopically visible change, the flawless healing-in, and the normal fresh appearance of the implant has been repeatedly checked and confirmed; concerning the color change nothing definitive can yet be said.

Among fishes, homoio- and heteroplastic transplantations have been carried out with success. These modes of transplantation proved themselves in all vertebrate groups. Although many successes have been achieved with the above plastic procedure on other organs, there are still always voices that dispute the possibilities of heteroplasty, even of homoioplasty. My extensive experiments show not only the applicability of homoioplasty but, as we shall learn later, of the most extensive heteroplasty (dysplasty). In the new terminology of developmental mechanics (edited by Roux) attention is drawn to the impediment to the homoioplastic procedure caused by the difference of the blood sera, and the proposal is made to equalize the "biochemical difference" of the stock by blood transfusions. To me even the most extreme heteroplastic transplantations have succeeded without blood transfusions.

The eyes of the *Triton* species are fairly different from one another. The smooth newt [*Triton vulgaris*, = *Lissotriton vulgaris*] possesses compact, resistant eyeballs. The eye color is strikingly lemon-yellow. The two yellow semicircles, however, do not form a uniform ring; they are interrupted on both sides in the middle by a black plate. The eye color of *Triton cristatus* [crested newt] stands midway between the copper-yellow and the orange tone. The yellow iris-ring already characterizes the larva of the newts and does not disappear during development. (On the eye transplantation in newts already carried out by Pardo, cf. Part I of this work, Przibram 1922.)

On 24. IX. 20 water newts were operated on homoioplastically. The transplanted eyes showed no changes, only in a few cases did cornea and lens opacity become noticeable. These unpleasant accompanying phenomena, however, regressed. The eyes became clear again and not even traces of the opacities remained. These clearing processes admittedly lasted from 1–15 November. After a few weeks the eyes became mobile in all directions. In the eyes inserted (for demonstrative purposes) a little obliquely, a muscle movement developed which from time to time steered the eyeball back into the correct position in the orbit. The lower eyelid and the nictitating membrane around the new eye are already functional by the 2nd to 3rd day, provided the operation has succeeded. In not a single case could I observe a bloody cornea, of which Uhlenhuth reports in his larvae. The transplants are holding up to the present day of writing (20. II. 21). All eye replants, insofar as their lifespan is not expressly stated in the text, are likewise living today.

I observed already at the very beginning of my experiments that not only the fish but also the amphibians become darker as a consequence of the eye-exstirpation. This darkening after blinding has been observed in *Salamandra maculosa* [fire salamander] by Fischel, Przibram, and Dembowski, and most recently the observations of Hirschler on various amphibian species confirm ours. The developed *Triton cristatus* too changed its color with the blinding: it becomes darker, blacker still, and the yellow back-stripe becomes dirty-yellow and almost completely fades out. In the normal animals the back-stripe is not uniform, it consists of individual small lines; in the blinded animals the boundaries are blurred. In the animals with homoioplastically transplanted eyes a "blind-habitus" likewise sets in at first; later the animal lightens, the back-stripe became lemon-yellow again and divided into sharp sections. On 18 November 1920 both eyes were exchanged between *Triton vulgaris* and *Triton cristatus*. The wound healing proceeds smoothly in these cases too, the eyes are not resorbed, they are preserved, the mobility of the eyes is restored, they become mobile again. At first the eyes remain in the state in which they were transplanted. Later, however, a remarkable change occurred which was especially conspicuous on the *Triton cristatus* eye transplanted into the pond newt [*Triton vulgaris*]. The conspicuous, lemon-yellow iris-ring of the pond-newt eye gradually fades away, and in its place black pigment appears in the eye. Whether this is a matter of an immigration of pigment from the host animal, or of an autonomous origin of the same on the spot, later experiments can give clarification. The color of the iris-ring too underwent a change, it became orange-yellow. The iris-ring became circular and coherent again. The substitution of the yellow pigment by black, the formation of a uniform orange-yellow iris-ring in the pond-newt eye, thus has the appearance, as in the replant (*Triton vulgaris* eye), as if it had been re-attuned in the sense of the host animal (*Triton cristatus*) (cf. this work Part VI).

In the urodeles still other heteroplastic combinations were also undertaken. The fire-salamander larvae excised from the uterus on 15 June served as experimental objects for this purpose.

On 30 September 1920 *Triton vulgaris*, partly *Triton cristatus*, eyes were transplanted onto the larvae unilaterally. The picture of the larval eyes with *Salamandra maculosa* eye was quite simple. Quite black with a narrow yellow ring. I wish to add that with the transplantation of a full-grown newt eye onto salamander larvae one must exstirpate the larval eyes together with the upper skin portions and deepen the orbit all around, so that the full-newt eye finds its place therein. The eye must of course be transplanted in such a way that that part of the eye where the optic stump lies comes into the correct position, and the possibility of the conjoining of the two nerve trunks is present. It is to be remarked here also that the iris color of the full-salamander eye is quite black.

In the transplantation of a *Triton cristatus* or *Triton vulgaris* eye onto fire-salamander larvae one notices already soon that the eye heals in and becomes mobile. In the first case, wherein I grafted the *Triton cristatus* with the upper eyelid, it showed itself later that the *Triton* skin (on the upper eyelid) free of yellow pigment recolored, and between the black coloring substances yellow pigments became deposited. When we transplant *Triton vulgaris* eyes without eyelids, then new ones form from the body, and there arises, already in the later larval stages, a peculiar kind of eye movement. On contact with the eye the animal draws the flat *Triton vulgaris* bulbus back in the direction of the optic axis.

Interesting is further the fact that even the body-foreign full-newt eyes, which possess a yellow iris-ring present throughout the whole life, did not become black-pigmented at the metamorphosis. However, experiments on this are not yet concluded, and these will be reported in later publications.

From the outset it seemed to me to be of use, both from the developmental-physiological and from the phyletic standpoint, to transplant full-animal eyes onto larvae and larval eyes onto full animals. In the transplantation of full-newt eyes onto fire-salamander larvae, molting processes in the replanted eye were at first noticeable; nevertheless the eye, after these processes, retained its complete intactness. So far as can be judged from the experiments hitherto, no molting occurs in the irreproachable eyes.

These viewpoints led me to similar transplantations in the group of anurans. Before I report something about these experiments, I would like to give a general picture of the eye types of the frog larvae. The iris color of the larvae of the mud-toad (*Pelobates fuscus*) is light gold, with a horizontal black stripe in the middle. The pupil is round. The iris color of the toad-frogs is dark gold above and below, laterally playing into black. Similar eyes are possessed also by the water frogs. The pupils are not completely circular, rather elliptical. The iris of the toadlet eye [Unke] is colored light-gold, without dark overlays. The pupil is triangular by day. On observation of the bulbus of the toads we can perceive a slight carmine-like coloration. On 12 October 1920 I transplanted frog eyes onto the tadpoles of the mud-toad. There I could make analogous observations with respect to the molting, as in the transplantation of the full-newt eyes onto the fire-salamander larvae. A complete re-attunement of the eye has hitherto not yet been observed; the applicability and the advantage of the autophorous plastic, however, show themselves especially in these eye transplantations on the neotenic tadpoles. As is known, these animals very seldom remain alive, are very sensitive, succumb to the slightest mechanical interventions. Otherwise too the mortality in these animals is very great. In the autophorous eye transplantation, which one would designate as an especially "severe" operation, not a single animal was now carried off during the operation. Also later, during and after the wound healing, the animals did not perish, whereas others — caught at the same spot and standing at approximately the same age — succumbed in a short time in the case of other, comparatively light interventions.

The most durable experimental animal, in which I undertook the autophorous eye transplantations, was the fire-bellied toad [Feuerunke]. It has already been mentioned that in this animal I first transplanted eyes into the neck and back region. The eyes grow into the pit, the artificially generated eyelids being motionless, only the eye itself is not rotatable. After the transplantation the pupil suddenly becomes narrow, and I must emphasize sharply that a later change in the size of the pupil in the replanted eyes was never observed in any case. Of a prompt pupillary reaction there can be no talk in these eyes. The replanted eyes are thus characterized by a (especially in the later stages) rigid pupil (as Uhlenhuth too has already observed).

On a fairly fully grown toadlet [Unke] the bilateral homoioplastic eye replantation was now undertaken (on 20 September 1920). In these successful homoioplastic eye-graftings one notices no essential change of the replant; only later does one perceive the likewise renewed pupil and light-color differences. In some cases opacities of the crystalline lens appear, but otherwise even in these the bulbus is completely healthy. The eyes became mobile, eyelid and nictitating membrane functioned flawlessly. As a morphological fact let it be remarked that the homoioplastically operated animals heal just as well as the normal ones, that thereby also the blinded and not freshly successfully operated toadlets are dark.

The homoioplastic eye transplantations of the anurans likewise furnished favorable results. The eyes heal in, become mobile and differentiate again well.

The heteroplastic transplantations are successfully feasible between *Ranidae* and *Unke* [toadlet], between *Ranidae* and *Bufonidae*, between *Bombinator igneus* and *Bufo viridis*. Especially flawless was the grafting of the *Bombinator igneus* eye onto the fire-toad. The eyes of the *Bombinator igneus* grew in, the eye-bulbus became mobile and the eye remained, despite the sufficient lifespan and functional capability, indistinguishable from the homoioplastic eye transplantations. The homoioplastic exchange of the left with the right eye in cold-blooded animals furnished less favorable results. About the disadvantages of the exchange of the right with the left eye in the *Triton* Pardo already reported (1906).

### 4. Replantation of the mammalian eye.

The immediate continuation of my experiments, which I have carried out on cold-blooded animals, forms the replantation of the mammalian eye. I confess that, after the numerous failed eye-transplantation experiments which have been carried out on various mammals, especially on rabbits (*Chibret*, *Königstein*), I had not much hope that these experiments would furnish analogous results to those in cold-blooded animals. It was generally asserted that the eyeballs, shelled out of the orbit, simply die off, become necrotic, and under no circumstances remain viable. It had also been doubted that the eyes can heal in and be nourished by the substrate even before resorption. The eye-transplantation experiments in mammals, which I took up following on from the cold-blooded animals, refuted these fears already at the outset.

The macroscopic appearance of the eyes of the murids, which I used as experimental objects, is fairly uniform. The iris of the gray and mottled individuals (so in the rats as in the mice) is black-pigmented; by contrast the iris in the albinotic animals is pigmentless, the whole eyeball appears red (blood color). In some albinotic mice the eyeball is rather whitish than red. The enucleated eyes shelled out of the orbit naturally lose the red blood color promptly. They are then pale white. The pupil of both eye types (black and albinotic) differs little from the surroundings; therefore the study of the pupillary reaction is essentially made difficult.

For the eye transplantations the mammals used offer favorable experimental objects. The eyes have at least the same capacity for healing-in as the lower vertebrates; moreover the wound healing can be essentially accelerated by the influence of warmth, without harm to the animals.

When we have not carried out the operation with the greatest care, an infection sets in, which expresses itself in that the bulbus shrinks and within 8 days at most perishes, accompanied by purulent processes. But when this is the case, the animal itself does not suffer from the destruction of the implant; the experimental animal lives on healthily.

Among the rats the most resistant are, as was to be expected, the wild-gray individuals (*Epimys norvegicus ferus*). Before the operation it is necessary to keep the animals in captivity for a longer time, so that they become accustomed. By more frequent narcotizing before the operation the animals can be made tamer, so that they then keep quiet after the operation. On 6 November the bulbi were exchanged homoioplastically between wild-gray rats. The animals were adult, almost old animals. On 9 November weak opacity processes set in in the replanted eye. Gradually the cornea cleared up again, without any special treatment¹). To secure the blood supply of the implant, no measures had been necessary. Precisely therein lies the advantage of our operation technique, that an extraordinarily rapid union between substrate and replant can come about.

Already at the enucleation of the eye there sometimes develops in it a subcorneal hemorrhage, which can later disappear.

The eye muscles grow on, as is to be recognized also from the fact that the normal eye movement is restored. The eyelids close completely, the glandulae lacrymales [lacrimal glands] too function flawlessly.

It should not pass unmentioned here that the replanted eyes usually, but not throughout always, lie deeper in the orbit (because they were just pressed in and held fast there at once) than the normal bulbi.

Purely homoioplastic eye transplantations have also been carried out between albinotic individuals. The pigmentless eyes are in general weaker, they are strongly degenerated, hence little suited for transplantation purposes. Nevertheless I succeeded on 30 October 1920 in the exchange of albinotic components on an older animal. The wound healing here too proceeds smoothly; the eyes become quite white, cloudy, not transparent; already on 4 November it was visible that the bulbus becomes traversed by small blood vessels. The replanted eyes showed a more or less pronounced exudate-type. As may already be seen from these communications, regeneration-processes appear in the replanted eye after the degeneration-phenomena. These macroscopic observations confirm the histological findings of *Pardo*, *Uhlenhuth* and *Ramón y Cajal*, which were established partly in transplanted eyes of lower vertebrates, partly in mammal-eyes whose optic nerves [Sehnerven] had been severed. The above-named authors observed, namely, that both in the optic nerve [Optikus] and in the retina, at first severe regressive processes [Rückbildungsprozesse] are perceptible, but later a complete restitution of the retina and a regeneration-tendency of both optic-nerve-trunks appear. When these restitution processes are complete, then (at least according to my experience) a resorption of the graft-piece is also excluded. This stage of the stimulus [Stadium des Reizes] I should like to designate as the stabilization of an implant. The stabilization-time [Stabilisierungszeit] is fairly specific. The higher we climb in the animal kingdom, the shorter becomes the "labile" condition of the implanted eyes, [and] the earlier they reach their stabilization-stage [Stabilisierungsstadium].

The homoioplastic eye transplantations between younger albino rats were repeated on 4 January 1921. The successes are the same, only the stabilization-condition sets in earlier.

In the rats, numerous alloplastic eye transplantations were also undertaken. The experiments were, however, not attended by happy success. When albinotic eyes were transferred onto wild-grey or piebald rats, a strong hyperaemia of the eye appeared as early as after 24 hours, and the implant perished within 8 days during the involution of the hyperaemia and the shrinkage of the bulbus. Some parts of the eye — so, for example, almost the entire tunica fibrosa oculi and the crystalline lens — remain preserved in the orbit for months yet. That too is a proof that it is not the deficient blood-supply, but other factors, that destroy the transplant.

Further pigmented eyes were then grafted onto albinotic rats. The dark eyes seem to hold better; an already quite well healed-in specimen I demonstrated at the session of the Vienna Biological Society (7 Dec. 1920). Unfortunately the animal lost the eyes through an accident, in that, on the way back from the demonstration, since it was shut up with several others in a small transport-cage, it was bitten.

In every respect it seems to me important that, in the rodents [Rodentier] too, heteroplastic eye-unions have succeeded, namely between brown rat [Wanderratte] and house mouse [Hausmaus]. To be sure, the host-animals and test-animals stood very close to one another; nevertheless the operation was thereby made more difficult, in that the eyes of the oldest mice are still always considerably smaller than the eyes of 14-day-old rats, which were used for the eye-transplantations. The eyes healed in flawlessly [and] remained completely intact when, in the heteroplastic eye-transplantations, in both mouse and rat, albinotic (that is, like-colored) eyes served for the exchange.

## 5. The "Dysplastic" Eye Transplantations.

The great majority of researchers have for long years stood, and even today still stand, in part, with skepsis over against heteroplastic transplantation (*Korschelt*, *Roux*, etc.). Essential successes were for a long time likewise not achieved thereby, and when one did undertake heteroplastic unions, these were carried out, with few exceptions, between individuals standing very close to one another, e.g. *Rana sylvatica* and *palustris*. In amphibians, *Harms* has applied heteroplasty with success; embryonic transplantations too succeed by the heteroplastic way (*Born*, *Harrison*, *Spemann*, *Lewis*, etc.). Nevertheless, the possibilities of the heteroplastic over-transplantations were very restricted.

In the eye-transplantations it was also soon established that components let themselves be exchanged with success not only within one genus, but between different genera, families, indeed even animal classes [Tierklassen]. The transplantations onto class-foreign individuals form the domain of dysplastics.

In the heteroplastic, but especially in the dysplastic, junctions of the eyes, it is a matter not merely of a substitution of the perception-organ; rather, there sometimes even class-foreign body parts are transplanted onto the stock, there other chemical substances, other potencies are transferred onto the host animal — thus a somatic species-transfer takes place. The specific-material and energetic properties are switched into another soma. My intentions, which led me to these dysplastic unions, were to investigate how these specific properties of the graft-scion, in the given case of the eye, behave in the new medium. Are they retuned by the medium, or do they influence the stock? Further, is it at all possible to establish junctions between such divergent forms as Cyprinidae and the toad [Unke]? Can the regenerative and reparative capacities of the stock and of the graft-piece be switched over to performances [executions]? Furthermore, do redifferentiation processes show themselves in the implant, when one transplants eyes from differentiated onto less-differentiated animals or vice versa?

In general it is said that the transplanted body-pieces are very conservative — neither are they retuned, nor do they retune the stock. *Crampton*, to be sure, attained, in his heteroplastic pupal-unions, in a few cases certain re-pigmentations [Umpigmentierungen]; in the embryonic transplantations in frog and salamander [Frosche und Molche] too, the components did not always preserve their own colorations — yet these cases were isolated and found no corresponding appreciation. The opinion has been expressed that a mutual influencing of the body-cells through somatic species-transfer is not possible, or at least was hitherto not possible (cf. *Przibram*, Experimental Zoology III. Phylogenesis including Heredity 1910).

Now let me, myself, take the experiments into view, as to how far they bear out the hypotheses of *Born*. First of all, the eyes between *Triton cristatus* and *Carassius vulgaris* were exchanged. The "phylanaplastic" unions, which designate the transplantation of a scion from less-differentiated kinds onto more-differentiated ones, yielded [the result] that no changes, at most clouding-processes [Trübungsprozesse], appear in the replanted eye. The "phylokataplastic" over-transplantations, i.e. those carried out from more-differentiated onto less-differentiated kinds of animals, on the other hand, as a rule cause molting-processes [Häutungsprozesse] in the replant, in which the eyes not seldom perish (infection?). What redifferentiation processes go on in the eye-layers, only the histological investigations will make clear; but already now I should like to assert that, in several respects, the consequent appearances of the phylanaplastic transplantations resemble those produced by transplantations carried out from amphibian larvae onto full amphibians [Vollurche] (ontanaplastic unions). Likewise, the phylokataplastic unions let themselves be analogized with the eye-transplantations which were carried out from full amphibians [Vollurchen] onto larvae (ontokataplastic transplantations). Hitherto these parallelisms have been observed only in fishes and in amphibians.

Further, salamander eyes were transplanted onto carp [Karauschen] in fairly grown individuals, and conversely, which held themselves very well for some time; reflexes were even noticeable in them. The carp were very usable objects, for further dysplastic eye-replantations as well. Thus, on November 19, 1920, the eyeballs were exchanged between *Bombinator igneus* and *Carassius vulgaris*. In these transplantations, further changes were observed in the replants. The toad-eyes [Unkenaugen] in the eye-sockets of the carp were held fast; after a few days, however, it became clear that the anterior half of the eyeball gradually flattens (perhaps in consequence of the water-pressure); in its form it became similar to a fish-eye. Whether a stabilization of the implant had taken place remains for the present unexplained, for one carp perished on December 27, 1920, [and] the other lost its bulbus in consequence of a Saprolegnia infection on January 16, 1921. — The carp-eyes in the toad's body [Unkenkörper] grew in successfully. The eyeball here too underwent transformation-processes. The surface of the bulbi became rounder, no longer so flat, until at last a real globe-form arose, exactly such as the amphibians possess. Moreover, the eye did *not* retain the characteristic iris-coloration; it became strongly pigmented, the eye was retuned [umgestimmt]. Here is again a case where the replants did not remain species-true [arttreu], but took on a foreign shape and a foreign color. The animals perished during hibernation on February 15, 1921, after the eye had already stabilized itself for several weeks.

Further, carp-eyes were over-transplanted onto neotenic tadpoles of *Pelobates fuscus* and onto fire-salamander larvae [Feuersalamanderlarven], unfortunately so far without satisfactory results.

The mud-biter [Schlammbeißer] (*Cobitis fossilis*) has, like other mud-dwelling animals (stone-biter [Steinbeißer], loach [Bartgrundel], etc.), a characteristic body- and eye-color, which deviates strikingly from the other Cyprinidae. The eyes are characterized by a nictitating membrane, a similar yellow iris, such as we see in the likewise mud-dwelling newts [Molchen]; the body-color too, especially the belly-color, has many similarities with the *Triton*s.

This is one of the moreover very frequent convergence-phenomena. *Triton cristatus*-eyes were grafted onto *Cobitis fossilis* (29. I. 1921). It was soon noticed that the convergence very much favors, or rather accelerates, the healing-in and stabilization of the replant. Already now, in mid-February, the replanted eyes are in a stable condition. The color of the eyes remained, naturally, uninfluenced; only the surface of the bulbus was somewhat flattened.

## 6. The Sight-Tests [Sehproben].

When one transplants an eye, [and] the graft-piece grows in and is nourished by the substrate, it still does not follow that this perception-organ is functionally capable. The *Uhlenhuth*ian eye-deplantations [Augendeplantationen] show us that the bulbi grow in also into the nape region, where they are certainly not functionally capable. This functional incapacity of the deplanted salamander-larva eyes was, moreover, proven by *Uhlenhuth* himself. One can also, as is known, bring foreign bodies to healing-in (alloplastic transplantations).

Even though in my eye-replantations the spontaneous rotatability of the eyes was established, this alone is yet only a proof for the innervation of the muscle-connection, but not yet for such [a function] of the organ of sight itself. In order to attain the functional capability of a transplanted sense-organ, the specific brain-nerve-connection (in our case of the optic [nerve, Optikus]) must naturally be re-established again.

Before the test-animals are sacrificed for the anatomical and histological investigation, [it] must be established on the living replant-animals whether there is at all a responsiveness to light [Empfindlichkeit für Licht], or even a perception of objects by means of the replanted eyes. To these ends, in all modifications of the eye-transplantation experiments, *blinded* and *normal control-animals* were also kept, partly because of the direct sight-tests, partly in order to study the general behavior [and] mode of life of the test- and control-animals.

In the cold-blooded animals [Kaltblütern], the study of the mode of life in winter is very difficult. Even though not all fall into the winter-sleep [Winterschlafe], their life-utterances [Lebensäußerungen] are nonetheless reduced. The animals are not at all lively, eat little (or nothing at all); in short their functions, especially their movements, are sluggish [schlaff]. Yet the following observations could be made in winter:

When one disturbs the *fishes*, we see that the blinded animals swim about very restlessly in the higher water-regions, [and] frequently plunge onto the surface; on the other hand, the normal animals and the replants hold themselves relatively quieter and with preference near the bottom of the aquarium. The fish with normal or replanted eyes swim toward a weak light-source, which the blind ones do not do. On the other hand, in contrast to the blinded ones, they again flee [from] very strong light.

The *Triton*s are still more sluggish [schlaffer] in winter than the fish. In the sunlight [Sonnenlicht] the normal and the replantation-animals become restless, otherwise they behave quietly. When one throws them into the water, they swim with certainty toward the shore. The blinded newts [Molche] are not bothered by the light; on the other hand, they move their heads very violently at the slightest touch, flee very clumsily, bump against hard objects, and in the water they reach the shore only after a longer searching-about. We have already set forth how the *Triton*s with replanted eyes lose the blind-habitus with the stabilization of the replant. Then they also show distinct cornea- and pupillary-reactions. The animals show light-reactions and seek out and find the food laid down for them.

The *frog-amphibians* [Froschlurche], especially the toads [Unken], which stood most at my disposal, showed, in respect to their behavior, the same phenomena. They too lost the blind-habitus with time, and showed very weak cornea-reflexes; upon being touched on a hair [there are] eye-twitchings, and they possess a *very prompt* pupillary reaction. In the sunlight the toads do not like to stay, whereas they shoot off toward a weak light-source when they are in the dark.

The first light-test, which can also be applied to the test-animals mentioned earlier, rests upon the flight of the normal toads from strong light (negative phototaxis).

One half of a glass container is clad on all sides with black pasteboard, so that darkness prevails in this half of the glass tank. In the immediate vicinity of the darkened half we install a thermostat, so that this spot shall have at least an equally high temperature as the other half of the container. This other half is, namely, strongly illuminated by metal-filament lamps of high candle-power, possibly with arc-light. If normal animals or test-animals are placed into the illuminated part of the container, we see that they immediately hop into the dark and remain there constantly. If we now carefully take the black pasteboard covering down from the hitherto darkened side and place it upon the other tank-half, the animal hops again into the dark within a short span [of time]. The blinded animals remain quietly in the illuminated part of the glass container; the light evidently does not bother them.

The animals with the replanted eyes can therefore distinguish light and shadow like the normal ones, but not the totally blinded ones.

Skin-sensations are excluded (blinded animals!); warmth-stimuli too cannot come into consideration (thermostat!).

On the basis of the positive phototaxis of the seeing animals in weak light, I have also conducted light-tests on the toads. A fairly long container was taken, completely clad in black on all sides, hence wholly darkened, at whose two ends there were two openings. The openings were well closable with zinc plates. Far from the openings, a not-strong electric light-source was installed. The test-animal was placed into the container, one opening was closed, and the lamp was turned on before the other end of the container. After a few seconds the otherwise sluggish [faule] animal shot directly toward the light-source and remained constantly in the end of the container weakly illuminated in this manner. If one darkens the half in which the animal now is and weakly illuminates the other end, the animal hops over again. Blinded animals do not show this reaction; they remain quiet even in the dark.

In the toads, a formal image-seeing [Bildsehen] was also observed, in that one placed the animal into a container in which one or two flies were located. The animals snapped at once at the flies, [they] soon wiped them off and devoured the prey. Blinded animals are not even to be moved to devour the fodder held before them. We are therefore compelled to stuff them [by force]. The toads with transplanted eyes do not bump, during flight, against any hard object either, [as] we see so often in blinded animals. From all this it follows that the function-capability of the eyes was re-established.

Already the behavior of the blinded and of the *test-rats* with replanted eyes differs strikingly from one another. The blinded animals are always restless, very often leave their hiding-corners, and when one frightens them, they do not run at once into the hiding-corner, as the normal animals and the animals with transplanted eyes as a rule do. The blinded rats are also very anxious. When one brings them into a container, they tremble and shake their head, held high, restlessly. The rats with transplanted eyes, and the normal control-animals, on the other hand, seek out a corner in the container [and] remain there quietly, with the head directed toward the ground, as if they did not want to make themselves noticed.

When one takes blinded albino rats into the hand, they let inarticulate tones (squeaking) be heard, which normal animals and test-animals (with replanted eyes) do not do. The blinded rats bump everything that one places before them, even the walls of the cage, with the snout, which in other animals we cannot perceive, or not so often. If one places an unusual object near the animals into the cage, the blinded animals bump it at once, without selection, with the snout; the other animals, however, first draw back, and only after a longer time do they go for the object.

The light-reactions were also conducted on rats, and it turned out that the animals flee from strong light into the darkness and constantly remain there. These light-tests, also on all the animals mentioned earlier, were demonstrated at the session of the Vienna Biological Society on December 7, 1921.

In the very suitable, strong light of the episcope in the meeting-hall, one half of the field of view was darkened, while the animals found themselves in the illuminated part of it. With this arrangement we saw the same characteristic reactions which we already cited.

In sunlight the rats with grafted-on eyes react distinctly to the showing of little rods [Stäbchen]; they follow the movements of the same with their heads. This appearance already speaks for an image-seeing [Bildsehen].

The seeing rats are also readily positively phototactic; the wild rats with transplanted eyes go after the light. Upon a stronger excitation of the animals, this reaction fails [to appear].

There is just one more fact to which I should like, finally, to draw attention. It has sometimes happened that some wild-grey rats got out of the cage. The rats with transplanted eyes flee with great speed; one could only catch them again with effort, whereas the blinded animals, when they chanced to get out, stopped in front of the cage, did not run away, and were easily caught on the spot. —

Moreover, the wild-grey rats with replanted eyes possess a jumping ability; they jump skilfully through the cage into which one wishes to catch them.

The ophthalmoscopic examination of the replanted eyes, which Prof. Dr. *Moritz Sachs* carried out with the utmost kindness, and for which I am permitted to express to him my most cordial thanks, showed that from the ocular fundus of the amphibians a whitish-blue light does indeed come, but in the case of the rat eyes he could perceive no red light. However, the more precise examination of the eyes with a *Lange* lamp shows that at least in some replanted rat eyes traces of the red light (which, however, are not beyond doubt) can be perceived.¹

## 7. Aims and Means of the Further Experiments.

We saw that the replanted eyes of the vertebrates can regain their functional capacity after a certain length of time. In order to test the functional capacity [Funktion] of the eyes, I have employed sensory-physiological methods, which are all the more conclusive because alongside the experimental animals the reactions of normal and of blinded control animals were always tested and observed at the same time. In the case of the mammals, the establishment of image-vision still requires further investigations. It is desirable to work on pronouncedly visual animals. Among the rodents, the squirrels would be suitable for this. The carnivores would also be good experimental objects; in cats one could, moreover, study the pupillary reflexes excellently. Young chickens would also be very suitable, especially for the feeding tests. The colour-vision of these animals will also be investigated in detail through training experiments.

Further lacking are the histological investigations into the structure of the transplanted eye and into the optic nerve [Optikus].² Since comparatively only few experimental animals were at my disposal, I have so far been unable to sacrifice animals for histological purposes. One albinotic rat, however, died months after the operation as a result of a bite,

> ² Compare, however, the later treatise No. V in the same issue.

and this [animal] will serve for the establishment of the structure and connections of the eye. Even on the anatomical preparation the adhesions of the eye muscles and of various nerves were clearly to be seen. I hope soon to be in a position to prepare microscopic preparations of all the transplantation modifications.

The aims of the further experiments are scientific and practical-medical. With the establishment of the fact that the blinded cold-blooded animals can again gradually lighten in colour after the eye-transplantation, I gained, as Prof. *Przibram* emphasized in the first part of this publication, a means which permits a deeper penetration into the coloration-problems of the animals. It would be possible, through the elaboration of the hetero- and dysplastic methods, to influence the colour-dress of the animals at will. The sequelae of a somatic transspecies transfer could also become clearly apparent at the eyes. A further field of the future investigations would be whether the species-characteristics can also influence class-foreign implants, the germ cells, through parallel-induction, [and] whether this "graft-hybridism" is heritable.

All the reciprocal relations (also the equalization of the biochemical difference) between graft-piece and host animal will have to be subjected to a precise investigation. Furthermore, it seems to be of significance how the visual capacity, the "world-view", of the dysplastically operated animals will take shape. At this point I passed over to another complex of problems, to brain-plasty [Gehirnplastik]. Do the capacities for the formation of a world-view lie in the perception-organ or in the apperception-organ? The heteroplastic brain-transplantations (which are already under way) will, it is hoped, also throw light upon this question. In the brain-transplantations, other most important problems too can naturally be taken up.

The exchange of eyes and ears will be of use for the re-examination of the *Müller* law of the specific sense-energies.

On the other hand, the possibility that my eye-transplantation experiments might also be transferred to man opens up wide perspectives for us. In eye-transplantation we have seen that the eye-transplantation, even when it does not succeed, is absolutely not dangerous for the animal. In no single case have animals died as a result of an eye-transplantation. When we see that in man one forces glass eyes into the orbit, we can at once raise the question whether it would not, even merely for cosmetic purposes, be better to transplant real eyes. This question had already occupied earlier researchers, who did not at all expect the functional capacity of the transplanted eyes.

Before, however, eye-transplantation in man can be proposed to the physicians, I should nevertheless first like to experiment on larger mammals, in order to ascertain whether the eyes heal in. Whether the functional capacity [Funktion] of the eye can be restored again is then a further question. If the replantations succeed also in larger mammals, then one could take up the eye-transplantations directly in veterinary-medical practice.

Furthermore, the question is not yet decided whether into such an orbit, out of which the bulb had already long been removed, an eye can be successfully implanted. What has so far been achieved is brought, in brief, by the following summary.

## 8. Summary.

1. The eyeballs of vertebrates were removed under narcosis by the autophoric method (cf. *Przibram*, this work, first part) and transplanted again into an eye-socket. "Replantation," in contrast to "deplantation" at other parts of the body, as it was carried out by *Uhlenhuth* on salamander larvae after *Thiersch's* method, [and] by me also on metamorphosed toads.

2. The autophorically replanted eyes of fishes, amphibians and mammals can heal in and remain preserved for months. A limit could so far not yet be established, since specimens from the beginning of the experiments up to the day of writing are still alive and have retained their second eyes.

3. Successful healing-in has been established for the eyes of the following species:

a) Eyes without lids:
1. *Carassius vulgaris* Nilss.
2. Tadpoles of *Pelobates fuscus* Wagl.

b) Eyes with developed lids and nictitating membrane (mostly inserted together with a piece of the upper lid):
1. *Molge cristata* (full-grown newt).
2. *Bufo viridis* Laur.
3. *Bombinator igneus* Rösel.
4. *Rana esculenta* L.

c) Eyes with lids, but with not well-developed nictitating membrane:
*Epimys norvegicus* Erxl. albinus et ferus.

4. The eyes have been replanted not only homoioplastically, onto other specimens of the same species, but also alleloplastically, onto other colour-races, heteroplastically, onto another species of the same animal class, and dysplastically, onto members of other animal classes, without having to die, namely: a) Fish eyes,
heteroplastically:
*Carassius* onto *Alburnus* and vice versa,

dysplastically:
*Carassius* onto *Molge cristata* (full-grown newt),
*Carassius* onto *Salamandra maculosa* (full-grown newt),
*Carassius* onto *Bombinator igneus* (full-grown frog).

b) Urodele eyes,
heteroplastically:
*Molge cristata* onto *Molge vulgaris* (both full-grown newts) and vice versa,
*Molge cristata* (full-grown newt) onto *Salamandra maculosa* (larva),
*Molge vulgaris* (full-grown newt) onto *Salamandra maculosa* (larva),

dysplastically:
*Molge cristata* (full-grown newt) onto *Cobitis fossilis*,
*Salamandra maculosa* (full-grown newt) onto *Carassius*.

c) Anuran eyes,
heteroplastically:
*Bufo viridis* onto *Bombinator igneus*,
*Rana temporaria* onto *Rana esculenta*,

dysplastically:
*Bombinator igneus* (full-grown frog) onto *Carassius*.

d) Mammal eyes,
alleloplastically:
*Epimys norvegicus* ferus onto *E. norvegicus* albinus.
heteroplastically:
*Mus musculus* L. albinus onto *Epimys norvegicus* albinus.

5. In animal species that moult, moultings of the replanted eye were observed after the transplantation, when the eye had been replanted kataplastically, i.e. from more-developed onto less-developed animals, whether the difference in development referred to the ontogenetic age or to phylogenetically different developmental height.

6. Shortly after the operation, clouding of the cornea is also to be noticed in mammals, which can later recede; inflammations and exudates also occur, which then mostly take a bad outcome.

7. After a relatively short time the eyes are wont to become at least passively rotatable; in cold-blooded animals pupillary and corneal reflex have appeared, which has never been observed in deplanted ones. The lids of the mammals function normally from the start.

8. The replanted eyeballs then differ, on viewing without optical aids, outwardly at most through the sometimes-present deeper position from normal ones, but may in special cases undergo far-reaching changes of their form and colour, which make them resemble the host animal:

a) The flat fish eye, on the land-dwelling amphibian, assumes a vaulted form; conversely, the spherical amphibian eye assumes a flatter one on the fish body in the water. (Influence of the water pressure?)

b) Eyes with little pigment, heteroplastically transplanted onto species with much pigment, become more pigmented, that is, are retuned in the sense of the host animal (*Molge vulgaris* bulb onto *Molge cristata*, *Carassius* eye onto *Bombinator igneus*). The otherwise never fully pigmented eye of the newts: *Molge cristata* et *vulgaris*, when replanted onto salamander larvae, sometimes loses, at the metamorphosis, its yellow iris-ring (just as the anaplastically-deplanted salamander eyes did in the *Uhlenhuth* experiments).

c) In the case of the rats and mice it appears that, with alleloplastic insertion of coloured eyes into albinotic animals and vice versa, damages set in which make this transplantation more difficult than the heteroplastic one between equally-coloured specimens.

9. On the successfully healed-in eyes a reaction to light-stimuli can be demonstrated:

a) The dark clouding setting in soon upon the blinding of fishes, which is connected with the switching-off of the optic nerve [Optikus], can recede again after eye-replantation; the amphibians (*Molge* and *Bombinator*), which take on the blinding-colour more slowly, behave similarly.

b) From strong light normal fishes, amphibians and Murids flee, whereas blinded ones behave indifferently towards it. From the dark, normal fishes, amphibians and Murids move toward weak light sources; blinded ones are unable to do this. The experimental animals with replanted eyes behave, both in the positive phototaxis towards weak light and in the negative one towards strong light, exactly like the normal ones, but not like the eyeless ones.

10. The flight from strong light is not to be attributed, say, to a heat-effect, for even when the temperature in the shade is deliberately made just as high as, or somewhat higher than, in the light-field, the animals flee out of this into the darkness.

11. Blind animals are more restless and more anxious than normal ones; those with replanted eyes behave in this respect as in normal ones.

a) The blinded fishes keep themselves more at the surface of the container and settle on the bottom more rarely.

b) The blinded amphibians are restless also in the dark, where the others remain quiet.

c) The blind albinotic rats squeak when one touches them, which the others do not do, and are especially solicitous about their young. Through the wire grating of the cage they are wont to push the whole snout through, the others on the contrary only the whiskers.

12. Animals with replanted eyes show, further, exactly such behaviour as the normal ones with respect to those movements which one was hitherto accustomed to trace back to image-vision:

a) Normal and replanted Urodeles take food, frog-amphibians snap at flies, whereas blind ones will not take any food at all and must, if necessary, be force-fed.

b) Normal and replanted amphibians and Murids are able to avoid all objects while running, blind ones on the contrary not.

c) Normal and replanted rats follow the movement of a rod with the head, but not the blind ones.

d) The wild-grey rats with replanted eyes jump, when one wishes to catch them, over the transport-cage held out before them and run away. Blind rats dare neither to jump nor to flee quickly.

13. From all these reasons the difference in the behaviour of the animals with replanted eyes as against eyeless ones cannot appear doubtful to us, nor the presence of the eyes as the cause of these differences.

14. The ophthalmoscopic examination of the replanted eyes showed that from the fundus of the amphibians a whitish light does indeed come, but in the rat eyes no red light could be perceived from the interior.¹

> ¹ In later experiments, however, such was obtained beyond doubt; see this work, part III.

15. After the experiments hitherto, no circumstance has come to light that would constitute a fundamental obstacle to the re-insertion of human eyes, nor even to the replacement of the same by eyes of a species closely related to man. Yet the behaviour of eyes of the same size as human ones must still be tested before one could proceed to a utilization in medical practice, even if only for the purpose of cosmetics.
