## Pupation of Headless Caterpillars of Butterflies
### (at the same time: Causes of Animal Colour-Clothing. VII)¹)

By

Hans Przibram.

(From the Biological Experimental Institute [Zoological Division] of the Academy of Sciences in Vienna.)

*(Received 2 July 1921.)*

*Archiv für Entwicklungsmechanik der Organismen*, vol. 50 (1922).

In recent years we have on several occasions taken the opportunity to address the problems of colour adaptation in butterfly pupae (the results have on each occasion been demonstrated in the Zoological-Botanical Society). The principal result of the experiments undertaken by Miss Dr. Leonore Brecher together with me, on the Cabbage White (*Pieris brassicae*) and several tortoiseshell species (Vanessidae), was the tracing-back of the pupal-colour modifications to the tyrosinase ferment, which is variable through light of differing wavelength. Doubtful, however, remained the role of the eye. According to Poulton's experiments — confirmed by Brecher — of switching off the sense of sight by painting over both eyes with black paint, the perception of the particular light-colour would not be necessary for the assumption of the surrounding colour. At least, even after the painting-over of the caterpillar's eyes, the pupal colours typical for the chosen background appeared, namely on black dark ones, on white light ones, on yellow or yellow-green pigment-poor ones, in the Cabbage White green ones, in the Peacock butterfly (*Vanessa Io*) golden-green ones, in the Small Tortoiseshell (*V. urticae*) golden ones, in grey or in darkness pupae of medium pigmentation. Already on the occasion of Brecher's first painting-over experiments I had voiced doubts about the effectiveness of this blinding method, and had sought to approach the question once more by a more radical removal of the eyes. By means of an electrocautery the surface of both eyes in the Cabbage White or Peacock butterfly was destroyed as thoroughly as possible, and the caterpillars surviving such a procedure no longer showed any reaction to differently coloured surroundings, but had all assumed a more or less medium colouration, as if they had been in darkness. With this it seemed to us proven that at least the presence of the eye is for the colour-

adaptation of the pupating caterpillars necessary, although it remained undecided whether the colour sensation thereby plays a part. Now, however, by Bernhard Dürken in Göttingen, who has likewise conducted colour-adaptation experiments with Cabbage Whites, the objection has been raised against the usability of electrocaustically blinded caterpillars, that the matter here might be one of the effect of the high heat unavoidable in this process upon the pigmentation, and not of the switching-off of the eye. We ourselves had indeed pointed out how the heat acts analogously to the infra-red rays of light, and thus, like the little-refrangible end of the spectrum, produces light pupae. It would therefore be quite possible that, with the addition or prevailing of the heat-effect, pupae of medium type would come to appear in every environment. Only on white, according to this line of thought, quite strikingly light pupae would have to establish themselves. The electrocaustic blindings had furnished too scanty a material to exclude this alternative with certainty. Admittedly, some [caterpillars] with one-sided electrocaustic blinding and injury of other segments along the same hot path were not favourable to this hypothesis, for such caterpillars reacted promptly to the surrounding colour. Nevertheless we could not content ourselves with these meagre results, and I therefore sought better methods in order to carry out a complete switching-off of the eyes without the possibility of such an objection. A simple cutting-off of the relatively very large eyes, which take up almost the whole head, is inadvisable because of the blood loss that ensues, as Prowazek had already experienced in occasional stimulation experiments. Attempts following the procedure of Kopeć (1918), to sever nerves, likewise failed, probably because our smaller objects tolerated these worse than the larger Gypsy Moths of the Polish researcher. A simple artifice, however, led to the desired result. If an ordinary sewing-thread is laid as a loop around the head of the caterpillar and carefully drawn tight in such a way that the forelegs do not get caught in the loop as well, then the head thus ligatured can be severed from the body by a single scissor-cut reaching through in front of the loop, without any bleeding occurring. The caterpillars, which writhe vigorously during the decapitation, remain sitting motionless after it. On being touched, however, they react, and objects pushed between the claspers or anal prolegs are seized and held fast. Life is therefore not extinguished by the decapitation, which need not astonish us, since the head is also not required for life in the imagos of insects, as one can occasionally observe oneself even in the open on beetles which, attacked by enemies, but after the tearing-off of the more easily removable head, were left alone on account of their too laborious working-over [by the enemy]. Another question was whether our decapitated caterpillars would proceed to pupation. To be sure, according to the mentioned experiments of Kopeć, an intact nerve connection in insects is as little necessary for the transformation of the parts cut off from the main mass of the nervous system as, according to Loeb's earlier experiments, in the Axolotl. Also the development of butterflies from pupae that lacked the head in part — in the regeneration experiments of Jan Hirschler — or entirely — in the transplantation experiments of Crampton — has been observed. Nevertheless it was not to be decided in advance whether the decapitated caterpillars could, in sufficient number, complete the so radical transformation process into the pupa. As I learned subsequently, Conte and Vaney (1911), by means of the same artifice, had obtained headless pupae in some moths, though only in small number; Kopeć too (1917) used this method on the Gypsy Moth. The result, however, exceeded all the hopes set upon this method. Of the decapitated caterpillars of the Peacock butterfly a relatively larger number transformed than of the electrocaustically blinded ones, and it was still better with the Cabbage White, in which in individual experimental series up to two-thirds of the experimental caterpillars carried out the transformation. Considerable differences in this percentage are probably to be attributed to the different stage of the operated caterpillars and to the season. In autumn, namely, even without operation a larger number of caterpillars tend to perish through parasites and other diseases. The decapitated caterpillars of the Peacock butterfly, then kept in differently coloured surroundings, showed not the slightest difference in the colour of the resulting pupae, which all turned out of medium type. Still more striking is the analogous result in the Cabbage White, because here the medium light-intensity of grey surroundings yields a type somewhat deviating from darkness. In the latter, namely, the white "saddle" that runs through the medium light-pupae is absent. This same [saddle] was now also absent in the headless Cabbage White pupae transformed on white, grey, yellow or black. In the absence of the head they therefore behaved just like the decapitated, or also undecapitated, pupae kept in darkness. From this now follows the compelling conclusion that in the electrocaustic experiments it is not the heat which disturbs the colour adaptation, but rather the defect — common also to the decapitated caterpillars — of the lack of the eyes. On the other hand, we did not succeed in the past summer in solving the question whether the severing of the nerves or the absence of the eye is decisive for the cessation of the colour change, and [we] shall have to begin with new methods in the next experimental period.

Two results with regard to the colour change deserve, however, already now to be noted, [obtained] in the experiments carried out in the past year. It was shown, in confirmation of the earlier experiments of various observers, that with the fixation of the caterpillars by the byssus emerging from the anus, the sensitivity to the surrounding colour is extinguished. If, namely, caterpillars of *Vanessa Io* were first kept partly on green plants, partly in grey surroundings, and only decapitated after their natural suspension, then the resulting pupal colours were different according to the surroundings, and had thus retained that reaction which had acted upon the still head-bearing caterpillars before fixation. Furthermore, for the switching-off of the colour-sensitivity it proved not to be necessary to actually cut off the head of the caterpillar before fixation, but rather a quite complete drawing-tight of the loop sufficed. For pupation, however, this procedure is less favourable than decapitation, because the head, remaining in connection with the rest of the body, makes the stripping-off of the caterpillar skin more difficult. With regard to the colour, the merely ligatured caterpillars behave just like the decapitated ones; their colour change is extinguished. In the decapitated pupae, besides the colour, the form too is of interest. It is best preserved in the decapitated Cabbage Whites, which can strip off the caterpillar skin quite smoothly, whereas the Vanessids mostly die in a curved position before the skin could be entirely drawn off from the wound-end. In Cabbage White pupae without a head, regeneration is lacking, just as Kopeć too saw no renewal of extirpated brain or other parts of the nervous system in his Gypsy Moths. The pupa decapitated as a caterpillar lacks, on the mask, the antennal sheaths, which normally are supposed to cover the antennae extended backward from the head between the legs. In their place a deep ventral furrow can be discerned. This proves anew the proposition, already set up by Newport (1839) and confirmed by Chapman (1900), that in the transformation of the Hexapods a self-differentiation of the individual parts takes place. Interesting too is the hollowing-out of the middle body region, occurring in dying caterpillars, particularly often in headless ones, which corresponds to the independent moulding of the butterfly "waist," normally concealed by the wing-sheaths growing over it. Whether the pupae from decapitated Cabbage White caterpillars — fully developed apart from the head organs — can let butterflies hatch out, I have not yet observed, but they continue to develop. A fundamental obstacle to the hatching of the butterflies is probably not to be foreseen, for in the work of Stefan Kopeć published 2 years ago (1918), Gypsy Moth pupae are mentioned which, after removal of the brain or other ganglia in the caterpillar, nevertheless yielded the butterflies. Kopeć's brain operation, however, corresponds exactly, with regard to the switching-off of nerve centres, to both our methods, the ligature as well as the decapitation. For according to Kopeć, in the caterpillar's skull proper lies merely the brain, while the sub-oesophageal ganglion already lies in the following neck portion, which remains available to the caterpillar. Also for stimulus-physiological experiments, such as Kopeć undertook, the ligature or decapitation offers a quickly executable, simple method in place of the laborious cutting-out and tearing-out of the brain given by him. In the mentioned work of Conte and Vaney it is stated that Gypsy Moth caterpillars, after decapitation, developed further up to the imago, but had to be artificially freed from the husks.

### Summary.

For the switching-off of the eyes of caterpillars in colour-adaptation experiments with butterfly pupae, the cauterization of the surface of both eyes had yielded the extinction of the capacity for adaptation to the colour of the substrate. In order to meet the objection raised by Dürken — that the identical colouration of the Cabbage White pupae on differently coloured substrate was to be ascribed to the heat-effect unavoidable in cauterization — a method was sought to remove the eyes by a cold route, without the caterpillars bleeding to death. This was achieved by ligaturing the whole head by means of a string and removing the head by means of a scissor-blow (a method which, as became known to me subsequently, had already been practised in 1911 by Vaney and Conte on moth caterpillars for other purposes).

Both in the Cabbage White, *Pieris brassicae*, and in the tortoiseshell species, *Vanessa Io* and *V. urticae*, the percentage of caterpillars pupating after such decapitation proved higher than after cauterization. The colour adaptation failed to appear throughout, in the decapitated ones just as in the cauterized ones. Accordingly it is not the heat-effect, but the destruction of the eyes, which in this case prevents the adaptation. The removed head was not regenerated; the hatching of headless butterflies was not yet observed (Vaney and Conte describe in the cited work the artificial freeing of some Gypsy Moths developed up to the imago without a head).
