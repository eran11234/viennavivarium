### I. Statement of the Problem.

In the preceding work IX in the series "The Environment of the Germ Plasm" (Bierens de Haan and Przibram 1922), it was possible, by means of artificial lowering of temperature using antipyretic [fever-reducing] agents, to furnish the proof that then, just as under the action of low external temperature, a relative shortening of the tail in relation to body length of white brown rats occurred. At that time, however, it had not been possible to produce fever artificially in rats, in order also to demonstrate whether the raised internal temperature, just as is known for the raised external temperature, might bring about a relative lengthening of the tail. For a long time it was impossible to think of a continuation of the experiments, and even in the most recent years the operation of the temperature chambers, in which the earlier experiments had been carried out, could not yet be resumed. Since, however, the filling of the gap that existed was actually independent of keeping the rats at constant external temperatures — because all that mattered in it was the comparison of the relative tail lengths of rats with normal and with raised body heat — young of the same litter could also serve for this, however the external temperature might fluctuate, so long as all the young of the litter remained exposed to one and the same temperatures. Thus, in the autumn of the year 1920, that is, after a six-year interruption, we set about searching for means of arbitrarily raising the internal temperature in rats. Bertold P. Wiesner undertook the testing of tuberculin, from which we expected fever. Although this expectation was not confirmed, the desired result nevertheless emerged, which is discussed in the theoretical part, after the experiments shall have been described.

### II. Experimental Part.

#### A. Material.

The material used consisted of litters of albinotic brown rats (*Mus decumanus*) [*Rattus norvegicus*], which had been bred for some time at the Biological Experimental Institute, but no longer belonged to the precisely registered strains used in the earlier experiments. The parents of these litters had been brought in the autumn from quite low temperatures into the heated workroom, where the litters were produced. Each litter remained with its parents in one of our rat cages. The individual cages were marked by numbered sheet-metal plates, the individual young of each litter by notches in the ears in the same way as in the earlier experiments. Only half of a litter, where possible males and females, was treated¹⁾; the others were to serve as controls with normal internal temperature. In order to keep as much as possible to the counterpart of Bierens' experiments, the litters were used at an age between 3 and 4 weeks.

> ¹⁾ The tail lengths are somewhat different in the two sexes (Przibram 1922, XII); but with few specimens a separation into groups is difficult to carry out, since the differences appear consistently only in the mean.

#### B. Length Measurements.

Just as before, the length measurements were always carried out under ether narcosis. The animal was laid on its back, gently pressed down, the body length determined by means of the same wooden caliper that Bierens had used, and in the same way, by setting one caliper point into the rectum and the other onto the tip of the nose, the tail length was read off on a measuring scale. The length measurements began before the first injection of the experimental rats and were repeated daily until the conclusion of the experiment. The measurements of the body temperature were made with the body thermometer for rats, also used by Bierens, in the rectum, deep.

#### C. Fever Experiments.

The first experiment with tuberculin was begun on a litter of rats which had been born on 9 October 1920 from normal parents and consisted of 8 animals. (The first length measurement took place on 5 November.) At the age of 27 days, 4 animals (2 ♂♂, 2 ♀♀) were injected, 4 animals (3 ♂♂, 1 ♀) were not injected, as controls. The experimental animals (1–4) were given, for 10 days running, injections of Old Tuberculin (Koch) in increasing concentrations. Contrary to expectation, the tuberculin produced no increase of body heat, but rather exerted a lowering effect. Thus, just as in our earlier experiments, it had altogether not been possible to find a fever-producing chemical. Now, however, all the young of the litter used for this experiment, including the control animals (5–8), showed a body heat substantially raised in comparison with the normal temperatures of young rats ascertained by Bierens (1922). A fortunate circumstance had placed in our hands the means to compare, after all, the relative tail lengths of rats with raised body heat with those of normally-temperatured ones. What this circumstance is, we shall hear in the theoretical part.

#### D. Temperature Elevations.

If, in a whole litter, we have obtained an elevation of body heat which goes beyond the norm, then we can make a useful application for the dependence of the relative tail length on body heat only if we have at our disposal sufficiently exact absolute values for the normal body heat of young rats and for its correlation with the relative tail length. Fortunately, Bierens' temperature measurements on rats of the desired age (1922) and Donaldson's (1915) tables for the body–tail relation at all age stages of the white rat provide us with the necessary basis for comparison. According to Bierens (p. 8), the body temperature of rats at the age of 25 days (thus between the 3rd and 4th week) is 36.20° C at 20° external temperature, and 36.80° C at 25°. Since the temperature of our laboratory had certainly remained, on average, below 25°, the latter value may count as a maximum not normally to be reached for the new experiments. Now, however, the mean of the body temperatures in the first tuberculin experiment, from all the young and over all 10 days, amounted to 37.90 (± 0.29), thus exceeded the norm in any case by more than a whole degree Celsius, a — in the poikilothermic — not negligible amount. If we consider the non-injected 4 animals (cf. Tab. a), there results a mean of 38.19 as against the tuberculin-injected ones with 37.61; but in the latter the elevation is still a full degree Celsius above the normal value, since the external temperature had indeed been below 25°. The measurements of the body and tail lengths now yielded, at the beginning of the experiment, a mean of body : tail (K : S) = 1.351, at the end of the experiment K : S = 1.234¹⁾. The initial value agrees very well with the (combined for both sexes) mean which Donaldson (1915, pp. 106 and 116) gives for 27-day-old albino rats as 1.366. On the other hand, for 38 days — into which age our experimental rats had entered at the conclusion of the experiment — the figure 1.287 is to be calculated. Hence in our experiment the tail in the rats with raised body heat grew substantially more strongly in proportion to the body than would correspond to the normal value. If we consider the injected animals 1–4 separately from the non-injected ones, then the initial value K : S was higher, 1.396, than the mean — that is, by chance the shorter-tailed specimens had been used; the end value after 10 injection-days was 1.237, thus even taken absolutely the ratio is still shifted yet more in favor of the tail than in Donaldson's data for the same age. For the non-injected animals 5–8, the initial mean was 1.307, the end value 1.197. We cannot well draw on Donaldson here for comparison, because the initial value is much smaller than his, and therefore nothing can be concluded from a small end value. Fortunately, however, we have at our disposal, from the preliminary experiments for the experiments in the temperature chambers, data for four-week-old and five-week-old albino rats (cooling experiments), whose initial value (28th day) is 1.298 (1.300 when the mean is formed from whole litters), whose end value (35th day) is 1.245 (or 1.260, respectively). The three days still lacking up to the 38th day could, according to the course of growth established by us (Przibram 1922, XIV; 1923, Tab. D), no longer yield so strong an increase of tail length as to push the end values below 1.197. Hence, in this series of the non-injected rats too, a stronger lengthening of the tail was bound up with the elevation of the internal temperature.

Quite similarly proceeded the repetition of the experiment, in which the transfer of the parent rats from the cold into the heated laboratory hall was done deliberately.

### III. Theoretical Part.

#### A. Removal of Objections to the Usability of the Experiments.

Since, in the works of Jackson (1915), Hatai (ibid.) and Stuart (1916), relative long-tailedness in rats has been described following undernourishment, the objection might at first be raised that, in the case of Wiesner's experimental rats, this is a matter of unfavorable nourishment. But that is by no means the case: the non-injected control animals grew well and healthy, gladly accepted the abundantly given food, which consisted of mixed nourishment, and in no way showed signs of constitutional weakness or illness. The tuberculin-injected rats remained somewhat behind in growth (cf. Tab. b), but since their tail at the end of the experiment was relatively shorter than that of the controls, a strong action of undernourishment on the tail lengthening cannot be assumed. The difference in the body–tail relation between the injected and non-injected rats has become smaller after the course of the ten-day experiment, which likewise does not speak for a decisive influence of the injections on the nutritional state and tail length (Tab. c): K/S of animal 1–4 amounted on the 1st experimental day to 1.396, of animal 5–8 it amounted to 1.307, the quotient of these figures is 1.069; K/S of animal 1–4 on the last experimental day 1.237, of animal 5–8 however 1.197, quotient 1.033. The body–tail relation of 1–4 at the beginning divided by that at the end of the experiment yields 1.128, for 5–8 at the beginning divided by that at the end 1.093; thus there is after all a somewhat greater increase of the relative tail length noticeable in the injected rats, which could be traced back to poorer growth — for otherwise they ought really, in accordance with their lesser body-heat elevation, to lead one to expect a lesser tail-length elevation than the non-injected ones.

A second and weightier objection against the use of the two experiments of Wiesner for our theoretical purposes can be sought in the small number of the experiments. The temperature elevation, which made it possible to establish the correlation between raised inner temperature and tail length, was observed only in the two experiments which had been set up for the testing of the tuberculin action. Perhaps the tail lengths behave differently in other cases of inner temperature elevation? Now, however, we do have at our disposal the measurements of body heat carried out in the temperature chambers at various constant external temperatures on the one hand (Przibram 1917, VI; Bierens 1922), and the determinations of the body–tail relation under the same conditions on the other hand (Bierens and Przibram 1922, Przibram 1922). If we set out in tabular form, side by side, for the various external temperatures, the inner heat-values of the rats and the body–tail relation of the same (cf. "Temperatur und Temperatoren," p. 118, Tab. B), then the assignment of the body-heat-values to the body–tail relations, in the sense of a relative lengthening of the tail with rising body heat, is beyond doubt. What we therefore still have to explain is merely why, in one and the same external temperature, in Wiesner's cases an elevation of the internal temperature could come about at all. If we have cleared this up, then the still-remaining gap in our investigation of the connections between external temperature, internal temperature and tail relation closes. The cause of the elevation lies in the sojourn of the parent animals in the cool, before they had been brought into the warmth of the laboratory. This strong rise of body heat upon transfer of rats from a lower into a higher temperature has already been described by Congdon (1912, III); but what is new is that the young retain this elevation!

Here too, however, it is not only the two experiments of Wiesner, but also all the series carried out in the temperature chambers, which corroborate such a behavior: the rats transferred from a lower into a temperature higher by 10° C produce young whose tail is too long for the new external temperature, thus permitting the inference of too high an internal temperature (cf. "Temp. u. Temp.," Chap. 8; p. 120, Tab. E).

#### B. Theoretical Evaluation of the Experiments.

We can now summarize our results on rats with raised as well as lowered internal temperatures to the effect that, for the body–tail relation, it is always the body heat, and only indirectly the external heat, that is decisive. The influence of the latter acts through alteration of the body heat. This is dependent on the intensity of the metabolic processes proceeding in the organism. If the intensity of these processes is maintained for a while upon transfer of rats into higher temperatures, then, in consequence of the constant heat production with lower output, an inner temperature elevation will be the result. Thus the rats transferred from cold into warmth will get too long tails. If the "higher metabolic level" persists not only in the animals themselves, but also in the young born in the new temperature, then these too will exhibit too long tails. If the parents are no longer modifiable, their tails already "fully grown" (which admittedly hardly ever occurs absolutely), then it will create the appearance as if the young, instead of repeating in attenuated form the "shorter cold-tails" of the parents, on the very contrary form a contrast of really "long warmth-tails."

Nevertheless, a uniform "after-effect" probably lurks behind this too, precisely in the maintenance of the "metabolic level." But the question of the heritability of acquired characters shall be reported on only by a further treatise, presenting all the experimental series (preliminary communication Przibram 1922, XIII; summary "Temp. u. Temp.," Chap. 8; 120, Tab. E).

### IV. Summary.

1. Four-week-old albinotic brown rats received, in the following 10 days of life, a relative tail lengthening, if their body temperature had been raised above the value normal at room temperature.

2. This increase occurred after the sudden transfer of the parents from the cold of the cellar into the heated laboratory.

3. The increase of tail length was no effect of hunger or otherwise deficient growth.

4. Injection of tuberculin produced no fever, but did exert an unfavorable influence on growth.

5. The enlargement of the relative tail length appearing upon elevation of the external temperature is to be traced back to the simultaneously occurring elevation of the internal temperature.

6. The reduction of the relative tail length appearing upon lowering of the external temperature has already earlier (Bierens de Haan and Przibram) been traced back to the simultaneously occurring lowering of the internal temperature: accordingly, the external temperature always acts only through the influencing of the body heat upon tail growth.
