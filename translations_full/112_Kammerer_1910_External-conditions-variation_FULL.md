When we celebrate Wilhelm Roux [41]¹) as the founder of developmental mechanics or experimental morphology, a science that has only made it possible to test exactly and to master the laws of development, it may not be inappropriate, on that same occasion, to point out in summary fashion a few principial points that have emerged from our previous experiences concerning the connection between the external world and the organism.

Developmental mechanics has promoted the cultivation of border territories and has accordingly united researchers of very diverse fields in common work, who until then, owing to their special endeavours, had had little to do with one another.

In view of how they are accustomed to conceive the reactive capacity of organic matter toward its environment, one can divide all developmental mechanists into two large groups: into those who come from a science where the method of methodical experiment had from of old been the prevailing method of investigation (physics, chemistry, physiology); and into those who come from sciences with a hitherto purely descriptive and

comparative method as their point of departure (anatomy, zoology, botany). According to this origin, however, the interpretations that the researchers give to their results are very different; and since the one party is still too little familiar with the needs and aims of the other, this remnant of former estrangement leads — so it at least seems to me — to a hindrance of that measure of progress to which the material at hand would already entitle one by its fruitful facts.

It is merely by a few examples and experiences of the most recent time that the views to be brought forward in the following are to be supported; and in doing so (with the addition of my own results not yet published elsewhere, namely on experimental melanism), there are to be preferred, moreover, examples that can be repeated several times over, because they serve for the illustration of various phenomena and thus can perhaps most clearly show the interlocking of the phenomena concerned. — As a stimulus the present writing is meant, naturally not as an exhaustive working-through of the so extraordinarily far-reaching and profound theme.

## I. One and the same factor, applied in equal degree, nevertheless produces different reactions in individuals of equal species and race, but of different descent.

As self-evident as it is for the physicist and chemist, in each of his experiments, that all the conditions, with the exception of the single one whose mode of action is precisely to be investigated, must be equal — so are the experimenters of other fields accessible to experiment still by no means sufficiently familiar with this fundamental requirement for an unambiguous outcome of the results. One point that is often neglected in this respect is this: the equality of the conditions must have prevailed already for a considerable time before the beginning of the experiment concerned; it does not suffice if their balancing-out coincides only with the beginning of the experiment, in order to avoid possible after-effects of the earlier differences.

When the chemist, in undertaking a synthesis, prepares in pure form the individual elements of which it is to consist, so that on testing, all marks, all reactions, accord with their purity, then he has thereby done enough for the purposes of his investigation.

Not so the biologist, who indeed has to reckon with infinitely more complicated sequences of phenomena. Thanks to the revival of Mendel's teaching [4], breeders today already know quite well that not everything that outwardly displays the criteria of a race in pure form represents a pure race. If, for example, we cross a white hen and a white cock having all the marks of a purely white fowl race, they need nevertheless not yield white chicks [4, Pl. IV); they thus prove to be impure, to be afflicted with marks that did not come to the surface externally on the soma, but were nevertheless present in the germ. In order to free the germ too of such admixtures, the breeder must, through pairing, determine a recessive, purely-breeding mark and use only the descendants bearing the latter as unobjectionable material for further experiments.

If matters stand thus with the inner factors of the living substance, then it is no different with the factors that act from without in a transforming way upon their material composition. High temperature, for instance, is such an external factor, which acts upon many organisms investigated thus far in this respect, among other things in such a way that it brings their dark pigments to rapid multiplication, to a gaining of the upper hand over the others — thus makes the organisms melanotic. The wall lizard (Lacerta muralis, forma typica) [Podarcis muralis] occurring at Baden (Lower Austria) appears as if sooted already on constant remaining at 25 degrees C., and at a still somewhat higher temperature becomes coal-black. Upper Italian specimens of the same species and subspecies, on the other hand — thus not, say, already belonging to Upper Italian varieties of Lacerta muralis, but specimens that in size, shape, and colour were not to be distinguished from their more northern conspecifics — retained their normal colour at 25 degrees and showed even at 37 degrees a comparatively weak darkening. And further: in this heat, in the Lower Austrian wall lizards the critical point is already exceeded at which the black pigments withstand the temperature elevation and react to it only with more rapid multiplication; rather, pigment multiplication has already passed over into pigment destruction, and the lizards affected by it acquire, especially on the underside, where they lie on the hot ground, white-edged scales, which lend them a half-bleached appearance [24].

In this case, then, I have brought an unequal condition into the experiment, which, although it no longer continued to exist reckoning from the beginning of the experiment, was yet in its after-effect mighty enough to disturb the unambiguity of the result. Certainly the diverse descent of the material has often already become a source of error and a cause of differences of opinion, in that one and the same experiment, conducted by different researchers at different places, despite application of the same external factor, ended with different results.

In the case adduced by us, the diverse behaviour of the Lower Austrian and the Upper Italian wall lizards was easy to understand: the latter are accustomed already from their homeland to a warmer climate and therefore do not react so quickly nor to an equally small measure of temperature elevation. This simple solution of an apparent contradiction would, however, not have been possible if I had not kept the entirely alike-looking animals, indistinguishable at the start of the experiment, separated according to their place of finding, or if this had not been known to me at all; I would then, in one and the same experimental container, toward the end of the experiment, have obtained specimens with and without black-colouring, and from this result would necessarily have had to draw the then obvious, wholly false conclusions: as, for example, that the phenomenon was not to be traced back to the applied factor at all, but to an unknown inner cause; or that warmth had indeed effected it, but that individual causes, such as differences of age and constitution, conditioned an unequal action, and the like. On going through the relevant literature, one will easily come upon cases where the suspicion of such an erroneous interpretation lies near at hand.

In order to avoid the diverse outcome and to balance out the diverse degree of their sensitivity, it would have sufficed to keep the two Lacerta muralis populations for some time under equal, intermediate temperature conditions — that is, to send ahead of the actual experiments a period of pure observation, which, in the breeding experiments with marks already given, not first to be induced from without, corresponds to the breeding-through for the testing of racial purity.

One will still have to object the following: if temperature elevation effects that the wall lizards take on a darker colour, then the Italian specimens should from the outset represent a darker race, as indeed actually in other zoological temperature experiments (butterflies) deviations were attained through keeping warm, such as occur in more southerly regions also in nature, and through keeping cool such as live in northern regions [18, 45). How does it come about, then, that nevertheless in Upper Italy, under climatic conditions such as the experiment produces artificially, and with which it attains the effect of the black-colouring, there live wall lizards that resemble those of distribution districts situated farther in the north? — The right place to meet this objection will arise only in a later section of our essay, in the chapter that speaks of the "observations in the open."

## II. One and the same factor, applied in different — although also closely adjacent — degrees, nevertheless produces sharply opposed reactions in individuals of equal species, race, descent, equal age, in general equal constitution.

In experiments by Tower [50] on potato-leaf beetles (Leptinotarsa) and by myself [24] on lizards, frogs, and salamanders, it has been recognized that in the moisture- and temperature-scale a definite point exists where pigment multiplication passes over into pigment destruction. This critical point lies fairly differently for each kind of pigment, and also for each kind of living being — indeed even, as we have heard in the previous chapter, for living beings of the same kind but of different descent, not at the same place of the scale.

If one raises the temperature, then this is, as already remarked in the previous section, a circumstance favourable to the multiplication of the black pigment. Just as every progressive phenomenon of development and of growth follows, with certain modifications, Van 't Hoff's rule [23] ("temperature elevation by 10° conditions a two- to threefold elevation of the reaction velocity"), so indeed do all pigments too: they multiply the more rapidly, the higher the temperature. But the individual pigments do not keep step with one another with respect to the velocity of their multiplication, so that the one can outstrip the other herein; mentioned in passing, this is in any case often the cause why certain animals, even under unchanging conditions, change their colour with advancing age, without thereby becoming poorer in pigment — the turtles Testudo marginata [52] and, somewhat less pronouncedly, also T. graeca [28], for instance, even becoming melanotic. Secondly, the pigments, on temperature elevation, do not keep step either with respect to the resistance capacity that they bring to bear against the altered conditions. The black pigments of the forms investigated in this respect thus far seem in both respects to go ahead of the others at first, so that at a certain stage of temperature elevation they crowd out the other pigments and lend their bearers the appearance of Nigrinos. Only at a still more considerable temperature elevation do they likewise perish, whereupon, if this happened at first only in stretches — say, at the most exposed places of the body surface — leucomelanotic and finally wholly bleached-out forms arise.

This insight, exactly tested as yet in so few forms, can of course once again uncover the gravest aberrations in the interpretation of experimental results. For one was hitherto accustomed to regard the action of the external factors upon living beings as a quite specific one: warmth may, as such, always bring forth only reactions of equal quality, which may indeed exhibit quantitative differences in the various temperature degrees, but merely an intensification in the one direction, a weakening in the other. This action was supposed, if possible, to have general validity for all organisms: if Lacerta muralis becomes dark-coloured at 25 degrees, then one would be astonished if a quite different organism, belonging in evolutionary history to a very distant group, did not also do so.

Now let one picture to oneself the case at hand (analogous examples could easily be cited by the dozen): one experimenter finds that in the organism-kind investigated by him a temperature elevation to 37 degrees brings forth artificial melanism; another checks this result, but finds, despite his efforts to establish every condition just as in the experiment of his predecessor — thus, namely, to choose so far as possible material of equal constitution and descent — that artificial albinism now sets in; he will therefore contest the result of the first experimenter and come out for its opposite. Self-recording apparatus, however, would show that the temperature, which in both experiments was supposed to amount to 37 degrees and in general was also maintained, in the first experiment more often fell back by something quite slight below this temperature, in the second more often rose by fractions of a degree above it; so that, on the basis of the regularity discussed in the present section, we would have to make the assumption of an observational error on the part of one of the two observers, and not assume any contradiction at all in the opposed results; rather, we would have to designate exactly 37 degrees C. as the critical point at which the black pigments have reached the limit of their resistance capacity against temperature elevation.

## III. One and the same factor, applied in its opposed extremes, produces equal reactions.

To physicists and chemists I have repeatedly put the question how they explain to themselves that in many experiments — for example temperature experiments on butterflies (Standfuss [46], Fischer 20), and others) — artificially blackened moths come out, quite alike whether one had let frost temperatures or very high degrees of warmth act upon the pupa. The answer ran to the effect: temperature in such cases could not have been the decisive factor; the other conditions of the experiment were probably not strictly equal, there had been involved as well the variation of other conditions (for example, in keeping the pupa on ice, the moisture; in keeping it hot, in consequence of stronger evaporation, the dryness), and among these the responsible influence would first have to be sought by further isolation of it.

But as the experimenters concerned had already surmised and in part proved, and as has recently been confirmed by exact histological investigation by Schuckmann [43], the state of affairs is not always to be explained thus.

Firstly, through both extremes of the same factor there can occur inhibitions of the natural developmental process, which need express themselves not only in a retardation of the total development, but express themselves also in this, that the expression of some local mark — for example the coloured scaling of the butterfly wing — remains permanently stationary at a subordinate stage that otherwise represents only a transitional stage. And this is demonstrably really the case in the cited butterfly experiments.

Secondly, a developmental process that has already taken place can be reversed again either by way of radical destruction of the plasmatic material formed, or by way of gradual involution, as soon as the factor favourable to the process concerned in moderate degrees takes on dimensions which can no longer be conducive to its continuance, for reasons that are mostly far from being specific, but rest upon the most general limits of life of the organic substance (for example coagulation of the albumen).

It also happens that the same end-effect — standstill at a subordinate stage — is brought about at the two boundary points of the scale of the factor in question not by the same intermediary cause, but at the one boundary by inhibition, at the other boundary by disturbance or destruction of development.

Where ferments condition an organic process, it is at the lower boundary at first mostly only the (re-cancellable) inhibition, but at the upper boundary the (no-longer-cancellable) destruction of the ferment-action, which draws after it the same end-effect, namely the non-occurrence of the organic process.

Developmental inhibition and retrograde development can therefore show, or feign, the same picture when the same factor was applied in its extremes. If retrograde development is present, then admittedly the end-results, in the case where one and the same experimenter has himself carried out both experimental series in the positive and negative direction, do not by any means always look to him such that one could confuse them with one another. I can lighten lizard-nigrinos in low temperature (or moisture, or both), and I can let them become leucomelanisms in excessively high temperature (or dryness, or both) [26]; but the bleaching-forms that come to light in the two experimental series are by no means identical with one another: there it is a more general lightening and a reappearance of the marking that had originally been rendered invisible by the darkening of the ground-colour; here it is the appearance of white spots, especially of white scale-margins, on the ground of the body that has still remained black — spots that spread more and more and finally make the animal appear predominantly colourless.

But the end-results are not everywhere so distinctly different — for instance, not equally so in the experiments on butterflies [20, 46]; and even if they were so, but the one experimenter carries out only the one experimental series, and a second, who wants to carry out the other, is dependent on the description of the first, then erroneous contradictions will arise, with which the literature in my opinion at present teems: if the description is not very exact and supported by illustrations — if the first observer speaks, say, only in a preliminary communication, reserving a more detailed description for later (I myself have done this [26]) — curtly of "bleaching-forms" that his heat- or dryness-experiments have yielded, while the second obtains similar approximate "bleaching-forms" in cold- and moisture-experiments, then the confusion in the literature is complete! Instead of being able to take up the relevant correct experimental series and their results as secured findings into the scientific factual material, they must now both be repeated together, if a clarification of the situation is to come about at all.

Besides **similar** reactions to opposite extremes of the same factor — reactions become similar, indeed, only mediately, as a consequence of developmental inhibition or retrograde development — I can still very well imagine that these extremes are also able to act immediately and really upon the organic substance in a concordant manner. Whether I burn myself with a glowing substance or by means of liquid carbonic acid is, with respect to the immediate effects and to the further effects following upon the immediate ones, all one: in both cases necroses set in within the affected tissues, which for their part again call forth like reactions in the tissues surrounding them that have remained viable. To turn to account in this sense any developmental-mechanical experiment, possibly a contribution to the variety-formation of organisms, we do indeed at present still lack the knowledge; but the eventuality must nevertheless be pointed out.

## IV. Different factors, applied to organisms of like kind and like constitution, bring about like reactions.

Already in the previous chapter I reported on my own experiments on calling forth **artificial melanism** in lizards: I was able, through quite different factors — through high temperature as well as through dryness — to rear nigrinos from the parent forms [24]. A third factor that promotes melanism — strong **insolation** [solar irradiation] — appears in lizards in captivity to be not so well applicable, because the stimulus-threshold lies mostly above the measure of radiant energy here available, and produces a lesser, yet at times distinct effect. On a relevant somewhat more sensitive animal applied [to which it is applied], for instance to the freshwater crab (*Potamon fluviatilis*), strong light alone brings about, in relatively short time, that quite black specimens are obtained [26]. To the three factors just described there is joined as a fourth the **sufficiently long sojourn on a dark ground**, which has as a consequence an adaptation of the animal's colour (investigated in *Lacerta* [lizards], several Anuran and Urodele species [27] and land-mollusc species, *Nemachilus* [44]) to the colour of the surroundings; as a fifth factor **excessive nutrition**, which, as we know e.g. from experiments of PICTET [39] and TORNIER [49], has in butterflies, or in the larvae of the garlic-toad (*Pelobates fuscus*), the effect of an abundant deposition of dark pigments in the body-surface.

A further phenomenon, which has recently become more exactly known and which can likewise be brought about through very many factors, is **neoteny** or the retention of juvenile larval form-states. I myself came to know, in amphibian and insect larvae, gradually darkness, cold, air-richness, large quantity and stillness of the water, as well as fattening after a preceding scanty nutrition, as factors that push back the date of transformation; further yet a factor: to take the larva out of the egg before the actual hatching-date, thus to make the embryo into a free larva, to compel it to accommodate itself prematurely to life outside the egg-membrane, in the water [25]. And even if some of these factors (cold, large quantity and air-richness of the water) can be imperfectly isolated from one another — inasmuch as e.g. the cold water perhaps acts as development-retarding chiefly through its air-richness (it is indeed able to hold in solution a greater quantity of atmospheric air than warm water) — there still always remain enough well-separable factors that permit the assertion that neoteny is not produced specifically by only one of them. With respect to the nutrition-factor, POWERS [40] had found the same thing in *Amblystoma* larvae; TORNIER obtained neotenic *Pelobates*-larvae when, from a medium-sized stage with short hind-limbs onward, the meat-supplement to the food was refused and only plant-food was offered [49].

Just as with the experiments using contrary extremes of the same factor very similar or in principle like results can indeed come out, but in these it can finally nevertheless be recognized, by the aid of certain characters, whether they were obtained with the help of the positive or of the negative extreme, so too in many of those cases where, upon application of qualitatively different factors, a like end-result came out. Thus TORNIER [48] obtained erythrosis of the skin of *Pelobates*-larvae both by means of chemicals (glycerin or MgCl₂) and by means of a puncture through the medullary plate into the yolk-region. But whereas the former show only an almost pure erythrosis, the latter have for the most part attained full erythrosis. Also in my lizard-experiments on artificial melanism the dryness-nigrinos are distinct, and not only through gradual differences, from the heat-nigrinos: the difference of the picture results namely from the fact that in the former the ground-colour is darkened so far that the marking can no longer be distinctly perceived; but in the latter, conversely, the marking spreads about itself so much that the bright ground-colour is crowded out. In the fire-salamander (*Salamandra maculosa*) there is, both when it is kept on yellow paper and when it is kept very moist, an increase of the yellow marking to be ascertained — distinct only after years — on black paper, and when it is kept relatively dry, an increase of the dark ground-colour. Only on yellow paper do merely the original spots enlarge, without new ones arising; in moist surroundings, neutral with respect to colour (sand), there arise between the old ones, which scarcely enlarge, numerous new, small, isolated spots. On black paper the original spots diminish, without forfeiting in colour-intensity; in dry surroundings they reduce their extent inessentially, but through the embedding of black pigments into the yellow they are wholly darkened, breathed over by twilight, and sink as it were into the depth [29].

The phenomenon-group "Different factors, applied to organisms of like constitution, bring about like reactions" is relatively the most frequent, hence the best-known and most appreciated among those that I have allowed myself to adduce in the present treatise. It has therefore also not been misinterpreted so often as the rest. Nevertheless, passages are indeed found — less in original works than in collective abstracts and critical reports — where the newly-found factor, which calls forth the same reaction as other, already earlier-found factors, is pointed to as the only correct one. POWERS, for example, in his work on the Axolotl [40] has championed the assumption that only the nutritional conditions clarified by him, and not the factors named before him as causal, chiefly by v. CHAUVIN [9, 10] — surplus of water and surplus of air in the water — condition the non-occurrence of the metamorphosis. And TORNIER [49] has expressed himself similarly. In these cases a true cause of the phenomenon has indeed been newly recognized; but on that account the previously recognized ones have not become invalid, but may with confidence be added to the new one.

It seems after all this, as if — as I have already indicated above, and as E. FISCHER too has repeatedly set forth in more detail [e.g. in 20] — the external factors altogether do not furnish specific reactions to the degree that one believed oneself entitled until now to assume. The considerable number of experiments in which **qualitatively different** factors, and those in which **qualitatively like, but quantitatively different** factors, call forth the like effect upon the variation of living beings seems to speak strongly for it that the properties of the organized substance are in a labile state. Many properties in a particularly labile, others in a less labile one. Notably the former are the so-called variable characters, which we also see in the open in certain species altering so much. Most frequently colour-characters belong here, but also size- and shape-characters and instinct-utterances can be such. These properties, which are the least stable, are the most affected when any change whatever in the external living conditions takes place. But of what kind the change is remains rather indifferent. Differences in the now recognizable reaction are scarcely of higher significance than when we see a steel pendulum — independently of whether a pull or a push or a magnetic or an electric force set it in motion, and accordingly by chance giving the deflection either first to the right and then swinging to the left, or whether, instead of the swing in the left-right plane and reverse, that in the front-back plane is preferred. So too is one of the previously mentioned labile characters of organisms — say the presence of black pigment — able to react to different factors in no other way than through gaining the upper hand and the thereby conditioned crowding-out of other pigments, or through disappearance and being self-crowded-out.

## V. Two successive developmental stages behave differently, or even contrarily, toward one and the same factor.

This phenomenon too, for which I will at once give examples, can give occasion to needless contradictions. By two successive developmental stages there are by no means, as I expressly remark, such ones to be understood that differ strongly morphologically and physiologically, like larva and pupa, nymph and imago; rather, say, even two adjacent larval stages can exhibit the mentioned phenomenon.

We have recourse to our repeatedly used example of the neoteny of amphibian larvae. In order to hold back from the metamorphosis a larva whose transformation-date has already drawn near, care must be taken that the now impending fasting-period or period of slight food-consumption — similar to the pupal rest of the insects — which essentially co-conditions the eventual completion of the transformation on account of the tissue-resorptions going hand in hand with it, does not set in. The larva must therefore be fed very strongly, downright fattened. In the frog-amphibians this critical stage is more easily recognized by the breaking-through of the forelegs through the skin spanning the gill-cavity than in the tailed amphibians, where the relevant stage does not appear so distinctly delimited. In order, on the other hand, to achieve neoteny in a larva whose transformation-date still lies far off, care must be taken that its period of strongest food-uptake — which lets it run through the stages up to near the transformation in rapid succession — is only meagrely completed; it must therefore be fed very scantily, almost starved. It is just the reverse when the metamorphosis is to be brought about in accelerated fashion: the same successive critical stages must here too be treated oppositely — only, however, all juvenile stages fattened, the more definitely fixed older stage abruptly starved. Documentation for these relations is found in the already cited work of POWERS [40], TORNIER [49] and mine [25], as well as probably first in Looss [34] and BARFURTH [2, 3]. TORNIER achieves the neoteny by feeding, from a medium-sized stage with short hind-limbs onward, purely vegetarian instead of mixed with meat. On a cursory consideration of this finding, a contradiction seems to lie before us with the experiences hitherto, inasmuch as neoteny sets in with at first good, then only subsequently poor feeding. The contradiction vanishes, however, at once when, on more attentive reading, we become aware that the stage at which in TORNIER's experimental larvae the change of food was undertaken still lies within that life-period during which weak nutrition acts as metamorphosis-retarding, and that the stage where it would act as metamorphosis-accelerating is just not yet by far reached.

BARFURTH [2] and PFLÜGER [38] have found that rest shortens the transformation of amphibian larvae; I have reckoned still water downright among the neoteny-producing factors, moved water among the metamorphosis-accelerating factors. Here too the contradiction is only an apparent one and rests, like the one cleared up in the previous lines, on the fact that two stages lying immediately one behind the other behave contrarily. Positive mechanical agents — say air- and water-currents, eddies, wave-beat — bring the entry of the transformation-process in the larva rapidly nearer; the actual transformation-process of an animal that has already entered upon the beginning of the process, on the other hand, is admittedly somewhat accelerated when the animal can keep itself motionless.

For stages that are separated by sharper morphological and physiological characters than adjacent larval stages, the different action of the like factor is from the outset more evident. Here belong e.g. the experiments of TOWER, to influence different developmental levels of the leaf-beetles *Leptinotarsa* through external factors, especially moisture- and temperature-extremes [50]: if TOWER let these external factors act only upon eggs or larvae, then the larvae indeed altered, but the beetles arising out of them are unaltered, and likewise their descendants; if TOWER let the external factors act upon the pupa, then he obtained altered beetles, whose descendants nevertheless still remained unaltered. Finally TOWER let the same factors act also upon the beetle itself, which, since fully developed, grown and coloured, no longer altered under their influence; nevertheless the descendants of these beetles, remaining unaltered in their external appearance, showed themselves hereditarily influenced, and indeed exactly in that sense which corresponded to the factors applied apparently in vain to the previous generation.

## VI. Physiological and morphological form-states, confused with one another, simulate the same picture with opposing factors, and an opposing picture with identical factors.

By a **morphological color change** one understands, as is well known, such a one as rests upon a real increase or decrease of the existing pigment, or upon the formation of new pigments. It is accomplished slowly: months or years are required for this; but it is then, even when the factors causing it reverse into their opposite, of considerable duration.

The **physiological color change**, on the contrary, rests upon a mere shifting-together [Zusammenballen] or expansion or wandering of the existing pigment, which remains the same in its quantity, but does not, on account of that process, remain uniformly visible. It is accomplished quickly: minutes, hours, or days suffice for this; but it is not of duration.

Both processes have, in experiments to influence the coloration of organisms through external factors, been confused with one another countless times, and have given occasion to violent controversies, all the more so as they possess the peculiarity, in many ⟨all?⟩ cases, of reacting to one and the same factor in opposite directions and to two contrary factors alike.

To name only one such controversy, let that between Fischel [16, 17] and Flemming [21, 22] concerning the salamander larva be brought forward, from which the first-named researcher emerged as victor, since he kept the physiological process apart from the morphological one, while Flemming, who confused them, fell thereby into contradictions. If one strongly illuminates a salamander larva (of *Salamandra maculosa*), it becomes almost instantaneously pale as a result of the contraction of its chromatophores. Nevertheless the pigment in its skin now increases; and when the glaring illumination has ceased to act as a stimulus upon the larva, this becomes apparent, in that it now darkens and remains dark to a degree which immediately distinguishes it from control animals that were kept in subdued light. If one keeps a salamander larva in the dark, it becomes dark quickly as a result of the slackening and expansion of its chromatophores. Gradually, however, under complete exclusion of light, the pigment perishes, and now the larva remains nearly colorless. Analogous reactions are yielded by temperature treatment: warmth yields physiological bleaching, morphological darkening; cold physiological darkening, morphological brightening.

Bright larvae with abundant, but — as a result of shifting-together — momentarily invisible pigment are not easily told apart, without microscopic investigation, from bright larvae with a deficiency of pigment, and the same holds for the dark ones.

When I keep toads (*Bufo vulgaris, viridis, calamita, Pelobates fuscus*), frogs (*Rana agilis, temporaria*) on the one hand on yellow loam soil, on the other hand on black garden soil, they develop preferentially just those among the pigments available to them which resemble the substratum in question [27]. In agreement herewith, according to Sčerov, the stone loach (*Nemachilus barbatulus*) behaves on gravel, sand, hornblende granite, differently colored paper (in incident light) and differently colored glasses (in transmitted light) [44]. But there are always a few specimens in which the respective physiological state of tension of the chromatophores simulates an entirely different picture. In the toads and frogs it is namely well-being and ill-being, hunger and over-satiation, an imminent or just-completed molt, rut and the like, which release physiological darkening of the morphologically bright-colored ones and physiological brightening of the morphologically dark-colored ones. In the fishes a similar thing (mostly sudden brightening, so that henceforth all transitorily look almost alike) is brought about by a disturbance, a fright, and thus usually just at moments of observation or demonstration. In fully developed earth-salamanders, by contrast, where no physiological color change is any longer present, such an unevenness and unclarity of the picture — often misleading the observer at first — also cannot occur [29].

## VII. Seasonal and other mnemically fixed alterations sometimes appear as called forth by experimentally applied external factors.

Particular caution is required also in the use of such organisms as exhibit phasogenic or periodic, chronogenic changes of form or color, be it, as in many insects, in the form of an alternation of generations, be it that one and the same living being regularly looks different at different times.

In the well-known cases of chronogenic alterations, such as e.g. the seasonal dimorphism of the butterflies, it will of course occur to no one to confuse some arbitrary factor — after, or better said, *despite* whose application precisely the due seasonal form emerged — with the factor responsible for the latter. Precisely here, indeed, there already lies a considerable body of experimental factual material, so that the factors which induce the seasonal dimorphism, namely the temperature prevailing during the pupal rest, are sufficiently investigated [11, 12, 51].

But there are other, less clear or less well-known seasonal dimorphisms which mislead, and have already misled, into errors. The factors, for example, which originally called forth the parthenogenetic and the bisexually reproducing generations of the rotifers and cladocerans are even at present still not elucidated to such a degree that one could, without new experiments, decide for the one or the other, whose decisive efficacy is asserted [32, 37]. Furthermore, most lizards take on, in autumn, before they withdraw into their winter quarters, a darker coloration than the one they display in spring and summer [14, p. 6]. This phenomenon too is still too little studied for its cause to be stated with full certainty. Two causes come into consideration: the brighter colors, which make all the elements of the markings stand out more glaringly, are mostly accompanying phenomena of the sexual drive, namely in the male, and outlast the actual egg-laying period. According to my experiments on artificial melanism [24], it could be, in the case of the darkening appearing in autumn, also a matter of the consequent phenomenon of the preceding hottest season — a partial melanism induced by the summer warmth, which is reversed again by spring as a result of the winter cold. But I have now heard this seasonal color change interpreted in two other, evidently unjustified ways: the fact that blackishly discolored lizards are found in the open precisely during the autumn was, instead of to the preceding high temperature, on the contrary attributed to the already-arrived low temperature. And an observer who kept lizards caught in late summer in a moist terrarium and saw them become dark here in autumn ascribed this to the action of moisture. ## VIII. Observations in the open.

The more exactly carried-out works lie before us which have won their results on the basis of **artificially altered conditions**, the more do we recognize that conclusions about the connection between external factors and the characters of organisms are possible, from **open-field observations**, only in the rarest cases. In Nature the factors act upon the protoplasm in such a complicated way that one really can never say with apodictic certainty: this character is to be ascribed to moisture, that one to the color of the surroundings, a third to the strong action of insolation, etc. I by no means wish to assert that one should not undertake such interpretations at all, and it would mean, in my opinion, falling prey to an exaggerated and unfruitful skepticism, were one to renounce entirely the direct and comparative observation at the natural sites where animals and plants are found. And it is certain that this method of investigation too has led us to beautiful and partly very probable, partly (insofar as they have been experimentally checked) also incontrovertibly correct results. It also gives hints as to where the experiment might set in; it furnishes working hypotheses for experimental biology. It is therefore not to be denied that progress in the knowledge of the mode of action of external factors can be furthered also in free Nature, especially when the local conditions bring it about that the possibilities are not all too manifold, the observed effects not all too ambiguous. Suited for such observations are therefore preferentially only regions of a quite sharply pronounced, monotonous landscape character: desert, cave, river and the like; whereas tracts of land with changing character, such as for instance forest, meadow, lake, conceal in themselves already far more sources of error.

On the other hand, it is to be admitted that much abuse has been carried on with conclusions derived from observations of Nature. And one must condemn it in the most decided manner when, with the help of such observations [35, p. 271; 36, p. 162], it is sought to contest results that have been ascertained by experimental means [24, 26]. That is of course a venture which can never turn out well.

For this, an example: melanotic animals are in many cases found at moist localities, e.g. black nun-moths (*Psilura mo-* *nacha*) in wet forests, black adders (*Vipera berus* var. *prester*) and mountain lizards (*Lacerta vivipara* var. *nigra*) in moors and in the high mountains. From this it was concluded that melanism is conditioned by moisture [33]. Now in many cases this may doubtless be correct. In butterflies in particular there are the experiments of Pictet [39], which prove to evidence that there is a moisture-melanism¹. No less unambiguously, however, have I too proved that there is, specifically in reptiles, a dryness-melanism [24, 26]. Yet melanic reptiles in particular I often found in the open under the following conditions [30]: the regions which they inhabited were indeed moist; but island-like there rose out of them small districts with falling-off margins, which guaranteed rapid run-off, rapid evaporation, and strong insolation. And these were the centers of distribution of the black reptile forms; since they stemmed from a moist region before they had colonized the dry island, they were all the more receptive to the deficiency of moisture prevailing here; the contrast-effect increased here the prospects of becoming melanotic, just as well as, in the example of the wall lizard mentioned at the outset, animals from more northerly districts of capture were more inclined to take on the heat-melanism than were such from more southerly places of capture. In the high mountains there are added the stronger radiation, the weaker vegetation cover, and the faster run-off and evaporation of the precipitation-water conditioned thereby, as well as by the steeper slopes; in moors, in addition, the — according to experimental experience — extremely effective color of the substratum (black), which can complete, or alone accomplish, the effect of the black-coloration of their inhabitants.

How little precisely some of those researchers who do not at all seek to support their observations made in the open experimentally understand how to appreciate physically the mode of action of external factors emerged from a question which was once put to me in a discussion by an adherent of exclusive moisture-melanism: Why, if dryness and strong insolation bring about melanism, are not all

desert animals black?¹ I posed first the counter-question: Why, if moisture brings about melanism, are not all water animals black? The answer to both questions is indeed almost self-evident and can be given for both in common: Because living beings are already, primarily, themselves different, and because precisely everywhere in Nature many factors act simultaneously upon living beings, which (factors) partly support, partly inhibit and cancel one another. And because, as is to become clear from the preceding pages, even a single factor does not act in all its degrees of strength and upon all living beings in the same sense. The isolation of the factors is indeed precisely the task of the experiment; their possibility, the merit of developmental mechanics; whereby simultaneously the misgivings of those collapse in themselves who therefore do not regard the results of experimental biology as binding because the organisms to be investigated are first placed in "unnatural" conditions, and hence could not react "naturally." Applied to the desert question, this answer gains the following more special form: the critical point at which an increase of the dark pigments passes over into their destruction has, with respect to the radiation factor, probably already been exceeded for many living beings. Precisely those which withdraw themselves from the direct sunburn (tenebrionids!) are really black. The eventual melanism-promoting effect of the high day temperature is cancelled by that of the low night temperature. The color of the ground favors the formation of a bright color-garment, whose pigments become at the same time very resistant against temperature and radiation, and so on. I should like to guard expressly against the supposition that I myself hold this argumentation of mine to be correct in all its parts. It is meant only to show how difficult it is, and into what contradictions one falls, if one wishes to draw upon conditions of free life for the explanation of organic form-building without systematic experiments.

For the illustration of the desert question, one more experimental example: the roller-skinks (*Scincoiden*) can hardly be made to aberrate by means of temperature extremes such as already produce in lacertids leucomelanism, local destruction of the melanotically-become color-garment.

> ¹ Similarly, moreover, Eimer [14, p. 4] asks: "Why are the reptiles of the desert glowing in sun-radiation not black, but of the color of the sand?" in reply to v. Bedriaga's [6] view of the origin of melanism through insolation.

The roller-skink [Walzeneidechse] (*Chalcides ocellatus*) inhabiting the desert margin I brought, at 40 degrees, to the point where its ground color appeared as if tarnished with soot; the apothecary's skink (*Scincus officinalis*), by contrast, inhabiting the actual sand-desert, I was unable to alter, although the experimental animals remained healthy for years.

A very instructive example, because at present it has been extensively analyzed through experiments, is furnished by the **melanotic island forms**. There are black lizard forms both on (almost) unvegetated small islets with nearly white limestone rocks (Kamik near Lissa, Ayre near Minorca), and also on such with blackish eruptive rock (Melisello near Lissa, Faraglione blocks near Capri, Filfola blocks near Malta). Since the data on the other physiographic conditions of these localities and their utilization for the explanation of island melanism diverge, on the one hand, widely among themselves [6, 7, 13, 14], on the other hand, often did not agree with my experiments [24, 26, 30], I sought out, in June 1909, the scoglios (Busi, S. Andrea, Melisello, Kamik, Bariazzo piccolo, Bariazzo grande) of the Dalmatian coast inhabited by melanotic forms of *Lacerta serpa*. This local inspection on the spot instructed me that the experimental results stand in the most beautiful harmony with the natural conditions. It is indeed not possible for me to go into it here exhaustively; I should only like to state this much, that three views which were pronounced concerning the causes of island melanism prove, if they are to hold generally, untenable, and have perhaps only contributed in special cases: first, the conjecture that it is everywhere a matter of adaptation to the dark rock [13, 53]; experiments showed that lizards kept on black ground do indeed become darker than those tended on white ground, but the effect is, at least during the lifespan of one generation, weak, much weaker than in other animals experimentally investigated to that end, and moreover the bright limestone rock of Kamik and Ayre at any rate by no means hinders the lizards there from becoming just as totally melanotic as those on the gloomy rock blocks of Melisello, Malta and Capri. Secondly, the conjecture appears untenable that it is a matter of adaptation to the dark shadows, rain-gullies and rock-fissures which, quite especially on the islets with white rock, contrast strongly with the sun-illuminated surroundings [14, p. 7]. Thirdly, the conjecture is to be refuted that the moisture-saturated atmosphere which comes about through the continuous strong evaporation from the sea surface is to blame for it [7, p. 20; 35, p. 271].

The factor that makes the animal forms on small islets (morphologically) melanotic is — in v. Bedriaga's view (although derived from the physiological color change of the chameleon) [6, 7] and in my own view — chiefly the immense radiant energy to which they are exposed there. The very same process of pigment increase that makes us humans return deeply tanned after only a few weeks in the high mountains or in the South operates all the more strongly upon the permanent animal inhabitants of those patches of earth ceaselessly shone upon by the burning sun, so that their pigment accumulates and condenses until they produce the appearance of complete charring. This also explains why precisely the lizards on Kamik, the scoglio with the lightestcolored rock, have attained the most perfect, hitherto known and conceivable state of black coloration, because here, in addition to the radiant energy from above, the reflected energy from below is also added. The stimulating action of the sun's rays upon the animal pigment has here reached its zenith; in the lizards of Kamik it has at least not yet been exceeded — but it has indeed been exceeded in the **snails**, so that the most intense pigment increase has already turned into pigment destruction. Although the land snails of the islands also otherwise tend toward the production of dark forms, those of Scoglio Kamik (*Macularia vermiculata*) are directly bleached and in this manner pure white, almost colorless, and without a trace of the banding otherwise typical of the said species. It goes without saying that I base this latter finding on living specimens, and not, say, on empty shells found lying about, where the bleaching is produced by chemical weathering (cf. also Sturany [47]).

And now at last we return to an example that we had left at the close of the first section: Lower Austrian and Upper Italian *Lacerta muralis*, selected specimens which outwardly cannot be distinguished from one another according to their place of origin, do not behave alike toward a constant temperature of 25 degrees C.: the former become blackish, the latter retain their normal color [24]. I explained this differing capacity for reaction by the fact that the Upper Italian specimens are, already from their homeland, adapted to higher temperatures and are therefore not so sensitive toward them. To this attached the question: If a rise in temperature is supposed to entail blackening, why are the Upper Italian specimens not in general already darker than the Lower Austrian ones? How is it that at the beginning of the experiment they resemble the latter?

Insofar as the answer must be derived from field observations, it cannot turn out unambiguous. To begin with, the Upper Italian *Lacerta muralis*, alongside the *forma typica*, which resembles the specimens occurring with us, in fact occur in several darker varieties, as is already expressed in part of the scientific names (var. *maculiventris* Wern., *nigriventris* Bonap., *Brüggemanni* De Bedr.). That alongside and among them the lighter type is not lacking would be sufficiently explained by assuming the still uncompleted transmutation process, still in progress — and indeed in either case, whether the dark Italian specimens represent the derived form, arisen from the parentform through darkening, or whether they themselves represent the parent form and, on passing over into the cool-moist Alpine climate, let the so-called "type" arise from themselves through lightening. We often commit the error of always regarding the adaptations that confront us in nature as something closed off, finished, and therefore of rejecting the existence of the adaptation when its criteria refuse to agree completely, while we yet know nothing of whether we do not have before us merely an initial or transitional stage of the adaptation. Furthermore, the "type" can be preserved through local conditions that work against the melanism-promoting factors.

But we also possess — carried out, to be sure, on a different animal group — experimental material to settle the question raised. Pictet [39] observed, in various butterflies whose caterpillars he fed not with the food plant familiar to them but with a foreign one, that in the first generation they yielded distinct deviations from the type, which were retained, somewhat weakened, in the following generations that were again fed normally — that is, they were inherited. But when Pictet fed two generations **in succession** with the new food plant, the butterflies, instead of intensifying the aberrant characters, as one should expect according to our other experiences, turned back unmistakably toward the normal type. On this Pictet founds a "law of habituation," which differs from the law of adaptation in the ordinary sense by the fact that no adaptation **characters** are retained, but rather that changes which may have appeared disappear again after the organism has become habituated to the new conditions. The organism returns to its state of indifference. But here one can now speak not of a "law," but only of a "rule," which is likely to hold validity mostly there where a factor does not bring about the reaction of the organism immediately as such, but through the mediation of constitutional alterations, strengthenings, or weakenings that it directly produces. If, for example, caterpillars are fed with hard and nitrogen-poor (nut) instead of soft and nitrogen-rich leaves (sainfoin), then they yield dwarfed and bleached butterflies, which points to a weakening of the constitution. This weakening is now evidently annulled again in the measure to which the caterpillars of successive generations learn to assimilate the food unfamiliar to them ultimately as well as the originallyfamiliar food. When Pictet sets back into normal conditions the daughter generation descending from an altered parent generation, then the hereditary potency of the morphological modifications becomes manifest; but when he leaves them in the abnormal conditions, then it is not these that are inherited, but rather the acquired physiological power of resistance of the organism against the influences that strive to modify it: in order to compensate those influences and to maintain its dynamic equilibrium, it now no longer needs to take on adaptation characters; indeed, it suppresses them with all the greater success the further it has become insensitive toward the external influences.

It would surely be conceivable that this rule of habituation and of the consequent return of the original form plays a great role in nature, where typical forms occupy a very large area of distribution, remain the same under the most varied conditions, and are also not entirely lacking there where, alongside them, aberrant forms have formed and branched off from them. The rule of habituation may, among other things, hold for the distribution of the typical brown wall lizard in Lower Austria and, on the other hand, in Italy.

## IX. The Role of Heredity

in some of the cases, discussed by us, of the action of external factors upon the variation of living beings.

With regard to the presence of particular experiments, it is first of all interesting to emphasize that for heredity it remains entirely indifferent whether an assumed change was produced by the negative or the positive pole of one and the same factor. Expressed more clearly: in those cases enumerated in Chapter III, where one obtains the same reaction whether one has applied extreme cold or extreme heat, one also obtains the inheritance of the reaction independently of the manner of induction. In the well-known breeding experiments of Standfuss with the small tortoiseshell or nettle butterfly (*Vanessa urticae* [46]), as well as in those of Fischer with the brown tiger moth (*Arctia caja* [19]), melanistic aberrations had been obtained by temperatures below 0 degrees; on the other hand, Schröder [42] placed the pupae of the gooseberry moth or harlequin (*Abraxas grossulariata*) used by him three times daily for one hour each at a high temperature of 38 degrees, and likewise obtained specimens which, compared with those of the normal control breeding, exhibited a greater richness in dark pigments. Here as there, however, the rearing of the offspring, undertaken at a moderate temperature, yielded a number of butterflies that were altered in the sense of the parents.

At the close of Chapter IV there was talk of the fact that organisms possess characters, of which some can be easily and others with difficulty altered, but which can in every case respond to **any arbitrary** factor only along **determinate directions** with changes. I believe that this insight is called upon to place the facts of "goal-strivingness" (C. E. v. Baer [1]), of the "orthogenesis" (Eimer [15]) of organisms, the doctrine of the varying of animals and plants along one-sidedly limited directions, upon a mechanically more comprehensible basis than has hitherto been accorded to it. It is precisely the keeping to such directional lines that the descent theory has hitherto been able to explain only imperfectly; it is precisely this that has given rise to the gravest objections against it and to the tenacious obstinacy of supersensible, metaphysical attempts at explanation.

Incidentally remarked, for evolution those characters have lesser significance which already fall out of equilibrium at the most minimal changes of the external world than such as already require a stronger impetus in order to lose their stability. For only the latter possess — when they inherit the change that has occurred (that they do this can today no longer be disputed [31]) — well-founded prospects of maintaining the newly won state of equilibrium sufficiently tenaciously, even if, in the changing external living conditions, a reversal into the contrary condition, hostile to the acquired change, should once more have set in. The wholly labile characters, however, sink back on such occasion immediately into the original conditions, even though they too made no exception from the hereditary, merely latent transmission. Only then can the changes assumed by such labile characters likewise draw lasting consequences for the descent after them, when the altering factors continue to act uninterruptedly during the later generations. Probably they then bring about a gradual increase in the stability of the equilibrium of those characters, so that, originally easily, amply, and suddenly variable, after a series of generations influenced in the same direction they firmly retain their change and can no longer so easily be reverted into their opposite, into the initial state.

The whole difference between "modification" and "mutation," as it is set forth, for instance, in a fine work by Wolf [54], is likely to consist in nothing other than that the "non-hereditary" modifications concern the most labile characters and that therefore the inheritance, without the further action of the modifying factor upon the following generations, does not come to appearance; whereas the "hereditary" mutations concern less labile characters, whose change is retained in the next, no longer influenced generation. The difference between modification and mutation would accordingly be a gradual one, not one of principle. If modification and mutation are outwardly not to be distinguished, but to be recognized only by the fact that the one is hereditary and the other not (e.g., Baur's [5] case with the white flowers of *Primula sinensis rubra*), then the modification is likely not yet to have been influenced long enough, not yet through sufficiently many generations, by the responsible external factor, and therefore not yet sufficiently fixed, whereas in the case of mutation this is already the case. If, on the other hand — as in some experiments with experimentally decolorized bacteria [54] — the influencing has taken place over equally many generations, and yet the altered property proves hereditary in the one case and not hereditary in the other: then it is to be recalled that, in a given generation, characters that look alike need not also be genetically alike, but may have converged out of changes of two different characters (colorlessness out of the disappearance of different pigments [4, Pl. IV]), of which then the one, which brought with it the non-hereditary modification, was labile, the other, which represented the hereditary mutation, comparatively more stable.
