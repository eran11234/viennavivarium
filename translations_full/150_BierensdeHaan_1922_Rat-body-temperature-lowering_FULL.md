## I. Statement of the Problem.

It was known from experiments by Sumner (1915) and Przibram (1909, 1910) that in young mice and rats the ratio between tail length and body length increased in favor of the tail length in individuals reared in heat, whereas in those reared in cold it, on the contrary, decreased. It was further shown by the same authors (Przibram 1917; Sumner 1913) that the body temperatures of the animals fluctuated with the temperature of the surroundings, especially in young animals in the stage of strongest growth, about which I have recently reported from my own experience (J. A. Bierens de Haan 1921). Hence the question now lies close at hand, whether this relatively greater tail growth is a direct consequence of the elevated external temperature (e.g. through warm radiation), or only an indirect one mediated by the elevated body temperature. To decide this question it is therefore necessary not to raise the body temperature with the aid of the elevated external temperature, as is the case with young animals that live at a higher or lower external temperature, but instead to lower the body temperature in some way while maintaining the surrounding warmth, and to compare the growth of these animals with that of their littermates.

The experiments on this question were carried out in the winter and spring of 1913–1914 at the Biological Experimental Institute, and then, with the outbreak of the war, broken off entirely before they had reached an end. As a consequence of the war and its aftermath, they are only now being processed and evaluated.

## II. Experimental Part.

### A. Material.

As material, various young litters of albino brown rats (*Mus decumanus*) from the Biological Experimental Institute were used. The animals were always taken at the respective rearing temperatures at which they and their parents had been kept. The animals were placed together with their parents in cages in which the known constant temperature reigned; each litter was, as far as possible, marked on the ears according to males and females, whereby the young could be told apart in advance. Then one part of the same litter was treated, where possible males as well as females, while the other animals of the same litter served as controls (one kept them thereby for comparison). Whether young animals show themselves sensitive to the treatment (injections) always depended on the animals being worked with, even when these were barely 3 weeks old.

### B. Length Measurements.

The length measurements were always carried out on animals lightly narcotized with ether. For this the animal was laid on its back; the body length was thereby measured from the tip of the snout to the root of the tail, while the tail was held straight and the body fixed in place by an inserted thumbnail. The tail length was then measured directly in millimeters on the divided measuring scale. This measurement of the tail lengths is fraught with error, since one cannot, on account of the body movements in small animals, always be entirely certain that the tail is held really straight; and the deviations that might result thereby were always somewhat small in proportion to the magnitude of the body and tail growth. The size of body and tail was likewise determined at the beginning, at the start of the experimental series, and then compared with the result at the conclusion of the experiments.

The measurements at the Experimental Institute took place on the 14th day of life; the day on which my experiments began I chose so that the body growth would begin one week after this measurement, that is, on the 14th–21st day of life, uninterruptedly. By calculation I then found the percentage growth-increases from this; but the result was, with these length measurements taken together for the litters in the cages, in which the young animals grew up at the known rearing temperatures, only inexactly to be determined. Each animal therefore had to be measured beforehand individually, in order to be able to expect more exact growth results. — The measurements of the body temperatures took place with a mercury thermometer, with which the temperatures could be read exactly to 0.2° C. Rectal temperatures were always measured. About more exact details of this procedure I and the other [author] likewise referred earlier in this journal.

### C. Fever Experiments.

As stated in the introduction, an attempt should be made to lower the body temperatures of the animals in a colder surrounding, in order to let them rise again in the same way as with fever, so that the body temperatures could nevertheless be brought into equality with the elevated body temperatures of young rats kept warm. But this was not so easy, since to raise the body temperatures of young rats by the not-unimportant amount of the temperature of the surroundings is an unfavorable temperature at which to excite full fever in rabbits and other warm-blooded animals. Whether the insensitivity of the rats to such means stands in connection with the well-known insensitivity to infections must here, however, remain undecided.

Some means were already practically excluded by us for known reasons. So, for example, the "warmth-puncture" [Wärmestich], for which one would need the most needy animals in order to be able to expect to a great extent the injury; and, even though these animals in general held themselves well against this puncture, nevertheless in young animals one such growth result was hardly to be expected so soon. Incidentally, it is doubtful whether the operation of temperature elevation gives the result in our sense, since it has been shown for the brain, e.g. by Citron and Leschke (1913), that the cerebral substance switches off the heat centers.

Because of the danger of contagion it was tried to excite fever through pathogenic bacteria. It remained, however, to investigate with the non-pathogenic cultures, and so I dared to choose the sterile cultures of *B. pyocyaneus*, which Ernst Pribram likewise knew of from "Krall's Museums." Table I gives the negative result of such injections. The cultures were reduced under physiological conditions such that the control animals *y* and *z* (as in all experiments) were injected with pure physiological cooking-salt solution. The concentrations varied from 0.05–2 ccm, but fever was not reached; the evening temperatures on 1. IV, even if pointing to a light fever (cf. the higher dose 0.02 ccm), showed on the next day, however, no trace of fever any more, and even the later injections gave no result. The higher evening temperature at *e* ½ on 7. IV is probably to be ascribed to a fever; here too it is not to be left unmentioned that Feré gave injections of 2 ccm to rabbits.

By way of comparison it was further tried to achieve temperature-elevation by anaphylactic routes. As is well known, rats and mice are very recently [found to be] insensitive, although Ritz (1911) succeeded in provoking fever-phenomena in white mice, even with the second [injection] intravenously instead of subcutaneously. Injected was 0.2 ccm of a mixture of horse-blood, hen-egg-white and 0.5% phenol-solution; later again subcutaneously a varying amount, from 0.3–0.4, of cow-serum. Fever was here in any case not observed. Incidentally, it is here beside the question whether a hyperthermia obtained in this way would be practically applicable for the problem posed.

There remained now still the chemical means. The strongest of these I now wanted to be Tetrahydro-β-naphthylamine; nevertheless, although Feré (1911) obtained a distinct fever — which could not be influenced by the usual antipyretics — with a subcutaneous injection of 40 mg in rabbits, before [trying] this in us [our animals it proved otherwise]. To injection in young rats served large doses of a Tetrahydro-β-naphthylamine-hydrochloride solution, of which 6 mg per [animal] was already deadly by itself, and indeed an injection of 4 mg already lowered the temperature within one hour from 35.4° to 31.0° under distinct poisoning-phenomena. As also injections of 0.2–0.5 mg gave no temperature changes (see Table II), and distinct fever-injections [gave] in any case no distinct fever, I have, also from fear of damaging side-effects, provisionally refrained from further injections. Later, however, the opportunity was lacking to try the means once again on these injections.

I now wanted to test further: zinc sulfate, turpentine oil and cooking-salt solutions. Zinc sulfate I had injected in doses of 0.5–4 mg; but it gave with all [animals] in any case a distinct temperature-elevation again on the next day. Kißkalt (1912) also found a distinct temperature-elevation in rabbits. Turpentine oil I had injected several times in the evening in doses of 0.05–0.4 ccm of a 10% dilution, without fever showing itself the next morning; and injections [of cooking-salt] gave an elevating after-effect, as Table III [shows] with its smaller cooking-salt fever, thus on the contrary a distinct temperature-elevation.

I have therefore not found the right means for exciting fever in rats. Perhaps one would, however, in further attempts find a more successful dose of one of the attempted means; but in the meantime I have now turned to temperature-lowering experiments in distinct cycles, and have later seldom had the opportunity to take up the fever-experiments again anew.

### D. Temperature Lowerings.

For the lowering of the body temperatures, naturally the various known antipyretics came into consideration. For the choice of the means, however, the solubility was decisive in the first place, so that various means were immediately excluded. Namely, it seemed to me preferable to inject the means rather than to feed it, especially since the injection brings about a faster and deeper temperature-lowering; but also because it is, even when one knows the full poison in this means, absolutely necessary for a comparative series of experiments to know what quantity of the antipyretic one brings into the body. Also, with feeding one is compelled to put or push the means into the animals' mouths, which is, however, more unpleasant to them and attacks them more than a subcutaneous injection. In addition, with feeding there exists the danger of provoking stomach disturbances in the animals.

Once I attempted feeding by way of trial, namely with antipyrine powders, each of which contained a mixture of 0.04 g antipyrine and 0.3 g milk-sugar. Although it succeeded for me only to push everything into the animals' mouth, the temperature sank, as Table IV shows, only 0.6° to 0.8°, and was again normal in approx. 4¼ hours. One may compare with this the temperature lowerings after injection of 1/8 mg antipyrine, that is, not even half the dose!

The injections were first carried out with quinine; it showed itself here, however, that harmful side-effects clouded the results. It was then experimented with antipyrine, which gave better results.

#### a) Quinine Experiments.

The quinine was injected subcutaneously in the form of a 2% solution of Chininum hydrochloricum, to which 0.75% NaCl was added. Injected were some animals of a litter, where possible some males and females, with 0.3 ccm of this solution, thus 6 mg quinine; the other animals of the litter served as control animals and were, with one exception, as later [described], always injected with a similar amount of physiological cooking-salt solution. The injections were presumably painless for the animals and were mostly borne quietly.

The body temperature sank rapidly down after the injection. Both in the 25°- and in the 30°-chamber the temperature was, a half-hour after the injection, sunk by several degrees, about which more below. Also in the control animals there were, from time to time, after the injection with physiological cooking-salt solution, small temperature lowerings, but never more than 0.4°. This was indeed a consequence of the normal fluctuations during the day and not, perhaps, a consequence of the cooking-salt injection, for in a control animal that received no injection at all (Table XIV), similar small fluctuations occurred.

It showed itself, unfortunately, that the animals did not always tolerate the consequences of the quinine injection well. That one could, some time after the quinine injection, tell apart the injected from the non-injected animals at first glance through their differing behavior, was indeed to be expected, but [it was a question] whether one could, with [animals] in such a condition, still expect so much growth result. Moreover, the animals were, after some injections, in most cases distinctly sick at the subcutaneous connective tissue at the sites of the injection (neck and back); in consequence, places appeared now and then on the skin, which the animals covered with [scabs], and there even came some wounds on the skin, so that I therefore sometimes also refrained from a second injection per day. After conclusion of the injections, however, the animals soon recovered again. It is clear that such phenomena had to influence the entire growth of the animals and cloud the answer to the question posed.

In total, six experimental series with the quinine injection were made. Three of these series were not completed, once because the injected animals died of an illness that at that time more often afflicted the young rats at the Biological Experimental Institute, and for which especially the treated animals seemed very sensitive; a second time from a cause that lay here in the 35°-chamber, about which more below. The experiments were made in the 35°-, 30°- and 25°-chambers. The results are given here separately.

##### 1. Quinine experiments in the 35° chamber.

The experiments in the 35° chamber proceeded quite especially unexpectedly. After I, on the first experimental day, had injected the animals as usual with 6 mg quinine, respectively 0.3 ccm physiological cooking-salt solution, I found after ¾ hour not the expected temperature lowering, but a temperature elevation of about 0.3° (Table V). As I ascribed it to an unexpected insensitivity, the dose was elevated on the next day to 9 mg quinine, respectively 0.6 ccm physiological cooking-salt [solution]. One hour after the injection I then found two of the treated animals dead, and the third dying, probably through hypersensitivity in the too-high temperature.

Whether I had perhaps committed an error on the first day was not for me to verify. Litters of young rats were, incidentally, seldom to be had in the 35°-chambers, so I have not been able to repeat the experiments any more. As also the antipyrine experiments in the 35° chamber gave bad results, similar [to those] not in the 35° chamber, it became probable that the animals in this high temperature find themselves in a state in which they are not suited for repeated injections.

##### 3. Quinine experiments in the 25° chamber.

The quinine experiments in the 25° chamber proceeded in principle similarly to those in the 30° chamber, so I may be brief about them.

In Table X one sees the temperature fluctuations after injection in a litter of 6 young animals of 24 days, of which one died during the experimental period from unknown causes, and one fell ill already from the beginning of the injections and had a much too low temperature. Of the four others, one male and one female were each injected; the others were used as control animals. Table XI gives the course of the temperature lowering: after 20 min. the temperature had fallen by about 2,5°, thus about as much as in the animals in the 30° chamber; after 5 hours the temperature was normal again. Temperature measurements on other animals of the same age, of which Table XIV gives the growth, yielded a similar lowering of 2,5° and recovery in about 5 hours. Otherwise it was with a litter of 7 somewhat older animals (47 days), of which Table XII shows the temperature fall; here the lowering was distinctly less; in the mean value of 4 experiment days a fall of only 1,5° was observed. Older animals are thus more resistant to quinine than younger ones, and since the growth rate also decreases at this age, there was double reason to carry out the experiments with the youngest possible animals. Fortunately I found in antipyrin a substance that better served the purpose.

#### b) Antipyrin experiments.

In the antipyrin experiments the precaution was taken, in each case to measure the lengths of fur and the temperature of the experimental animals before the start of the injections, whereby the growth and injection periods coincided and the expected differences, the leveling influence of the first undisturbed growth, were switched off.

Antipyrin was injected as 0,3 ccm of a 5% antipyrin solution, to which 0,75% NaCl was added. The experimental animals thus received 15 mg antipyrin, the control animals likewise 0,3 ccm physiological saline solution. Although one could here also distinguish the injected from the non-injected animals only with difficulty, it was nevertheless to be seen that antipyrin attacked them less strongly than quinine. Bare spots appeared much less and only later; the animals emaciated less and behaved more like the normal ones. It was thereby possible to continue the experiments longer and also to inject more often.

In all I had the opportunity to set up 7 experiment series with antipyrin. Of these, one was interrupted because the injected animals died after their temperature had unexpectedly sunk low for several days. Of the remaining 6 experiments there is mention below.

##### 1. Antipyrin experiments in the 35° chamber.

The experiments in the 35° chamber were here too not crowned with success. The body temperature did indeed sink low, to about 3° after one hour (Table XV), but probably because of the somewhat higher age of the experimental animals used (37 days at the first experiment day), whereby their growth was already reduced, the result was that of the three injected animals one grew only quite slightly and the two others even went back somewhat in body length. During the same time (from the 36th to the 42nd day of life) the control animals grew on average only 7,6%. In the first-named of the three experimental animals the tail still grew 3 cm, but the two others remained at equal length. Next to the greater age, the greater sensitivity at the higher temperature also probably brought these experiments to failure.

##### 4. Antipyrin experiments in the 15° chamber.

In the 15° chambers I had the opportunity to work with somewhat larger litters, namely once with 8 and once with 9 young. The temperature lowering was here again almost 3° C (Table XX) and lasted again about 6 hours, was thus not less than at 35° and 25°. The experiment series were undertaken on animals of 27 and 23 days. Both times the animals were injected in a growth period of 11 days, 10 times in 10 days.

In the younger animals (Table XXI) the average body increase was 19% in the injected and 21% in the non-injected, the tail increase 22% respectively 28%. ## III. Theoretical Part.

### A. Removal of an objection to the usability of the experiments.

Our experiments with the rearing of rats in various, constantly held temperatures had with certainty yielded a gradual dependence of the body temperature of these warm-blooded animals on the external temperatures.

In the experiments of Bierens de Haan, 3- (to 4-) week-old rats were subjected to injection of antipyrin for 9–11 days, hence at an average age of 31 days at the end of the experiments. At this age, in Jackson's control animals (according to his Table p. 107 and Abb. 2, S. 109), the ratio of tail to body was — 0.78; rats which had been kept at a constant body weight from the 3rd to the 6th week of life showed at the end of the experiment, hence at 42 days of age, the same ratio. Normally fed rats had at the same age a ratio of 0.88, so that the starving ones at the end of the 6th week in tail growth had lagged behind by at most 0.88 : 0.78 = 1.13. If we calculate from Jackson's table the percentage increase in body and tail length for his normally fed rats from the 3rd to the 6th week, then the body increased by 42.78%, the tail by 88.03%, whereas in the underfed ones in the same period the body increased by only 9.01%, the tail on the other hand by 27.95%. If we now form the quotients of the percentage increase of tail : body, then we obtain for the normal ones 2.058, for the underfed ones 3.102, i.e. the relative tail increase is by half more pronounced in the underfed ones! Calculated more exactly, the quotient from the relative percentage tail increases is 2.058 : 3.12 = 0.663.

In Bierens' experiments with antipyrin injections the analogous quotients are: at 25°: 0.94, 1.42; 20°: 1.10; 15°: 1.17, 1.21; average from all 1.17. The percentage shortening of the tail is thus greater after antipyrin injections than the percentage [shortening] under underfeeding. But if in both cases we compare the percentage growth, then we find that in the injection experiments it was precisely the injected animals that were the short-tailed ones as compared with the controls, the starved ones on the other hand the long-tailed ones. Here we must, in comparing the experiments of Jackson and Bierens, take into account, besides the growth-inhibiting factor — here injection, there underfeeding — yet other differences: whereas Jackson's rats were kept at constant weight and almost constant body length, the measurable difference in body length in the injected animals as compared with the normal animals of the same age was extremely small (in the experiments even reversed), and likewise the time during which the inhibiting factor could act was at most half as long in Bierens' experiments as in Jackson's. Since both circumstances would thus have to produce a much slighter inhibition of tail growth in the antipyrin-injected ones than in the starving ones, whereas in fact these latter are relatively shorter-tailed, we can now state with certainty that the general nutritional state could not come into consideration for the short-tailedness in the cold.

These circumstances also remove any misgivings against the direct causal connection between short-tailedness and temperature elevation, which Uhlenhuth had occasionally raised against our earlier collaboration. He held that the relative short-tailedness of the heat-rats might be connected with the diminished food intake of the animals at elevated outside temperature, and not depend directly upon a temperature factor. Now there are admittedly indications of a relative long-tailedness in rats exposed for a long time to hunger as compared with well-fed ones in Jackson's experiments, but his experiments do not speak in favor of [the view] that such [a long-tailedness] would appear in the heat-rats. Especially are in the heat-experiments the tail-elongations occurring even with the most extreme food deprivation by Jackson considerably greater, in that already the simple body-tail ratio, not first the percentage increase, unambiguously yields essentially different figures. In this it is to be noted that the heat-rats indeed took up so much food, as they wanted to take, and accordingly also lagged behind only slightly in total growth as compared with [those] grown at lower temperatures, but by no means as the underfed ones at equal weight or equal length remained. We may therefore, despite the failure of the fever-experiments¹, regard the direct connection between inner body temperature and tail growth as assured.

> ¹ Is the difficulty of producing fever in rats by chemical means perhaps connected with the slight constancy of the inner temperature? Poikilothermic animals can probably indeed not contract fever.

### B. Theoretical Utilization of the Experiments.

The direct connection between body temperature and tail length in the rat is of considerable theoretical interest in several respects. It signifies the exclusion of a "stimulus effect of the external temperature" for the formation of a long- or short-tailedness. Rather, with increasing external temperature degrees, the gradually increasing relative tail length must consequently be ascribed to a direct effect of the inner temperature, which likewise increases gradually with the external temperature, upon the tail growth. If we regard the body of the warm-blooded rat as "the environment of its germplasm," then this is influenced by the higher temperature of the external world only insofar as the inner temperature is raised; it appears extremely improbable that the germ cells should be influenced directly through the stimulus effect of the external factor, if the body cells themselves are not changed in their growth through the stimulus of the external factor, but rather through the inner temperature. If furthermore the tail length goes back directly to the change of the inner temperature, then the germ cell itself will indeed be accessible in the body to the higher temperature (compare, however, the independence of the gestation period and [oestrous] period from the temperature in Przibram: Environment of the germplasm VII) and may in the descendants show analogous changes as in the directly changed germ-cell generation, but the body cells appearing in the next generation will again depend directly upon the inner temperature influenced by the external temperature, and therefore presumably experience no continuation of the tail length altered in the parents upon being put back into the temperature of the grandparents. Series of experiments which, independently of the experiments of Bierens de Haan, were conducted by Przibram (1909, 1910, 1918) as well as Steinach and Kammerer (1920), confirmed the correctness of these two conclusions: whereas the hypertrophy of the male testis of the heat-rats accessible to the external temperature can persist in the sons even at normal temperature, the long-tailedness induced by temperature elevation is not transmissible. Contrast-phenomena, however, do occur, which warn against an all too simple interpretation. Besides the problem of the inheritance of acquired characters there arises secondly the question along which path and through which the somatogenic elevation of the body temperature can call forth the elongation of the tail. Since temperature elevation in general calls forth an acceleration of growth processes, this problem falls into two parts: 1. whether we are to ascribe the considerable changes of tail length obtained with the slight inner temperature differences to the velocity-quotients otherwise observed in biochemical processes, and 2. why precisely the tail and not the body parts proportional to it experience an acceleration of growth through temperature elevation? Thirdly, the differing length of the tail in males and females, with their differing inner temperature, is to be pursued further in comparison and this kind of linkage of the sex-characters [is to be pursued further]. In further works the answer to all these questions is given and the whole material on the rat-experiments laid out over several years presented.

## IV. Summary.

1. Three- to four-week-old albinotic brown rats (*Mus decumanus*) receive in the next 9–11 days of life a relative shortening of the tail, if the body temperature is lowered by injection of fever-producing agents.

2. This tail-shortening is the more considerable, the slighter the side-effect called forth by the injection [is] in its impairment of total growth, hence the more distinctly with antipyrin than with the poisonous quinine.

3. From this ground and according to the experiences of Jackson and Hatai gained on underfed rats, the tail-shortening cannot be traced back to a general growth-inhibition in consequence of unfavorable well-being.

4. The tail-shortening as compared with non-injected control animals is greater at low than at high outside temperature.

5. The shortening of the relative tail length always occurring upon lowering of the outside temperature is, according to the experiments with reduction of the body temperature by chemical means, to be traced back to the simultaneously occurring lowering of the inner temperature, not to a stimulus effect of the outside temperature.

6. Although it has so far not been possible to induce artificial fever in the rats, in order also to test whether, conversely, by elevation of the body temperature without elevation of the outside temperature a relative long-tailedness can be produced; nevertheless it is, according to the mentioned hunger-experiments, not doubtful that the tail-elongation brought about by elevation of the outside temperature cannot be ascribed to an insufficient food intake, but rather to the body warmth rising with the outside temperature, for the rats kept by extreme fasting at constant body weight show a far slighter relative tail-elongation than the ones fed *ad libitum* and grow up almost as well as the heat-rats growing up at normal outside temperatures.
