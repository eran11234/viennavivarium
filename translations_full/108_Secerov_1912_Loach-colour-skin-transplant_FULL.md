## I. On the Duration of the Darkness- and Blinding-Reaction.

As I have already reported in the first communication (Colour-Change Experiments on the Stone Loach, *Nemachilus barbatula* L.), the unblinded control animals, as well as the blinded stone loaches, become red-brown to dark-red-brown in darkness on any background, in a certain colour-relation. Now one might be of the opinion that this darkening of the stone loach in darkness, or also the darkening of the blinded animals, is only a transient state, and that one would need merely to let a longer period of time elapse until the reaction of lightening sets in, as is for example the case with the crayfish. Thus FRÖHLICH says of *Palaemon* in his summary: »1) After bilateral blinding there appears first in *Palaemon* the red »night«-colouration resting on the expansion of the chromatophores; 2) this passed in the course of a few weeks into a chalk-white or at least light-yellow colouration.« I have therefore pursued experiments in this direction further, and have kept blinded and unblinded control animals alive for almost 2 years. The experiments began on 9.X.1908; the blinded ones lived until mid-August (17.VIII.1910), thus more than 22 months, then they perished in my absence; the control animals, the unblinded stone loaches, were alive until 8.X.1910, then perished as a result of an accident.

As regards the colour-state, on the back it was dark-red-brown, on the upper side-half brown, on the lower side-half reddish, on the belly light-green, and in the direction of the ventral fins weakly pigmented, the fins are more or less reddishly pigmented; thus the description corresponds in the main with the colour-state that has been given in Table XII under f.b. in »Colour-Change Experiments on the Stone Loach«, except that it is somewhat darker.

The control animals were in general lighter; on the belly they are not pigmented; the fins are reddish, the back brown, on the upper side-half one still sees light stripes and parts between the general brown colour; such parts, on the other hand, one sees in the blinded animals only on the lower side-half.

It is therefore the relation, which is presented between the colour-states f. and f.b., as it has been set out in Table XII, still always perceptible, and the proportion l.:f.=f.b.:l.b. one can therefore still always accept as valid.

One can therefore with certainty draw from these experiments the conclusion that the colour-state of the blinded as well as the unblinded stone loaches is a permanent one. One can therefore at least for the elapsed experimental period maintain this, because no trace of a lightening was perceptible.

There is therefore a certain contrast in the reaction-mode of the colours between the stone loach and *Palaemon*, or, generally speaking, between the fishes and the crayfishes.

## II. On the Coloured Adaptation of the Blinded Stone Loach.

I already wanted earlier to bring about an experimental colour-alteration of the blinded animals; but the experiments were not clear and convincing enough. Thus I said on p. 643 verbatim: »I originally held the view that the blinded animals could react to the white background and become light, and that the lightening is not a reflex process triggered through the eyes, all the more so since F. GAMBLE and FR. KEEBLE have also made the statements that blinded animals react in the opposite direction to normal, etc.«; whereas on p. 644: »One sees further that the bilaterally blinded animal does not react on the white background, while the other animal, which from the beginning was in the light, reacts«.

On account of this ambiguous result I have busied myself further with the question of the colour-alteration of the blinded animals; only with the difference that in the experiments I used not only the white but also the orange-coloured background.

I have set up two experimental series; in each experimental series I have kept blinded animals on white and orange-coloured background, and besides this, in the second experimental series, I also used black paper as background. In all cases the glass tanks were surrounded with black, white or orange-coloured paper.

The animals were, after the blinding, kept on white background and then distributed according to the above scheme. In the first experimental series I kept the animals (10 pieces) for only a short time (from 27.X.1909 to 13.XI.1909) on the white background; in the second experimental series, on the other hand, I operated on 15.XII.1909 (6 pieces) and on 25.III.1910 placed them into coloured glass tanks.

This difference in time had also produced a difference in the experimental results.

In the first experimental series, the animals kept on the orange-coloured background showed a reaction. Thus one could already notice on 16.XII.1909 a difference between the animals kept on white background (l.) and those kept on orange-coloured (o.) background: the o.-animals were coloured orange to dirty-brown on the back, likewise on the upper side-half, light-orange on the lower side-half, on all fins one could notice an orange tone. This colour-tone was still noticeable on 25.III.1910, however significantly weaker, more indistinct, and becoming ever more brown; on 19.V.1910 these tones had disappeared and the animals were thus more of a brown to red-brown colouration. The animals kept on white background, on the other hand, showed at the same time the following colour-state: back dark-green, upper side-half dark-green, lower side-half green, dorsal and pectoral [fin], caudal fin green, ventral and anal fin reddish; on 19.V.1910 they were dark-brown to black with the usual colour-state resulting therefrom.

The animals of the second experimental series showed no such perceptible difference; they were at first yellow-brown-green-brown on the back, green-yellow on the upper side-half, light-green-yellowish on the lower side-half, light-green on the belly, yellowish-reddish on the dorsal, pectoral and caudal fin, and coloured yellowish on the ventral and anal fin. There were thus two differently coloured types present. This colour-state was [present] on 15.XII.1909 before the beginning of the operation; on 25.III.1910 the animals were, before the distribution into the differently coloured glass tanks, in general red-brown on the back, red-brown-reddish on the upper and lower side-half, light-green on the belly, red-brown-reddish on the dorsal, pectoral and caudal fin, coloured reddish on the ventral and anal fin. After this colour-state, no change occurred until June 1910, when the experiment was broken off. The colour-state became at most somewhat darker.

From these experiments it therefore likewise follows:

1) the blinded stone loaches show a colour-difference, i.e. they react only in the case when one places them, after the blinding, in good time, i.e. not a long time after the blinding, on the coloured background;

2) if one takes this fact into account, it follows further that this colour-alteration is a physiological process, and that the adaptation follows probably through the mediation of the optic nerve. This assumption would also explain the non-adaptation of the animals placed [in the tank] too late, because later the eye, actually the eye-socket, becomes overgrown with the skin and the access of light to the nerve is thereby prevented. A lightening of the bilaterally blinded trout was established by K. v. FRISCH (p. 25); likewise BABÁK reported a colour-change reaction in blinded *Amblystoma*.

## III. Skin-Transplantation Experiments.

The results of the skin-transplantation experiments with regard to colour- and pigmentation-relations one can divide into three groups: 1) There takes place an overlapping of the pigmentation from the transplanted little piece onto the neighbouring tissue (after L. LOEB and CARNOT-DEFLANDRE); 2) the pigmentation overlaps from the neighbouring tissue onto the transplanted little piece (LOEB, KARG) and 3) there occurs no change in the pigmentation-relations (WINKLER).

I have carried out four different transplantations: 1) from the back onto the belly-side, 2) from the belly onto the back-side, 3) from the blinded onto the normal unblinded stone loach, and 4) from the normal unblinded onto the blinded. In the two last cases the skin was taken from the back and transplanted onto the back. The observations are macroscopic. The transplantation of the back-skin onto the belly-side never succeeded for me; the animals perish on the 1st–2nd day, probably as a result of some infection. I have tried it three times (28.X.1909, 8.XI.1909, 25.XI.1909), but always without success, even though larger numbers of animals were operated on.

In the second case the operation succeeded; the belly-skin held itself on the back. The transplantation was usually carried out in such a way that, on the back, the skin was cut out in a quadrangular shape with a scalpel and the edges were raised up; the transplant is then simply inserted (pocket-method). The transplant undergoes a change; the belly-side is, as is well known, not black pigmented; the transplant, by contrast, becomes black pigmented, but one still sees, even after 5 months, the difference between the transplant and the rest of the skin. The transplant is lighter than the normal skin. The black pigmentation of the unpigmented transplant probably stems from the neighbouring tissue, and this would agree with KARG's experiments.

In the third and fourth case the transplantation again succeeds; the transplant does not change its colour, so that one can still, after about 2 months, notice the difference between the dark, almost black skin-piece originating from the blinded animal and the green, more weakly black-pigmented surrounding skin (Fig. 1 f shows the transplant), and likewise vice versa.

This would therefore agree with the results of WINKLER, although not entirely, for the transplant shows later a certain change, indeed more in the sense of a migration of the pigment from the neighbouring tissue onto the transplant. Thus one could perceive, in an unblinded stone loach with the transplanted skin from the blinded stone loach, the following: after 10 months after the operation (operated 21.X.1909, conserved 21.VIII.1910) the transplant is still always sharply delimited against the surrounding skin (Fig. 2 t); the transplant is in the middle still always of black colouration, as it originally was, whereas the edges are light, or one can say, almost white. When one examines this stage with the magnifying glass (Fig. 3), one sees that in the middle of the transplant there are still two larger accumulations of the black pigment (t) present; they appear as if they were covered by a skin, and one sees individual smaller accumulations of the pigment, which, by its more intense black colour, is still recognizable, and which directly make the impression — judging by the paleness of the black colour — that they are covered with skin. From the edges of the surrounding skin, individual portions of pigment extend into the white portion of the original transplant. One can, after all this, conclude the following: the transplant is gradually resorbed, and displaced through regeneration of the surrounding skin; the pigment-extension appears slowly, it takes its origin through immigration from the neighbouring tissue.

## IV. Summary.

1) The unblinded as well as the blinded stone loaches retained their dark colour-state continuously in darkness during 22–24 months.

2) If one places blinded stone loaches on the coloured background a short time after the blinding, they still show a certain colour-adaptation; those transferred later, however, no longer show this reaction.

3) The skin-piece transplanted from the belly onto the back becomes black pigmented (immigration from the edges), but always remains lighter than the surrounding skin. The transplants from the unblinded onto the blinded stone loaches, and vice versa, show no change for a long time, but later, especially in the unblinded stone loaches with the transplant from the blinded ones, [there is] a change in the sense of the immigration of the black pigment from the neighbouring tissue and resorption of the transplant.
