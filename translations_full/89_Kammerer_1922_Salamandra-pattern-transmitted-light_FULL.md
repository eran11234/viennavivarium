## I. Statement of the Problem.

In order to learn whether incident coloured light influences the colour-dress pattern at all, and in the same sense as reflected light does, I conducted several experiments on the black, yellow-spotted fire salamander (*Salamandra maculosa*, Laur.) [*Salamandra salamandra*], intended to supplement my earlier experiments (in particular Kammerer 1913), in which the salamanders had been kept on various coloured grounds.

In the experiments now to be described, they had to be reared under variously coloured glasses: in this form

the experiments speak — apart from the solution of the question just named — also as a further contribution to the solution of the problem, whether it is the graduated degree of brightness that produces the colour effect of the animal, or the colour quality of the surroundings that accomplishes this.

This latter question was indeed already answered in my previous treatise concerning salamander coloration (Kammerer 1913) in the sense that for the salamander pattern the specific colour effect, the colour quality, presupposes a certain light intensity: for both the lightening and the darkening factor proceeded better on yellow and on black grounds, whereas red, green, blue grounds lagged behind in their effect on the skin of the salamanders. But now I wanted to find out, under simpler conditions, the effectiveness of the degree of brightness: namely, whether the coloured layers, which the animal body reaches when it is penetrated [by the light], instead of a coloured surface upon which the light is reflected, become operative; for in such penetrations the light must lose somewhat of its intensity, whereas the light intensity in control experiments under colourless glasses remained undiminished: it was thus the intensity, and not the colour quality of the light which governs the colour-changes, that should here be more strongly affected in the control cultures than in the actual experimental cultures.

A third question concerned the experiments of Pogonowska, of rearing salamander larvae in common-salt (sodium chloride) solutions, which possess the property of suppressing the yellow pigment in favour of the black almost completely by chemical means: I wanted to test the effectiveness of this chemical factor and at the same time let it enter into competition with the colour factor, letting the common salt act in combination with the coloured surroundings.

The use of larval material, in which the permanent pigments of the full-grown animal are only first being formed, was finally also to offer the opportunity of pursuing the connection between physiological and morphological colour change. To be sure, my conjecture announced in this regard (1913, pp. 135—146) has in the meantime been confirmed by v. Frisch (1911 on the minnow, *Leuciscus phoxinus*), by Babák (1913 on the axolotl, *Amblystoma*) and by Herbst (1919 on the salamander itself); but here too the effect of incident light was to be supplemented by that of correspondingly transmitted [light], as well as set upon a more exact basis, at least in the salamander, by methodical cell counting.

## II. Experimental Arrangement.

As colour-dispensers I used Senebier bells: double-walled flasks, the inner space of which is filled with coloured liquid, so that the experimental animal looked out through coloured liquid over the experimental ground. As coloured filling of the flasks I used: for the yellow-coloured ones, picric acid and potassium bichromate; for any green ones, copper-oxide-ammonia. Picric acid yields a pale greenish-yellow (citron-yellow); potassium bichromate a saturated yellow (orange-yellow); copper-oxide-ammonia gives a light blue, in its mixture with potassium bichromate only a dark-violet solution.

As controls of the Senebier bells, those filled with pure water served. Such ones should, in several specimens, have been set up next to one another (in the brightest places of the experimental space). Since the available control bells did not suffice for this, some of the dampened-down [in brightness] cultures were set up as simple glass bells, beneath which the living conditions with respect to space, air composition and light reception probably were not essentially the same as under the double-walled, liquid-filled flask space.

Besides, cultures were reared under glass in the dark-chamber with exclusion of light, as well as one open (uncovered) culture each here and in full light (next to the colour-cultures).

Compared with the experiments conducted in terraria with reflected light, the experiments sheltered under coloured bells offered the disadvantage that they can hold relatively few experimental animals at one time. For the purchasable bells have only one diameter — some of 15 cm, some of 23 cm; of the former, four stood, of the latter, six in use. An increase of their stock was prohibited not only by the financial setbacks of the institute in its straitened circumstances, but also because the so-named glassware was completely sold out at the local firm. Moreover, the glasses, where the parallel (belonging-together experiment- and control-) cultures were to be treated, must always be used either of the larger or of the smaller size, otherwise the size-relations exert their influence as well, even when the living conditions in both kinds were unequal, in that for the smaller sort the air-consumption is quicker and especially the supply [of food] more sparing, and so must turn out worse.

In order nevertheless to obtain reliable results, I resorted to the following information-aids:

1. The number of cases seen could, through repetitions of the experiment — thus through renunciation of the juxtaposition of one large series of cultures, through the succession of several smaller experiments — be increased.

2. The comparability could be improved through use of contemporaneous siblings (in at least some cases), at the very least of mothers of known kinship.

3. The quantity of the experimental animals could be enlarged somewhat through choice of small specimens. Chiefly on this ground I first availed myself of the method tried out by Secerov, of preparing the production of the definitive colour-dress already on the larvae: i.e. not only then (as in my earlier experiments) on the full salamander, but on the salamander-larva from birth onward to expose [it] to the coloured influences.

Since the process which Secerov had established, and which has since been established likewise by v. Frisch, Herbst and Przibram, runs in complete agreement with that process which I had perceived in already-transformed salamanders, I hold myself entitled not to see, in the larval and the imaginal process, two fundamentally different sequences; but rather to see therein, in one and the same sequence, this run through, which is only furthered — by its earlier beginning at the larva and as we shall see — by accelerated transition into this more plastic youthful stage, also essentially according to its scope.

The larvae were held in round glass vessels, fitted as far as possible into the free space under the glass bells. The glass vessels were either filled with clear brook-water up to the same height, or else left empty, and were carefully kept clean; especially had the cleaning of algal-cloudings of the water and algal-coatings of the wall to occur daily in the warmer, brighter season. As food-animals served brook-tube-worms (*Tubifex tubifex*), which are most readily taken. To be sure it does happen that killing germs are introduced through this food into the gut of the salamander-larvae; but no living food is free from infection-danger, and *Tubifex* is in any case more nutritious than the small water-crustaceans (*Daphnia, Cyclops, Cypris*), which are harder to procure for the larvae and are besides

## III. Course of the Experiment.

### 1. Experiment.

In May 1914 I set up three large coloured Senebier bells outdoors (in the garden of the Biological Experimental Institute): an orange-yellow, a dark-violet and a white (colourless) bell.

Under each bell came a round glass dish of 14 cm diameter, 6 cm height (4 cm water-level), the glass dishes being stocked with six newborn salamander-larvae each (siblings of the same brood of forma *typica* as in all the experiments discussed here). So that lateral entry of daylight under the bell on the uneven earth-ground might be avoided, I let sand be heaped up first at the chosen place: this sand-layer then surrounded the lower glass-rim on all sides and let no gaps arise between it and the ground. So that under the free sky strong insolation and overheating, which must have become deadly for salamander-larvae, did not enter, I chose for the set-up place the shadow of an old weeping-willow.

At the time of transformation-ripeness, four animals in the yellow bell, five in the violet, three in the colourless had remained alive. They show clear differences in their spot-size: the larvae out of the dark-violet possessed few, small flecks; the larvae out of the yellow bell, large yellow flecks. The larvae out of the colourless stood in the middle between these and those.

I saw myself compelled to refrain from registering and depicting the result of this first experiment more exactly: for an error-source had crept in, delivered by a gold-green alga, which despite frequent water-changes daily arose anew in considerable amount, just as also quite disturbing green-algae appeared among the other cultures, especially the colourless control-culture. Thereby the larvae of the yellow-culture — apart from the yellow glass under which they lived — were [exposed to] yellow surroundings; and I would not have undertaken its appreciable water-greening, were there not the possibility that not the through-flowing yellow light of the glass-bell, but rather the reflected [light], caused the colour-development of the salamander-larvae. Indeed it is probable that the organic colourings — of the animal-body and of the vegetation — the yellow-colouring of the incident light to a common samelike cause had; that thus not merely the salamander-larvae, but also the alga-cells depended upon those [conditions], or rather that in the case of the latter the yellow light had favoured a yellow alga-species in its appearing.

Yet there remains that other possibility — participation of the yellow alga, in its self-coloration as yellow ground of the surroundings, in the embossing of the salamander-dress — to exist; and I would have undertaken this culture also in the present report, had the following experiments not confirmed it. They were not set up again in the garden, but in the brightest skylight-rooms of the institution-building, where indeed not in such degree, but still — given the unequal lighting-conditions produced there — an alga-plague was to be fought as under the free sky.

### 2. Experiment.

An isolated Vienna-woods-female (forma *typica*, to which the whole experimental material belongs, Fig. 1) bore in the night from the 23rd to the 24th December 1918 sixteen larvae. Of these, six were reared under a light (citron-)yellow bell, six under the dark-violet, four under a colourless bell, whither they had been brought early in the morning of the 24th of December.

As set-up-space served the device-space of the warm green-house, where a temperature of 20—23° C reigned. The green-house receives the natural forenoon-sun and bears, naturally, a glass roof, which in winter — given otherwise undiminished brightness — is all the more necessary, the less the experiments at the slighter light-intensity succeed so well at length as at high [intensity]. And direct sun-rays were in no way to be feared at that season; the more were the light-strengthenings welcome, to give the result a more clear-cut expression, which otherwise in winter readily fails.

Already on 30 December the larvae under the yellow bell (Pl. II, Fig. 1) were somewhat lighter coloured than those under the dark-violet bell (Pl. II, Fig. 4), since their black-violet toning was no uniform-colour [uniform tone], but rather arose from cloudy, though many-times confluent melanin-accumulations. The larvae under the colourless bell (Pl. II, Fig. 3) showed no uniform colouring, were partly lighter, partly darker, none of them however so distinctly toned as those under the yellow bell.

Through the sun on 30 December dismal [i.e. clouded], there occurred, on the part of the institution-gardeners, an irregularity in the taking-down of the shade-covers — removed [only] after three days — which in winter are otherwise spread over the green-house windows only at night, in order to protect the interior from frost. These straw-covers now remained, instead of being rolled up early in the morning, let down also during the day, whereby the light-intensity in the same period was a lesser one. To what extent this episode impaired the end-result remains undecided.

It is certain however that also this time the (five surviving) larvae out of the yellow bell entered the transformation with large, confluent flecks, the (four surviving) sibling-larvae out of the dark-violet bell with few, far smaller flecks. Both cultures thereby departed decisively from the mean of the variation-range valid for the forma *typica*. By contrast the (four) freshly-transformed animals reared under colourless glass made an entirely normal impression; although transgressive variability of one of the animals approached the Dark-experiment and a second let this measure of the won Dark-colouring be fully reached, the Yellow-experiment attained not a single measure even of the Yellow-colouring produced by it in somewhat similar degree.

### 3. Experiment.

On 8 January 1919, out of another salamander-female caught in the Vienna-wood, 23 larvae were born. Of these, six came at once under an orange-bell, six under the dark-violet bell, six into the dark-chamber, five were left standing in an open glass-dish in the green-house next to the light-cultures.

For accuracy's-sake it must be noted that the culture in the dark-chamber stood essentially cooler than the cultures of the green-house: the conditions are thus here and there not strictly the same. The larvae of the dark-culture were scarcely half-grown at a time when the others were already entering into their metamorphosis, and could therefore not keep pace with these simultaneously in growth and in development. For the retardation at the dark-chamber-experiments bears, in my experience, not merely the lower temperature the blame, but just as much the exclusion of the light, and indeed not directly, but only or chiefly indirectly through a lowering of the nutritional-state, because the animals in the dark, on the one hand, cannot find their food so easily, on the other hand are also less voracious, as I had already stated it in 1913 and as Herbst (p. 35) confirms it. With the larvae there reigns indeed not, as in many of the full animals held in the dark, complete refusal of food; but they eat decidedly less than the cultures under the free sky.

Through yet a further circumstance the usefulness of the darkness culture as such was restricted. Through the oversight of an employee, the door of the dark chamber remained open on several occasions; the salamander experiments situated therein were thus temporarily weakly illuminated and may therefore not be regarded as pure experiments with exclusion of light.

In their appearance, the four survivors in the dark chamber, at their eventual metamorphosis (end of August 1919), with their medium spot-size and -number, approached a neutrally coloured and illuminated control culture. This admittedly does not signify much, since darkness cultures are in general very similar to normal cultures: what I had established in this regard in 1913 in experiments which had been begun with transformed animals was now confirmed in such as had been exposed to darkness almost from birth as larvae. Yet the latter yield somewhat darker, i.e. less yellow-marked, animals than in neutral coloration and illumination, without however even approximately attaining the strongly illuminated animals kept on a black substrate with respect to the predominance of the black ground-colour.

For the rest, there is nothing new to report concerning the result of the 3rd experiment: it confirmed the two previous experiments. The yellowest animals here had grown up under the orange bell (Plate II, Fig. 2), of which five — the darkest — came to development under the violet bell, which one and all attained transformation-ripeness in the first half of May. The latter are on average darker than normal ones, also still darker than the — see the above qualification — specimens kept in darkness, but do not, with respect to blackness, attain the animals kept in my earlier experiments and in those of other authors under great brightness on black substrates.

From each culture one animal, when it had arrived near transformation and already permitted the lasting distribution of spots to be judged (notwithstanding the changes which can still take place during and after the metamorphosis, at least as regards style and rough extent), was killed by decapitation and used for microscopic investigation, on which there is to be reported in a special section.

Still further supported by the more advanced season as compared with the previous experiment, the development and increase in size here proceeded extraordinarily rapidly. The state of growth and coloration already permitted, by mid-April, the inference of half-grown larvae, and by mid-May of transformation-ripe larvae. Yet the metamorphosis of the survivors then set in — only slightly anticipating the normal age for it — only after the beginning of June.

On 14 April, 8 May, 14 May, 21 May, one larva each (thus four in all, in as many stages) was killed for the purpose of investigating skin preparations. Thereafter, since the missing ones had died off, there remained no specimen in the blue and colourless bells, one specimen in the dark chamber, and two each in the bright- and orange-yellow bells. All these were left in their culture vessels until after completed metamorphosis, then likewise preserved for the microscopic skin examination.

In the present place only the macroscopic findings shall first be noted. Here one must distinguish how the larvae looked at the time of their fixation for the histological purpose, and how later the survivors, ultimately likewise fixed full animals, looked.

The appearance of the larvae under the bright-yellow bell at first departed far from that of all other larvae — a difference which, as we shall see, did not, with respect to the dark-yellow bell, persist to such a degree beyond the transformation. As early as 14 April, namely, under the bright-yellow bell the larvae, "half-grown" at that date as stated, displayed whitish- to pale-greenish-yellow spots of sharp delimitation and of such extent that the greatest part of the body surface was taken up by them. The districts still spared in between were indeed not deep-dark coloured, but only grey-green, yet through the absence of the yellow tone nonetheless, as emphasized, sharply set off from the confluent yellow districts. Through this advanced state of spotting one was tempted to expect an unusually early onset of the transformation; an expectation which — as already emphasized — by no means fulfilled itself in the supposed degree in the two survivors. Hence rather the thought suggests itself that, independently of the otherwise prevailing state of development of the animals and of their transformation-ripeness, a premature expression of the colour-dress, especially of its yellow areas, may have taken place.

And that some of these yellow spots, especially on the middle of the back, showed a tinge toward greenish, could (although it occasionally also occurs in control contours, even if hardly to the same striking degree) rest upon the fact that not only the general environmental ground-tone (yellow) was imitated by the experimental larvae in this case, but also its particular degree of saturation and admixture (bright green-yellow). For in this the larva indisputably accomplishes more than the full animal, in which at least I could always influence only the marking-style (spots or stripes: many small or few large spots, and the like) and the marking-extent (little or much yellow, or black respectively), but not constantly the intensity of the yellow marking on the colour-specific grounds.

Very bright and yellowish (but not greenish, rather bright amber-coloured) the larvae under the orange-yellow bell presented themselves on 14 April, still more on 8 May and the following dates. Yet in them the sharp delimitation of the spots was lacking, in favour of a more cloudy-blurred passing-over of the yellow into the darker brownish-grey districts, which at their eaten-away edges were thus yellowish-grey. Only on 21 May was that definiteness of outline of the spots — which, moreover, on average took up somewhat less room than under the greenish-yellow bell — established also under the orange-yellow bell.

Under the blue bell the larvae on 14 April looked deep-dark, blackish; on 8 May lighter, in the sequel passing more and more toward the normal culture, yet in extreme variants always still remaining darker than this.

The normal culture itself, finally, was on 14 April likewise quite dark, but brownish, and remained so until before the metamorphosis the yellow spots emerged. — The darkness culture yields at first black-brown, then (from 14 May) wholly light-grey larvae with translucent, colourless-appearing places, but not in the place of later spots. As the microscopic investigation confirms, the chromatophores, despite initial expansion, have later partly passed over into contraction and have been locally displaced, destroyed. Incidentally, the stages of the darkness culture are also in the presently discussed experiment comparable with those of the light cultures only as regards age, but not as regards development and consequently also not as regards colour-development, because in both respects badly retarded.

The transformed animals finally offer again the picture familiar to us of a spreading of the yellow within yellow, whereby the difference between bright- and orange-yellow has now become a fluctuating one; and of a restriction of the yellow in darkness, yet not to such an extent that the sole surviving full animal would be far removed from a normal animal less richly provided in this respect and from its own mother-animal. In the blue and colourless bells of the experiment under discussion, reserved chiefly for microscopic examination, no longer were any fully transformed animals available for the macroscopic comparison. If one takes into account their nearly transformation-ripe coloration-state at the conservation on 21 May, one might hold the blue, the colourless, and the darkness-kept culture to be approximately identical, with a decided predominance of dark pigment in the greatest part of the dark-blue-kept culture. The counting of the chromatophores under the microscope does however then after all still arrive at the establishment of differences which do not reveal themselves to the unarmed eye.

## 5. Experiment.

A salamander female (Fig. 2) caught by Herr Finkler at the beginning of April 1919 near the Knödelhütte at Hütteldorf (Vienna), and a second one, which had already been at the institute since 1918 but had likewise been caught in the Vienna Woods (Fig. 3), furnished the larval material which on 7 May 1919 served, according to the following arrangement, for the new setting-up of the experiment. The freshly caught female gave birth on 28 and 29 April, the long-caught one, which had over-wintered in captivity, from 1 to 5 May. Since the former, with the 17 larvae born by it, had not fully covered the requirement of the experiment, this time planned on a larger scale, the giving-birth of the second, visibly highly gravid female was awaited. The maternal animals are, as one sees, very similar in their coloration-type, indeed marking-style; their larvae had been obtained separately, i.e. the females had been kept apart until after completed birth. That then, because otherwise the material would not have sufficed, in three cultures the young of both females were mixed, may not weigh very heavily, with regard to the similarity of the mothers as well as with regard to the fact that the larvae, despite the slight difference in age, already showed distinct differences in size and could with certainty be recognized in the mixed cultures for many weeks as siblings or non-siblings respectively. Before the growth-intervals had wholly become blurred, however, the coloration-state, as it presented itself up to the end of the observation, had already to some extent emerged.

I resolved to strike "two flies with one blow" and from 7 June filled the designated vessels with 0.22% common-salt solutions, which promised to kill the mould-turf, hence to rescue the remaining stocks of the animals, and at the same time to re-examine the interesting experiment of Pogonowska, according to which salamanders kept in sodium-chloride solutions lose almost their whole yellow marking in favour of the sole advancing of the black ground-colour.

In culture No. 7 and 9 the blackening action of the salt could moreover enter into competition with the yellowing action of the yellow bell-colour; in No. 6 the darkening action of the dark-violet bell could still support that of the salt solution.

According to this, then, the way in which the salamanders of the 10th experimental glass, transformed at the beginning of September, coloured out, one may well say that the experiment confirms the statements of Pogonowska. The three full salamanders that remained over — after the fortunate extermination of the Saprolegniaceae — belonged to the blackest that I have ever seen in a freshly transformed state, and only a few that had grown up under blackening conditions from my older experimental rearings still surpassed them. In comparison with the photographs of Pogonowska, the degree of blackening attained in my re-examination experiment must indeed be designated as a weaker one, for there are still always more little yellow spots present than in the animals of Pogonowska; but on the other hand that degree of blackening is wholly equal to that of a culture on black ground, and since in the present case it was a matter of a normal culture on a brownish-grey paper substrate, it cannot be doubted that the blackening, wholly unaccustomed for such a setting-up, especially still in comparison with the siblings housed in colourless bells 1–4 and much more richly yellow-spotted, must be set purely to the account of the salt-content.

Already Przibram (1914, p. 52, footnote) had drawn attention to the fact that this result of Pogonowska, now rediscovered by me, is of significance for the interpretation of the uni-coloured black pigment-covering in the *Salamandra atra*, which probably descends from *Salamandra maculosa* or from a common ancestral form. Here the larva, namely, spends its whole post-embryonic development up to the transformation as a foetus in the maternal uterus. And in that *Salamandra maculosa*-descendency whose mothers I had compelled, by withdrawal of the birth-water basin, likewise to retain their young in the uterus, there resulted an extensive displacement of their yellow spot-marking by melanin-depositions, without application of other melanin-increasing factors! Since now the lymphoid fluid, in which the intrauterine larvae — in *Atra* normally; in *Maculosa* in the experiment aiming at full-newt-bearing — lie and breathe, is, like all body fluids, sodium-chloride-containing, there appears herewith given at least the one factor which, in the species-change of the fire-salamander or of the common ancestral form into the moor-salamander, made the yellow marking of the former disappear and let it be filled up by the black ground-colour now attaining sole dominance.

Similar to the control vessel No. 10 looked also the salamanders which survived the transformation in the dark-violet bell No. 6. The yellow marking had in none entirely or down to quite minimal remnants vanished, but in all had been preserved in nevertheless considerable elements. Yet the animals were blacker than such from earlier experiments described here, which remained exposed only to the influence of the dark-violet bell and not also to that of the salt solution; they were, understandably, further blacker than the animals of the experiment now described, which remained exposed only to the influence of the light-blue bell. But they were not blacker than those in the salt-containing, colourless glass: hence their blackening, however high-grade it proved to be, could not be the cumulative result of a blackening through the gloomy bell-colour, increased by that of the salt-content. Even should both factors have a share in the result, which goes up to the limit of the individually attainable, or at least the share of the one not be separable from that of the other in our case: they had even united not been in a position to overstep that limit of marking-displacement and to get beyond that degree of yellow-disappearance which the salt solution alone in the open glass No. 10 had brought about.

Very noteworthy was the result in bells 7 and 9: here, namely, the salt solution had to enter into competition with the darker or lighter yellow-coloration of the incident light, thus the blackening factor with the yellow-colouring one, the chemical with the colour-radiating factor. The contest was from the outset wholly decided in favour of the latter, the retarding one. And indeed not decided in compromise-form, so that the animals at first or at last held the middle between maximally black and maximally yellow-coloured, or so that in temporal transition a colour-change would have taken place, a contest ending in favour of the yellow coloration between black and yellow pigment. Only one morphological difference still exists between the many-yeared result of 1913, several salamander-generations old, and the four-month-old result of 1919, which concerns only one generation and even this only at its beginning: those animals (1913) had — although likewise descended from the irregularly speckled forma *typica* — since F₁ assumed the coloration-style of the regularly striped forma *taeniata*; these animals, however (1919), belong — despite the fact that they possess a wholly yellow back — evidently still always to *typica*. The diagnosis is given by the irregular delimitation of the large yellow dorsal zone, so that, notwithstanding its expansion approaching yellow uni-colouredness, no bilaterality comes about.

If it is permitted, on the basis of my earlier experiences and of the newest ones of Herbst, to predict how the salamanders transformed in 1919, just described, would have shaped themselves further had they been left alive, then I should prophesy that their transformation into forma *taeniata* — but in them reliably — would have set in; also then, if the natural growth of the colour-pattern had permitted the black pigment, before me, still some progressions, as e.g. some melanin-islands left over under the dorsal surface should have been admitted.

Thus there confirms itself the experience — gained also from other experimental investigations on variation and heredity (Papanicolaus on *Simocephalus vetulus* and *Moina rectirostris*, Steinach's and Kammerer's on *Mus decumanus*) — that for the progress of a variation it depends not in the first and proper line on the number of the influenced generations, but on the duration or on the strength of the influencing. The latter comes into question for our case: the favour of the summer of 1919 — sustained fine, sunny weather, and withal relatively so cool that direct insolation could bring the animals no harm — dispensed a fullness of light which, with the help of the larva-induction method, which in any case works incomparably more rapidly, had attained on the same specimen in a few months that for which otherwise many years and several generations are necessary.

There still remains something to be said of the remaining experimental glasses (1—4, 5 and 8, 11 and 12). Of the orange experiment without salt solution (8), that it was equivalent, with its inmates surviving in full number, to the orange experiment in salt solution (7), and that both, in the degree of their yellow-coloration, lag indeed somewhat behind experiment 9 (lemon-yellow bell) (Plate II, Fig. 6): but none of the normal cultures set up in manifold graduated degrees of brightness (1—4, 10—12; Fig. 7) approaches — not even at the highest light-intensity (4) — those yellow cultures, including the orange cultures, even approximately. The latter are moreover connected with one another by transgressive variability (the orange-yellow with the lemon-yellow): if the orange culture, on average and in its minimal cases of yellow-spreading, lags a little behind the lemon-yellow culture, this has to do, to be sure, with the degree of brightness; namely with the fact that the light-strength (as Dembowski measured — see the following work of Przibram) under the orange-yellow bell, in consequence of the lesser light-permeability and filter-quality of the potassium bichromate, is less than under the lemon-yellow bell, hence less favourable for the colour-specific process, which however requires a higher light-strength as its realizing factor. In both yellow bells, however, the light-intensity is of course less than under the colourless bells receiving the full overhead light and the free-standing culture covered by no bell: if now, despite this, nowhere here did salamanders so yellow appear as under the yellow bells, then the extraordinary yellow-coloration of the animals there can only be a colour-qualitative action of the yellow light in which they live.

The light-blue and the dark culture are, with respect to distribution of ground-colour and marking, not constantly to be distinguished from normal cultures. The blue culture (Plate II, Fig. 8) may unhesitatingly be regarded as equal to a normal culture, as I already stated in 1913 of blue substrates and as Herbst confirms in a single experiment, without indeed wishing to enter further into this result, inconvenient to him on more than one ground (p. 16). The dark culture shows again — after deep darkening of the animals in the first weeks of larval life — a subsequent bleaching and, before the transformation, a certain, moreover not high-grade, gappiness of the pigment-stock with half-translucent places.

Also in the 5th experiment just described, from the main experimental series (bright-yellow, orange-yellow, blue, dark-violet, colourless) one larva each was preserved, after the becoming-visible of its definitive colour-dress, and supplied to the histological working-up.

Against the obtaining of the result on larvae, which indeed stand just before their metamorphosis but do not yet have it behind them, reservations could become loud. Herbst, to be sure, may not raise this objection; for after he has already raised it against Secerov (p. 19), he himself makes use (p. 31) of the freedom to judge the definitive colour-state by adult larvae and even assures «in the most determined manner» that he does not thereby err [in this]. The nonetheless possible reservation would namely consist therein, that precisely during the transformation phase and the months following it colour-changes can take place which pre-metamorphically cannot be foreseen. The same, however, holds equally for every post-metamorphic phase, since the colour-dress has even after years still not become unmouldable. On the other hand, the larva standing shortly before its transformation undoubtedly already allows marking-style and marking-extent to be judged in the rough; and such judgement-possibility suffices us, in consideration of the constant strong differences in experiments which were necessary only in order to confirm, also for incident light, facts which for reflected light in any case already stood firm. Colour-changes which might perhaps have set in subsequently — and even had they themselves, which is very improbable, run not in the direction of the experiment but in its opposite direction — could scarcely have been able to shake the fundamental result.

## IV. Mikroskopische Untersuchung [Microscopic Investigation].

### 1. Methode [Method].

a) **Auswahl der Objekte [Selection of the Objects].** For the microscopic assessment of the chromatophore-tissue two age-stages of salamander-larvae were drawn upon: 1. About half-grown larvae, where the colour-distribution of the future full animal begins to be laid down and is already to be surveyed in the rough. From later stages this differs — notwithstanding extensive changes which may still take place in the marking-style — chiefly only through the lesser density in which the two kinds of main pigments of the skin are embedded. — 2. Larvae standing just before the transformation. Still older stages, that is, salamanders already transformed, no longer permit the pigment-bearing skin-elements to be distinguished individually: they then lie too densely for their boundaries to be perceived and for their quantity to be assessed even approximately under the magnifying-glass; for these older stages, therefore, the investigation with the free eye yields more than the apparently more exact one with the lens or microscope.

b) **Abtötung [Killing].** Three larvae each destined for comparison preparations (each time from lemon-yellow, orange-yellow, and a third culture, either blue or colourless or dark) were killed by decapitation, three other larvae of in each case the same origin by ether-narcosis. This double pre-treatment pursues the purpose of eliminating the source of error which could arise from the to-be-presumed irritation and contraction under decapitation, slackening and expansion of the chromatophores under narcosis.

c) **Fixierung [Fixation].** From all larvae the back-skin was then dissected off in the same zone, from the attachment of the fore-legs to the attachment of the hind-legs, stretched with needles onto small cork-plates, and fixed in Zenker's fluid, to which some drops of glacial acetic acid had been added, washed out after the lapse of 24 hours, and transferred into alcohols of rising concentration. To the same treatment was also subjected what remained over after the removal of the back-skin, that is, the un-skinned larval trunks and heads, which therefore stand ready for later section-preparations should the occasion arise.

For the purposes of the present investigation — on the advice of Herr Prof. Dr. Heinrich Joseph, to whom I am very grateful for this advice-giving — section-preparations were dispensed with, because they would presumably let too little of the mutual quantity-relation of the two pigments be surveyed. Only whole-mounts of the removed skin-surface were prepared, and indeed by Frl. Olga Kermauner, to whom for her trouble-taking, as well as to Herr Prof. Dr. Eugen Steinach for the permission thereto, which he granted to his just-named preparation-assistant, I am very much obliged to give thanks.

The spreading-out of the skin-pieces on the slide took place in inverted position, that is, with the cutis facing upward, because we promised ourselves from this likewise a facilitation, especially for the determination of the cell-number.

d) **Zellenzählung [Cell-counting].** Although the skin-pieces, with the slide moved back and forth, already permit one to see qualitatively whether the black or the yellow chromatophores are present in the majority, this insight — apart from the separate perception of the individual chromatophores themselves and of their state of expansion — would have little advantage over that sight which the whole animal already affords to the unarmed eye. Indeed, here it becomes even momentarily still more distinct which sort of chromatophore represents the majority; with microscopic adjustment and an unmoved slide, on the other hand, there are naturally always, even in very melanin-rich skin, fields of view which let more yellow pigment be seen than other fields of view in otherwise more xanthorin-rich skin, and vice versa. Adjustment of the objective onto a yellow patch of an otherwise almost black animal, or onto sparingly remaining black ground-colour of an otherwise almost yellow animal, affords misleading images which remain excluded in the contemplation of the whole animal.

Therefore a quantitative method was taken in hand which, in similar manner, was first applied by Steinach-Kammerer for the determination of the distribution of Leydig's cells in the interstitial substance of the testis: the counting of the chromatophores in question. Because of their greater distinctness and sharper delimitation, I confined myself thereby in the present work to the counting of the black pigment-cells, the «cellulae stellatae» which in the extended state are richly branched.

Already to Herbst, as he states on p. 28, the idea of their numerical determination had occurred; he took distance from it again, however, because it would be too difficult to ascertain exactly the number of those chromatophores. Herbst probably used stages that were too old, already too densely pigmented; on half-grown stages as well as on larger larvae, in which the black coloration recedes in favour of the yellow, the counting is possible with all only desirable exactness. And whoever applies much trouble to it and has acquired great practice therein may even venture to give the number in more densely-stored stages, when the processes of the chromatophores already form a matted network: from the number of darkest central points, which on adjustment of the micrometer-screw to various heights stand out more sharply, the number of the cell-bodies can then still be estimated with approximate exactness. Repeatedly Herr Walter Finkler — who in unselfish, uncommonly thankworthy manner subjected himself to the great trouble of these countings — and I as well as Herr Prof. Steinach have mutually checked our counting-results and have always ascertained satisfactory agreement, in that greater deviations than by one cell in the field of view did not occur at all.

From this one may draw the conclusion that the pigment cell in the expanded state more readily loses its cohesion — that is to say, it now divides more readily. Hence the kind of chromatophore that expands — that is, in experiments such as those described so far, the kind that is the same colour as its surroundings — passes over into more rapid multiplication, and is thus placed in a position to displace other kinds of chromatophore that contract — that is, the kinds differing in colour from the surroundings. On the basis of concurring observations on fishes, this same conclusion was already drawn by **Frisch** 1911 (though without cell counts), and on the basis of similar observations on axolotl larvae by **Babák** 1913. **The rapid physiological colour change, which at first rests only on movements (changes of position) of its thereby constant-remaining stock of pigment cells, is superseded by the slow morphological colour change, which rests on multiplication of the expanded chromatophores, and hence on growth of the skin tissue, agreeing with the surroundings, that carries the pigment.**

The coloured adaptation thus furnishes a fine example of Roux's struggle of the parts in the organism: for between the chromatophores that match the colour of the surroundings and those that do not, there is competition, which must end with the destruction of the non-matching chromatophores. The struggle is decided by the intervention of radiant energy from the outer world, since this external energy places the coloured tissue elements it strikes into states of stimulation: the one stimulus-effect (expansion) then brings about the gaining of the upper hand, the other state (contraction), indirectly through the advance of the favoured elements, the retreat of the affected parts.

## V. Summary.

1. If fire salamanders (*Salamandra maculosa* Laur., *forma typica*) are exposed nearly from birth, under correspondingly coloured glass covers (Senebier bells), to incident yellow light, then at metamorphosis they don a more yellow garb than corresponds to their colour-race and than is shown by the uninfluenced mother as well as by the equally aged siblings reared under differently coloured and colourless bells, and finally in the dark.

2. The transmitted yellow light therefore does not differ, in its colour-specific effect, from the incident (reflected) light, whose influence had been tested in earlier experiments of my own (**Kammerer** 1913) on metamorphosed salamanders, and in experiments by other researchers (**Secerov**, **Frisch**, **Dembowski**, **Herbst**, **Przibram**) on larvae, with respect to the persisting colour-garb.

3. At high, summer light-intensity, freshly metamorphosed salamanders of the same generation (experimental duration 4 months) attain the same high degree of yellow colouration that, in those earlier experiments of my own — with influencing of the full salamanders, non-influencing of the larvae — could be attained only in the course of three generations (breeding duration 9–10 years): **total yellow colouration of the back, interrupted only by a few narrow tongues of black ground-colour running up from the belly side over the likewise predominantly yellow flanks.**

4. These irregular indentations of the otherwise pure-yellow dorsal zone suffice, however, to preserve for the salamanders metamorphosed in yellow light the habitus of the *forma typica*, whereas the breeds influenced through several generations in the direction of becoming yellow had, from F₁ onward, transformed into the bilaterally marked *forma taeniata*. The richness in yellow pigment therefore does not depend on the number of generations, but exclusively on the **intensity (and duration) of coloured irradiation**: the **style of pigmentation**, on the other hand, seems to be capable of being altered only with the cooperation of the **generative process**.

5. If glass bells of **different degrees of brightness and saturation of the yellow** (light lemon-yellow, darker orange-yellow) are used, then in the larvae reared under light-yellow light the persisting yellow chromatophores develop earliest and attain the maximum of their spread over the pigmented body covering. But even the larvae reared in darker-yellow light attain, by the time of metamorphosis, a degree of yellow colouration that falls far outside the frame of all those cultures in which (instead of the yellow) colourless bells or open, uncovered vessels were used.

6. The colour changes of the fire salamander in differently coloured light are therefore effects of the **colour quality** and not merely of the **light quantity**. The latter is not without significance, but acts merely as a **presupposing factor for the specifically determining colour factor**.

7. If fire salamanders are exposed nearly from birth, at high light-intensity, to incident dark-violet or dark-blue light, then at metamorphosis they don a **blacker garb** than corresponds to their colour-race and than is shown by their uninfluenced mother as well as by siblings of the same litter reared under differently coloured, colourless, and dark-chamber-standing glasses. The black colouration (displacement of the yellow marking) does not, however, even in the distinctly influenced animals, reach that which was attained with keeping on a black substrate (in reflected ultraviolet light).

8. If fire salamanders are exposed nearly from birth to mixed daylight in manifoldly graded light quantities, incident through glass bells like those that found use in the colour experiments, then at metamorphosis they don the colour-garb that fluctuates only slightly about a mean value of yellow and black pigment quantities, as corresponds to their race and as the uninfluenced mother too very similarly always shows. A light-blue bell yields the same result as colourless bells.

9. If fire salamanders are kept nearly from birth in (rarely and briefly interrupted) exclusion of light, then they lag behind in growth and develop into full salamanders much later than all the siblings kept in the light. After transient, deeper darkening the larvae bleach out, but in the freshly metamorphosed state cannot be constantly distinguished, macroscopically, from normal animals that have metamorphosed in mixed daylight. Microscopically, spots of non-dense layering of the black pigment are conspicuous, without these gaps being filled with yellow pigment.

10. If 5–6-week-old salamander larvae are kept in 0.25% NaCl solution, then they reach metamorphosis (in confirmation of a finding of **Pogonowska**) with a minimum of yellow marking, a maximum of black ground-colour. The animals then resemble those that had been kept on a black ground.

11. By the use of the dark-violet bell this effect of the common-salt solution is no longer strengthened; but by the use of yellow bells it is completely annulled. In the competition between yellow light and blackening salt solution the former wins to such a degree that nothing remains of the influence of the latter (which, in the experiments now before us, admittedly had occasion to take effect only several weeks later).

12. Salamander larvae that are kept under a lemon-yellow bell form, in absolute terms, fewer black chromatophores than any other culture; but, relatively as well as absolutely, the most black chromatophores remain in the state of contraction.

13. Equally aged salamander larvae that are kept under a dark-violet or dark-blue bell form, in absolute terms, more black chromatophores than any other of the cultures described in the present work; and, absolutely as well as relatively, the most black chromatophores remain in the state of expansion. The kind of chromatophore, therefore, that passes over into expansion and remains expanded is favoured in cell division: **sustained expansion of the pigment cells that are the same colour as their surroundings results in their multiplication, indirectly in the displacement of the differently coloured pigment cells persisting sustainedly in contraction.**

14. Hereby is confirmed what **Frisch** (1911) had already observed on fishes, **Babák** (1913) on axolotls, and what **Kammerer** (1913) and **Herbst** (1919) had likewise surmised for the fire salamander. Without prejudice to chemical explanations, and without prejudice to the possibility that, in mnemic repetition of the process at a later age, the first, purely physiological stage of persisting colour changes may more or less drop away or remain imperceptible, **that transition from cell expansion to cell division** reveals how physiological and morphological colour change condition and supersede one another.
