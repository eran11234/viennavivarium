In the year 1900 I was, in connection with regeneration experiments on the little crab *Alpheus* at Naples, surprised by the finding that after amputation of the "larger" snapping-claw a reversal of the chela-relationships set in (Exper. Stud. on Reg. Arch. f. Entw.-Mech. XI. 1901. p. 329), in that now the chela of the opposite side was transformed into the larger, more richly differentiated "snapping-claw," a phenomenon which I designated with the name "compensatory hypertype" (loc. cit. p. 334).

In the next year I sought to carry out analogous experiments on lobsters and crab-species, but obtained in the former the direct regeneration of the amputated "snapping-claw" — in the latter, to be sure, by way of hypotype of the regenerate, that is, in line with the conclusion of the experiments, no transformation of the opposite side (Exper. Stud. on Reg. Arch. f. Entw.-Mech. XIII. 1902. p. 514). The massive chela-form was thereby designated in general terms as the "K-claw" (knot- or knuckle-claw), the more slender as the "Z-claw" (tooth- or pincer-claw) (loc. cit. p. 522). At the close of this second communication I announced the continuation of the experimental and systematic working-through of the chela-forms in the decapod Crustaceans (loc. cit. p. 524), a promise which I redeem only now with the present third communication.¹

In the meantime Ch. Zeleny described a case of compensatory hypertype, after amputation, in the operculum (= snapping-claw) of a tube-worm that is formed unilaterally to begin with, under the colourful name: "Compensatory regulation in the regeneration of the *Hydroides dianthus*" (Arch. f. Entw.-Mech. XIII. 1902. p. 596). E. B. Wilson published in 1903 (Biol. Bull. Vol. IV. 1903. p. 197) "Notes on the reversal of asymmetry in the regeneration of the chelae in *Alpheus heterochelis*," and T. H. Morgan in 1904 (Biol. Bull. VI. 1904. p. 159) "Notes on regeneration," wherein the expression "transpositional regeneration" is used instead of compensatory hypertype, and fruitless attempts to obtain the same phenomenon in the hermit-crab and in the fiddler-crab (*Gelasimus*) are reported. The works mentioned last came to my notice only incidentally. My own experiments were meanwhile continued at the Biological Experimental Institute in Vienna. The Trieste experiments on the lobster and on the sexually dimorphic crab *Xantho rivulosus* had already been concluded in 1902 and find their description at the listing of the species concerned in the following systematic presentation of the chela-relationships.²

There stood at my disposal for this the rich Crustacean collections of the Vienna natural-history Court-Museum, of the Museum in Munich, the Munich zoological Institute, and the London "British Museum." For the permission to use these collections and the kind support during the work I am obliged to warmest thanks to the following Gentlemen: Herren Hofrat Steindachner, Hofrat Brauer, Hofrat Fuchs, the Herren Custoden Rebl,

The introduction of the following termini may facilitate the brevity of the presentation: For the presence of equal chelae on the limbs of the same leg-pair . . . "Homoiochely" (ὅμοιος, gr. = alike, χηλή, gr. = cleft claw, chela); for the presence of unequal chelae on the limbs of the same leg-pair . . . "Heterochely" (ἕτερος, gr. = different). Homoiochely or heterochely can be either "native" (von Geburt aus = from birth) of the species concerned (where applicable belonging only to the one sex) or "regenerative" (then mostly arisen through hypotype regeneration of the stronger chela). The native heterochely can, in the same species, either consist in the favouring of an arbitrary side, in that, in roughly equal numbers of individuals, now the right, now the left chela-hand alternately is the stronger one, "Poterochiry" (πότερος = one of the two, χείρ = hand), or [consist] in the previously [mentioned] favouring of the right-handers, "Dexiochiry" (δεξιός = right), more seldom of the left-handers, "Aristerochiry" (ἀριστερός = left). Finally there can (in *Nika*) be a chela merely on the right, on the left an end-hook present: "Dexiochiry."

## II. Chela-relationships of the now-living decapod Crustaceans.

(Hereto Plate VIII—XI.)

As a catalogue for the treatment of the recent decapod Crustaceans, may the presentation of the systematics by Ortmann (in Bronn's Cl. u. Ordn. p. 1116ff.), which connects on to Boas, serve. The latter distinguishes two main divisions: Natantia and Reptantia (Boas).

### 1. Natantia.

(Abdominal-appendages suited for swimming.)

The three subdivisions of these are to be distinguished by the distribution of the chelae over the various leg-pairs:

a) *Penaeidea* Sp. Bate. "The three chela-pairs are rather alike and not strong (occasionally reduced)."

Both sexes [are] homoiochel on all chela-bearing leg-pairs.

b) *Eucyphidea* Ortm. (= *Eucyphotes* Boas). "The third pereiopod-pair never bears a chela."

The two forward leg-pairs in the 1st–4th Family (Pasiphaeidae Bate, Acanthephyridae Bate, emend. Ortm., Atyidae Kingsley, Stylodactylidae Bate) [are] equal-sized, homoiochel; in the 6th Family (Psalidopodidae W.-Mas. et Alc.) the chelae are absent on the second [pair], in the 7th Family (Pandalidae Bate, emend. Ortm.) those on the first leg-pair [are absent]; the 8th–10th Family (Rhynchocinetidae Ortm., Hippolytidae Ortm., Latreutidae Ortm.) again possess chelae on both forward leg-pairs (homoiochel), whereby commonly the first pair is somewhat shorter and stronger than the second; the 14th Family (Crangonidae Bate) is distinguished by imperfect (but equal) chelae on the first leg-pair and stunted formation of the second leg-pair (with or without chela); the 15th Family (Gnathophyllidae Ortm.) is homoiochel on both first leg-pairs, of which the second [is] more strongly formed.

From all these families no cases of native heterochely are known to me; to the regenerative heterochely belongs the case described by Fritz Müller of a specimen of *Atyoïda Potimirim* (Haeckel's biogen. Grundges. bei d. Neubild. verl. Gliedm. [Haeckel's biogenetic fundamental law in the renewed formation of lost limbs]. Kosmos. VIII. 1880). There remain for discussion the 5th Family (Alpheidae Bate) and the 11th–13th Family (Pontoniidae Bate, Palaemonidae Bate, Processidae Ortm. = Nikidae Bate).

The chela-relationships of the Alpheidae have been treated most fully by Coutière (Les Alpheidae. — Thèses pour le doctorat à la Fac. des Sc. Paris, Masson, 1899). All genera have, on the first leg-pair, strongly developed chelae, on the second leg-pair small ones sitting on thread-shaped, many-jointed stalks. The individuals are, however, according to species or sex, now homoiochel, now heterochel; the heterochely always concerns only the more strongly developed first leg-pair. The genera established by Coutière are often founded only on single specimens (which moreover may be lacking chelae); where several specimens are at hand, it shows itself that with heterochely the one chela-form comes to stand now on the right, now on the left side of the body.

The genera (and species) of the *Alpheus*-family are arranged by Coutière, according to the progressive over-roofing [overarching] of the eyes, into form-series, which are distinguished by ever-further-going heterochely.

The starting-forms are provided by the genus *Athanas*. The chelae [are] formed differently according to sex; in the ♀ without armament, in the ♂ with few wrinkles.

The heterochely of the *Alpheus*-group is a native one, as emerges not only from the constantly encountered difference of the chelae in sexually mature animals, but also from direct observation of the freshly hatching little crabs in those species in which it is sufficiently developed (Coutière, p. 452; W. K. Brooks and F. H. Herrick, Embryol. a. Metamorph. of *Macrura*. Mem. Nation. Acad. Sc. 1892).

The interchange of the chelae of *Alpheus* by the regenerative path ("compensatory hypertype") has been described in my first communication (p. 329ff. Pl. XIII) and has formed the starting-point for the further pursuit of chela-asymmetry.

To the findings of that time it is to be added (after observations in Trieste) that in the larger specimens of *Alpheus ruber* no transformation of the pincer-claw is yet to be noted after the first moult (after loss of the snapping-claw), while in place of the lost snapping-claw a second small pincer-claw has sprouted forth (Pl. VIII Fig. 1). Such specimens thus show, with respect to chela-form, at least temporarily a regenerative homoiochely, in contrast to smaller specimens, which, in consequence of the transformation of the pincer-claw that has remained in place into a snapping-claw, already appear (now inversely) heterochel after the first moult. Since in the earlier experiments the specimens of *A. ruber* and *A. dentipes* were smaller than the specimens of *A. platyrrhynchus*, it remains doubtful whether the phenomenon — that the chela-transformation in the latter species had only set in after two to three moults — is not to be traced back to the size of the individuals, and not to their belonging to the species *A. platyrrhynchus* (cf. Exp. Stud. I. p. 330); with compensatory hypertype of the chelae the regenerates in the latter species were similar with respect to size, but different in form (ibid. p. 331).

When Coutière remarks with respect to *Automate dolichognatha* (p. 196) that often the chela of the one side is the larger the smaller the other [is], then it may easily be that an enhancement of the heterochely conditioned by regenerative processes is present.

The chela-relationships of the Pontoniidae Bate, since neither regeneration-experiments nor direct observations of the native origin of the heterochely are at hand, give occasion first to the question whether characters are not to be found on the individual specimens which permit a sure inference as to native or regenerative origin?

Apart from the homoiochel *Corallocaris* (*graminea* Dana) and *Anchistia* (*lacustris*, *notata*, *amethystina* Heller; *scripta* Roux?)¹, heterochely occurs in the Pontoniidae on the second, stronger chela-pair.

*Typton spongicola* Costa (= *Pontonella glabra* Heller) (Pl. IX Fig. 1). Very strong size-difference of the chelae; in the ♂ the larger, inflated chela [is] often absolutely larger than the whole rest of the body; the larger chela stands, in both sexes, now on the right, now on the left.

In one specimen of the Vienna Court-Museum (Pl. IX Fig. 1 a) only the right chela of the type of the smaller chelae is completely preserved, while the left chela-leg, broken off at the preformed breaking-point, bears only a small regeneration-cone. The segments preserved from this limb, lying proximal to the breaking-point, now show the size-relationships of a larger chela, whereby we can thus recognize that heterochely existed before the regeneration-process. The fact that the limbs broken off in chela-losses occurring in nature are detached almost exclusively by autotomy at specially preformed breaking-points (cf. H. Przibram, Regeneration. Erg. d. Physiol. I. 1902. p. 83) thus gives us, for all those heterochel forms in which the asymmetry can already be established on the basal segments, an almost infallible means to decide the native or regenerative origin of heterochely even without experiments.

*Pontonia tyrrhena* M. Edw. and *P. margaritifera* Lkgtr. have quite similar native heterochely to *Typton*, only somewhat slighter difference.

The genus *Calappa* F. possesses fairly equal-sized claws, which however are typically differently shaped (the sexes behave alike): the one claw bears at the base of the dactylopodite a curved, club-shaped process, which I will designate as a »clapper«, and is more thick-set in build than the other, which lacks the clapper-tooth; claws of the former form, in connection with a terminology set up earlier (Exp. It now turned out that among 62 specimens of various *Calappa* species presented for comparison (namely 11 *Calappa hepatica* L.¹), Honolulu; 4 *C. philargia* L., Nagasaki; 1 *C. fornicata* F., Aden; 18 *C. marmorata* = *granulata*, Mediterranean; 4 *C. lophos*¹) Hbst., Indian Ocean; 1 *C. cristata* Latr., Sumatra; 1 *C. sp.*?, Quajaquelle, and 23 *C. tuberculata* F., Red Sea) in 60 the K-claw stood on the right side (Pl. XI Fig. 1)¹). In one individual (*C. tuberculata* ♀, Pl. XI Fig. 1a) both claws were developed as Z-claws, the right one, however, recognizable by its small size as a hypotypic regenerate; only in the last animal (likewise *C. tuberculata* ♀, Pl. XI Fig. 1b) might one at first believe that one was dealing with native heterochely, in which the K-claw stood on the left side. On closer inspection, however, it turns out that the claw more strongly developed on the left does not show that typical K-development which the right-standing K-claws show. Whereas in these the margin of the propodite rises steeply and in a straight line above the insertion of the dactylopodite, in the left K-claw an arch-shaped projection is noticeable at this place, such as is otherwise seen in a similar way in the Z-claw of this species; furthermore both the dactylopodite (with the exception of the present clapper-tooth) and the claw-cutting-edge of the propodite show a formation approximated to the Z-claw; and in other respects too this left claw forms a transitional form between the K- and Z-claw. If one finally compares the claws of the left-handed specimen with the bilaterally Z-clawed one recognized as a regeneration, one finds that already in this latter the larger left Z-claw has begun to take on individual characters of the K-claw: the shortening of the claw-cutting-edges, the different size of the arming teeth, the arch-shaped curvature of the dactylopodite, the bump-like elevation at the inner angle of the propodite (at the dactylopodite-insertion), are such indications. Everything speaks for our being concerned, in the left K-claw, not with a native form but with a »compensatory hypertypy« (Exp. Stud. I. p. 334) of the originally Z-claw after loss of the K-claw and its regeneration as a Z-claw (hypotypy). Thus all (62) specimens of various *Calappa* species presented for comparison would be »right-handers«, i.e. the right claw-hand shows a stronger anlage and armature than the left. ## III. Claw-relations of the fossil decapod Crustaceans.

## IV. History of Claw Asymmetry.

(For this, Plate XIII.)

The investigation of the claws in the known fossil decapods allows us to recognize that in the Earth's history equal-clawed [gleichscherige] species first appeared, and this is also confirmed by comparison with the living forms, since for those heterochelous forms to which no living homoiochelous ones stand close, such [homoiochelous forms] are demonstrable in older Earth strata (e.g.: *Nika*–*Blaculla*, *Homarus*–*Eryma*). In general, however, even now homoiochelous genera or species are still present in all families, even when these same harbor heterochelous species; and here too it shows itself again that the equal-clawed ones are then to be regarded as the lower forms (judged by the general developmental level) (e.g. *Athanas* as against *Alpheus*, *Pylocheles* as against *Parapagurus*, etc.).

We are here, then, dealing with "Heterepistasis" in EIMER's sense: at every developmental stage of the decapod Crustaceans (whose course of development, as is well known, moves in the direction of a strong reduction of the posterior body sections — as, moreover, in all metameric animal classes) some genera or species remain at the original homoiochely (Epistasis), while others have developed toward an ever-further-reaching heterochely (Heterepistasis); thereby the male sex often runs ahead (Genepistasis: e.g. *Xantho*, where already in single ♀ the male character of the "right knot-claw" [rechte Knotenschere] occurs, without it being possible to ascertain a relation to a defective development of the female sexual organs, such as occurs for example as "parasitic castration").

Departing from all hitherto investigated cases of "Orthogenesis" is the running-ahead of one body-side in development, namely the one-sided appearance of the "snapping-claw" [Knackschere]. It seems, namely, that the second claw too possesses the tendency to snapping-claw formation [Knackscherenbildung], as is evident from the compensatory hypertypy. Few forms, and among these mostly only old specimens, bring snapping-claws [Knackscheren] to development on both sides (*Astacoides madagascariensis*, *Thalassina anomala*, *Birgus latro* (old ♂), *Neptunus* (old); *Charybdis lineata*, *Thalamita coerulipes* and *Eriphia laevimana* in individual specimens).

The manifold grounds for the assumption of the small-tooth claw [Zähnchenschere] as the older one have already been discussed in the II. Communication; on this occasion it may be added that with all regenerates of claws there first appears a form resembling the pincer-claw [Zwickschere] (for which the figures furnish many proofs), whose transformation into the snapping-claw [Knackschere] in the species with direct claw-regeneration, to be sure, soon becomes noticeable.

Remarkable is the appearance of the "knob-tooth" [Klöppelzahns], which is always predominantly bound to the right claw and occurs in a single series of forms (Oxystomata — Oxyrrhyncha — Ciclometopa — *Geryon*, *Goneplax*, among the genera formerly placed with the Catametopa). If, with DOFLEIN, we place the *Geryon*, *Goneplax* and some allied genera hitherto placed with the Catametopa still among the Ciclometopa, then the "dexiochiry with knob" [Dexiochirie mit Klöppel] forms a characteristic feature of the Ciclometopa as against the Catametopa, which can develop indeed a quantitatively stronger, but merely poterochirous, heterochiry without knob [Klöppel].

Much speaks for it that the percentage of left-handers among the dexiochirous species indicates to us nothing other than the measure of the compensatory hypertypy that has occurred. The "dexiochiry with knob" [Dexiochirie mit Klöppel] can also be demonstrated in the fossil relatives and must accordingly have maintained itself unchanged for a long time: this speaks for it that the compensatory hypertypy does not lead to hereditarily fixed left-handers.

The question arises whether the dexiochiry can be traced back to an earlier poterochiry, or whether it arose directly out of the homoiochiry?

Unfortunately the fossil material here leaves much to be desired. On the other hand it is to be considered that, although indeed many homoiochelous species and genera standing close to the dexiochirous species occur, no poterochirous ones with knob [Klöppel] do. The few occurring poterochirous ones without knob [Klöppel], which are unquestionable Ciclometopa (*Cancer isarcus*?, *Chlorodius niger*, *Trapezia* ♂, *Tetralia*, *Parathelphusa tridentata*), point rather to another tendency toward a qualitatively lesser, quantitatively more powerful poterochiry, such as the Catametopa then display.

The living Anomura (in the strict sense), which according to the general view represent an older branch of the brachyuran stem, show us the tendency to form, out of equal-clawed [gleichscherige] forms, right-handed and left-handed species and genera; the right-handers that appear, however, have no similarity to the "knob"-right-handers [Klöppel-Rechtshänder], and regenerate, moreover, directly (*Parapagurus*, Pl. X Fig. 1 to 1 *a*, *Eupagurus* after MORGAN's experiments; analogously the left-hander *Calcinus*, Pl. X Fig. 4–4 *c*).

In the genus *Clibanarius*, within each species quite equal claws or right-handers occur (without this yielding a sex difference), and the origin of the dexiochiry out of homoio-, not poterochiry, here probably comes clearly to light. Within the genus *Pagurus*, homoiochelous, right-handed and left-handed ones occur, however separated according to the species, so that the independent development of left-handed and right-handed species out of equal-handed ones is probably likewise the most probable assumption.

As already mentioned, the view is widespread that the claw-asymmetry of the hermit-crabs [Einsiedlerkrebse] stands in intimate connection with the inhabiting of snail-shells¹). Now,

It is therefore precarious to make any "functional adaptation" responsible for the claw-asymmetry.

As regards the stunted abdomen, the following considerations may here find a place, although this question stands only in a loose connection with our theme here:

In the progressive shortening of the decapod abdomen, various paths were taken, one of which was the softening of the posterior segments. This compelled the animals to seek a protection at first in straight snail-shells; the stunting could, however, also proceed asymmetrically, so that thereby a curvature of the hindbody came about, and then still better-wound snail-shells could be used for cover: since the snails are predominantly right-wound, it could be maintained on the part of the adherents of the selection doctrine that precisely those hermit-crabs in which a suitably wound abdomen occurred were at an advantage and have therefore been preserved; perhaps, however, a particular side was also favored from the very beginning, as is the case with the right-handedness of the "knob-claw" [Klöppelschere] in the Ciclometopa. Here it seems indeed excluded to discover an advantage that the favoring precisely of the right claw could bring with it.

Even the asymmetrical development of the leg-pairs following upon the claws need not stand in any genetic connection with the snail-asymmetry: one need only think of the inequality of the second leg-pair in the Processidae, where no shells at all come into consideration!

It may further be warned against, claiming the function [as responsible] for the arising of the heterochely in the individual: the claw difference visible in single cases already in the egg and its heritability in this case (*Alpheus*, cf. I. Comm., p. 330, and COUTIÈRE, Alphéidae. Paris 1899. p. 452), further the direct regeneration without prior possibility of function of the regenerates (in the directly-regenerating forms, e.g. the lobster, and in the bilateral regeneration of *Alpheus*) speak against it. Still less is it to be seen how, in species-formation, the function should let, say, a right-handedness arise, since, after all, if a tendency to right-handedness does not already exist, the left side just as well as the right could have been employed for the function in question. There would still remain the expedient of assuming that, through some circumstance — as we already mentioned, however, a wholly undiscoverable one — all corresponding left-handers had been annihilated from the very beginning. The extraordinary improbability of this assumption becomes clear when we consider that the "knob-right-handers" [Klöppel-Rechtshänder] proceed in many parallel series from homoiochelous crabs, and thus the elimination of the left-handers would have had to take place not through annihilation of actual left-handers, but only of "tendential" left-handers! We also see, in the example *Eriphia spinifrons* statistically investigated by DUNCKER, no destruction of the left-handers actually occurring, since these belong predominantly precisely to the higher size-groups (for which I have given the explanation above through regeneration and compensatory hypertypy). There remains therefore nothing else but the assumption that in the structure of our Ciclometopa group the tendency to the development of the right-handed knob-tooth [Klöppelzahn] was present, even before any differing function had differentiated the two claws.

Thus the original investigation — of the manner in which an injured body is able to restore its unity — has led us to results in a quite different field: We have before us a case in which we discover a tendency of an animal group to form a particular organ, and here we are not, as in the examples adduced by EIMER and his pupils of coloration, marking, etc., dependent solely upon the ever more or less uncertain conclusions from investigations of recent animal species, but have the significant fossil evidential material at our disposal.

Even less is it to be seen how, in the formation, the function should let, e.g., a right-handedness arise, since after all, if a tendency to right-handedness does not already exist, the left side could just as well have been used as the right for the function in question. There would still remain the recourse of assuming that through some — as we have already mentioned, yet wholly undiscoverable — circumstance all corresponding left-handers had been destroyed from the very beginning.

The extraordinary improbability of this assumption is evident when we consider that the "Klöppel-right-handers" [hammer-right-handers] would have had to arise in many parallel rows from homoiochelous [equal-clawed] crabs, and that the elimination of the left-handers would have had to occur not through destruction of actual left-handers, but only of "tendential" left-handers! According to definite developmental directions ("Orthogenesis", Eimer), not swinging irregularly to all sides, the phyletic development advances forward, and, arrived at analogous developmental heights, lets different species also bring forth analogous formations of homologous organs ("Homoiogenesis", Eimer).

## V. Developmental Mechanics of Claw Asymmetry.

The developmental mechanics of the primary formation of differently differentiated Scheren is unfortunately, in consequence of the unfavorable experimental conditions in the Crustacean-larvae and -eggs, at present difficult to take in hand. The heritability of the right- or left-handedness, however, speaks rather, in particular, for the already complete formation within the egg, in the *Alpheus*-species born at a very late Stadium, for a pure "self-differentiation" in the sense of Roux.

The appearance of the heterochiry as a male secondary sex-characteristic in many species would suggest the action of an inner secretion. Then, however, one would indeed expect that "castrated specimens" would show the peculiarities of the other sex. Direct castration-experiments at young Stadia are, however, unfortunately impracticable, so that we are referred to the parasitic castration occurring in the natural way: the investigation of crabs afflicted with Sacculus has furnished me no points of attachment in this direction. Admittedly the crabs probably become afflicted with Sacculus only as Imago, and then it is perhaps already too late for an influencing of secondary sex-characteristics. The assumption of an influencing through inner secretion is also unfavorable precisely to the one-sidedness and its heritability.

If we take the regenerative asymmetry into account, then a theory of inner secretion fails entirely. It can give an explanation neither for the direct nor for the hypotype regeneration nor for the compensatory hypertypie.

E. B. Wilson (Reversal of Asymmetry in *Alpheus heterochelis*. Biol. Bull. IV. 1903. p. 197 ff.) set up the hypothesis that for the compensatory formation of the uninjured Zwickschere [pincer-claw] into the Knackschere [snapping-claw], after amputation of the latter, the nerve of the Schere is responsible, for which he attempted the experimental proof. When, in *Alpheus heterochelis* (which, in respect to the occurrence of the compensatory hypertypie, joins onto the *A. dentipes, ruber* and *platyrhynchus* investigated by me), after removal of the Knackschere the nerve of the Zwickschere was cut through, then — these were mostly autotomized and there followed (as with simultaneous amputation of both Scheren) direct regeneration. Only four specimens retained the non-amputated Schere, which was naturally at first immobile. Two of these likewise did not attain mobility during the duration of the experiment, and were also unable to pass the next moult in the normal way. By this time a regenerate had grown in place of the amputated Knackschere, which, according to Wilson, was to show the characteristic form of a "Knackschere," that is, was to show direct regeneration. Wilson's figures I should indeed not trust to assert this unconditionally: they (p. 205, Fig. 3 *H* and *F*) show quite unpronounced forms, the articulation of the Dactylopodite in the middle of the Propodite corresponds to the Zwickschere (in the Knackschere the articulation, even according to Wilson's own figures, cf. p. 202 Fig. 2 *B* and *E*, p. 205 Fig. 3 *B*, [is] significantly farther distal), the claw-cutting-edges show equal breadth, whereas in typical Knackscheren the Propodit at the end is significantly broader and blunter, the constriction of the Propodite proximal of the Dactylopodite-articulation, conspicuous in normal Knackscheren, is hardly stronger than in the one figured by Wilson as a typical male Zwickschere (p. 199, Fig. *A*): only the more compact form makes the regenerates in question similar to the Knackschere; but it must be taken into account that regenerates as well as embryos at early Stadia exhibit a stronger roundness owing to their more fluid constitution. As regards now the other Schere, the original Zwickschere, whose nerve had been cut through, it could in the one case, owing to the moult that did not occur, not be ascertained whether a transformation was under way (p. 205, Fig. 3 *G*); in the second specimen, a male [Männchen], the Schere was drawn out of its skin and showed the loss of the bristle-row characteristic of the male Zwickschere of this species on the inner surface of the Dactylopodite, and an indication of the "Hammer-form" [Hammerform]. The Zwickschere was thus, in spite of the nerve-section, engaged in transformation, only this is slower compared to a Zwickschere without nerve-resection.

In two other specimens the Schere whose nerve had been cut through regained its mobility in part before the onset of the next moult: one of these specimens showed, after the moult was passed through, the usual exchange of the claw-forms, the other, however, two Knackscheren (Wilson, p. 205, Fig. 3 *J*, *y*), which show very distinctly the cited characteristic differentiations of the Knackschere. Wilson interprets this case to mean that the stump of the Knackschere had already begun directly to regenerate before the nervous connection with the other (Zwickschere) was re-established, but that, after re-establishment of the nerve-connection, this direct Knackschere-regeneration had not advanced far enough to be able to hinder the transformation of the Zwickschere into a second Knackschere.

T. H. Morgan likewise undertook experiments on the cutting-through of the nerves (Notes on Regeneration. Biol. Bull. VI. No. 4. 1904. p. 169: Transpositional or Compensatory Regeneration of the Large Chelae in some Crustacea). Unfortunately he used the hermit-crab [Einsiedlerkrebs], which always regenerates directly, and the fiddler-crab [Fiedlerkrabbe] (*Gelasimus*), whose Scheren degenerated after the nerve-section (he would moreover here too presumably have obtained direct regeneration, cf. above, also Plate XI Fig. 7—7 *a*). Morgan is of the opinion that simultaneously with the severance of the nerves blood-vessels too are destroyed, and traces back the formation of the compensatory Knackschere to the increased blood-supply to the Zwickschere after amputation of the original Knackschere.

This hypothesis has in its favor that it could in the same way also trace back the primary formation of the Knackschere to a difference in the material supplied to one side during the embryonic development: Wilson too, by the way, assumes for the primary release of the Knackschere such a difference (p. 210). For in the nerves namely C. T. Brues, who at Wilson's instigation investigated the nerve-relations in *Alpheus heterochelis*, found no difference between Knack- and Zwickschere: the two sides differed from one another neither in the thickness of the nerves, nor in the number of nerve-fibrils, nor in the size of the corresponding ganglia demonstrably; therefore the different formation must come about through hypertrophy of other tissue.¹

> ¹ Krukenberg (Vergl.-physiol. Stud. II. 4. T. 1887. S. 159) found the difference in *A. ruber* in the preponderance of narrow muscle-fibers in greater number in the "great Schere," whereon attention had already been drawn in my 2nd Communication p. 517.

Since the asymmetric Scheren are approximately equal at the first Stadia, the primary asymmetry comes about through a different developmental velocity of the two sides: the greater developmental velocity determines the appearance of the Knackschere, while the other is formed as Zwickschere.

With increasing age, however, the habitus of the Zwickschere too is, in many species, more and more approximated to the Knackschere. Differentiation of the Knackschere is thus dependent on the growth-velocity and the (absolute) age of the Schere. If we regard regeneration as an accelerated growth¹, then for the formation of the regenerating Schere there will exist, on the one side, more favorable conditions owing to the greater growth-velocity, on the other side less favorable conditions owing to the lesser absolute age of the structure. If the plus in growth-velocity of the regenerate outweighs the minus in age of the same, then direct regeneration will set in; if the plus in absolute age of the non-amputated Zwickschere outweighs its minus in developmental velocity, then it will sooner attain the further-going differentiation than the opposite side (compensatory hypertypie). If both Scheren are amputated, then the earlier K-Schere remains in advance and direct regeneration arises. If nerves (and possibly also blood-vessels) of one side are cut through, so that the growth-velocity of this side is inhibited, then possibly neither side can gain an advance over the other that would hinder its formation into a Knackschere, and there arise two (slightly developed) Knackscheren (Wilson's cases).

If, in Wilson, both Scheren were amputated and both nerves cut through, then (3 cases) slower, but direct, regeneration set in. This accords with my explanations, but not with the assumption of a decisive influence of the uninjured nerve-connection for the typical formation of the asymmetry: according to this, indeed, with doubled nerve-interruption an harmonious result would be still less to be expected than with simple [interruption].

## Plate XIII.

[Phylogenetic tree of the decapod crustaceans according to the fossil remains, for special illustration of the behavior of the different groups with respect to claw difference (heterochely) and its various cases (poterochiry, aristerochiry, dexiochiry).

The geological periods are plotted in the vertical direction, ascending from the oldest decapod-bearing strata up to the present day.

Ascending from the lower right corner at an acute angle toward the left, the general developmental direction of the decapods is indicated, which is expressed in a progressive shortening of the posterior segments. From the common stem there first arise equal-clawed forms, which often, little altered, outlast long earth-periods (e.g. deep-sea forms). From these, heterochirous species branch off, then the family of the Processidae, dexiochirous in the second claw-bearing leg pair (*Blaculla*), which in the present day is represented by the isolated genus *Nika* with "dexiochely". Further on, among the Anomura, all the remaining cases occur ("dexiochiry," "aristerochiry"), yet without the formation of a "clapper tooth."

This appears, always bound to dexiochiry, as a characteristic feature in the brachyuran group: Oxystomata (*Calappa*) — Cyclometopa (including *Geryon* and *Goneplax*), whereas, however, homoiochely and poterochiry (the latter then without "clapper tooth") are retained in a few genera and then occur once again exclusively among the Catametopa.]
