## I. Statement of the Problem.

Among the properties of the water beetle that make it so especially suitable for head transplantation, its easily visible mating and its typical execution were already named in the first communication. To this is added the fact that copulation is not, as in many other animals, carried out at night or otherwise in secret. Almost all water beetles caught in spring prepare themselves at once in the aquarium for mating. Thus, although I used for the sexual experiments only animals encountered in copula, I never had to suffer from a lack of material.

By means of head transplantation the following question was to be answered:

*What influence does the transplanted head exert upon the body of the opposite sex with respect to its behavior at the beginning and during the execution of copulation?*

The male of *Hydrophilus* climbs slowly onto the back of the female, settles down there parallel to the female, and fastens itself by the adhesive disks of the foremost extremities — which belong only to the male — to the thorax. The last pair of legs most often holds the analogous limbs of the female or, if the female has already drawn in its feet, serves to rub the last abdominal segments of the ♀. The middle pair of legs is free and rows during copulation. The antennae tap the head of the female. The latter behaves passively. It is a consequence of the species-specific phlegm that the *Hydrophilus* female, in contrast to the *Dytiscus* female, makes no defensive or rejecting movements. The longer copulation lasts, the more the female leaves the legs to the arbitrariness of the male. For from various views of the matter, which become understandable during mating, that not the head but the abdomen of the female plays a part in the friction movements of the last male pairs of legs, and that thereupon the female holds completely still, I conclude, in short, that I may regard the drawing-in of the legs as an expression of the female organism and as typically female throughout the entire course of copulation. On the other hand, I would regard the anchoring on the part of the male, with the male limbs, as a criterion of male copulatory behavior.

For the unobjectionable application of these two criteria, on which the experiments described in what follows had to be based, the question to be posed had to be clearly set out, namely that the anchoring is a typically male property; conversely, that the drawing-in of the legs is typically female. Otherwise the application to male or to female would not work. Inversely: if the female puts its extremities under the male, or if other water beetles too do not draw in their feet, then the question arises here, with avoidance of homosexuality, of a misjudgment of the error sources of this part of the experimental results.

*Hans Blunck*, in a thorough investigation of the sexual life of *Dytiscus marginalis*, writes the following about the abnormal sexual intercourse of the beetle: the *Dytiscus*, apart from the cross-pairings, shows yet one or the other anomalous activity of the sexual drive that must be designated as especially abnormal, namely the pairing of males among themselves. Of all animal classes the beetles furnish the most examples of pederasty (meant is probably homosexuality, note of the reviewer). Cases of this kind first became known probably in *Melolontha*, and were made the object of philosophical speculations by *Gadeau de Kerville* (1896, p. 85—87), *Karsch* (1900) and others. Later such aberrations were also demonstrated in *Lucanus cervus*, *Rhizotrogus solstitialis* and in various Malacodermata. *Kerville* (loc. cit.) distinguishes between pédérastes par nécessité and pédérastes par goût, according to whether the animals enter into a copulation in the absence or presence of females. The first case seems to be the more frequent and was achieved artificially by *Féré* (loc. cit., p. 549—551) in *Melolontha*. Of 718 specimens, 38 males entered into a pairing among themselves. *Kerville* (1897, p. 92) sought to bring also *Dytiscus* to pederasty, by keeping a number of males for a longer time united in small cuvettes; the animals, however, did not begin to mate.

«My observations on *Dytiscus* I owe to chance. During an occasional revision of my aquarium I once found two specimens of *Dytiscus punctulatus* ♂♂, on another occasion *marginalis* ♂♂, in copula with one another. Two further cases in *marginalis* I observed in freshly caught beetles in the transport vessels. The partner functioning as the male behaved in all cases quite as toward a female. The anchoring was normal. Shaking movements, antenna-tapping, and rubbing of the hind legs on the genital organs were not lacking. Above all, lively attempts were made to introduce the penis. To an injury of the organs of the passive part, such as is said to occur in *Melolontha* (*Karsch* 1900), it came, however, only in one case. I also never saw the rod penetrate into the anus. With the mere application of force the attacking males seem [unable to bring it about]; without the cooperation of the passive partner the coming-about of the sexual act of two males in *Dytiscus* can be grasped just as little as in *Melolontha* (see *Karsch* [loc. cit.] and *v. d. Osten-Sacken*). It would then remain unexplained that the individual functioning as female, as a rule, thoroughly assumes the behavior of such a one. In the reported cases only one male defended itself against the attacker; the others remained motionless with drawn-in legs and under-folded antennae, two even extended the abdominal tip and opened the slit in the 9th sternite, which falls into the genital pouch. The union was always very intimate and lasted several, in one case over 24 hours. After 18 hours the male had not yet ceased the attempts to introduce the penis. A sperm transfer, however, took place in no case. . . . . The accompanying circumstances of the two last cases of pederasty that came to my knowledge are suited to bring some light into the coming-about of these puzzling aberrations. I had caught the beetles, about 100 specimens of their kind, in *one* pond, left them 3—4 hours in small transport cans, and then separated them in two aquaria according to sex. In the vessel housing the males, sexual intercourse came about. The catching time fell in a period of heightened sexual drive of the beetles. During the transport at least two pairs had entered the normal copula. I now assume that in the narrow vessels the males, in constant contact with the females, had impregnated themselves with the latter's sexual scent and, after the separation of the sexes, were mistaken by some especially aroused same-sex individuals and taken to be females. So the absence of the female and the confusion of the sexual scent may likewise explain other cases of pederasty. *Féré* too (loc. cit.) brought about the copulatory act of males among one another in *Melolontha* by bringing these together beforehand with the females and then removing the latter.»

I have here, with good reason, cited so thoroughly the description of the previously described perversities of the beetle. With it I wished to meet beforehand the objection: the beetles are so perverse that nothing can be established on them concerning sexual instincts.

If we wish to recapitulate briefly what comes into consideration for my experiments of the head exchange between the two sexes. Nothing more than:

1. Males can also make anchoring attempts upon males.

2. Males can, in the copula (!), behave like females.

Apart from the fact that the described cases represent exceptional cases and were observed only in *Dytiscus* and *Melolontha* — in *Hydrophilus* I was able, despite two years of nearly daily observation, to establish [no] perversities² — not a single result of the experiments to be described can be called into question:

1. The sex of the animal mounted by the female with male head plays no part.

2. Passive whole-males or male bodies did not occur at all in the copula in these experiments.

We can therefore formulate the following questions:

*What influence does the transplanted head exert upon the body of the opposite sex with respect to its behavior at the beginning and during the execution of copulation?*

The problems that stand in connection with this are: Which ganglion is the seat of the sexual instincts? And the problem whether in insects there exists a dependence of the copulation drives upon the primary sexual organs or upon some other gland, unknown to us, with its secretion in the abdomen.

For the solution of these questions I made use of the following experimental arrangement.

## II. Experimental Arrangement.

The water beetles were caught in spring and transported without distinction of sex and kept in the aquarium.

2 months before the operation the copulating pairs were selected and the sexes housed separately in containers.

The operation was carried out, as already described in the first communication, by decapitation by means of a scissors cut above the head. The heads were exchanged between males and females. Although the vessels were precisely labeled, the animals were still marked by small cuts at the tips or at the side of the elytra, and the kind of operation undertaken on them was made recognizable.

The beetles were placed in glass tubs, two at a time — in one case three at a time — in the following combinations:

1. Normales Männchen — normales Männchen.
2. Normales Männchen — normales Weibchen.
3. Normales Männchen — Männchen mit Weibchenkopf.
4. Normales Männchen — Weibchen mit Männchenkopf.
5. Normales Weibchen — normales Weibchen.
6. Normales Weibchen — Weibchen mit Männchenkopf.
7. Männchen mit Weibchenkopf — Weibchen mit Männchenkopf.

[Translated:]

1. Normal male — normal male.
2. Normal male — normal female.
3. Normal male — male with female head.
4. Normal male — female with male head.
5. Normal female — normal female.
6. Normal female — female with male head.
7. Male with female head — female with male head.

8. Experiment 6 was combined with experiment 4 in such a way that to the female with male head, besides a normal female, a normal male was also added.

To eliminate the individual differences, partly an animal was withdrawn from one vessel and the vessel was stocked with another one of the same kind, partly each combination was set up several times.

All vessels were, in order to make a comparison possible, set up in a row at a sunny window. The sun, like warmth in general, favors and greatly promotes the sexual drive in our beetles, as is also mostly observed in other diurnal animals.

Before the animals were used for the experiment, they were well fed in solitary confinement.

The observation was carried out for a week through the forenoons and afternoons. In the evenings and during the night the animals came again into solitary confinement.

When the normal control animals did not copulate, the negative results in the remaining containers were not taken into account, since a separation between the influence of the external circumstances (sun, light, warmth, etc.) and that of the transplanted head upon the non-occurrence of the copula could not be carried out.

In the observation vessels there was no feeding, on the one hand so as not to make observation more difficult, on the other hand so as not to distract the experimental animals. In the solitary containers there was abundant algae, on which they could feed their fill the whole night through.

## III. Results.

Ad 1. In the vessels in which only normal males were present, no copula and not even a mounting attempt was observed. The pédérastes par nécessité in the sense of *Kerville* thus did not occur in *Hydrophilus*. As cited above, I used in these experiments only animals eager to copulate.

Ad 2. In all vessels the normal copula is carried out.

Ad 3. No copula. Complete indifference of the two animals to one another.

Ad 4. The male in copulation position. The female with male head has not laid on the legs.

Ad 5. At the same time at which copulation takes place in 2., complete indifference. Self-evidently also otherwise.

Ad 6. Copulation position of the female with male head observed. The normal female is tapped with the antennae. The movement, posture, and exertions of the operated animal [are] typically male, so that the female falls into orgasm and lays on the legs (see above 1). The female with male head remains in the copulation posture 2 to 3 hours. Once even the whole forenoon through.

Ad 7. No copula.

Ad 8. The female with male head copulates with the normal female.

For the active sexual life, thus the seeking-out, mounting, and erotic arousal of the female, the head is therefore determining. The being-recognized, which leads to being-mounted, the body determines.

The experiment was repeated with the same animals one month later with the same result. There thus took place no influencing of the head by the opposite-sex germ-gland since the operation (about 2 months). This result stands in agreement with the inconsequentiality of castration in insects, which *Meisenheimer*, *Kellogg*, *Kopec*, *Oudemans*, *Regen*, and others have carried out.

## IV. Summary.

In the first communication the replantation possibility of insect heads had been described. This exchange can be carried out also between different sexes (xenoplastic transplantation). It was now to be examined how the sexual instincts behave in such xenoplastically operated animals. The normal male of *Hydrophilus piceus*, in the preparations for copulation, fastens itself by the forward pair of legs to the thorax of the female, while the last pair of legs holds fast the swimming-feet of the female and the middle pair is free and serves for slow locomotion. The female behaves passively.

The heads were exchanged between males and females, which had previously been kept separate. After complete healing-in had taken place, the animals were placed in the various sexual combinations, two at a time, in a vessel with water and observed further.

a) Females with male head made the above-described preparations for copulation, thus behaved as if they were males, and indeed toward the female specimens. By normal males they were treated as females;

b) Males with female head behave passively toward both sexes. No preparation for copulation was observed in them. Normal males regard them with indifference.
