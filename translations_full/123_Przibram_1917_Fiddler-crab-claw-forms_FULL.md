## A. Programme of the Investigation.

In many heterochelous crabs the stronger development of the one claw is restricted solely to the male sex. There thus arose, in connection with my earlier experiments on claw-reversal in the decapod crustaceans, the problem of whether in such species unalterably fixed claw-relations are present, or whether it is possible to impose modifications upon them. Such modifications might, for instance, consist in the assumption of the claw-relations of the opposite sex, or in the reversal of the sides in the males.

A claw-exchange following autotomy of the large claw I had observed (1907) in *Athanas nitescens* ♂, a species in which, however, the heterochely is restricted to only a part of the males; in the genus *Xantho* both hetero- and homoeochelous species occur in both sexes (cf. Przibram 1905, p. 212).

Against this, direct regeneration in *Gelasimus* had been described by Zeleny (1905), a genus in which all males are distinguished by the possession of an extraordinarily enlarged "winking" claw; yet Baudouin (1903, 1906) obtained a report on a claw-exchange in *Gelasimus tangeri*, so that a further re-examination seemed desirable.

The first question therefore runs: Does claw-reversal or direct regeneration of the K-claw take place in the genus *Gelasimus*?

To this is then immediately joined the second question: Does the claw-relation in this genus allow itself to be modified at all by altering the mode of operation on the claws?

As a third question there presses upon us the inquiry whether, in the case of operations on the claws being successful, operations on the other characters would also be possible.

The fourth question concerns the influence which total extirpation of the claws exerts upon claw-regeneration: here interesting results emerge with respect to homoeosis in arthropods.

As a fifth question it shall be attempted to answer whether also in the genus *Gelasimus* the same rules hold good as for the other heterochelous forms, or whether the apparently deviating results could be traced back to the particular experimental circumstances.

## B. Provenance and Maintenance of the Living Material.

Since in none of the European marine stations I visited do species of the genus *Gelasimus* occur, and since suitable material of an Egyptian-Sudanese expedition was not brought in, I seized the opportunity, offered to me on the occasion of my participation in the international Zoologists' Congress at Boston 1907, to bring American fiddler crabs to Europe. Thanks to the kind care of the gentlemen Dr. Alsberg and Sumner I received from Woods Hole a tub with about 100 *Gelasimus pugnax* Smith, which, packed with moist seaweed and with several changes of seawater, reached the travel destination of Vienna in excellent condition on the crossing. Their maintenance proved very easy.¹ Isolated in battery- or hermit-glasses with a sandy bottom and a low seawater level, many of them held out for years. As a resting place and hiding place each crab was given a larger stone. For nourishment, cut-up fish served, the pieces of which were hung on threads into the glasses every second day and removed again the next day. The vessels were provided with wooden lids against the intrusion of dust and the eventual escape of the experimental animals, which were cut out on one side for the passage of the aeration tube. Yet continuous aeration proved not necessary at the low water level.

## C. Operative Technique.

The experimental technique practiced on the claws is very simple and follows entirely the one described in the fourth communication (1907, p. 273) as Op. 1, only with the difference that, for producing the autotomy, not a severing of the claw at the propodite but at the meropodite (cf. 1907, p. 275, Fig.) was employed.

Experiments with nerve-severing were not carried out, because these had already, in the earlier experiments, failed to reveal any particular influence beyond that of a delay in regeneration. In total extirpations the procedure followed exactly the earlier method "Op. 3," i.e. with a small pair of scissors the hip-segment was cut out of the body.

In this case too there was measured, as the length of the claw, after the regeneration, the distance of the tip of the preformed propodite from the lower angle of the break-fracture of the amputated claw. From a measurement of the total length of the whole crab, which would have required an artificial stretching of the folded-back carried abdomen, was refrained. The measured stretches are therefore: carapace length, carapace breadth, length of the right claw, of the right propodite, length of the left claw, of the left propodite. The reading was carried out on a millimeter-scale graduated into half millimeters; where deviations could still be observed, these were entered as + or — into the experimental protocols.

It still remains to speak about the castration and testis-implantation (which I shall here designate as Op. 2a and 2b). Since this part of the experiments came to grief on the early death of the experimental animals, I can keep myself very brief here. It was attempted, from the dorsal shield, to penetrate to the testes of the male narcotized with ether, and to implant these then into the female likewise opened from the back. The closure by collodion, however, prevented the bleeding only briefly and failed completely on contact with seawater.

## E. Answering the Programme Points.

### 1. Question: Does claw-reversal or direct regeneration of the K-claw take place in the genus *Gelasimus*?

In confirmation of the experiments of Zeleny on *Gelasimus pugilator*, the regenerated K-claws of *G. pugnax* returned again as K-claws after autotomy, without the Z-claw of the opposite side having undergone an essential change. Just as little did the remaining Z-claw of a female enlarge itself when the other Z-claw had been removed from it by autotomy or total extirpation.

### 2. Question: Does the claw-relation in this genus allow itself to be modified at all by altering the mode of operation on the claws?

The male fiddler crab Nr. 27 exhibited, through several molts and transitorily, two toothlet-claws; the formation and completion of the claws is also otherwise different with the various modes of operation, so that one need not think of an unalterable relation.

### 3. Question: Do the sex-organs have an influence on the claw-form?

This cannot, unfortunately, yet be answered, since the experimental animals did not survive the necessary operations.

### 4. Question: What happens in total extirpation?

After total extirpation there occurs in general a delay in the regeneration, which makes itself felt in the delayed appearance of the regeneration buds, their slower development and typical formation. Namely, in the case of simultaneous autotomy of the Z-claw, there occurs at the place of the totally extirpated K-claw cleft-leg- to walking-leg-similar transitional forms, such as have already been observed by me and others in deep-reaching operations on the appendages of other crustaceans (first by me on the maxilliped of the crabs 1901, 325).

— spaltbein-like to walking-leg-like transitional forms, such as have already been observed by me and others in deep-reaching operations on the limbs of other crustaceans (first by me on the maxilliped of the crabs, 1901, 325).

These cases are compiled in the paper: The Homoeosis in the Arthropods (1910). Judging from the results in *Gelasimus*, the homoeotic regenerates observed by Haseman in other crustaceans should likewise represent transitory stages, whose further development simply did not come under observation because no further moults followed. In a forthcoming paper on homoeotic feet on the antennae of *Sphodromantis*, I shall again give a compilation of the cases that have become known to me since the first paper on Homoeosis, and discuss them theoretically.

**5th Question:** Do the same rules hold for the claw-regeneration of *Gelasimus* as for most of the remaining heterochelous crustaceans?

After the answering of the previous questions, it might appear as if *Gelasimus* behaved differently from the other heterochelous crustaceans with respect to the regeneration and reversal of the large claw. This, however, need by no means be the case: in the "Claw-reversal" [Scherenumkehr] I was able to write, in summarizing the experimental results (p. 312, F, ε, 2): "The claw-reversal proceeds the more slowly the larger a crab is, so that at sizes above 10 mm carapace length at least two Z-claws are present transitorily."

Now all the *Gelasimus* used by me, and probably also those used by Zeleny, are above the stated limit, so that the direct regeneration of the K-claw, or transitory equal-clawedness under otherwise equal experimental conditions, was also to be foreseen in equally long specimens of those species of which it is known that smaller specimens exchange the claws after loss of the K-claw. Now the statements of Baudouin, which apparently contradict the other authors, can also be understood; he may have heard about smaller specimens; but since he himself seems not to have carried out any experiments, he could not stress the consideration of size.

That *Gelasimus*, in the claw-questions, also otherwise conforms to most of the remaining heterochelous crustaceans is shown further by our male No. 27, if we turn to page 310 of the "Claw-reversal": "If we take into consideration the results after bilateral 'autotomy', then the differences between reversing and directly regenerating forms become blurred still more: remarkably, it seems to be precisely those forms which most readily reverse after K-loss alone that never reverse after bilateral loss, whereas the more reluctantly reversing middle sizes (*Callianassa*, *Carcinus*) sometimes do so and sometimes refrain from it. In part this is explained by the fact that in slowly reversing ones a bilaterally very similar Z-Z stage is reached, which can equally easily form a K-claw on the one side as on the other; it has not so far been possible to discern what here gives the decisive turn...." In our male the equal-clawed condition persisted, undecided, through several moults, until, through autotomy of the one regenerate, a difference arose again, whereby the new regenerate developed into the K-claw (as before). In this connection it may also be pointed out that the bilateral autotomy of an abnormally equal-clawed lobster, according to Emmel (1907), again led to the regeneration of two toothed-claws [Zähnchenscheren]; here the disturbance was the same on both sides, so that no alteration of the claw-relations could come about.

## F. Summary.

1) The large "fiddler-claw" [Winkerschere], developed on the one side of the body in all males of *Gelasimus pugnax*, regenerated, in the specimens used — of over 10 cm carapace length — again directly as a fiddler-claw, corresponding to the K-claw of other heterochelous crabs of the same size.

2) After total extirpation of the fiddler-claw and autotomy of the smaller claw, the formation of two equal (Z-)claws can take place and persist through several moults. Upon subsequent autotomy of the former fiddler-claw, a fiddler-claw now regenerated at the next moult.

3) Males resembling the equal-clawed female with respect to the claws would therefore not have to be traced back to an alteration of the sexual glands (castration). On the other hand, the uninjured, further-growing claw of the females, even after total extirpation of the second Z-claw, took on no enlargement reminiscent of a fiddler-claw. Whether this would be possible through testis-implantation could unfortunately not be determined, since this operation was not survived by the experimental animals.

4) Total extirpation has, in comparison with autotomy, an essential delay of the regeneration process as a consequence. The regenerates, forming slowly in the course of several moults, pass through cleft-foot-like or walking-leg-like forms (transitory homoeosis).

5) According to the present experiments, the "fiddler-claw" of the *Gelasimus* male behaves no differently from the large or K-claws of most heterochelous crabs, so that in specimens substantially under 10 mm long claw-reversal should be expected to occur, and only the Paguridae, with their direct regeneration even in quite small specimens, retain their exceptional position.
