# Regeneration of the Extirpated Antenna and Eye in the Mealworm Beetle (Tenebrio molitor).

By

Isaak Werber.

[From the Biological Experimental Station in Vienna.]

With Plate XIV.

Received on 7 December 1904.

*Archiv für Entwicklungsmechanik der Organismen*, vol. 19 (1905).

> **Full translation.** A complete English rendering of the running text of “Regeneration of the Extirpated Antenna and Eye in the Mealworm Beetle (Tenebrio molitor)” (Werber, 1905), including all tables, figure and plate legends, and footnotes. Numbers and table cells were transcribed from the page images, not the noisy OCR.

The experiments carried out on the mealworm beetle by Tornier (1901) did indeed yield as their result the regenerative capacity of the antennae; it was not, however, entirely certain whether the entire antennae can be regenerated, since Tornier in his experiments had cut off the antennae and not extirpated them. In order to establish this, I now undertook the extirpation of the entire antenna. Since, however, it is difficult from a technical standpoint to remove the very small antenna entirely, in the larvae used for this experiment I cut off on the right side a piece of the head together with the antenna and the eye (Pl. XIV Fig. 1). Thus the right antenna and the right eye were now extirpated. The experiment, which unfortunately succeeded only in a relatively very small number of specimens, yielded as its result the regeneration of the entire antenna and of the eye. The course of the regeneration may be seen from the following experimental protocol:

## Series I.

### A. Operation.

30. IV. 1904. Right antenna and right eye extirpated in 6 specimens.

### B. Observations after the operation.

6. V. 1904. 2 specimens died.

7. V. 1904. 1 specimen

11. V. 1904. 2 specimens

20. V. 1904. One pupa.

30. V. 1904. On the pupa a regenerated antenna and a regenerated eye can be clearly seen.

6. VI. 1904. The pupa has transformed into the beetle (Pl. XIV Fig. 2). The regenerated right antenna counts only eight segments: thus three segments fewer than the primary one. The right (regenerated) eye differs somewhat from the normal one in outline and in external structure.

## Series II.

### A. Operation.

4. V. 1904. Right antenna and right eye extirpated in 17 specimens.

### B. Observations after the operation.

7. V.–11. VI. 1904. 16 specimens died.

21. VI. 1904. Molting of one larva, antenna and eye regenerated. (Preserved.)

After the operation a very strong bleeding occurred, after which a wound scab formed at the injured site. Since the operation was a very deep-reaching one, almost all specimens died off. Only two remained alive, in which I was able to ascertain regenerates of the right antenna and of the right eye. In one case it was a pupa, in which the regenerates could be seen after the transformation from the larva, and in the other a larva, in which the regenerates came to light after the casting off of the molt. The regenerated eight-segmented antenna of the imago is probably entirely equivalent, with respect to its external constitution, to the one described by Tornier (Zool. Anz. 1901), wherefore I refrain from entering into a description of it and refer to the photographic plate. Of greater interest here is no doubt the regeneration of the eye, since such a case is not yet known among insects. Unfortunately, however, I must for the present restrict myself merely to the announcement of this fact, since owing to a lack of material (only two specimens with regenerates, of these one larva and one beetle) I was not in a position to undertake an exact histological investigation of the regeneration process and of the regenerates. Since these experiments must still be continued, I will then return to this in a forthcoming communication.
