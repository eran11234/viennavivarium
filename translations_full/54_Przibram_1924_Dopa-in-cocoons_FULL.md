## 1. Colour adaptation in moth cocoons.

After Barber (1874) had described colour adaptation of the pupa in a swallowtail species, numerous similar phenomena have been found in butterfly pupae. As Mr. Dr. Brecher and I (1922, Colour-clothing VII) were able to establish, this adaptation proceeds through the caterpillar's eye²) and is accomplished by means of a light-sensitive tyrosinase, which is able to oxidise the tyrosine present in the pupal body to various colours (1919, Colour-clothing I, II; Brecher 1917, 1919, 1921, 1922). Now, however, the cocoons of certain moths likewise have an adaptation to the surroundings, insofar as they are mostly found dark in dark places and light in light places.

> ¹) A preliminary communication appeared under the same title as No. 103 from the Biological Experimental Institute of the Academy of Sciences in the Akad. Anz. Wien 1923, No. 17.

> ²) In Vol. 5 of my "Experimentalzoologie," 1914, p. 25, on the contrary one reads: "Extirpation of the eyes in the caterpillar also changes nothing in the outcome of the experiments." At that time I had not yet carried out any experiments of my own and believed that Poulton (1890, p. 140), to whose experiments this citation refers, meant by blinding ("larvae blinded") the removal of the eyes. He has, however — as is indeed evident from various other passages of his writings — merely undertaken the painting-over of the eyes with black colour, which, as we have found, by no means prevents the penetration of stronger light (cf. L. Brecher, Verhandl. d. zool.-bot. Ges., Wien 9. December 1921).

A first such case is described in Wallace, "Tropical Nature" (1890, p. 143), an observation of *Poulton's* own. On the adaptation of W. H. *Harwood* (Colchester), *Poulton* (1886) established the same thing for the lighter night-pupal-suit, *Saturnia carpini*. W. J. H. *Newman* (1887) likewise found it for *Poulton* in *Eriogaster lanestris*. The experiences gathered in free nature were at first confirmed in captivity, in that the caterpillars were set with deliberate intention on black and white paper for the purpose of producing darker and lighter cocoons, or were left upon the spun-over twigs. The results were in fact actually the same. As, however, *Bateson* (1892) established a stricter uniformity in the treatment-conditions for *Saturnia carpini* and *Eriogaster lanestris* (over the colour-adaptation), an adaptation by colour could not be confirmed, and the conclusion was reached that here only a chance similarity might exist. This disagreement was however resolved by the investigations of *Dewitz* (1905—1921); he set aside the colour-adaptation and found the moisture, effective above all for *Saturnia*, *Eriogaster* and other cocoons, to be the promoting factor. Since in free nature mostly dark, dry places appear, in the dark they are found mostly dark, in the light light places are found. Likewise paper and rough twigs would now appear differently coloured, lighter cocoons would be spun, since in the rearing of the caterpillars in containers from plants, which afford a certain moisture, this moisture is preserved. If these overspinnings were placed correctly, as in *Poulton* and others, it was indeed ventured against the colour of the surroundings, but to escape the withdrawal of the moisture, which brought forth an approximation of the cocoon-colour to the surrounding colour. There thus remains only the fact established, that in free nature a certain agreement between cocoon-colour and surrounding-colour is present, but the explanation for it through such causality afforded by the colour itself was inadmissible. To make the cocoon-spinning dependent on the usefulness of such an agreement is a procedure that can scarcely be pursued. It seemed therefore appropriate to subject the objects anew to experiments, following the proceedings as before. As *Eriogaster lanestris*, which I have at my disposal (about these experiments I have already reported briefly in the *Hofmeister*-Festschrift 1922), did not yield the *Dewitz'schen* statements, in order, however, to convince myself of the correctness of the same for *Saturnia*, I also undertook for *Saturnia*, although under various moisture-relations and in the darkness, to bring numerous caterpillars to overspinning, and also brought numerous fresh cocoons into water and afterwards placed them subsequently in darkness, as *Dewitz* had stated.¹)

> ¹) The directory of the species so far known with variable cocoon-colour is appended at the close of the present work.

With *Eriogaster*, 15 cocoons were spun over light in white, black, yellow, grey surroundings and in the darkness, without moisture; were they, on the contrary, subsequently moistened, they were spun over dark. It would perhaps therefore be most likely to assume that the penetration of water directly into the colour of the cocoon, in that it changed the colour of the reflecting interstitial surfaces, would alter the colour, whereby the difference in the natural surrounding-objects would arise. Then the blackening would have to be reversed again by subsequent drying-out; both is, however, not the case. Once an entered blackening is no longer brought back even by the strongest drying-out, and there are indeed species whose cocoons, by their texture similar to *Bombyx*, no longer change their colour upon immersion in water, as one has learned in *Anthera pernyii* and the mulberry-spinner, *Bombyx mori*. *Anthera* belongs, like *Saturnia*, to the night-peacock-eyes, and likewise gives a cocoon similar to that of the species of the genus *Saturnia*; a pale-greenish cocoon. Moistening brought about no darkening; it remains all the same light-greenish. *Bombyx*, on the contrary, the caterpillar spins a black colour, used in the cocoon. Here neither the surrounding-colour nor the moisture exerted an influence on the colour-change. In any case the result is, with regard to the retention of inherited cocoon-colour in dependence on moisture, that one knew one had to keep the pupae [away] with hot water, in which one expects a physical colour-change instead of the adaptation of the cocoon-colour; one could come upon a chemical [one]. Of course a light-sensitive substance must therefore remain entirely chemical. This chemical-suit must likewise be the cocoon-fibres themselves, no liberation being able [to occur] through penetration into the cocoon-fibres, of an organ of the caterpillar or pupa sensitive to light, to be addressed. Thereby it was very probable that some kind of analogy of the procedure of pupal-adaptation with the cocoon-adaptation [exists]. The dark, light-sensitive tyrosine even of the eye could be the latter into consideration. To this comes still in the substance the iron-bonds present; even with a higher water-content, melanin could form, as the dark substance is bound. Such a substance has even been learned in the 3,4-dioxyphenylalanin, *Bloch's* "Dopa." Dopa (cf. 1921, Colour-clothing IV) blackens spontaneously upon the addition of water, i.e. without ferment-addition, in the dark; especially rapidly and intensively does the melanin-formation occur in the presence of alkali. Now *Dewitz* has even referred to the alkalinity of the caterpillar-secretion, to the presence of carbonate of soda in the spinning-glands. There would thus also still be the melanin-formation of a dopa with the necessary further substances: namely alkali, oxygen and water, of which the former is present in the cocoon-mass itself, the second in the surrounding air, and the moisture-water equally present at the entrance of the cocoon. Is now dopa demonstrable in the cocoons? Of this however one can very easily convince oneself. It is sufficient to prepare a watery extract from the cocoons of *Saturnia* or *Eriogaster* spun over in dryness, and to carry out the reactions characteristic for dopa. Most positive turns out the iron-chloride-reaction, which the dioxyphenyls indicate, to which the orthodioxyphenylalanin (e.g. as *Halimasch* prepared) also belongs, and in particular, for differentiation, likewise the catechin-belonging brenzcatechin (e.g. as *Halimasch* prepared), which besides dopa likewise also as tyrosine, but not the brenzcatechin, for *Saturnia* and *Eriogaster*, with 2—5 drops upon a watery extract, strongly blackened. But if 2—3 drops of concentrated sodium-carbonate are carefully added, there enters at once a colour-change of the soil of the test-tube, rising to a beautiful cardinal-violet. About the colour-changes which the dopa-demonstration makes possible, the work in the *Biochemischen Zeitschrift* has already reported at the cited place (1922, p. 290—292), so that the demonstration has generally been made that the light-sensitive tyrosinase plays no role in the melanin-formation in the colour-changeable cocoons of moths. With the blackening of the cocoons one has, even without dopa-demonstration of the extracts, which remain colour-changeable, [a process] enzymatically weak, always weaker than corresponds to the expectation when one produces melanin from dopa. If dopa is really that substance which leads to the dark-colouring of the cocoons of *Saturnia* and *Eriogaster*, then the close-lying [conclusion was confirmed] for the non-colour-changeable cocoons of *Anthera* and *Bombyx* and the lack of dopa in their spinning-products. The factors alkali, oxygen and water were now even not in the situation to call forth melanin. The cocoon of these kinds would therefore even be incapable of the colour-adaptation of the dopa-stated procedures. A further proof for the correctness of our consideration leaves itself further procure thereby. To this aim it sufficed even to bring the natural colourless dopa-yielding cocoons to blackening through impregnation with dopa. To this purpose let the cocoons of *Bombyx* [be taken], at best with removal of the outer, disordered tissue. Becomes such a cocoon of the length accordingly cut into strips, and one of the same immersed in water, one in a tyrosine-solution, one in a brenzcatechin-solution, one in a dopa-solution, which begins at first colourless and dry, then in a few days the strip immersed in dopa takes on a black colouring, while it remains immersed colourless, the brenzcatechin-solution remaining light reddish. The black colour of the dopa-strip is not changed by subsequent drying-out, whereas the dark dopa-demonstration-particles of the surrounding cocoon-particles [adhere] to the cocoon-fibres. The dark colouring thus remains subsequently dried-out. The silk of the cocoon lying on the surface shows analogous relationships, only with much less intensive blackening. This experiment seems nevertheless, although in the cocoon of *Bombyx mori* no effective tyrosinase can be present — for even upon the immersion in tyrosine, from this the blackening must be grounded, the extract obtained from the silk-glands of the *Saturnia* or *Eriogaster* strongly blackened, and therewith one knows that the blackening in dopa is not led back through the help of co-active oxidising ferments in the cocoon-fibres.

## 2. Colour modifications in sawfly cocoons.

Until now the moths were the single group with which colour-changeable cocoons were known and brought into connection with colour-adaptation. As I reared, for other purposes (Homöosis), sawfly-larvae of the genus *Cimbex axillaris* Panz., the differing colouring of the cocoons struck me, varying from a bright Havannabraun to a black-brown. Attempts to imitate these variations through altered illumination failed, just as with the moths. A difference could be noticed according to white, black, yellow, grey background as against those taken in captivity, when otherwise this spraying was applied in quite identical containers. Now, larger quantities of caterpillars had at first been separated in the darkness into [three] parts, of which the one [was] not at all, the second daily weakly, the third strongly sprayed. The consequence showed just as clearly, or perhaps still more clearly than with *Saturnia* and *Eriogaster*, the influence of the moisture. The unsprayed caterpillars spun quite light, the somewhat moistened-sprayed [spun], besides the watered, quite dark cocoons. Were the cocoons spun in dryness and various darkness subsequently moistened, then according to the intensity of the moistening any desired degree of blackening could be reached. The application of the moisture can take place by direct spraying, laying in water, or laying upon moist filter-paper (mould-fungi are to be kept away). As I thus came upon a large number of the gregariously living sawfly *Lophyrus pini* L., I did not let this opportunity go unused, in order to investigate the sensitivity of their cocoon-colour to moisture. The result was quite analogous: in the dry [state] light, light-grey to yellowish, then with rising moisture an ever darker brown colouring. We thus have before us, with these sawflies, the same dependence of the colour on the surroundings as with the colour-variable cocoons of moths. I was now eager to see whether the same chemism as with *Saturnia* and *Eriogaster* would also be decisive for the blackening by the moisture with *Cimbex* and *Lophyrus*. Thus those reactions which were already applied by us for the demonstration of the dopa content and for excluding a tyrosinase-action with the butterfly-cocoons were repeated on the cocoons of these two sawfly-species. The dopa-reactions (iron-chloride, sodium-carbonate; ammonia; silver-nitrate; sodium-hydroxide; tyrosinase) turned out positive, the tyrosinase-tests (tyrosine; peas) doubtful. There exists thus no doubt that also in the colour-changeable cocoons of sawflies dopa is present and responsible for the melanin-formation. In the colours themselves each of the sawflies showed peculiarities which may still be traced back to particular substances; thus a ¹⁄₁₀₀—¹⁄₁₀ normal-hydrochloric-acid-prepared extract of *Lophyrus*-cocoon coloured itself pink, while under the same conditions the three other species yielded uncoloured extracts. My initial supposition, that it might be a matter of a reaction of the food-plant, could not be confirmed. *Cimbex*-cocoons, heated to 80°, develop a pleasant smell standing between chocolate and malt, and take on a somewhat more reddish tone. In tyrosine, an inhibited cocoon of this species coloured itself pink, which might perhaps be traced back to a tyrosinase of slight effectiveness or also to another "fermentation-ferment" (cf. *Gortner* in Colour-clothing I, p. 120). The extracts appear golden-yellow in tyrosine, red in ¹⁄₁₀ normal-hydrochloric-acid.

## 3. Chemical sites of attack of melanisation.

If on the one hand in the pupae melanin is produced from tyrosine by tyrosinase, on the other hand in cocoons from dopa without ferment-action, then we must put to ourselves the question whether in the course of the former process dopa is not at first oxidised from tyrosine, and only then this is further condensed to melanin. Then the melanin-formation could proceed uniformly everywhere without ferment, and it would only come down to whether dopa is present first, or such must first be produced through synthesis of a hydroxyl-group from tyrosine under the influence of a light-sensitive ferment. To answer this question, I have examined "spontaneously" coloured tyrosine-samples of various colour-series, collected over several years, for the presence of dopa-traces, but found no sign of dopa-formation. If thus indeed first a dopa-formation occurs, then this must lie quite at the very beginning of the "starting-up," perhaps still before the visibility of the starting-colour.

Already earlier I have (1921, Colour-clothing IV, p. 161) drawn the conclusion from the quantitatively equal attack of tyrosine and dopa by the same tyrosinase, that the chemical site must be sought in the "side-chain" common to both substances. This same argument now speaks against the interposition of a dopa-formation in the tyrosinase-action upon tyrosine, for one would then at least thereby have to expect a certain delay of the tyrosine- as compared with the dopa-blackening, whereas equimolecular solutions agree quite exactly in time in the melanisation-strength. That brenzcatechin, distinguished from dopa merely by the lack of the side-chain, is not subject to the tyrosinase-action (ibid., p. 159), is a further reason to place the tyrosinase-attack into the side-chain, for which moreover also yet the parallel of the attackability of side-chains in other light-sensitive processes has already been adduced (1919, Colour-clothing III, p. 268). It has also repeatedly (1919, Colour-clothing I, p. 119; 1919, Colour-clothing III, p. 269; 1921, Colour-clothing IV, p. 161) been pointed out by me, how this theory accords with the temporary desamidation in the "red-stage." If this red-stage of tyrosine were nothing other than the dopa robbed of its side-chain, then it ought as brenzcatechin to show the orthodioxyphenyl-reaction, which it however, as we heard, does not do¹). In contrast to the tyrosinase-reaction common to tyrosine and dopa, lacking in brenzcatechin, the responding to the addition of strong soda-lye with yellow-colouring, lacking in tyrosine, is proper to dopa and brenzcatechin (cf. 1922, *Hofmeister*-Festschrift, pp. 290 and 292). The point of attack for this yellow-colouring should therefore lie in that group which dopa has in common with brenzcatechin, but which is lacking in tyrosine, i.e. the hydroxyl in position 4.

> ¹) In *Winterstein's* Handbook of comparative Physiology, Vol. I, 1, p. 669, 1923, Fr. N. *Schulz* subjects our pigment-formation works to a by no means unbiased criticism. When he says: "The whole presentation is one-sidedly cut out to the effect that really such simple relations are present," I do not regard this as a reproach, for it is one, or even the task of science, to reduce the complicated to the simple, to present the unsurveyable in a simpler manner. When, however, he then plays up the splitting of the tyrosinase into two components by *Haehn* (1919—1921) — which so competent a researcher as *Chodat* (1922) moreover rejects — as an essential advance over against us, then he has evidently overlooked or forgotten the cited passages from my writings, which have anticipated the stages of the melanin-formation quite similarly as he depicts them after *Haehn*, without having needed the two-component-thesis contested by *Chodat*.

From the yellow colour there develops, at weaker alkaline grades (n/40 and below), melanin in the case of Dopa; but in the case of Brenzkatechin [pyrocatechol] the colour recedes and fades to a pale grey. This may be interpreted as follows: the begun colour-development is able to advance to melanin formation only when a side-chain of suitable kind is available, which is indeed the case with Dopa, but not with Brenzkatechin. Just as through the earlier works, we thus arrive at the result that the melanin formation consists, in the last analysis, in the recruitment of the side-chain, although the first point of attack need not be located there. It remains still open whether, besides the site of attack to be assumed in the side-chain through light-sensitive Tyrosinase and the non-enzymatic process attacking at position 4, there is also still played out an enzymatic dark reaction, which would be common to Tyrosin [tyrosine] and Dopa and would have to lie in the nucleus (cf. 1919, Farbkleidung [Colour-clothing] III, p. 268). As the site, position 3 would then come into consideration, which as a hydroxyl would be favourable for the further oxidation (cf. 1921, Farbkleidung IV, p. 161). That would perhaps be that same site which conditions the spontaneous browning of Brenzkatechin, to which this group indeed also belongs, but without an encroachment of the process onto the side-group being able to lead to melanin deposition. Further progress in this field will depend in large part on reliable analyses of the melanins produced by various routes.

## 4. Summary.

As described elsewhere (Biochem. Zeitschr., Vol. 127, *Hofmeister*-Festschrift, p. 286, 1922), "the colour-development of the pupal cocoons of certain butterflies (*Eriogaster*, *Saturnia*) is a typical Dopa reaction." It takes place through the spontaneous melanin formation of the Dopa contained in the cocoon-threads of these genera upon the access of water. The presence of Dopa (3,4-dioxyphenylalanine) in the cocoons spun on a light, dry ground can readily be demonstrated by the typical Dopa reactions (with ferric chloride, sodium carbonate, caustic soda [sodium hydroxide] solution of various grades, silver nitrate). The extension of the experiments to cocoons of sawflies (*Cimbex*, *Lophyrus*) yielded exactly the same result: here too the dark colouration appearing on a moist, dark ground is to be traced back to melanin formation through the demonstrable Dopa. Since now, out of doors, with great moisture most objects appear dark, and with great dryness light, the Dopa content of these cocoons serves an adaptation to the brightness of the substrate, which, contrary to that in the colour-variable butterfly pupae [day-butterfly pupae], is brought about not by light factors but by moisture factors. In the cocoons the spontaneous blackening of the Dopa is employed, whereas in the day-butterfly pupae the light-sensitivity of the Tyrosinase ferment plays a role in the melanin formation from Tyrosin, which is, moreover, influenced by the eye.

Since Dopa differs from Tyrosin merely by the addition of a hydroxyl group at position 4 of the carbon ring, it would have been possible that the melanin formation from Tyrosin proceeded by way of Dopa. But since I have not succeeded in finding Dopa, even merely in traces, in the precursors of the melanin forming from Tyrosin by means of the very sensitive reactions cited above, the oxidation leading to melanin would presumably not attack at position 4 of the Tyrosin molecule.

Conversely, the point of attack of the oxidation in the spontaneous melanisation of the Dopa would presumably lie precisely at position 4, since this rapid, non-enzymatic melanin formation, much promoted by caustic soda solution, is lacking in Tyrosin. The site of attack in the fermentative, light-sensitive Tyrosinase action must, on the other hand, lie in the side-chain, for this is the same in Dopa and Tyrosin, which are attacked quantitatively alike; if it is lacking, as in the Brenzkatechin otherwise not differing from Dopa, then the enzymatic melanisation through Tyrosinase is lacking as well. Whether dark reactions do not at the same time take place, say, at the hydroxyl group at position 3 common to Tyrosin and Dopa, and whether, despite the different sequence of the sites first attacked, one and the same end product does not come about in the various melanin formations, cannot at present be determined for lack of exact analyses of the melanins from Dopa and Tyrosin.
