H. Przibram found that, both in the cocoon of certain butterflies (*Eriogaster, Saturnia*, see Hofmeisterfestschrift, Biochem. Zeitschrift, 127, p. 286, 1922) and in the cocoon of certain hymenopterans (*Cimbex, Lophyrus*, Arch. f. mikr. Anat. u. Entwicklungsmech. 102, p. 624. 1924), a dioxyphenylalanine was formed, called "Dopa" by Bloch.

I carried out investigations on larvae and pupae of *Lophyrus Pini* L. to establish at what time in the life of this insect this dopa develops and whether it localizes itself in some part of the body.

To establish clearly whether the formation of the Dopa occurred in the larva or in the pupa, I tested the first reaction of Przibram both in the one and in the other. Taking a larva that had not yet abandoned the plant to spin its cocoon, I cut it into pieces, separating from one another the various segments of the body, isolating the intestine and the head. I did this hoping, if the Dopa had formed in the larva, to be able to determine in which part of the body it develops or localizes itself. On each little piece of larva I poured a few drops of a very dilute solution of ferric chloride, I added a drop of acetic acid, and finally a few drops of a saturated solution of sodium carbonate. If the reaction had been positive, one should have obtained first an emerald-green coloration that, with the addition of sodium carbonate, should have changed into crimson red. The reaction was instead always negative; first the ferric chloride remained unaltered, then with the addition of sodium carbonate one obtained an abundant yellow precipitate. But the little piece of larva did not change color, and this precipitate is obtained also by making the sodium carbonate react on the ferric chloride in the presence of traces of acetic acid. Since the reaction was negative on the larva in the cold, I attempted another route. I took several larvae, I put them in a little tube with a bit of water and boiled the whole thing repeatedly. I divided the extract thus obtained from these larvae into several portions and tested the reaction on the various portions of this liquid, both while it was still hot and after it had already cooled. But I obtained the same results obtained on the little pieces of larva in the cold. In the doubt that the extract had not been well prepared, I took some larvae and triturated them finely in water, then boiled this water with the larvae cut into tiny pieces, and tested the reaction again. But the reaction on this extract was always negative, both in the cold and in the hot. Finally, as a last test, I extracted with a syringe the blood of several larvae and tested the reaction on this liquid; the result was always negative.

Then I tested the reaction on the larvae that had already abandoned the plant and were already beginning to spin their cocoon. In this case too I cut the larvae into pieces and tested the reaction on the various pieces in the cold. The reaction was always positive, but the emerald-green coloration was a little dark, and with the addition of sodium carbonate one obtained a crimson red not very intense. Making, however, the extract also of these larvae, the reaction occurred normally and the reaction was entirely similar to that which was obtained with little pieces of cocoon. The slight difference in color that was obtained in the cold had to be attributed to some other cause; very probably to the presence of much melanin which masked with its darkness the true coloration. I then tested the reaction on the larvae that had almost finished spinning their cocoon. But both on little pieces of their body and on their hot or cold extract the reaction was always negative. This shows that the Dopa had already passed into the cocoon, where indeed one had a positive reaction. Finally I tested the reaction also on the pupae; I opened some cocoons, took the pupae and cut them into pieces. On the various pieces I tested the reaction in vain, which always gave a negative result. The same negative result I obtained with the hot or cold extract of these pupae. On the little pieces of cocoon, instead, the reaction always gave positive results, both in the cold and on the hot or cold extract of the cocoons. In this case, however, the reaction was much more rapid, and this because with the boiling the Dopa was dissolved all or almost all, and one therefore had a greater quantity of substance, all the more since I used several cocoons to obtain the extract.

From all these experiences I have therefore been able to conclude that the Dopa forms only when the larva abandons the plant to spin its cocoon. This dopa is spread a little everywhere, since I obtained the positive reaction by taking various pieces of the body of the larva. This dopa remains very little in the body of the larva and passes immediately into the cocoon. The cocoon is not even finished being spun when the dopa has already entirely passed into it.

## 666 I. Sciacchitano: When the Dopa Forms in the Cocoons of *Lophyrus Pini* L.

### Summary.

H. Przibram has found that, just as in the pupal cocoons of certain butterflies (*Eriogaster, Saturnia*), so too in those of sawflies, dioxyphenylalanine (Bloch's "Dopa") can be demonstrated by its characteristic chemical reactions. I have investigated at what time of larval life these reactions turn out positive, that is, when the presence of the Dopa would be ascertainable. I used only the first of the groups of reactions employed by Przibram (which is also characteristic of the other ortho-phenyls, but since here it was no longer a matter of the demonstration of which ortho-phenyl is in question, it is sufficient for determining the time of appearance of the Dopa). I thus placed the cocoons or larval pieces into dilute ferric chloride solution, added a trace of acetic acid, and finally a few drops of saturated sodium carbonate solution. The reaction on the cocoon pieces was, as was to be expected from the cited experiments of Przibram, always positive, likewise in aqueous cocoon extracts. In the larvae that had not yet left the plants to pupate, the reaction always turned out negative. I divided the larvae into several pieces, but nowhere could a positive result be achieved at this stage, just as little through aqueous extractions. Also the blood of the larva drawn out with a syringe gave a negative reaction. On the contrary, in the larvae that had already left the plants to spin the cocoon, the reaction was always positive, although the color changes (emerald-green before, cardinal-violet after the addition of the sodium carbonate) were not so pronounced as on the cocoons; possibly because of the abundant melanin, which dulls the colors. In the aqueous extract of the larvae of the same stage the reaction was clearly pronounced. In the larvae, finally, that had already spun the cocoons, even before these were completed, no positive reaction could be perceived. I also took the larvae out of the cocoon some time after completion of the cocoon, with the same result.

As a result of the experiments it thus follows that in the larva (Afterraupe) of this sawfly the Dopa appears only during the spinning of the cocoon, passes over onto the cocoon threads, but has disappeared again from the larva proceeding to pupation within the cocoon.
