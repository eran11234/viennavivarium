## I. Introduction.

In experiments which I carried out in the early summer of 1914 at the Biological Experimental Institute in Vienna, and concerning which a report appears in another place in this journal (p. 13), I carried out a number of temperature measurements on young rats in various surroundings, partly as control measurements, partly belonging to the experiments. It seemed to me worth the trouble to group these data and to publish them, since up to now only little is known about the temperatures of such young animals, in the stage of the strongest growth.

The material consisted of young rats, which were bred in the well-known temperature chambers of the Biological Experimental Institute. The animals lived there together with the parents in their cage and were marked on the ears, whereby confusion of the individuals was excluded. The measurements of the temperature were always made on a whole litter of the young animals. Before, however, the results are to be discussed, it is necessary to say something about the technique of these temperature measurements.

## II. Technique of the Measurements.

For the measurement of the body temperature a mercury thermometer was used, which had a total length of about 10 cm and ended in a bulb that was 18 mm long and had a diameter of 3 mm. The scale made it possible to read off temperatures from 27° to

43° C accurately to 0.2° C. When the mercury did not exactly reach a scale division, the nearest one was assumed to be the correct temperature value. The mercury could, in contrast to the usual fever thermometers, sink without further intervention and thus follow the temperature fluctuations during the measurement, which is absolutely necessary for a reliable measurement.

For the measurement the animal was taken in the left hand and laid on its back. With two fingers of the left hand the tail was held fast, and the thermometer, made slippery with a little Vaseline, after it had been pre-warmed with the right hand to about 30°, was by means of this then pushed into the rectum up to the end of the bulb. It is of the greatest importance to push the thermometer in always equally far, for a somewhat deeper or less deep insertion can be the cause that the thermometer notes apparent temperature differences of up to 1° C. Sumner¹) for this purpose made a rubber ring around the neck of the bulb and always pushed the thermometer into the rectum up to this ring; I do not believe that this is necessary, if one only keeps a certain point of the bulb in memory. To this comes that one, on the other hand, must also not take care all too anxiously that the bulb always disappears up to a definite point behind the anal sphincter, for the somewhat older animals often make in- and out-everting movements with the anus during the measurement, especially when they have just defecated, and if one follows these anal movements too painstakingly with the thermometer, the temperature read off always remains fluctuating. With some practice, however, one soon learns to find the point up to which the bulb is to be lifted into the rectum, in order in such a case to be able to compare the temperature with that of the non-moving animal.

As soon as the thermometer is inserted, the mercury begins to rise, and has reached its high point after about ⅔ minute. If nothing happens, the mercury stays standing at this height, and so in the normal case the temperature was always read off after one minute and regarded as the correct rectal temperature. Changes occur only when the animal begins to wriggle; hereby the thermometer is pushed in deeper, or pushed out more or less, but when the animal has ceased these movements, the mercury at once returns to its earlier level. If the animal remained restless, it was set back into the cage for a few minutes, after which it was then always much quieter.

> ¹) Francis B. Sumner. The effects of atmospheric temperature upon the body temperature of mice. Journ. of exper. Zool. XV. 1913.

A lasting temperature elevation I have, after such short struggles, never observed; with the rest the temperature also comes back lastingly to its earlier level.

Another disturbance arises when the animals begin to defecate during the measurement. The approach of the feces always shows itself by a sudden rise of the mercury, which comes into contact with the warm feces. After the detachment of the feces, however, the mercury at once sinks back again to the correct height. Only this (especially in the early morning often vigorous) defecating often makes the temperature measurements rather time-consuming.

I have not had the experience of Congdon¹), that it is necessary first to accustom the animals for some time (3 weeks!) to the thermometer and the handling. I have, however, worked mostly with quite young rats, and it is possible that with older rats it is more necessary. The young animals are much more passive and patient than the older ones; never has it happened to me with young animals, as once with animals of 55 days, that it was impossible to measure the temperatures because the animals remained too restless. To be sure I have a few times seen that the very first measurement in young animals gives a somewhat too low temperature, and have taken account of that. That it was not necessary to accustom the young animals to the handling is also shown by Table I, where one finds no trace of the temperature elevation feared by Congdon in the first days, although the animals before the beginning of this series had been measured only a few times.

## III. The Fluctuations of the Body Temperature.

The differences of the body temperatures one can in general bring into two groups, namely 1. those conditioned by inner factors (sex, time of day, etc.) and 2. the consequences of the temperature of the surroundings. First the inner factors are taken into consideration.

### A. Dependence of the Body Temperatures on Inner Factors.

That various inner factors influence the body temperatures of the animals is no novelty, and I could leave it unmentioned, were it not that I, through a series of measurements over 10

consecutive days, could trace the role of some of these factors in young rats. These measurements were made on a litter of 7 young rats, which, born in the laboratory stalls, had been brought on the 37th day of life (one day before the beginning of the measurements) into a chamber kept uniformly at 25° C. Each day the morning temperature and mostly also the evening temperature of these rats was measured (Table I). This was the temperature course that I followed for the longest time. Through the unalterability of the temperature of the experimental chamber, the influence of the surroundings is hereby excluded from the variations.

At first glance the **sex differences** stand out here. In all measurements the mean values of the females were distinctly higher than those of the males; also the absolutely highest temperature (37.2° C) and the absolutely lowest (34.8° C) occurred in a female and a male respectively. This difference was greatest on the afternoon of December 5th, when the 4 males had on the average a temperature of 35.5°, the 3 females of 36.8°. On the average the sex difference amounted to 0.74° C. Since the matter here concerns animals of about 6 weeks, which therefore are not yet sexually mature, these sex differences are not influenced by being in heat or by pregnancy.

Similar differences Sumner (opus citatum) found in mice that were 8—12 months old. On the average the temperature of the females here was 0.76° C higher than that of the males. Yet Sumner's results are not well comparable with mine, because they are other animals of another age, while also the temperatures of the surroundings were not constant. Congdon (opus citatum) unfortunately neglected the differences of the sexes in his investigations. Better comparable are my results with those of Przibram¹). He found in *M. rattus* of 426—503 days, at 25° outside temperature, as mean value in females 36.25° and in males 35.80°, that is a sex difference of 0.45° C. And in *Mus decumanus* albino of 92—242 days (I use only the figures which he himself interpreted as well usable), as sex difference: at 5° outside temperature 1.05°; at 15°: 0.2°; at 20°: 0.5°; at 25°: 0.1°; at 30°: 0.4° and at 35°: 0.9°, on the average therefore, if one neglects the extreme temperatures, a sex difference of 0.3° C. Besides with the sex, the body temperatures also vary with the time of day. Yet these variations in our case were not very great. I have in Table II assembled these differences between morning and evening temperature somewhat more clearly. Almost always the evening temperatures were somewhat higher, on the average 0.14° in the males and 0.28° in the females, or 0.16° in the whole. Only once (13. XII.) had the mean temperatures fallen somewhat in both sexes, whereby, however, it must be kept in mind that after the 10th of December the evening temperature could be given by only three animals. The influence of the time of day is therefore not great, which is probably connected with the calm and uniform life of the animals in the cages and the unalterability of the outside temperature.

Then the body temperature varies further with the day. Best one compares here the morning temperatures (10.30—11 o'clock A.M.). Days with low temperature were the 5th of December (here the evening too was low) and the 18th of December. High days were the 6th of February and the 13th of December. Causes for these differences cannot be given; only two of the high days fall on a Saturday (6. XII. and 13. XII.), when the animals in the experimental institute received meat as fodder, so that here perhaps an influence of the nutrition is to be recognized. But other causes too (restlessness of the animals, e.g. through the cleaning of the cages) may have raised the temperatures.

The **individual variation** too was not lacking. Thus the male *a* almost always had the lowest, the female *e* always the highest body temperature. On the average the individual differences among the males amounted to 0.4° C, among the females to 0.2° C.

Finally, attention may still be drawn to the fact that the animals of Table I, although they had only on the 1st of December, that is one day before the beginning of the measurements, been brought from the unheated stalls into the 25°-chamber, yet showed no rise of temperature during their stay in the chambers. On the first day of the series the mean temperatures were 36.3° C and on the 16th day 36.1°! The animals seemed to have adapted themselves at once. About their temperatures in the stalls I unfortunately have no reliable data, but I can only say that the temperatures there were noticeably lower.

### B. Influencing of the Body Temperature by the Temperature of the Surroundings.

To this problem, which is more important for the question of the environment of the germ-plasm and which in this respect was already studied by Congdon and Przibram, I can contribute a series of measurements on quite young animals of about 3½ weeks (Tables III—IX) in surroundings that ranged with 5° difference from 10°—35° C. Hereby only animals were worked with that had been born in the temperatures in question.

Table III gives the temperatures in the 35°-chamber. This was a litter of 6 animals, unfortunately by chance all males, and the animals were already somewhat older than the others, namely 37 days. The temperature varied between 37.0° and 38.0° and was on the average 37.50°. Of animals that agree in age with those of the other tables, I had data only over two days (Table IV); since between the two days there is a rather strong difference, I cannot regard these data as of equal value with those of the other table. The single female of this litter had as mean value of the two days 38.00, the males 37.80. The total mean value was 37.85. The sex difference was 0.2° C, the mean value somewhat higher than in the animals of Table III.

Table V gives the body temperatures of a litter of 4 females and 3 males at 30° outside temperature. The temperatures varied from 37.2°—37.8°, the mean values were 37.50 for both sexes, so that a sex difference does not come to the fore. At 25° outside temperature (Table VI) the temperatures vary from 36.4° to 37.2° and the mean values are for females 36.85, males 36.80, total mean value 36.80. Here one has therefore a small sex difference of 0.05° C. The temperatures fall and the sex differences become greater when one comes into the cold: at 20° (Table VII) the mean values are for females 36.30, males 36.15, total mean value 36.20, sex difference therefore 0.15; at 15° the figures are respectively 35.55, 35.45, 35.50 and 0.10 (Table VIII). Finally, at 10° (Table IX) the figures are respectively 34.80, 34.60, 34.75 and 0.20.

In Table X the results of Tables III—IX are assembled (those of Table IV are placed between brackets as somewhat less reliable). It shows itself at first glance that the body temperatures rise with the temperature of the surroundings, and indeed on the average by 0.70° C for every 5° C by which the outside temperature is higher. These differences seem to become smaller in the higher temperatures, as also seems to be the case with the sex differences. The mean values of Table III are, however, relatively too low, firstly because the animals are already somewhat older and secondly because no female is among them to raise the total mean value somewhat.

It is well worth the trouble briefly to compare the results of this table with what has been found by others about the influence of the surroundings on the body temperature, although, as said, this comparison is not sharp, because of the relatively large differences of temperature which one gets with small differences of technique. ## IV. Zusammenfassung [Summary].

A. In rats of 38—54 Tage [days], which lived at a constant temperature of 25° C, the average Körpertemperatur [body temperature] was 36.4° C. Here a clear difference between the Geschlechter [sexes] was to be observed: on average the body temperature was 36.87° in the Weibchen [females], 36.13° in the Männchen [males]. The Sexualunterschied [sexual difference] therefore amounted to 0.74° C in favor of the female. The differences between Morgen- und Abendtemperatur [morning and evening temperature] were only slight, on average 0.16° C. Furthermore, days with higher and days with lower temperatures were to be distinguished.

B. The Körperwärme [body heat] of young brown rats (3½ Wochen [weeks] old) varied with the temperature of the Umgebung [surroundings], such that a rise of the Außentemperatur [outside temperature] by 5° C caused an increase of the body temperature of on average 0.70° each. The Geschlechtsunterschiede [sexual differences] in the body heat become larger when one moves into lower temperatures, but in these young animals at 10° C amount to on average only 0.20°.
