# Experiments on Regeneration in Freshwater Snails.

### (First Communication.)

By

Adolf Cerný.

(From the Biological Experimental Institute [Biologische Versuchsanstalt] in Vienna.)

With 2 figures in the text.

Received on 7 December 1904.

In the series of the gastropods, phenomena of regeneration had already been known for some considerable time in several land-dwelling pulmonates (*Helix*, *Limax*, and others). Experiments were also carried out on freshwater snails, namely by Justus Carrière in the year 1878.¹ He experimented with *Limnaeus auricularis* and *Planorbis carinatus*, but he was unable to arrive at any positive result. The animals all died soon after the amputation of the tentacles. Carrière therefore expressed the supposition that these animals possessed the capacity for regeneration either not at all or only in a very insignificant degree.²

> ¹ Justus Carrière, Studies on the phenomena of regeneration in the invertebrates. I. Regeneration in the pulmonates. Würzburg 1880.

> ² Morgan, on the other hand, found that various freshwater snails are able to regenerate the foot (Morgan, Regeneration. p. 104. New York, Macmillan, 1901.).

I used *Planorbis corneus* and *Paludina vivipara* for experiments, which initially extended to the regeneration of the tentacles. *Planorbis* is able to reproduce cut-off tentacles in a short time. *Paludina* likewise regenerates, though somewhat more slowly. Here the circumstances take on a particularly interesting form. As is well known, this snail is of separate sexes. One can very easily recognize the sex already from the outside. The females, namely, possess tentacles that taper conically into a point, while in the males (Fig. 1) the right tentacle distally displays a club-shaped thickening. In this tentacle lies the penis of the animal, and it opens to the outside at the end of this knob-like swelling.
