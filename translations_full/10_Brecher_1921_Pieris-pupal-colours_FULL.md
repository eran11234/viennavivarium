## Introduction and First Experiments Toward Demonstrating the Colour Sensitivity of the Tyrosinase.

After the analysis, carried out, of the influence of light and colour on the pupal colouration of *Pieris brassicae* (Brecher 1917: Part II, 1919 and 1920: Part V), we now turn to the process of colour adaptation itself and to how it comes about in the body of the caterpillar, or respectively of the pupa, under the action of the surrounding colour.¹

The influence of the surrounding colours on the pupal colouration rests, as has already been established, upon a specific efficacy of the kinds of light rays reflected by them, acting upon caterpillars in the stage that is ripe for pupation or "sensible" (Poulton): yellow rays, and herewith the green leaves which reflect much yellow, bring about the least formation of the black pigment or melanin and the strongest formation of the green pigment, and, except for the saddle region, a prevention of the white — green pupae —; blue, violet, but especially ultraviolet, and herewith also a black surrounding, bring about the strongest formation of the melanin and a slight formation of the green and an opacification of the integument — dark to darkest pupae —; a white surrounding, or respectively ultrared rays as well as other warmth, bring about an inhibition of the melanin, a diminution of the green, and a strong formation of the white opacity — that is, pale pupae.

Finally, a neutral surrounding, e.g. grey, and further a dark background, has as its consequence the more or less uniform middling formation of all the colour components, so that middling pupae arise. Thus in this way, with the surroundings that come into consideration in nature — green leaves, white walls, black-painted laths — an agreement of the pupal colouration arising upon them with the surrounding colour is attained, that is, a colour "adaptation."

Proceeding from the basic idea that the various contrivances of organisms come about by means of the direct effecting through external factors together with the specific reactive capacity of the organism toward that same factor, it was a concern of mine to investigate this in an organism

on an experimentally accessible characteristic — that is, the colouration — and on a definite object, by the hand of experiments, in that on the one hand the action of the external factor, in this case of the light, and on the other hand the reactive mode of the organism, were investigated.

That, in the action of light upon the pupal colouration, it must be a matter of definite chemical changes called forth by light of various wavelengths, has been shown by the investigations into the chemistry of the colour types communicated in the third part of the work (1917), since the various colour-induced pupal colour types differ from one another not only morphologically but also through their chemism, and indeed:

1. With respect to the dark pigment: Melanin is, as was already known earlier from the investigations of Fürth (1902), Przibram after v. Fürth (1903), Dewitz (1902), Biedermann and others, formed through the action of a ferment, the tyrosinase, upon tyrosin or a tyrosin-like chromogen in the presence of oxygen. For this reason the distribution of the melanin in the pupal integument — the pattern (Zeichnung) — is bound to the surroundings of the pore canaliculi, which evidently stand in connection with the oxygen of the air, and spreads from here more or less into the remaining canaliculus system of the chitin. (Cf. Part I 1917, p. 91.) The tyrosinase of the various pupal colour types shows a different efficacy upon the tyrosin blackening, which agrees with the quantity and the degree of formation of the melanin present in the integument of these pupae. The pale pupae have a tyrosinase that colours tyrosin rose and a weak, pale-brownish degree of melanin formation in the integument. (III 1917, p. 159.) All other types have a tyrosinase that colours tyrosin violet, of various strength; it is weakest in the green, strongest in the dark pupae. (III 1917, p. 157.) In agreement with this stands the sepia-brown formation of the melanin in these three types, which in the green pupae is reduced to a minimum and indeed is found only around the pore canaliculi, but in the darkest also fills almost the entire horizontal canaliculus system of the chitin and makes the ground colour appear blackish.

2. With respect to the green pigment: The green pupae have the most green pigment. Their integument extract and their blood colour are, independently of sex, greener than those of the other types. (III 1917, p. 162.) A greening of the tyrosin mixed with the blood of the green pupae was also observed.

Through the action of external factors, namely warmth, a transformation of the tyrosinase of any one type from a tyrosin-violet-colouring into the tyrosin-rose-colouring kind could be attained, such as is normally characteristic only for the white pupae.

The greening of the blood samples was likewise observed in the other non-green colour types after warming (III. T. 1917, p. 166).

The possibility of a transformability of the tyrosinase through the action of an external factor thus seemed to be established, and it was natural to trace back the diversity of the tyrosinase, and consequently of the pigment formation in the various colour-induced pupae, to an influencing of the ferments in the sensible stage of the caterpillar by light of various wavelengths. An incitement to this was also given by the work of Wolfgang Ostwald on the light sensitivity of animal oxidases (1907), according to which two ferments present in the insect body, the peroxydase and the catalase, undergo an oppositely directed influencing through light of various wavelengths, in that violet and white light act so as to increase the peroxydase and destroy the catalase, whereas yellow and darkness act so as to destroy the peroxydase and favour the catalase. I have already, in the III. part p. 144, pointed to the interesting parallel that exists between these results and the antagonistic action of the yellow and violet spectral region upon the formation of the dark and the green pigment in the pupae, whereby the tyrosinase behaves similarly to the peroxydase, while the dependence of the green pigment on light agrees with the behaviour of the catalase toward light.

It hereby seemed not hopeless to be able also to influence the tyrosinase in vitro through irradiation by various colours, and thus to imitate the influence of light in vivo and to bring it nearer to understanding.

The first experiment to this end was carried out in September 1916 by directly dropping blood of caterpillars ripe for pupation into tyrosin and irradiating this mixture in the spectrum.

Since I shall have to come back below only very briefly to this first experiment, and since it actually represents the starting point for the further investigations, but also differs from the other experiments with respect to the methodology applied, it may be discussed here in greater detail:

**Experimental technique:** From caterpillars ripe for pupation, which were afterward used for irradiation in the spectrum (s. IV. Part 1919, Spectral experiment p. 304), in each case one drop of blood was let fall, by means of a puncture between two legs of an abdominal leg-pair, into a small flat-standing test tube (Eprouvette) of 3 cm height, ½ cm diameter, containing 1 cm³ of tyrosin solution. These test tubes, containing the tyrosin–blood mixture, were set up for irradiation in a spectrum produced in the dark chamber by means of a 1000-candlepower arc lamp, glass prism, and lens system —

Table) that only in the rear row of test tubes did the green, alongside the yellow and even still stronger than this, act upon the blackening with the further progress of the reaction. This row was, however, as mentioned earlier in the experimental arrangement, displaced more to the left toward the red end, so that the test tube hung in the yellow-green region. The green in the front row of test tubes, on the other hand, had a much weaker action, corresponding roughly to that of blue; here the test tube hung more to the right, that is, in the blue-green region. If we compare with these blood-blackening results the pupae arising from the same caterpillars under simultaneous and entirely identical irradiation (s. IV 1919, p. 304), then we see, in contrast to the results in vitro, that the very darkest pupae with the strongest melanin formation arise in ultraviolet, with stepwise lightening in violet and blue, whereas green pupae with the least formation of black pigment arise in yellow, which moreover agrees with the known modes of action of the surrounding colours.

So already this simple experiment allowed the influenceability of the tyrosinase of the caterpillar blood of caterpillars ripe for pupation by the various coloured rays to be recognized; yet it still harboured a great riddle, namely how this antagonistic action of yellow on the one hand, or respectively ultraviolet, upon the living object, and on the other hand in the test tube, is to be interpreted.

In what followed, the experiments undertaken to this end before me (1919), and further by Przibram and Dembowski (1919), with various plant and animal extracts, have brought the understanding of the animal-colouration problem nearer to us, and have furnished the experimental foundation for the causal element of a theory concerning the causes of animal colour-clothing (Przibram 1919, II. Theory).

1. Through irradiation experiments carried out chiefly on Halinasch tyrosinase, the colour sensitivity of the tyrosinase was now unambiguously proved: yellow rays act at first to promote the tyrosinase activity, blue rays to inhibit it; with longer pre-irradiation this relationship reverses itself. (Przibram-Brecher p. 100.)

2. Przibram has soon recognized the importance of the reaction state for the colour sensitivity of the tyrosinases, which themselves again are influenced in various ways by the various rays: alkaline-reacting tyrosinase extracts such as Halinasch bring about a red initial colour of the tyrosin, acid-reacting ones such as the animal tyrosinases a violet or blue one. (The melanin formed precipitates out of acid media in net-like forms.)

A slight acidification accelerates but soon destroys, a slight raising of the basicity inhibits and protects, the tyrosinase activity. The action of yellow and blue rays on the tyrosinase, as well as the reversal, is thus explained by the acidifying action of yellow and the alkalizing action of blue rays. (Przibram-Brecher p. 114.)

3. Przibram and Dembowski (1919) showed on Halinasch tyrosinase that the colour sensitivity of the tyrosinase depends on the presence of oxygen: Halinasch tyrosinases sealed air-free into glass tubes showed, in contrast to those set up open, no differences in their activity after pre-irradiation in colours. Halinasch tyrosinase preserved in this way retained its activity undiminished for an unlimited time, whereas open ones gradually lose their activity.

If the explanation of the colour influence given above under 1 and 2 could also be applied to the colour adaptation of the pupae, as it has been shown by Poulton, Petersen and others for various light-variable pupae and lately by Dörken (1916, 1918) and by me (1917, 1919) especially on *Pieris brassicae* (Przibram 1919 II, p. 216), then the demonstration must first be carried out on the object itself, and the process of colour adaptation must be derived step by step from experiments. Even though experiments rich in results with Halinasch tyrosinase had opened wide vistas onto the animal-colouration problem, this material nevertheless permits only a one-sided treatment of the question, namely the behaviour of the extracts, whereas in *Pieris*, on the other hand, alongside the colour adaptation existing on the living object, it is possible to study in parallel the processes on the extracts and to bring them into accord. It was therefore necessary to complete, and if possible to conclude, the analysis of the light influence begun by me on *Pieris brassicae* through the uncovering of the processes going on under the action of the light. For the time being the animal tyrosinases seemed indeed, as in our experiments (Przibram-Brecher), to behave fairly resistantly toward such intentions. Nevertheless, the colour action, and even the reversal (e.g. in *Smerinthus ocellata* p. 97), has several times come to distinct expression even with such.

The experiments were carried out both with tyrosinase from pupae and with caterpillars ripe for pupation and pre-pupation. In most cases the tyrosinase was used in the method given by v. Fürth and hitherto always employed by us (see Brecher 1916, Przibram-Brecher 1919): half-saturation of the freshly drawn blood with ammonium sulphate, washing of the precipitate with half-saturated ammonium sulphate solution, and dissolving of the residue in a quantity of 0.05% caustic soda [sodium hydroxide] equal to the original quantity of blood (= strength 1). Direct dripping of the blood into tyrosine was no longer applied, because it blackens too quickly. In some cases, instead of the tyrosinase, blood was dissolved in definite dilutions of caustic soda and thus exposed to the pre-irradiation, and then was further used just like the tyrosinase by withdrawal of drops and addition of these to tyrosine. The action of differently coloured light upon the tyrosinases took place in the experimental arrangement given in the experiments of Przibram-Brecher, by setting them up in prismatic boxes lined with coloured paper and set up in daylight, in likewise lined test-tube stands. As colours, red, green, yellow, blue, white, black, grey, and further darkness were used, but not all colours came up for arrangement in every experiment. Either the tyrosine–tyrosinase mixture (usually 1 drop to 1 cm³ tyrosine) came directly into the coloured surroundings, or only the tyrosinase did. In the latter case (pre-irradiation) drops were withdrawn at various intervals from the pre-irradiated tyrosinases and dropped into tyrosine samples set up in mixed white light. In the sequence in which the tyrosine samples turn on [begin to react], in the stage of colouration, as well as later in the falling-out or remaining-suspended of the melanin, the activity of the tyrosinase and also its reaction state make themselves known.

Although these experiments too, at first, up to before the autumn of 1918, turned out in the majority (33 out of 55 experiments, Or.-No. 1–54) negative with respect to the demonstration of colour sensitivity, they nevertheless gave a decisive direction to the further, more successful pursuit of the path, through the uncovering of the different reaction state of the different developmental stages from the caterpillar to the pupa: it turned out, namely, that the pre-pupation-ripe, feeding, green-faeces-depositing caterpillar stage and the pupa (after pupation) were more alkaline, in that the melanin formed by the tyrosinase of these stages remained in suspension¹); the stage just before fixation (after the secretion of red faeces) was more acid, since with it very soon a

Acidimetric determinations on the samples already converted to melanin, by means of an indicator as well as Traube's viscostagonometer, yielded a confirmation of this conclusion. Butterfly tyrosinase could not be tested, since the *Pieris* butterflies had much too little blood to be able to obtain it pure and in sufficient quantity for the preparation of tyrosinase; in any case, however, the behaviour of the tyrosinase, with falling-out melanin formation, in pupae at a far-advanced developmental stage (in April) points to the fact that, before the transformation into the imago, an acidification takes place again. During the transformation from the caterpillar stage into the pupal stage, the tyrosinase accordingly passes from a more alkaline state before pupation to a strongly acid state, with deposition of the melanin in the envelope during pupation, and after completed pupation has become more alkaline again. Thus, for the formation of the pigments in the pupal envelope, the action of the surrounding colours precisely during this transition from the more alkaline into the more acid state, with falling-out melanin, must play a decisive role through small alterations of the reaction state. This agrees with the statements of Poulton, Petersen, as well as with my own experiences about the sensible stage of the caterpillar that alone is decisive for the colour-influencing. Hence precisely the tyrosinase of this stage must permit, by the irradiation, the attainment, in the test-tube too, of the same conditions as on the living object.

For the time being, however, both the experiments with pre-pupation-ripe as well as pupation-ripe caterpillars — excepting the first experiment described in detail above, with blood dripped directly into tyrosine — and further also most among the numerous experiments with pupal tyrosinase in this first experimental period, here described summarily, had allowed no differences to be recognised in the action of the different colours.

The difficulty for success, as against the successful experiments with Halimasch [Armillaria] tyrosinase, is connected with the reaction state — different from that of the Halimasch — of the animal tyrosinases used for the experiments. The Halimasch tyrosinase is alkaline, turns on red first, the melanin formation remains, even with older tyrosinase, normally in solution or suspension, and the tyrosinase retains its activity for a long time, so that the influence of the pre-irradiation can be observed continuously on the same tyrosinase (straight turning-on and reversal). Air-tightly sealed-in Halimasch tyrosinase retained its activity even for months, undiminished. (Przibram-Dembowski 1919.)

The animal tyrosinase used hitherto for the experiments is from the outset more acid than the Halimasch tyrosinase. Its activity is therefore 1. at first accelerated; it turns on violet at once. The further acidification stages, which it passes through in rapid succession from the time of preparation — if it is not already acid from the outset, as in the stage during pupation — bring about, after a short time, 2. a rapidly falling-out melanin formation, and finally 3. already after a few days from the preparation, an extinction of the activity.

Each of these three stages was to blame for a number of negative experiments, where the colour action could not come to expression.

1. All samples turned on alike. This was the case a) with direct setting-up of the tyrosine–tyrosinase mixture in the colours (Or.-No. 6, 7, 10, 16, 18): the samples turned on within a short time, before a light action upon the tyrosinase could have occurred; b) after too short a pre-irradiation of stronger tyrosinase (strength ⅛), before the inhibiting or exhausting action of particular colours had brought the tyrosinase to the limit of its capacity to turn on (Or.-No. 40–43).

2. All samples turned on uniformly, but with soon-falling-out melanin formation, in the use of already acid tyrosinases, where the light action could have no influence any more (Or.-No. 11, 12, 14, 15).

3. No samples at all turned on any longer
a) with tyrosinases when, through all-too-long duration from the obtaining to the setting-up of the experiments — e.g. with the usual preparation duration of 2–3 days favourable for Halimasch, or still longer (Or.-No. 19, 20 quite weak), further through delay of a couple of days for the purpose of setting up preliminary experiments to ascertain the dilution favourable for the pre-irradiation (Or.-No. 8, 9), and finally as a consequence of the duration of the pre-irradiation itself (Or.-No. 21) — the activity had already become extinct.

With blood dripped directly into NaOH concentrations favourable to the activity of the tyrosinase, the tyrosinase is inactive still more quickly, namely already on the fourth day or even earlier (Or.-No. 23, 24, 36a, 36b).

b) With tyrosinases in too strongly alkaline solution during the experiments with blood, which for the purpose of pre-irradiation had been dripped directly into concentrations of caustic soda that inhibited the activity of the tyrosinase (¹/₆₀ and ¹/₈₀ normal) (Or.-No. 25–27, 28–32, 37–39).

The positive ones, too, among these first experiments — which will be mentioned further below at the corresponding places — did not yet quite correspond to the expectations entertained. There arose either too small and soon self-equalising differences (Or.-No. 2 and 3, 4 and 5, 50, 51), and not such great ones as with Halimasch (too strong tyrosinase), or only after very long pre-irradiation (use of pupal tyrosinase, Or.-No. 44–49, 52–54), which is not in accordance with the conditions on the living object, where the adaptation has taken place within a span of 2 days. Finally, good differences did also appear, but not always reproducible ones, and ones which still gave no complete picture of the process.

It would therefore be necessary, as a first requirement for success, to apply some corresponding alterations to the usual experimental technique, favourable for Halimasch, for the *Pieris* tyrosinase. In the use of short-lasting stages, such as those before pupation, the rapid acidification and the becoming-inactive of the tyrosinase must be prevented still during the preparation:

1. Through shortening of the preparation duration.
a) In the preparation of the tyrosinase in the usual manner mentioned above, by precipitation with ammonium sulphate and dissolving in 0.05% NaOH, the duration of the preparation would have to be reduced gradually from at least 3 days to 3–4 hours.
b) Another way to the greatest possible shortening of the time interval from the withdrawal of the blood from the body to the setting-up in the colours had been attempted, through direct dripping of the blood into definite NaOH concentrations favourable for the activity of the tyrosinase (from n/80 to n/640), namely 1 drop of blood to 1 cm³, and pre-irradiation in the colours, where then, just as with the usual tyrosinase, the testing of the activity took place through dripping into tyrosine, 2 or 4 drops to 1 cm³ tyrosine. This method, which with the application of definite concentrations has yielded differences after pre-irradiation — it will be necessary to return further below to these results —, is to that extent not particularly favourable, in that here the tyrosinase is destroyed much more quickly than with the previous precipitation by ammonium sulphate, namely already on the fourth day. The pre-irradiation cannot thereby be continued for long on such samples, so that both colour actions — direct and reversed turning-on on one and the same blood solution — could scarcely be observed.

It was therefore to be preferred to use the former method, with preparation of the tyrosinase in the shortest possible time, rather than the second, direct method.

2) Dissolving of the ammonium-sulphate-precipitated residue in a concentration of caustic soda that protects the tyrosinase activity, that is, is sufficiently alkaline. Preliminary experiments with testing of the activity of the *Pieris* pupal tyrosinase by dissolving of the blood in various concentrations of caustic soda showed that ¹/₈₀ normal caustic soda solution, that is, the 0.05% solution always used by us, had precisely the best-protecting action, in that it at first inhibits the activity, but then after 1–2 days lets it turn on optimally. (Cf. below I C.) The tyrosinase residue was therefore also in the further experiments dissolved in n/80 NaOH. By contrast, less alkaline concentrations such as ¹/₆₄₀ normal NaOH bring about at once an optimum of the blackening, but then exhaustion. (Cf. below I C.) But these points of the earlier and later optimum vary with the reaction state of the blood used.

3. Dilution undertaken as quickly as possible after the preparation, through ¹/₈₀ NaOH, down to a still well-turning-on tyrosinase. Usually one started from strength 1, that is, residue dissolved in a quantity of NaOH equal to the original quantity of blood. The dilution to ⅛ of the original concentration mostly yielded still a very well-turning-on tyrosinase and was thereafter always used. ¹/₁₆ too was set up a few times with success.

4. Finally, since it had shown itself that in air-tight sealing one had a means for the months-long preservation of the Halimasch tyrosinase (Przibram and Dembowski 1919), this might likewise constitute a means to maintain also the activity of the animal tyrosinase for a longer time. To be sure, the experiments with Halimasch had shown the dependence of the colour sensitivity of the Halimasch tyrosinase on the presence of the oxygen, in that air-tightly sealed-in tyrosinases had yielded no differences after pre-irradiation; yet I believed firmly from the outset that the caterpillar or pupal tyrosinases would behave differently in this respect from those of Halimasch, and that precisely this method would have to yield the most favourable results with respect to the proof of colour sensitivity, since on the one hand, through exclusion of the oxygen of the air, the too-rapid acidification and destruction of the tyrosinase would be held back, while on the other hand the enclosed oxygen would suffice to make the colour action possible.

Such pre-irradiation experiments, first carried out with fixed caterpillars of *Vanessa io* in July 1918, were able to confirm this supposition, so that the application of this enclosure method seemed indicated for the solution of the question in *Pieris*.

Through the taking into account of these requirements, namely: the undertaking of the various stages before, during, and after pupation, and the testing of their normal behaviour, as well as also their colour sensitivity, under application of the improvements of the experimental technique mentioned above for the prevention of the rapid alteration of the tyrosinase (preparation as rapid as possible, dilution, immediate enclosure, setting-up in the colours — for control, open tyrosinase tubes also had to be set up —), I at last succeeded in the autumn of 1918 in showing the colour sensitivity of the tyrosinase on the object itself, and in following the colour-adaptation process, at least with respect to the melanin formation, from the experiments described in the following, which is then to permit me, on the basis of these data inferred from the experiments, to sketch the path that Nature takes in the colour adaptation of the light-variable pupae.

These experiments relate to the light sensitivity of the tyrosinase, hence actually concern with certainty only the melanin formation.

With regard to the green, its origin, its dependence on light, is planned for the future, but at present not yet so clarified; I must here confine myself provisionally to a few indications.

The investigations into the nature of the white (opacity) in the envelope remain, since they require a different methodology, reserved for a later publication.

## I. Developmental Stages.

Caught up in the idea of a colour-photographic process in the colour adaptation of the pupae, the earlier authors had assumed that the influence of the surrounding colour acted on the fresh pupal skin, by analogy with a photographic plate. It is only to Poulton (1887) that we owe exact records about the time and duration of the various stages before pupation, as well as experiments on their influenceability by the surrounding colours, and the establishment of a stage sensitive to the influencing by the surrounding colours, the "sensible" stage, which falls not in the pupal period but in the last caterpillar period before pupation. His observational data relate chiefly to *Vanessa urticae*. From the moment when the caterpillar turns away from the food, until its pupation — the "preparatory period" — the caterpillar passes through three stages:

I. The caterpillar leaves the food plant and wanders about restlessly, in order to seek out a place for pupation (variable duration).

II. The caterpillar sits motionless in a curved position on the chosen place, and towards the end of this stage begins to spin a silk thread (duration for *Urticae* 15 hours).

III. The caterpillar has fixed itself by means of the silk thread to the substratum. (In the Vanessids attached only by the last abdominal prolegs, it hangs with the head directed downwards; in the Pierids it is also held in addition by a silk thread around the middle of the body in a horizontal position on the substratum.) This stage ends with the casting-off of the caterpillar skin. (Ascertained duration for *Urticae* 17–18 h.)

Since the caterpillar wanders about everywhere in stage I, effectively only stages II and III would come into consideration for the influence of the substratum colour. He proved, by means of the "transference experiments," the light sensitivity of the caterpillar during the whole second and the beginning of the third stage, in all 20 hours before the last 12 hours which precede pupation, and named this stage the "sensible" stage. In a later work (1892) Poulton stated something different about the duration of the stages, since he had made the observation that caterpillars taken too early from the food plant needed longer for pupation (which I have likewise observed, IV, p. 280) than such as were brought into the conditions together with the food plant and only wandered away from it at the right point in time, as he did in the later experiments. According to this, the whole preparatory period was said to last 20–24 h, namely stage I and II together 5–9 h, stage III 15 h, for *Vanessa urticae*.

For *Pieris rapae* and *brassicae* too he made some observations regarding the sensitivity of the stages through "transference experiments." The influenceability during the third stage is very slight. The ascertainment of the duration of the stages is not carried out so exactly for *Pieris brassicae* as for *V. urticae*. For the whole period the data fluctuate between less than 48 hours and over 100 hours. Since the caterpillars were collected in the open and judged only by size, it is possible that in the former case they had already begun the preparatory period, while in the latter case they had been brought into the conditions still too early. For the third stage a span of 20–30 hours is given, in some cases, with transference experiments, over 37½ hours. Darkness delays, yellow rays accelerate pupation, which I too was able to observe.

Petersen (1891), independently of Poulton, carried out experiments for the determination of the point in time of the influenceability of the Pierid caterpillar, with the same result: the receptivity for the surrounding colour falls into the caterpillar stage, namely into the period from the cessation of food intake to the stripping-off of the caterpillar skin. He believed, however — still holding fast to the analogy with a colour-sensitive plate, as which he regards the hypodermis — that he had to assume the stage of greatest reactivity to be after the spinning. The statements of Poulton I found in general confirmed in my experiments (but I have made no exact observations about the duration of the stages). With regard to the sensitivity of the third stage I would here merely remark that I have begun experiments on *Vanessa io* (for which Poulton assumes precisely a longer influenceability during the third stage than in *Urticae*), which are to be discussed elsewhere, in which I had to make the experience that, after fixation had taken place, the action of a coloured surrounding no longer yielded the characteristic pupal colouration — in that, for example, in yellow no green but dark to medium pupae arose.

On *Pieris brassicae* I have until now always avoided, in order not to risk the failure of the experiments, bringing already fixed caterpillars into the experimental conditions. For this purpose, the caterpillars that turned away from the food and were in the process of wandering were always taken. Among these I could soon distinguish two stages, whose differentiation proved practical for what followed. Their outward conspicuous distinguishing mark is the colour of the faeces: the caterpillars that at the beginning of the wandering, just like the still-feeding ones, secrete green faeces — green-defaecating stage — pass over, some time after the cessation of food intake, into a stage that one recognizes by the **red shimmering-through** hindgut; this stage ends with the secretion of a red faecal pellet — red-defaecating stage. In the following, this stage shall, for brevity, be designated the "red-wandering" stage. Now its gut has become completely translucent and empty, and the caterpillar now has a much smaller size than at the beginning of the wandering. There follow the stages designated by Poulton as II and III: the stage where it remains still, shortens and thickens its body through the retraction of the segments, and begins to spin itself in. Its skin appears at this time more lustrous and greener; the caterpillar loses its mobility. Hereupon it has entered the fixed stage.

Gräfin Linden (1903) too observed in *Vanessa* a similar colour change of the gut contents from green to red in the last stage before pupation. The distinction of the green- and red-defaecating stage when bringing them into the experimental conditions has no significance for the colouration of the *Pieris* pupa; yet it proved more practical to put them into the conditions as soon as they entered the "red" stage (cf. Brecher IV 1919, p. 280), since they were then already pupated after 2–3 days and yielded large, fine pupae, whereas one cannot always separate the "green-wanderers" from the still-feeding ones, and the caterpillars brought in at the younger stage, owing to the too-early withdrawal of food, pupated only very late and mostly delivered smaller pupae, "hunger forms." The influence during the "red" stage up to fixation seems to me decisive for the colouration of the pupa.

In the following, the differences of these stages shall be treated, namely:

1. the green-defaecating caterpillar stage before the wandering,
2. the wandering red-defaecating caterpillar stage,
3. the stage of the fixed caterpillar and
4. the pupa,

with regard to their chemism, especially the behaviour of their tyrosinase, its reaction state and activity, as well as their light sensitivity.

## A. Normal Course of the Stages up to Pupation.

### a) Experimental Technique.

For the experiments concerning the blood colour, tyrosinase activity, and onset-colour as well as the behaviour of the melanin formed, the caterpillars were continuously observed, and as soon as a sufficient number had entered a particular stage, they were quickly taken for tyrosinase extraction. The processing followed as nearly simultaneously as possible for each two stages, and for the other two on the next day at the same time. In repetitions of one or the other series, the same intervals were likewise observed as far as feasible. Since not all caterpillars of one rearing are at the same stage at the same time, the caterpillars of one rearing could be investigated in two stages; this was the case, for example, with the "red-wandering" ones, which were processed on 18. IX. (see Or.-Nr. 59–61) and the fixed ones from 19. IX. (Or.-Nr. 62–64). In view of the difference of these stages, it is of great value to know that they stemmed from one rearing. With all four stages the same quantity of blood was obtained and processed into tyrosinase in just the same way, very quickly, namely: obtaining of blood, in the caterpillars by cutting off the tip of an abdominal leg, in the pupae by puncture at the wing-sheath — duration about ½ hour; thereupon immediately precipitation with an equal quantity of saturated ammonium sulphate; after one hour filtering off of the precipitate, then washing with half-saturated ammonium sulphate solution, and already two hours after the withdrawal of blood, dissolution of the precipitate in ⅟₂₀ normal sodium-hydroxide solution. A part of this tyrosinase was used immediately for the preparation of several dilutions with ⅟₂₀ normal-NaOH — down to ⅛ — and tyrosine samples of each 1 Cc of tyrosine were set up with each 1 drop of tyrosinase of strength 1, ½, ¼, and ⅛.

The bled-out green- and red-defaecating caterpillars were then brought into yellow and black surroundings for pupation and still showed the characteristic influence of the colours. (Fifth Part V.)

### b) Experimental Course.

#### 1. Feeding green-defaecating caterpillars.

These caterpillars have yellow-green blood, which does not blacken especially fast or strongly. After half-saturation with ammonium sulphate, only a weak precipitate forms. The first filtrate, which contains the green blood pigment as well as the chromogen, is beautifully yellow-green. Their tyrosinase produces a rosy onset-colour of the tyrosine.

The fresh or one-day-old tyrosinase up to strength ½ takes [colour] on in 10 min., the ¼ somewhat later, but its activity is a very slight one; the samples treated with ⅛ go not much further than through a light violet to a light sepia-brown. The melanin normally does not precipitate. (Cf. also, in this regard, an earlier experiment mentioned in the Introduction with caterpillars of this stage, and Table Or.-Nr. 16.)

#### 2. Wandering red-defaecating caterpillars.

Already the outer habitus of these caterpillars is different from that of the earlier stage: the skin colouration is, in comparison to the lively green and black of the prepupation-ready ones, off-coloured [discoloured] — by which I had also earlier always distinguished them, before I came to know the other criterion. They are yellower; on the underside they shimmer through entirely yellow. The gut shimmers through along its whole length and (with further progress of this stage) still more intensively at the hindgut runs red through.

The blood is reddish-yellow, blackens immediately in the air, gives with ammonium sulphate a weak precipitate.

The tyrosinase is extraordinarily active; the samples all take [colour] on almost instantaneously, and indeed exactly like *Halimasch* [honey fungus, *Armillaria*] with red colour, which intensifies up to intense cherry-red and then passes over into a quite dark, almost opaque sepia-brown. In the samples charged with freshly prepared tyrosinase, normally no precipitation of the melanin occurs. The samples remain black-brown like those of the *Halimasch* tyrosinase. Samples that were set up with the tyrosinase standing in the air on the next day after the preparation likewise took [colour] on red, but precipitated out one day after the setting-up with rich sepia-brown melanin formation, while the upper liquid was still coloured red (!).

The latter result is also in accord with the behaviour of a tyrosinase prepared in the previous year from red-defaecating caterpillars (see Introduction and Or.-Nr. 10–12, 13–15), which was prepared in the usual slow way over 3 days and therefore immediately brought about a strong precipitation of the melanin.

Unfortunately, in this year I no longer succeeded in obtaining the same stage for a repetition of the experiment. It is not easy to obtain a sufficient number of caterpillars at the right stage simultaneously and at a time of day favourable for the processing, which lasts after all 3–4 hours. The attempt was undertaken three more times to process caterpillars at the red-wandering stage for the purpose of obtaining the red-taking-on tyrosinase, but each time, at the carrying out, the right stage had already been exceeded, since they had begun, from the preceding day, to wander too early and to pass over into the red stage in the late afternoon hours, when an immediate processing would no longer have been feasible.

As proof that the stage at the processing was already too far advanced, let it be cited here that the bled-out caterpillars of these series, which on the afternoon after the processing were put into coloured boxes for pupation, were already fixed the next morning. (Admittedly, here the influence could follow immediately, and they nevertheless still yielded the pupae characteristic of the colours.)

Since it forms the transition to the next stage, the fixed caterpillars, these observations too may be briefly mentioned here:

The caterpillars no longer shimmered through yellow. The blood colour [was] yellow-green; after addition of ammonium sulphate a yellowish-green strong precipitate forms, the first filtrate is yellow-green. The tyrosinase produces a **violet** taking-on, is strongly active; all samples with the tyrosinase strengths 1–⅛ take [colour] on in a short time. The colouration of the samples deepens from violet to quite dark sepia-brown. On the next day the melanin mostly precipitated out.

#### 3. Fixed caterpillars.

In one of the series (in all, two) the caterpillars were taken from the same rearing from which the red-defaecating wandering ones with the beautiful red-taking-on tyrosinase stemmed.

The fixed caterpillars have a greener colouration than before the fixation; on the underside too the body shimmers through entirely green. The blood, in contrast to the earlier stages, has no yellowish admixture at all any more, but is dyed deep green. It blackens very rapidly in the air, with falling [precipitating] melanin formation. After addition of ammonium sulphate, a strong grey-green precipitate forms. The first filtrate is green, likewise very different from the yellower green of the earlier stages.

The tyrosinase at once calls forth a violet onset-colour of the tyrosine; it is very active: strength 1–¼ take [colour] on in 15 min., ⅛ somewhat later. Afterwards the samples become dark violet, finally entirely black-brown, opaque. Then the melanin precipitates out.

#### 4. Pupae.

(Short time after pupation.) Mid colour-type.

Yellow-green blood, yet much paler and clearer than that of the caterpillars; it blackens much less than the latter. In 25 min., as long as the bleeding lasted, only a brown ring formed at the surface, whereas in the fixed ones in the same time melanin precipitated very abundantly, and in the red-wandering ones too a strong blackening had occurred. On addition of ammonium sulphate, only a slight clouding forms; the liquid becomes translucent, and on filtering off, only a very small quantity of precipitate remains, a much smaller one than e.g. in the wandering red-defaecating caterpillars. The filtrate is yellow-green as in the caterpillars before fixation. (With the same blood quantity, ⅓ more liquid.)

The tyrosinase takes [colour] on at once violet. The activity is slighter than in the red-wandering and fixed caterpillars: at the control, 1¼ hour after the setting-up, 1/1–¼ had taken [colour] on well, but ⅛ scarcely a trace. We shall see, at the discussion of the light experiments, that the ⅛-diluted pupa tyrosinase of this experiment, already after two days, never took [colour] on at all any more, whereas the tyrosinases of the red-wandering and fixed ones yielded blackening up to complete opacity even later, and that of the feeding caterpillars too had still taken [colour] on, even if much more weakly than that of the two subsequent stages. The activity of the tyrosinase in the first pupal stage is thus a much slighter one than in the pupation-ready caterpillars. The stronger concentrations 1–½, however, yielded well-coloured samples. The melanin does not precipitate out. This accords with the result of the earlier experiments (see Introduction).

According to the multi-year experience gained through many experiments concerning the behaviour of the tyrosinase of *Pieris* pupae, I can add to the results of this experiment that at later time points after pupation the activity of the tyrosinase again increases. (On the increase and decrease of the activity of the tyrosinase before and after pupation, cf. also the data of Dewitz (1905), v. Linden (1905), Steche and Waentig (1913).) Normally (i.e. with the exception of the red-taking-on light, white-induced pupae as well as of the tyrosinase altered by warmth), it always takes [colour] on directly violet. The melanin does not precipitate out. Only with far-advanced development toward the butterfly (pupae of the autumn generation, processed at the end of April, Or.-Nr. 40–54) does the pupa blood, when treated with ammonium sulphate, give a very abundant residue. The eightfold-diluted tyrosinase is still well active (the ¹/₁₆ too), takes [colour] on blue-violet, but the colouration remains at the bottom of the test tube: the melanin formation is precipitating already right at the beginning of the reaction.

### c) Experimental Results.

The comparison drawn here between the developmental stages of the caterpillar before, during, and after pupation has thus yielded a far-reaching difference of these with respect to onset-colour, activity of the tyrosinase, and melanin deposition: the not-yet-pupation-ready, i.e. feeding, green-defaecating caterpillar has a weak tyrosinase producing a rosy onset-colour and a non-precipitating melanin; wandering red-defaecating caterpillars [have] a very strong tyrosinase taking [colour] on red like *Halimasch*, with a strong, non-precipitating melanin formation, yet with further progress of this stage with abundantly precipitating melanin; fixed caterpillars [have] a strong tyrosinase taking [colour] on immediately violet with abundantly precipitating melanin; and the pupae [have] a violet-taking-on tyrosinase, less active right after pupation, with non-precipitating melanin. These three characteristics permit us to draw conclusions about the reaction state of the various stages, whereby these changes of the reaction state would in any case probably have their cause in the physiological processes during the transformation from caterpillar to pupa.

The red onset-colour, the melanin remaining in solution in the pre-pupation-ready and "red"-wandering caterpillars, point to an alkaline reaction in these stages, whereby the weak activity of the tyrosinase of the former stage is probably caused by a too-high degree of alkalinity inhibiting the activity, and the increase of the activity in the "red" stage would be owed to a decrease of the basicity. The precipitation of the melanin, the violet onset-colour in the subsequent stages, would point to a strong acidification during the fixation. In the pupae, the remaining-in-suspension of the melanin points to a decrease of the acid reaction state, the slighter activity to an exhaustion. With these findings on the reaction state, the determinations made in an earlier comparative experiment with the tyrosinase of various stages are also in accord, in which, owing to the long preparation duration (see Introduction), only the differences in the precipitation of the melanin had still come to expression, and acidity determinations of the [colour]-taken-on melanin samples by titrimetric means could also confirm these conclusions about the reaction state drawn from the behaviour of the melanin formation.

We thus have the following

**Change of the reaction state of the tyrosinase during pupation:**

|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Tyrosinase:** Onset-colour | rosy-red | red | violet | violet |
| **Activity:** | weak | strong | strong | weaker |
| **Melanin formation:** | in solution | in solution, [then] strongly precipitating | strongly precipitating | in solution |
| **Reaction state:** (inferred from the preceding 3 characteristics, as well as in earlier comparative experiments on stages [cf. Introduction] by melanin formation and acidimetric determination) | alkaline | alkaline (perhaps decrease of the alkalinity), [then] acid | strongly acid | weaker acid |

> ¹ Saturated solution of phenolphthalein in normal sodium-hydroxide solution diluted 1000-fold with distilled water; then, to 1 Cc of indicator, the liquid to be determined (in this case the tyrosine samples charged with the tyrosinases in question and converted to melanin) was added dropwise until the complete disappearance of the red colour.

The reaction state thus passes from an alkaline one in the pre-pupation-ready caterpillar (rosy onset-colour, weak melanin formation remaining in solution) over into a less alkaline one in the pupation-ready red-defaecating stage (red onset-colour, increasing activity of the tyrosinase, remaining-in-solution of the melanin). Toward the end of this stage it experiences a sudden strong acidification (strong precipitation of the melanin), is strongly acid at the transition into the fixed stage (violet onset-colour, abundantly precipitating melanin), in order, after pupation, again to exhibit a lesser degree of acidity (remaining-in-solution of the melanin). Here, however, exhaustion has at first weakened the activity of the tyrosinase.

For the correctness of this inference regarding the reaction state from onset-colour, activity, and melanin formation, I need only refer to the chapter on the reaction sensitivity of the tyrosinases in our work (Przibram and Brecher 1919, pp. 107–116), according to which alkaline tyrosinases, like *Halimasch* or alkalized ones, take [colour] on with red onset-colour or rather show a colour-change toward reddish tones, [whereas] acid ones, like butterfly-pupa tyrosinases or acidified ones, take [colour] on with violet to blue colour or rather change over into violet tones; an inhibition of the tyrosinase activity sets in in a too-strongly alkaline solution; a promotion and acceleration of the activity [occurs] with weak acidification; a precipitation of the melanin out of acid solutions; an exhaustion and a becoming-inactive of the tyrosinase after a stronger acidification has taken place.

We were later able, after the publication of the work cited above, to convince ourselves of the dependence of the tyrosinase action on the reaction state by yet another route, namely by dripping the blood into various concentrations of alkali and acid: close to the neutral point the tyrosinases go off most rapidly (optimum). Acidic grades bring about precipitation of the melanin that has formed; still stronger acidic grades bring about ineffectiveness of the tyrosinase (exhaustion); stronger alkaline grades inhibit at first, but also protect the tyrosinase, so that it later possesses the optimum of its capacity to go off. The alkalinity grades once established for a given blood, for the earlier and for the later optimum, do not always hold for another blood as well, but depend on its reaction state, and therefore vary according to the origin of the blood, that is, according to species, sex, developmental stage, colour type.

For the special problem occupying me, the explanation of the colour adaptation of the light-variable butterfly pupae, what would come into consideration first, in the investigation of the one species *Pieris br.*, would be in particular the determination of the optima in the two latter categories, namely in the various developmental stages before pupation, as well as in the various pupal colour types. — The sex, as we already know (Brecher, Part III, 1917, p. 164), makes no thoroughgoing difference for the colouration of the pupa; in any case this too would nonetheless have to be taken into account. — For the time being, in *Pieris brassicae*, I have carried out investigations on the behaviour of the tyrosinase upon dissolution of the blood in various alkali concentrations for one pupal colour type (medium pupae), which I shall describe somewhat more fully further below, because on further continuation they might perhaps also contribute something to the understanding of the colour sensitivity. With regard to the reaction state of the stages, it will be my further task to establish the optima of the going-off for the various stages and pupal colour types, as well as, by a direct route, through titrimetric acid determinations of the blood, to demonstrate these reaction changes during the transformation as well as the differences of the pupal colour types.

Summarizing, then, we can note on the living object the following normally occurring processes during pupation:

The feeding caterpillar has an alkaline reaction, a weakly active, rose-going-off tyrosinase, no deposition of melanin. As soon as it ceases to feed and begins to wander, a quite weak acidification or neutralization sets in (concerning the existence of a connection between reaction state and movement in the lower animals, cf. Przibram, Theorie 1919, p. 246, on phototaxis); the tyrosinase becomes very active and goes off, like Halimasch, strongly red. This red stage is therefore, in this stage, run through even within the body of the caterpillar itself. A proof of this is the no longer green but reddish-yellow colour of the blood (its immediate blackening in air?) and the red gut contents. Countess Linden (1903), who in Vanessa describes a transformation of the gut contents from green to red shortly before pupation, traces it, to be sure, back to a transformation, brought about by acidification, of the chlorophyll of the food into a red pigment, which leads her to the further conclusion of bringing both the green insect pigment and also the red Vanessa pigment in the butterfly into genetic connection with the chlorophyll of the food. But she further also states that the red gut contents turn black in air and transform into melanin. This afterward-darkening in air I too was able to observe in the red faeces of *Pieris*. Regarding the nature and origin of the red Vanessa pigment I can for the present allow myself no expression of opinion at all; but as concerns the change of the green into a red gut content of the *Pieris* caterpillar in the sensitive stage, I believe I am not going astray if I assume that this involves a red stage which circulates also in the gut and is perhaps here, through more favourable oxidation conditions, coloured still more strongly, formed out of the chromogen and the tyrosinase of the body fluid: the transformation of the gut contents from green to red coincides indeed precisely in time with the strongly red-going-off tyrosinase as well as with the running-through of the red stage in the haemolymph (reddish-yellow blood) in the living body. Since in the gut too there is body fluid containing chromogen and tyrosinase present — I recall the experiments of Biedermann (1898, after v. Fürth, Chemische Physiologie der niederen Tiere, p. 94) on the occurrence of tyrosinase and the blackening of the gut contents in mealworm larvae — and since they perhaps here stand under more favourable oxidation conditions, an even more decided red colouration of the fluid arises. The food remnants still left over are coloured red by the red fluid; hence the last faecal pellet excreted before pupation is red.

After this time the fixation takes place. A strong acidification of the tyrosinase brings about a precipitability of the melanin. (This property of the tyrosinase coincides in time with the formation of melanin in the new pupal envelope, where, upon the casting-off of the caterpillar skin, it comes — proceeding from the places of richest oxygen supply, the pore canals — to formation and deposition in the preformed spaces of the chitin.) In the fixed caterpillar stage the red stage is no longer present in the blood; the blood is now quite intensely green, without a tinge toward the yellowish or brownish, which otherwise would presumably betray an admixture of melanin. The tyrosinase has now been transformed from a red-going-off one into a blue-violet-going-off one, and under normal conditions will never again go off red. During the fixation the further acidification takes place. As regards the chromogen standing under the action of the tyrosinase in the new pupal envelope, then, as soon as the caterpillar skin has been thrown off and the oxygen can amply reach the fresh envelope, it will — proceeding from the places of greatest oxygen supply, the pore canals, and spreading itself out in the remaining preformed canal system of the chitin — rapidly run through the further stages of the colouring-out from red to black and deposit itself as fixed melanin and so form the marking. On the fresh, still green pupal envelope one can observe how the markings gradually become red, then black.

With the formation of the melanin in the pupal envelope the tyrosinase is, immediately after pupation, exhausted and therefore much less active than in the preceding stage. At the same time, with the conclusion of the transformation, a decrease of the acid state has again occurred. The pupal tyrosinase, however, under normal conditions never again goes off red. Dembowski (cf. Przibram and Dembowski 1919) was able, after air-free enclosure of red-gone-off Halimasch–tyrosinase–tyrosine samples, to observe a complete reversal of the colouration. On re-opening, the samples went off again instantly, but no longer red, rather violet, exactly like the pupal tyrosinases. It was as though the reaction had not been interrupted at all for any arbitrary length of time and had not meanwhile reverted. Dembowski therefore already at that time voiced the surmise that, regarding the explanation of the violet going-off colour of the pupal tyrosinases, there might be involved a similar change in the oxygen supply during the metamorphosis as in the experiment with the little tubes. It is therefore very interesting that I have now in fact been able to establish that the caterpillars have a red tyrosinase, that before pupation the red stage is run through in the body itself, and that from this point in time the tyrosinase, as soon as it comes into contact with the oxygen of the air, immediately goes off directly with violet colour: from a red-going-off one in the caterpillars it has become, in the fixed caterpillars and the pupae, a blue-going-off one. (The footnote on p. 267 in the work Przibram–Dembowski, however, does not refer to this analogy with the Halimasch tyrosinase, since my experiments were carried out only later, but to the conserving of the tyrosinase by exclusion of air.)

For the success of the experimental demonstration of the colour-adaptation process, the discovery of this stage was decisive — the stage which possesses a strongly red-going-off tyrosinase like that of Halimasch, which toward the end of that same stage suddenly undergoes a strong acidification and brings about a strong precipitation of melanin. At this sudden transition from the strongly red-going-off, Halimasch-like tyrosinase into the acidic one with self-depositing melanin must lie the point of attack for the light action, and the slight changes of the reaction state evoked by the various colours may already suffice to yield great differences in the amount of melanin deposition in the envelope, such as distinguish the colour types from one another. In this reversal of the reaction state lies the key which permits us to take the path to answering the question concerning the influenceability of the pupal colour through the action of the surrounding colours, precisely in this "sensitive" stage of the caterpillar.

## B. Colour sensitivity of the tyrosinase in the various stages.

### a) Experimental technique.

The tyrosinases prepared from the various stages, whose differences I have just discussed, were each — as soon as their activity and going-off colour had been established — at once diluted, in their whole amount, eightfold with 1/80 normal sodium hydroxide solution, and, since everything dissolved, drawn up unfiltered, on the one hand, into glass tubes drawn out to a capillary on both sides, and the capillary fused shut at the pilot flame of a Bunsen burner (just as is given in Przibram–Dembowski 1919, p. 264) and immediately set up in the colours; on the other hand, the tyrosinase still left over was divided into equal amounts into small test tubes and likewise — though closed only with a cotton-wool plug — placed into the same colours for pre-irradiation.

This time care had been taken for as rapid a working-up as possible and for the prevention of further changes of the tyrosinase before the bringing-in into the colours, through timely exclusion of air and setting-up in the conditions. The preparation time of from the beginning of the bleeding-out until the fusing-shut and setting-up in the colours amounted to 3½ hours; but since the working-up could always take place only in the afternoon, the tyrosinases came into the colours only in the evening, so that actually the irradiation could begin only from the next morning. The tyrosinases of the various stages had been brought into the colours two at a time, either simultaneously or with an interval of one day, in order to be exposed to as nearly as possible the same light-intensity conditions. As light variants there were used White, Black and Yellow, and further complete darkness, and every tyrosinase was set up both open and fused shut for pre-irradiation.

For the red-going-off [tyrosinase] of the wandering red-defaecating caterpillars, the fused-shut ones came to be set up, besides in the colours mentioned, also in Red and Green, whereas the open ones only in Yellow and Black, and these too only in very small amount. Of fixed caterpillars two tyrosinases were set up, namely in the first series both open and also fused-shut ones came to be set up in White, Black and Yellow, but in darkness only open ones. The tyrosinase of the second series was set up in open and fused-shut tubes in Yellow, Black and darkness. ### c) Experimental results.

The sharpest and most unambiguous differences in activity through the pre-irradiation in different colours has without doubt been given by the red-turning tyrosinase of the wandering, red-defaecating caterpillars, and indeed after four days of pre-irradiation in the colours of the tyrosinases fused-shut in little tubes the same differences in melanin formation have come to light as are expressed by the action of the same colours on the living caterpillars of this stage in the colouration (melanin formation) of the pupae: On the one hand, Black, Red and Darkness have conditioned a strong activity of the tyrosinase, through whose action on tyrosine quite dark samples have arisen, just as the irradiation of the living caterpillars of this stage in these surrounding colours brings forth the dark pupae; on the other hand stand White, Yellow and Yellow-green with almost inactive tyrosinases, which could scarcely colour the tyrosine samples a trace, whereby this slightest melanin formation did not precipitate in White, but in Yellow and Yellow-green precipitated at once. Parallel to this, the light and typically green pupae respectively coming into being in these surroundings are both likewise of the very slightest melanin formation, whereby, as between the samples, a difference of the melanin-formation stage exists between the light pupae from white surroundings and the green pupae from yellow and yellow-reflecting surroundings (cf. Brecher 1917, Part I, p. 93).

First of all, then, the demonstration has succeeded, of influencing the tyrosinase prepared from the blood of the caterpillars in the sensitive stage also outside the body through the surrounding colours in exactly the same way as in the living caterpillar itself, and thus of providing the proof for the direct effecting of the pupal-colour modifications through the differently coloured light.

Now, as regards the explanation for the definite effect of the various colours on the tyrosinase which came to light in this experiment, and why precisely in this stage it is so extraordinarily colour-sensitive, this can readily be given on the basis of the connection between colour-effect and reaction-state of the tyrosinase so happily discovered by Przibram (Przibram-Brecher 1919, p. 107): Yellow rays act acidifyingly, blue to ultraviolet alkalescizingly; therefore, with slight pre-irradiation, Yellow will accelerate the tyrosinase-activity, Blue and Violet will inhibit it — direct colour-effect. — With longer pre-irradiation, Yellow acts over-acidifyingly, hence tyrosinase-destroyingly, whereas Blue has protected the tyrosinase and now lets it turn on optimally — reversal — (Przibram-Brecher, p. 114 and 115). White acts even more alkalescizingly, hence inhibits the tyrosinase still longer (Przibram, Theory, p. 216). Przibram has indeed in his theory attached to this a hypothesis about the coming-about of the pupal colourations, by tracing them back to a reversal of the colour-effect (Theory, p. 216). Yet since only now the actual processes in pupation and the light-sensitivity of the Pieris-tyrosinase have been uncovered, I should nevertheless not like to forgo now following, on the basis of these facts, the course of the process, even if in doing so some repetitions are unavoidable, since gratifyingly the explanation derived from these investigation-results agrees with the hypothesis. New in any case is the particular course of the reaction-state of the sensitive stage, from which now the colour-effect can be understood without gaps.

Let us therefore consider, from the above-mentioned standpoint of the colour-sensitivity of the tyrosinase, as the action of the colours on their reaction-changes, the result before us of the pre-irradiation of the tyrosinase of pupation-ripe caterpillars in fused-shut little tubes: we see that here it is a matter of the "reversal," where the blue-containing Red, the ultraviolet-reflecting Black yield the strongly active tyrosinase, yellow rays an already inactive-become tyrosinase. Darkness has likewise still protected the tyrosinase, so that it in any case still turns on optimally, and White has through its hyper-alkalescizing action still inhibited it. The colouration of the pupae therefore in fact represents the reversal of the colour-effect, as Przibram had surmised.

But there must still be an earlier phase of the shorter action of the colours on this tyrosinase, where Yellow brings about the strongest, Blue, Violet and Ultraviolet on the contrary the weakest, activity of the tyrosinase. In this experimental series we unfortunately do not find this sought-after state. The pre-irradiation of the air-free fused-shut tyrosinases had up to the first removal after 2 days not yet been able to bring forth any differences in activity — we shall see further below why — and after renewed sealing and a further two days' pre-irradiation had already yielded the reversal. But the openly set-up tyrosinases were in such insufficient quantity that they cannot be drawn upon for comparison. With the single setting-up after 2 days of pre-irradiation, Yellow did, to be sure, turn on first, but these were only transient differences. Let us, however, recall that very first experimental series described in the introduction, with direct irradiation in the spectrum of blood dropped into tyrosine of pupation-ripe caterpillars, that is, the shortest duration of action of the colours (Or.-No. 1), of that result, then still so inexplicable, later confirmed by the Halimasch [honey fungus, *Armillaria*] experiments, which stands in complete opposition to the pupal colourations obtained in the same colours, in that with the blood samples the strongest blackening occurred in Yellow and the weakest colouration at the short-wave spectral end, then we have all that we need to close the ring of proof.

The tyrosinase of the red-defaecating wandering caterpillars is in a state at first still alkaline, red-turning, but soon changing in the direction of a strong acidification with strongly precipitating melanin. A quite brief action of the colours on the tyrosinase of this stage, that is, in the tyrosinase still on the alkaline branch, will, as was the case with the first experiment (Or.-No. 1) with direct dropping of the blood into tyrosine, produce a slight acidification of the yellow-irradiated ones and, compared with the other colours — but especially the alkalescizing, that is, inhibiting, blue to violet ones — a head start for them in the normal acidification process, so that the yellow-irradiated ones will be blackened more quickly than the blue- to ultraviolet-irradiated ones. (This phase of the colour-action runs its course in the living animal still in the first red stage of the caterpillar and does not come to expression in the colouration of the pupa.)

With somewhat longer pre-irradiation, the progressive acidification through the yellow rays will soon have brought the tyrosinase to the limit of its effectiveness, so that the yellow-pre-irradiated tyrosinase yields scarcely more than a slight acidification of the tyrosine and the little melanin formed precipitates at once, owing to the acidic state; whereas, through their alkalescent action, the ultraviolet rays somewhat hold back the acidification normally proceeding in this stage, so that the tyrosinase now stands at the optimum of its effectiveness and yields the strongest, but non-precipitating, melanin formation. Darkness has no influence at all — perhaps a slight retarding influence as compared with light — and therefore the action of the dark-tyrosinase can be regarded as the normal behaviour. (In fact, after the pre-fused melanin samples had stood for a longer time, the one irradiated with the darkness-tyrosinase had precipitated somewhat, while red and black still remained in solution.)

White acts hyper-alkalescently, will therefore cause the tyrosinase to persist in the alkaline state, perhaps even set it back into the still more alkaline state such as the still-feeding caterpillars show, and permits only a slight activity of the tyrosinase. The tyrosinases pre-irradiated in white therefore start up very late and very weakly, and the melanin does not precipitate as it does with the yellow- and green-pre-irradiated ones.

This represents the result as it showed itself after the second removal following four days' pre-irradiation of the red-starting tyrosinase, and as it is depicted at the lower left of Plate II. That the reversal and such sharp differences in the activity of the tyrosinase through the action of the colours occur so rapidly precisely in this stage lies in the sudden change of the reaction-state — connected with the transformation — from an alkaline, red-starting tyrosinase, like that of *Hallimasch* [honey fungus, *Armillaria mellea*], into a strongly acidic one with precipitating melanin formation, where the colours, merely through slight shifts in the reaction-change, already bring about extreme differences in the activity of the tyrosinase. With *Hallimasch*, on the other hand, the action of the colours is not supported by any normal acidification, so that a longer period is necessary before differences appear at all, and a still longer one before the reversal occurs. Likewise, in the other stages of the caterpillar or pupa respectively, we shall not be able to ascertain so great a colour-sensitivity (see further below), and this owing to the absence of a reversal in the reaction-state.

If we now apply this explanation to the colour-adaptation of the pupa accomplished in life, when we bring the caterpillar in the sensitive stage into the colour-conditions, then the colour-action characteristic of a short irradiation time — the greatest effectiveness in yellow and the least in blue to ultraviolet — does not come to expression in the pupal colouration. The time up to pupation is set precisely so that already before pupation the reversal of the light-action takes place, whereby a certain correspondence of the pupal colouration with the surroundings comes about. Over-acidification of the tyrosinase by the yellow rays gives rise here to pupae with the least melanin formation; alkalescence through the blue to ultraviolet rays has held back the normal acidification, and the strongest effectiveness of the tyrosinase has thus made possible a strong development of the melanin and given rise to dark to darkest pupae. White, through its still more alkalescent action, has held the tyrosinase back at the red-starting stage of the caterpillar entering the sensitive stage (perhaps even set it back into a still more alkaline state), whereby, owing to the fixing of the state through the hardening of the chitin, the pale pupae are characterized only by little melanin and by melanin that has remained at the reddish-brown developmental stage. Connected with this is also the retention of the red starting-colour of the tyrosinase of the pale pupae — a colour that otherwise characterizes only the caterpillars — in contrast to the violet starting-colour characteristic of all other pupal colour-types. That the acidification process is also reversible is shown by the change of the starting-colour from violet to red brought about by the action of heat on the tyrosinase (Brecher, Part III, 1917, p. 171). Presumably the especially protective alkalescent action of white is the reason that the tyrosinase prepared from the pale pupae starts up with a strongly red colour and, on further oxidation, passes over into violet and yields the samples most strongly coloured of all the types. In this way it is also to be understood that the blood of the palest pupae gave precisely the strongest blackening (Part III, 1917, p. 168). Darkness shows the normal behaviour in the development of a medium amount of melanin.

Owing to the definite time-duration at pupation, a certain correspondence of the pupal colouration with the surroundings, in respect of melanin formation, already comes about. We shall see further below that the other colourations as well, green and white, enhance this correspondence.

It would have to be possible in principle, however, if the transformation were already to take place earlier, at the point in time of the direct colour-action with the strongest effectiveness of yellow, to obtain blackish forms in yellow, and forms with the least melanin formation in black (whereby no "protective colour" but rather a "warning colour" would arise). With the caterpillars, where the light-action falls within a quite definite time, this requirement is not easily possible; but it is so with forms that are exposed to the colour-action throughout their whole life and do not undergo any such abrupt transformation, but only ordinary moults, which are probably likewise linked with reaction-changes. Here it would very much depend on temporal differences in the light-action whether the direct, or the reversed, or an intermediate colour-action would come to expression in the new integument. In other animal species we have observed such cases, which, however, remain reserved for a later publication.

Perhaps in some experiments certain of the deviations from the characteristic types that appear sporadically in the case of the pupae are also to be explained in this way. It would indeed perhaps also be possible, by bringing the caterpillars into the colour-conditions at a somewhat later stage, to bring the direct action in the colours to expression, and to obtain the darkest pupae in yellow but pale ones in black. Precisely *Pieris brassicae* — where the darkest pupae from black differ from the pupae from neutral surroundings or darkness, and one can therefore very easily distinguish a positive darkening action from non-influence — would make such an experiment extremely rewarding. We shall indeed see further below that with pre-irradiation of tyrosinase of the fixed caterpillar stage, yellow yields the most strongly effective tyrosinase.

In any case, then, the influence of the pupal colouration through the colour of the surroundings, through the definite time-duration from the onset of the light-action up to pupation, and the definite reaction-state of the tyrosinase, are so attuned to one another and so adjusted to the mode of action of the colours, that the end result yields a correspondence of the pupal colour with the colour of the substratum.

As far less colour-sensitive there has proved to be the tyrosinase of the fixed caterpillars, which is in agreement with the experiences concerning the failure of the characteristic action of the colours upon fixed caterpillars. But now we also understand why: the reaction-state of the tyrosinase, and with it the melanin formation, have already been decided in the previous state of the red-starting tyrosinase, and now in the fixed stage the tyrosinase is in a too-acidic state with falling melanin formation, than that the action of the colours could now still have a significant influence. In the experiment cited here (Or.-Nr. 62–65), with the openly pre-irradiated ones, after four days of pre-irradiation, yellow is the most strongly effective (Or.-Nr. 63, Plate II, upper right group), but the differences are not so great as with the red-starting tyrosinase of the wandering caterpillars, whereas the sealed-up samples, opened for the first time after four days, show no differences in effectiveness at all.

Just as little does the already violet-starting tyrosinase of the too-far-advanced wandering caterpillars show any colour-sensitivity.

Standing just as far behind the great colour-sensitivity of the tyrosinase of the caterpillars in the sensitive stage as that of the fixed caterpillars are also the tyrosinases of the pre-pupation and post-pupation period — which, as we know, also holds for the non-influenceability of the pupal colouration by the action of the surrounding colours upon these stages. This slight colour-sensitivity is explicable from their reaction-state. If, therefore, this influence can have no significance for the question of the colour-adaptation of the pupae standing in the foreground here, then the colour-sensitivity coming to expression even in these stages is nonetheless of interest in view of the light-variability of caterpillars and butterflies known from the literature.

With the pre-pupation-ripe caterpillars the tyrosinase is in a too strongly alkaline state, so that the action of the colours in the change of the reaction-state receives no support from a normal, rapidly proceeding acidification as in the next stage, from which the only slight differences brought about by the colours can be explained. Nevertheless the actions are in the same direction as in the sensitive stage; that is, owing to the acidification through yellow, the tyrosinase pre-irradiated in yellow starts up first and also precipitates first. Then, with longer pre-irradiation (sealed up, 4 days), the one from black starts up first, and finally, after 4 days' pre-irradiation of the open ones, white has gained a lead over the others — which agrees entirely with the explanation given.

With the pupal tyrosinase, on the other hand, we are dealing with a tyrosinase that has already passed over into the acidic state, so that the differences evoked by the light can be only slight. What therefore in the earlier stages first meant an inhibition of the action — namely the alkalescent action of black and blue — is here at once, already on direct setting-up of the reaction in the colours, the occasion for a slight lead over the other colours, while the acidification through yellow conditions the weakest effectiveness of the tyrosinase. Here, then, we have at once the reversal, as the experiments Or.-Nr. 2–5 illustrate. This colour-action forms, as it were, the continuation of the period begun with the light-action of the tyrosinase of the fixed caterpillars. There we have the persistence of the direct effectiveness of the colours, with the strongest starting-up in yellow and the weakest in black; with the pupal tyrosinase, not long after pupation, at once the reversal.

As far as the tyrosinase of pupae in the advanced developmental stage is concerned, it is, right from the very beginning, already in a very acidic state with the formation of immediately precipitating melanin, so that, as soon as the first differences appear, after 5 days of pre-irradiation the most strongly alkalescent action of white still lets it start up, while the effectiveness of the others is already very much weakened (Or.-Nr. 44, 45, 46). (Darkness and green too still start up a trace.) After longer pre-irradiation the effectiveness diminishes still more (Or.-Nr. 47, 48), and considerable differences appear in the starting-times, as well — interestingly — as in the starting-colours and definitive colour-tones of the samples set up with tyrosinase of different pre-irradiation. It is to be noted that now the starting-colour, too, is no longer directly violet, but first yellowish to rose. It would lead too far to discuss the sequence of starting-up and to try to derive it from the reaction-state. One could understand this sequence only if one were to assume not merely a single reversal, but a periodic recurrence of the same colour-actions upon the tyrosinase. Such a periodicity in the tyrosinase effectiveness will occupy us further below in another case besides that of the action of colours.

I should like to draw attention here also to the reappearance of a rose starting-colour at a stage standing before a transformation, in connection with a stronger difference in the light-action; and further to the difference in the starting-colour and the colour-changes undergone in the case of the tyrosine samples with the tyrosinases of the different pre-irradiations, which show a certain correspondence with the colours in the case of the pupae. Thus the white-pre-irradiated tyrosinase brings about a rose colour of the tyrosine, as does also the tyrosinase of the pale pupae. Dark, grey, and especially yellow-green passed, by way of a rose starting-colour, into violet, then brown, and finally turned over into green. The yellow-pre-irradiated one at first did not start up at all for a long time and then became directly green. With the other colours, black, red, blue, on the other hand, there is no green.

They appeared, they are weakly brownish coloured. In discussing the green pigment I shall have to return to this "greening" as well; in any case it is certainly no accident that precisely the yellow-reflecting colours yield this green starting-colour, and they presumably are connected with the least effectiveness of the tyrosinase after pre-irradiation in these colours. In any case this could be a pointer also for the manner of origin of the pupae characteristic of yellow, with the least melanin formation and the strongest development of the green, and one would be able to manage, for the explanation of colour-adaptation, with the colour-sensitivity of the tyrosinase alone.

Clear differences were also yielded by the pre-irradiation experiments on blood dripped directly into NaOH. But this method has the disadvantage that the tyrosinase, since it is not preserved by ammonium sulphate, soon decomposes, so that even in the most favourable case it has already become ineffective by the 4th day and no longer colours the tyrosine samples. Consequently the pre-irradiation experiments cannot be continued for long by this method, so that one can scarcely observe on one and the same tyrosinase both the direct starting-up and the reversal. Besides the process of the acidification of the tyrosinase, the rapid becoming-ineffective of the tyrosinase must evidently have yet another cause, which probably consists in the decomposition of the tyrosinase, which occurred more rapidly with this direct method than with that obtained by ammonium-sulphate precipitation. In this way it is to be explained that the use of n/60 and n/80 dissolved blood, where after all the rapid acidification is avoided, nevertheless did not yield the expected favourable results.

The best results were yielded by the pre-irradiation of blood dripped into n/640 NaOH. In this medium the tyrosinase starts up at once — the blood blackens immediately — and the drops taken after 1 day of pre-irradiation show differences, in that the alkalescent black lags somewhat behind the other colours in the blackening. After 2 days of pre-irradiation the acidification has gone further, the effectiveness of the tyrosinase has decreased considerably compared with the previous day. Least decreased, however, has it in the most strongly alkalescent white and red (warmth), so that these are now the most intensely coloured. After 3 days the effectiveness has decreased still more, all have become ineffective; yellow alone has still preserved its effectiveness. As one sees, here, as also in the previous experiment, one no longer manages entirely with the simple explanation of the reversal in connection with acidification. For how, after this, is the strongest effectiveness of the yellow-pre-irradiated tyrosinase to be explained?

Should this signify a recovery of the yellow, or the preservation of the first optimum? I believe that more complicated processes lie here which, even if we do not yet know them, nevertheless have a parallel in other experiments, the further continuation of which would perhaps facilitate the interpretation. I shall briefly mention these scarcely-begun experiments, of which there is talk here, in connection with this chapter. I should like to remark once more that the results of the pre-irradiation of the pupal tyrosinase are not strictly comparable with those of the other stages, since they differ somewhat in respect of preparation etc. The one experimental series set up simultaneously with the other stages and in analogous fashion had unfortunately yielded no results, owing to the lesser effectiveness of the tyrosinase at the dilution employed.

Even if the efforts to prove the colour-sensitivity of the pupal tyrosinase have had, properly speaking, no direct connection with the task I had set myself in regard to the colour-adaptation of the pupae, nevertheless, in view of the admittedly still not numerous experiments on the influencing of butterfly colouration through keeping the pupae in coloured surroundings (v. Linden, Poulton 1892, etc.), the demonstration of the colour-sensitivity of the butterfly tyrosinase, also during the pupal stage, is of interest. In any case, however — as these pre-irradiation experiments show, in agreement with the statements in the literature concerning the influencing of butterfly colouration through light — this colour-influencing does not occur as easily and rapidly as does, for the pupal colouration, the influencing of the tyrosinase of the caterpillars in the sensitive stage. The latter show a light-sensitivity "par excellence," which we have learned to understand from the reaction-state.

Before I turn to the next chapter, it remains to touch upon two questions which arise out of the methodology employed in the pre-irradiation experiments, concerning the behaviour of the sealed-up *Pieris* tyrosinases towards light in comparison with the open ones, as well as in comparison with the behaviour of *Hallimasch* tyrosinase under the same conditions. I mentioned in the introduction that I made use, for the *Pieris* experiments, of the method of preserving *Hallimasch* tyrosinase by means of air-free sealing-in, found by Przibram and Dembowski (1919), in the hope of preventing, through the exclusion of air, the rapid acidification and becoming-ineffective of the tyrosinase, and thereby gaining time for the light-action. According to the results of the experiments of Przibram-Dembowski, air-free sealed-in *Hallimasch* tyrosinase retains its effectiveness undiminished for months and is insensitive to coloured irradiation. As from the footnote on p. 267 of this work emerges, an examination of the behaviour in this respect of animal tyrosinase is also intended by Professor Przibram. I can therefore be brief about my occasional observations on the *Pieris* tyrosinase: What strikes us first as a contrast to the behaviour of the *Halimasch* is that it has in fact proved possible, by means of this method of closing-in, to demonstrate the colour-sensitivity in the butterfly tyrosinases, and indeed as rapidly and with as sharp differences as in the tyrosinase of the red-wandering caterpillars. And yet this, paradoxical as it may seem, is no contradiction of the oxygen-dependence of the colour-sensitivity of the *Halimasch* tyrosinase. In the animal tyrosinase there prevails, right from the start, an already acid reaction-state and, in comparison with *Halimasch*, a much more rapid tempo of its course, which is perhaps brought about by the fact that the animal extracts are from the outset richer in oxygen than those of *Halimasch*. This co-enclosed oxygen then also suffices to bring about great differences in the activity of the tyrosinases through the mediation of the differently-coloured rays. By contrast, with unhindered access of oxygen the whole process is probably too greatly accelerated for the colour-action still to be able to exert a significant influence. The dependence on oxygen shows itself precisely in the fact that the oxygen co-enclosed at the first melting-in did not actually yet suffice to bring about differences — e.g. at the first removal after 2 days in the case of the red tyrosinase of the wandering caterpillars, and even after 4 days in the case of the fixed ones. Only the unavoidable renewed access of oxygen on the occasion of the first removal for setting up the samples — whereby the capillaries were broken off, the tyrosinases poured over into test tubes, and only after the setting-up of the samples, that is after about 1–2 hours, melted in again — was able, on further pre-irradiation of 2 days, to bring about the differences. The positive outcome of the experiments on the colour-sensitivity of the butterfly tyrosinase through closing-in of the samples therefore changes nothing in the general validity of the explanation of the photo-catalysis of the tyrosinase by oxygen, as Przibram and Dembowski (1919) found by experiments with *Halimasch* tyrosinase, in analogy to the photo-bromination processes of toluene (Brunner and his pupils 1907, 1910 etc., according to Przibram and Dembowski 1919).

That the butterfly tyrosinase too, just like *Halimasch*, can be preserved for a fairly long time by exclusion of air, emerges from the comparison of the onset-times and the definitive blackening-tones between the melted-shut and the open tyrosinases of the same provenance and the same age, to which I have drawn attention on the occasion of describing the course of the experiment in each experiment. But here too we see an apparent difference from *Halimasch*: whereas *Halimasch* tyrosinases kept free of air retain their original activity undiminished for a very long time, in the case of butterfly tyrosinase there is, with each removal, a noticeable decrease in activity and a duration of activity limited to about one month. I have unfortunately omitted, after the determination of the colour-sensitivity of the various stages of *Pieris*, to continue the removal of drops from time to time for the determination of the temporal limit of the activity of the enclosed tyrosinases; yet this emerges from the experiment with tyrosinase of *Vanessa io* mentioned earlier. But if one considers that even the activity of the openly-kept *Halimasch* tyrosinase probably, in consequence of its reaction-state, has an incomparably longer lifespan — over a month — than that of *Pieris*, here limited to a few days, then one must recognise that the activity of the melted-shut animal tyrosinase, limited to one month, probably stands only in the same ratio to the month-long preservation of the *Halimasch* tyrosinase.

## C. Effect of various concentrations of sodium hydroxide solution on the activity of the tyrosinase.

In this work I have had to mention repeatedly — namely on the occasion of describing the pre-irradiation experiments, and further of deriving the reaction-state of the stages from the activity of the tyrosinase, the melanin formation and the onset-colour — a method which consists in dripping the blood into various concentrations of sodium hydroxide solution, and which on the one hand was used in some pre-irradiation experiments for the purpose of shortening as far as possible the duration of preparation of the tyrosinase, but mainly because, in consequence of the variation of the tyrosinase activity with the alkali-grade of the solvent, it at first seemed extraordinarily well suited for the determination of the reaction-state of the tyrosinase. Although the determination of the reaction-state of the various influenced and uninfluenced stages and pupal colour-types, which is what matters to me for this work, by this route — which, as subsequently became apparent, is after all much more complicated — as well as by direct acidimetric determinations, and furthermore the hope of being able, by the drop-method, to bring some light into the darkness of the periodicity of the colour-action, for the present constitute only the programme of further investigations, I should nevertheless not like to close this chapter on the conditions of melanin formation in the *Pieris* pupae without communicating something about the orienting experiments hitherto carried out with this method on *Pieris* pupal blood. These experiments constitute, as it were, a further elucidation of the reaction-sensitivity of the tyrosinase (cf. Przibram-Brecher 1919, p. 107), since in them the influence of the various alkali-grades on its activity shows itself very nicely, and they are therefore a necessary corroboration of the statements made in the present treatise about the reaction-state of the tyrosinase. They therefore also constitute an illustration for the explanation of the action of the various colours as alterations of the reaction-state.

### a) Experimental technique.

Starting from normal-strength sodium hydroxide solution, various dilutions were prepared with distilled water, namely for the first experimental series in geometric progression: n/80, n/160, n/320 and n/640; for another experimental series in arithmetic progression with an interval of 20: n/40, n/60, n/80, n/100, n/120, n/140, n/160.

In each case 1 drop of blood was dripped into 1 cm³ of sodium hydroxide solution of differing concentration. In the first experiments, with fewer grades, the blood-drop was let fall directly from the pupa into the sodium hydroxide solution; when more grades were used, the blood of several pupae was first collected in a test tube and then, by means of a pipette, 1 drop of the mixed blood was dripped into the various dilutions. After the dripping-in of the blood the mixture was shaken through and the influence of the alkali-grade on the activity of the tyrosinase was tested, and the earlier and later optimum determined: on the one hand by observation of the onset of the blood-blackening in the various concentrations in the solutions themselves; on the other hand, in order to be able to compare the pure onset-colour of the tyrosinase, by transferring drops of the blood-solutions into tyrosine (2 drops to 1 cm³) and comparing the onset-times, intensity of the colouration, onset of the melanin-precipitation, as well as the influence of the various concentrations in general on the tempo of the course of the activity-curve of the tyrosinase. For this purpose the drops were taken out at various times and tyrosine-samples charged with them, namely the first time immediately after preparation of the blood-solutions, then daily until the complete extinction of the activity. The concentration which yields the most rapidly-blackening tyrosinase was designated the "earlier optimum"; that with the latest-appearing activity, which however is then the strongest and is retained longest, the "later optimum".

### b) Course of the experiment.

Dilution-grades of the sodium hydroxide solution in geometric progression from n/80 to n/640 (see Table B a).

Of the blood-solutions, the first to blacken (after 40 min. from setting-up) were n/320 and n/640; 3 hours later n/160; n/80 at first not at all, but only after 2 days, and was then the most strongly blackened of all, whereas n/320 and n/640 had declined.

Of the tyrosine-samples set up with the fresh solutions, likewise n/320 and n/640 are the first to go on [react] very soon, and indeed violet; 2½ hours later n/160, and n/80 only after 2 days; but n/160 has soon overtaken n/320 and n/640, these have declined, and n/160 is now, on the 2nd and 3rd day after setting-up, the most strongly coloured sample. After a further day, n/160 too has declined and n/80 is a darker violet.

On the removal of drops from the blood-solutions on the 2nd day, n/160 went on first (one hour from setting-up), then only (half an hour later) more weakly n/320 and n/640; n/160 now shows the strongest violet colouration, while n/320 and still more n/640 have declined 2 days from setting-up. n/80 in this experiment goes on already after one day, but does not yet reach the highest blackening-grade.

On the removal of drops on the 3rd day after preparation of the blood-solutions, n/80 goes on first, and indeed as quickly as did the lower concentrations on the earlier days, that is after 25–30 min.; later n/160. On the following days the samples have darkened, but n/80 is distinctly more strongly coloured; n/320 and n/640 have not gone on at all any more.

On the fourth removal of drops on the 4th day after preparation of the blood-solutions, the tyrosinases have already become much weaker; really only n/80 goes on well, n/160 hardly at all, the others not at all any more.

On the 5th day the activity of the tyrosinase is completely extinguished. The earlier optimum of activity therefore lies at n/640 or n/320, the later optimum at n/80.

This experiment described here was carried out with the blood of a male medium pupa.

An experiment carried out simultaneously with a female pupa shows in general a similar behaviour towards the various alkali-concentrations. It goes beyond the scope of my task to enter into possible differences in the behaviour of the sexes, which moreover in these experiments on *Pieris* are not clear, since for the colour-adaptation the sex plays no role, and as we have already seen in Part III of this work (1917, p. 164), the difference of the blood-colour of the colour-types by far outweighs that of the sex.

Concentrations of sodium hydroxide solution in arithmetic progression n/40 to n/160 with an interval of 20 (see Table B b).

The relationships present themselves much more complicated when one takes the concentrations much closer to one another, for example in arithmetic progression with the interval 20. I have tested the relevant behaviour for the interval n/40 to n/160, that is n/40, n/60, n/80, n/100, n/120, n/140 and n/160. Here we see something very remarkable. Whereas we knew from earlier set-ups that n/80 in *Pieris* at first inhibits the tyrosinase activity, and one could suppose that the higher concentrations would allow no tyrosinase action at all any more, we see here, right after the setting-up of the tyrosine-samples with drops of the fresh blood-solutions, at n/40 an optimum of the onset, at n/60 a minimum, and from here a gradually rising activity up to n/140, which represents again an optimum, perhaps even a stronger one than n/40; n/160 is again a minimum. Thus an optimum is immediately followed by a minimum, and the concentrations adjoining the latter form a gradually rising series of activity until the attainment of the next optimum, whereupon immediately another minimum follows. I have unfortunately not tested the concentrations following n/160 in such close intervals. Perhaps this alternation of optima and minima continues periodically further on at definite concentrations.

On registration of the samples on the next day, n/140 had already declined, n/40 was ink-black. The other concentrations have darkened and approach gradewise in colour-tone the optimum lying after them.

On the removal of drops on the next day, n/40 has now reached the minimum of onset-capacity. The samples with the tyrosinases from the following dilutions form an ascending series of activity, but already at n/120 lies the optimum, while the other optimum of the preceding day, n/140, has also sunk to a minimum of activity. The n/160 following thereupon, which had been a minimum, has now become an optimum, and indeed stronger than n/120. We thus see in this experiment the strongest activity at n/160, which earlier was a minimum, further at n/120 a second optimum, while the earlier optima n/40 and n/140 are now minima and go on hardly at all. As regards the activity in general, it is much weaker than on the first day.

The intensity of the colour-tone of the present optima almost equals the intensity of the samples of the previous day from the same concentrations when these were still minima, whereas the earlier optima have forfeited their activity much more.

On the third removal of drops the activity has decreased still more: n/120 is still optimum, but n/100 as a second optimum is almost equally active. There still go on n/140 very weakly, after n/100, further n/80 a trace; the others not at all any more. Taken absolutely, the blackening is weaker than in the series of the previous day.

On the removal on the 4th day after preparation of the blood-solutions, nothing at all has gone on any more.

### c) Experimental results.

These experiments give us again a confirmation that the activity of the tyrosinase depends on the reaction-state: low alkali-grades, which just neutralise the acid reaction of the blood, activate the tyrosinase at once, so that the blackening begins immediately and yields an optimum of action. Higher alkali-grades inhibit the activity of the tyrosinase, so that it goes on only much later. In consequence of the acidification process taking place in the blood, however, a precipitation of the melanin will soon set in at the earlier optimum and the activity will very soon be exhausted altogether; whereas the higher alkali-grade, which inhibits its activity for a long time, has at the same time protected it, so that, if one repeats the experiment after 2 days, it then attains the strongest activity — later optimum of activity. There is therefore here too a "reversal" present, similar to that in the influence of the colours: the action of yellow, which at first yields the strongest activity of the tyrosinase, but on longer pre-irradiation lets it exceed the activity-limit, agrees with the "earlier optimum"; that of blue to ultraviolet, which at first inhibit the activity but on longer pre-irradiation let it go on most strongly, agrees with the "later optimum". The result of the first removal in this experiment, with the strongest onset of the earlier and the failure of the later optimum, would correspond to the direct colour-action with the strongest onset in yellow, and the result of the third removal of drops, with exhaustion of the earlier and strongest onset of the later optimum of the "reversal", with the inactivity of the yellow-pre-irradiated and the strongest onset of the tyrosinase pre-irradiated in short-wave light. Interestingly, here too the "reversal" follows after 2 days, exactly as in the influence of the colours on the caterpillar tyrosinase.

This experiment is a very graphic proof of the correctness of the explanation of the colour-action as alterations of the reaction-state. (Cf. Przibram and Brecher 1919, p. 114.)

In the experiment described, with medium *Pieris* pupae, the earlier optimum is n/640, the later optimum n/80. Yet these two points will vary according to the reaction-state of the blood, that is according to the provenance of the blood — determinations which are still to be made for the various pupal colour-types and developmental stages.

The relationships present themselves not so simply, however, when one tests smaller gradations of sodium hydroxide dilutions on the activity of the tyrosinase, as was the case in the second experiment communicated here. According to this, there seems to be no proportionality between alkali-grade and tyrosinase activity, but rather a periodic alternation of optima and minima which recur at various alkali-grades: from a grade of least activity they rise gradually to the optimum and from here fall abruptly to a minimum. I have tested only the intervals 40 to 160 in this way; it may well be that these waves continue thus further on as well. In this interval we see optima at n/40 — that is, beyond the supposed activity-limit — and at n/140, whereas at n/60 and n/160 the points of least activity. Now, however, of these, remarkably enough, it is precisely the more acid grade n/160 that is in fact a later optimum, which on the next repetition goes on most strongly; whereas the more alkaline solvent n/60 NaOH in fact represents an inhibition or exhaustion in which the tyrosinase never comes to an optimum. The earlier optima have, on the removal on the next day, already become minima. The first optimum n/140 also drops out before n/160, although the latter is the less alkaline grade. Noteworthy too is the after-ringing of the first optimum n/140 before the complete extinction of the activity of the tyrosinase, which next to the new optimum still keeps going on, while all the other grades do not go on at all any more.

I cannot for the present give any conclusive explanation for this behaviour. That it is not, say, a matter of irregularities is evident already from this experiment; a similar omission of definite grades and appearance of several optima has, moreover, also been observed on the occasion of other experiments. Many more experiments of this kind must still be carried out, in order to penetrate more deeply into this mystery and to explain this periodicity. In any case, the alkalinity-grades resulting after the dripping-in of the blood into the various concentrations would also have to be determined by titrimetric comparisons, and, besides sodium hydroxide solution, various other bases in graduated dilutions would have to be used as solvent.

A possibility of explanation forces itself upon me, which, as a non-chemist, I can put forward only with some reservation (cf. the chapter on ionization in Hollemann, *Textbook of Inorganic Chemistry*, 11th ed. 1913):

When we dissolve blood in the base NaOH, we bring a weak acid SH together with a strong base BOH. If we assume an equal number of ions, then the H-ion of the acid will combine with the hydroxyl ion of the base to form water and so establish the neutral state, which precisely promotes the activity of tyrosinase and lets it proceed maximally at once. But if hydroxyl ions remain in excess, then an alkaline state prevails in the solution, which inhibits the activity of tyrosinase. Thus far this agrees with the first experiment, and accordingly the weaker degrees of alkalinity would bring about the earlier optimum, the higher degrees of alkalinity would first bring about the inhibition. As the blood progressively acidified over the next days, in the former case an acid excess, in the latter case now a neutral state, would be reached, which bring about the exhaustion of the earlier optimum and the activation of the later optimum.

On the other hand, we also know that only the ions, not the undissociated molecules, have this property of combining to form water; and further, that the degree of dissociation depends on the concentration: with increasing concentration the ionization decreases, on dilution the ionization increases. It is therefore quite easily possible that at a higher concentration of NaOH, although in spite of this more molecules of NaOH are contained in the solution, yet only so many ions are free that they just neutralize the H-ions of the "blood acid" and let the tyrosinase proceed optimally; as also at a stronger degree of dilution, where absolutely less NaOH is present, but in return the degree of ionization is a stronger one. On the other hand, at another degree of dilution, likewise either through an absolutely stronger NaOH concentration or through stronger ionization, an excess of OH-ions causes a state too strongly alkaline for the tyrosinase activity, and consequently inhibition. (Cf. Hollemann, 11th ed., p. 96.)

Only seemingly have these discussions taken us away from the subject treated in this part; rather I believe that in this behaviour towards the various degrees of alkalinity I may discern a parallel to the influence of the colours, which would permit one to follow this periodicity of the colour-influence on the tyrosinase and perhaps to understand it. Let us now take, for example, the results of the preliminary irradiation experiment with pupal blood dissolved with n/640, then we find — if we wish to compare the behaviour of the tyrosinase in the various concentrations — that, although the degrees do not match (for this there would have to be an experiment between 320 and 640 with closely neighbouring concentrations carried out), yet some analogies:

1. that black-pre-irradiated tyrosinase, without reaching an optimum, comes again to a weaker degree, as for example n/80, which in this experiment was never an optimum;

2. that the first optima, at the removal on the 2nd day, become minima: yellow or yellow-green, while other alkaline tyrosinases: white, red, take their place;

3. that the first optimum persists, even though the other tyrosinases have already been extinguished: n/140, and in our colour experiment yellow.

I believe we should here have, through the setting up of such parallels and the determination of the degree of acidity by titration, a means to establish the influence of the colours almost quantitatively at each moment.

## II. The green colouring matter.

The present work has chiefly the influence of the surrounding colours on the formation of the dark colouring matter as its object. The pupae, however, show themselves in their green-colouring to be coloured differently from one another according to the surrounding colours, in that the ones out of yellow are fully green-coloured, the bright ones out of white and the dark ones out of black are only very slightly green, and the middle pupae out of grey and other surrounding colours, further out of Finsternis [darkness], likewise take up therein a middle position.

By Poulton (1887) and Petersen (1891) it is stated that the hypodermally situated green colouring matter, as well as the deeper-lying tissues and the blood, in the pupae arising in yellow surroundings, in consequence of the absence of melanin in the shell, shimmer through the transparent cuticle and therefore make the pupa appear green; whereas in the dark pupae the green is covered by the melanin embedded in the cuticle.

In fact, however, the green pupae possess more green colouring matter than the other types, of which one can convince oneself at the underside of the chitin shell: with the green pupae an intensely green pigment-layer lies against the transparent cuticle, whereas with the non-green types it is lacking, and the cuticle itself possesses on the underside a white lustre-layer, which conditions its opacity. Further, the green colour of the ether-extracts as well as of the blood of the green pupae, in comparison to the more yellow-green of the other pupae, speaks for this. (Brecher III 1917, p. 162.) On the other hand, there has also been observed the arising of a green colouring — greening — in the tyrosine-samples treated with the blood of green pupae (Brecher III 1917, p. 165), as well as in the aqueous shell-extracts of the green pupae (ibidem p. 170), in comparison to that of the non-green pupae, to which cases yet a further one is to be added below.

If one observes a fresh pupa which has just shed its caterpillar skin, it is always green. While it, however, in yellow surroundings remains green even after the hardening of the chitin shell, it has, in white or in the other surrounding colours, 1–2 days after pupation, as soon as its shell has hardened, become more or less de-greened and whitish-opaque. That the green colouring matter as such is light-transient is known (Przibram 1913). The question now arises: are the pupae in yellow surroundings so green because the green colouring matter present at the beginning is best protected in yellow surroundings, whereas it is destroyed by the light in the other colours?

It would therefore in this way be possible to apply, for the green colouring, the principle of body-colour-photography (Wiener 1895) (Przibram 1919, II. Theorie [Colouring: II. Theory], p. 209), according to which a light-sensitive substance is best protected in the colour reflected by it, and most destroyed in the one absorbed by it. The sensitivity-maximum is, however, shifted somewhat towards the red end relative to the absorption-maximum (Acworth 1891, cited after Przibram 1919, Colouring: II. Theory). With this latter fact, too, the green-colouring of the pupae is in agreement, since they are indeed more beautifully green-coloured in yellow and orange than in green, whereas in other light they are de-greened.

Or is the formation of the green colouring matter causally connected with the action of the yellow light?

Evidently both factors here act together (green colour of all fresh pupae, which is then destroyed; greening of the samples treated with the blood of the green ones), and therefore the influence of the coloured light must be tested

1. on the already-formed,
2. on the formation
of the green colouring matter.

### A. Colour-susceptibility of the green colouring matter.

#### a) Experimental technique.

Experiments on the colour-susceptibility of the green pupal colouring matter can be conducted in various ways: a) by ascertaining whether the green can still be subsequently altered on the living pupa; b) by irradiation in various colours of the green colouring-matter-extract, and indeed either of the shell-extract, or, in order to go back still further to the origin, of the green blood-colouring-matter.

a) Since a subsequent influencing of the pupal colour after the cessation of the sensitive period has been stated by no observer and has also hitherto not been noticed by me, only this latter way remains for the time being to be taken.

b) The extraction of the green colouring matter from the blood succeeded for me in the manner already communicated in the work on the *Causes of animal colour-clothing I*, p. 139 (Przibram and Brecher 1919): the first filtrate obtained after half-saturation of the fresh blood with ammonium sulphate and filtering off the tyrosinase-rich residue, which contains the green colouring matter and the chromogen in dissolved form, was heated to 90° in the water-bath in order to destroy the tyrosinase particles still carried along, whereby it coagulated with de-colouring. After cooling it was filtered through a hardened filter. The quite faintly yellowish-coloured filtrate contains the chromogen of the melanin in dissolved form, which colours on addition of tyrosinase. The whitish-brownish coagulum on the filter was, after some days, after it had become quite dry, dissolved in 75% alcohol, whereby it dissolved with a beautiful green colour, as the blood had originally possessed; likewise, and much more beautifully still, it dissolves also in ether. On the other hand, the still-moist coagulum does not yield up the colouring matter (Brecher 1917, p. 146).

Alcoholic extracts of the green blood-colouring matter obtained in this way, of:

A. pupation-ripe red-defaecating caterpillars,
B. pre-pupation-ripe green-defaecating caterpillars,
C. middle pupae,
D. green pupae,
R. chlorophyll-extract from *Rubus* leaves (ordinary alcohol-extract),

were, at ½ cm³ each in thin test-tubes: a) set up in cases covered with coloured gelatine, which had been brought to equal optical or chemical intensity¹. As colours there were used yellow-green, yellow, further white and Finsternis [darkness] for the irradiation of all extracts, further also red, orange and blue-green for A, C and R; b) in the coloured reflection-cases: red, yellow, yellow-green, blue, white, grey and black, and indeed only the extracts C and R.

> ¹ The establishment of the conditions for this irradiation experiment as well as the further continuation of the same and registration of the changes was done by Dr. Dembowski.

#### b) Course of the experiment. (Cf. Table C.)

In the reflection-cases, with both the pupal extract and the chlorophyll extract, after an exposure of 6 weeks, the destroying action of the reflected white light on the colouring matter is clearly to be noticed, as against all the other kinds of light. Between the other colours no difference whatever in the action on the colouring matter is discernible.

With the pupal extract, the one irradiated in white has become quite faintly yellowish, while in the other colours no de-colouring has taken place. After a further month the difference between white and the other colours is still greater, since the extract from white is now only faintly yellowish-coloured to the extreme.

With *Rubus* there seems to exist an opposition to the animal extract, in that the *Rubus*-extract appears, precisely in white, now greener. This finds its explanation in the fact that obviously the white light has destroyed the yellow component, the xanthophyll, whereby the green colour of the chlorophyll predominates in the solution. The animal-green thus behaves towards the light like the xanthophyll. After a further month the *Rubus*-extracts are also somewhat faded in the other colours, but they do not approach the strong action of the white.

This experiment has thus clearly shown the de-greening action of the strong white reflected light, while among the other colours no differences are to be noticed.

With the gelatine-covered little cases the results are less regular. After six weeks, with the colours brought to optically equal intensity, the extract of the pupation-ripe caterpillars is most faded in blue, that of the middle pupae in blue-green, and that of the pre-pupation-ripe caterpillars in yellow-green. The others have remained unchanged. With *Rubus*, blue and blue-green have acted most destroyingly, whereas orange and Finsternis [darkness] have best preserved the original colour; in red, yellow, yellow-green and white the extracts are somewhat faded. With the colours brought to chemically equal intensity, the extracts of the pupation-ripe caterpillars have all remained unchanged, that of the pre-pupation-ripe caterpillars is preserved only in yellow-green, while in yellow, blue, white, Finsternis they are somewhat faded. With the middle pupae it is faded only in blue, whereas in the other colours (red, orange, yellow, yellow-green, blue-green, white, Finsternis) it is preserved. With the green pupae it is most faded in white, in blue somewhat less, whereas in yellow, yellow-green and Finsternis it is preserved. *Rubus*-extract is somewhat faded in all colours except Finsternis, and indeed most in yellow. Very interesting hereby is the result in blue-green, where the extract now shows entirely the colour of the irradiation and has likewise become blue-green. The blue-green light has destroyed the yellow component of the leaf-extract and protected the colour of the chlorophyll corresponding to its colour, so that the extract is now coloured fairly intensely blue-green, in contrast to the yellow-green tone of the other *Rubus*-extracts.

When we look through the various experiments with a view to which colours it is that have acted most de-greening, and which have protected the green most, we find that the experiments are still too inadequate and the results still too irregular to be able to draw conclusions from them. The only result certain up to now is the de-greening action of strong reflected white light, as it has shown itself in the experiments with the reflection-cases. Weaker intensity of white light, as prevailed in the white in the series with gelatine-covered little cases, has not acted so de-greening. In one case — *Rubus*-extract in blue-green — the protecting action of the green has clearly shown itself, and it has even come to an agreement in the colour-tone.

#### c) Experimental results.

From these experiments it emerges that reflected strong white light acts most strongly de-greening.

This has come to expression in the experiment with reflection-cases, where a great light-intensity prevailed. Between the action of the other colours, no differentiation has resulted with this experimental arrangement, in which the mixed white light was not excluded.

With the experiments with gelatine-covered little cases, no such regular results have been recorded, because evidently the intensity was too low to achieve a clearer action. Nevertheless it shows itself that the colours which in the majority of cases have acted most fading are precisely blue and blue-green. The colours which have most protected the colouring matter are orange, yellow, yellow-green, and likewise also Finsternis [darkness].

If, after these few experiments, it is not yet possible to draw conclusions, there is nevertheless according to this an appearance, as though the preservation, respectively destruction, of the green colouring matter in particular colours were in accord with Wiener's theory of body-colour-photography, in that colours which are reflected by the extract, or better still those which are shifted somewhat away from the sensitivity-maximum towards the red, that is the yellow-containing ones: yellow-green, yellow and orange, as well as complete Finsternis [darkness], would best protect the green colouring matter. On the other hand the colours complementary to these, blue and blue-green, further especially strong white light, would most readily destroy it. Similarly behaves also the leaf-extract. In the one case it has, precisely hereby, even come to an agreement with the irradiation-colour, and indeed with *Rubus* in blue-green, in which the blue light has destroyed the yellow colouring matter (xanthophyll) and has preserved only that corresponding to its own colour; therefore this extract appears coloured blue-green.

In any case, such irradiation experiments with extracts must still often be repeated before anything positive can be stated about it.

To what extent this colour-susceptibility of the green colouring matter is decisive for the intensity of the green-colouring of the pupae in various surroundings cannot be asserted with certainty merely after these experiments. The de-greening action of white surroundings on the pupae can, however, indeed be inferred with sufficient probability.

### B. Influence of the coloured light on the formation of the green colouring matter.

The facts which speak for the view that the yellow rays also condition an increased formation of the green are the following:

1. Clear differences in the colour of the blood:

α) It is, with the green pupae which arise in consequence of yellow irradiation of the sensitive stage of any cabbage-white caterpillars, luminously green; with the other types yellowish-green (Brecher 1917, III, p. 162). This difference outweighs possible smaller colour-differences which perhaps set in according to the sex (ibidem p. 163).

β) The same differences are shown also by the ether-extracts of the green blood-colouring matter (obtained from coagula, see preparation above, or also in Przibram-Brecher 1919, p. 139).

2. Preference for "greening" in the extracts originating from green pupae:

α) With aqueous shell-extracts, which after some days of standing let an increase in intensity of the green-colouring and an assumption of a beautiful blue-green colour-tone, both in the light and also in the dark, be recognized in the extract of the green pupae, as compared with the weaker and more yellow-green tone of the other types, and which permanently retained these differences (Brecher 1917, III, p. 170).

β) With tyrosine-samples into which pupal blood had been dropped, and whose initial colour was a weak yellow, after the settling of the formed melanin a green colouring appeared with those treated with the blood of green pupae (Brecher 1917, III, p. 166).

With the blood-samples of the other colour-types a greening occurred only when heated tyrosine had been used. (Ibidem.)

γ) With chromogens, obtained both from green and also from middle pupae (by heating the ammonium-sulphate-dissolved blood-fraction to 90° in the water-bath and removal of the formed coagulum by filtering, see above), which greened most rapidly — one day after setting up — and most intensely when they had been treated with tyrosinase from green pupae, and finally assumed a blue-green colour, which was somewhat more beautiful with the chromogen of the green pupae. The chromogens of both types treated with tyrosinase of the middle pupae also began to green 2 days later, but attained only a weaker yellow-green colouring; and finally the chromogens set up without tyrosinase also showed, but only six days after setting up, a quite weak greenish colouring. Pupal-chromogen treated with *Halimasch*¹-tyrosinase showed only the blackening, but no greening. (Cf. course of the experiment in Table D.)

> ¹ [*Halimasch* (Hallimasch) = honey fungus, *Armillaria*.]

3. Direct appearance of a green going-colour of the tyrosine, respectively turning-over into green of the rose- to violet-gone samples, with acid, weak pupal-tyrosinase after longer pre-irradiation in yellow respectively yellow-green, yellowish-grey, as well as Finsternis [darkness] — in these two latter, alongside brown ones, brownish-green samples — in contrast to the other colours, whose tyrosinases, although still weaker than the three last-named, had given no greening of the samples (see Table A, Or.-Nr. 47, 48, 49 as well as Text I, B).

Unfortunately, it is here for the present only a matter of a compiling of observations which let the connection of the influence of the coloured light with the formation of the green colouring matter too be glimpsed, and not yet of a systematically conducted demonstration as with the dependence of the melanin-formation on light. For here it is still a matter of first doing preliminary work and of ascertaining the formation of the green at all precisely, just as has happened for the melanin thanks to the investigations of v. Fürth among others. Most researchers are still too caught up in the idea of the chlorophyll-nature of the green colour in animals for its formation to have been pursued. Przibram has repeatedly (1906, 1913, 1919) opposed this view by means of the setting up of reactions which yielded a differing behaviour of chlorophyll and of animal-green, by spectroscopic investigations, and in a particularly convincing manner by the feeding of animals (1906) which, despite completely chlorophyll-free nourishment, yielded beautifully green specimens.

Unfortunately we still lack to date closer information about the chemical constitution of animal-green, such as Willstätter, for example, ascertained for chlorophyll.

Yet with respect to its formation we are not lacking in clear indications. The cases of the greening have, since the first noticing of it (Brecher 1917, Przibram-Brecher 1919), increased considerably and now leave no further doubt that here it must be a matter of a fermentative formation of the green colouring matter; in particular it is the last cases, cited above, of the more rapid greening under the influence of the tyrosinase of the green pupae, the appearance of a green going-colour preferentially with yellow and yellow-containing pre-irradiation, which speak for this genesis of animal-green. The direct proof of the chemical identity of the finished colouring matter and of the green from the greened samples, such as is available also for natural and artificial melanin, is for the present still lacking, since up to now it has not yet been possible to obtain sufficient quantities of the greened samples and to transfer them into ether, in order at least to be able to carry out the analogous reactions with them.

With the reservation that the identity of animal-green with the greening appearing in the pupal extracts and tyrosine has still to be demonstrated, we will now see under which conditions we have at all so far been able to observe the greening. I will here once more bring together the data given in the earlier treatises and the observations that have since been added, which relate to *Pieris*:

1. With chromogens, which were obtained from the pupal blood by boiling or heating to 90°, greening occurred:

a) particularly intensely on addition of tyrosinase of the green pupae, already after one day (blue-green colour),

b) on addition of tyrosinase of another pupal colour-type, later (after 3 days) and only up to a yellow-green tone,

c) by itself, but much later (after 6 days) and less intensely.

2. With tyrosine:

a) non-pre-heated tyrosine treated with the blood of green pupae after the settling of the melanin,

b) non-pre-heated tyrosine treated with acid, rather weak pupal-tyrosinase, after pre-irradiation in yellow, after a state of complete ineffectiveness, finally went directly green (I B),

c) non-pre-heated tyrosine treated with the same pupal-tyrosinase, after pre-irradiation in yellow-green, yellowish-grey and keeping in Finsternis [darkness], over a rose going-colour to violet, then to brownish, subsequently underwent a turning-over into green (with grey and darkness, alongside a brown sample) (I B).

d) Finally, let there be recalled here also the cases, cited in the work of Przibram and Brecher, not relating to *Pieris*, namely tyrosine with *Dixippus*-tyrosinase, which 6 days after setting up turned green (p. 112), with a mixture of *Agaricus*- and warm-blooded-animal-tyrosinases alongside completely failing ones, and, when using very dilute tyrosinase still just sufficient for the going-on, of *Smerinthus* or *Agaricus*, with hydrogen-peroxide-dissolved tyrosinases.

e) Heated tyrosine on addition of blood of each colour-type after the settling of the melanin (Brecher III, p. 166).

f) With tyrosine that had been boiled for a longer time and had become yellowish, there occurred, on addition after the cooling of a drop of weak tyrosinase — which with non-pre-treated tyrosine had clearly yielded a violet to brown colouring — no blackening, but rather a more greenish colouring (Brecher III, p. 178).

g) Tyrosine boiled for 7 to 8 hours and still longer turns of itself yellowish-greenish. (Ibidem p. 178.)

h) According to data of Ducceschi (1901), oxidation of tyrosine in hydrochloric-acid solution by means of a few drops of potassium chlorate and warming over the red-stage led to melanin; with too much potassium chlorate, over the red-stage, into yellow-green. Exactly the same occurred in oxidizing with sodium nitrite.

i) The known Denigès colour-reaction of tyrosine: if one boils tyrosine in formalin and sulphuric acid, one obtains a deep bottle-green colouring.

To be sure, it is for the present not yet at all demonstrated that in all these cases it is a matter of the same green colouring matter. It thus shows itself that the greening can occur also by itself in the chromogen, under circumstances usually after pre-heating or boiling, but that the addition of ferment accelerates this greening and especially favours it further, when the ferment originates from green pupae or has been exposed to yellow pre-irradiation. This behaviour agrees completely with a fermentative mode of formation, as indeed also with the melanin-formation the tyrosine can oxidize of itself to melanin, but requires a long time for this, whereas addition of tyrosinase greatly accelerates the process, in which these too, according to the pupal colour-type or pre-irradiation, bring about a differently rapid going-on. It is therefore probably permissible to conclude that under the influence of yellow light on the ferment the formation of the green is favoured.

The question now is: is it here a matter of the same ferment as for the melanin, that is, of the tyrosinase itself, which under the influence of the yellow rays is indeed so strongly acidified that the minimum of effectiveness for the melanin-formation is reached? It is, according to the observed cases, indeed mostly at the minimum of tyrosinase-effectiveness that the greening has occurred. Likewise there exists also, according to other experiments, a connection between acid reaction-state and green-colouring. There would then, after the maximum for the melanin-formation, be a still more acid degree which brings about the green going-colour (yellow), whereas at the optimum of the going-capacity, that is, with ultraviolet and blue irradiation, as likewise with the still more alkaline white and warmth rays, no favouring of the green-formation would be present.

Or is it for the green-colouring a matter of a second ferment, which is present alongside the tyrosinase in the pupal blood, comes to effectiveness only when the tyrosinase is ineffective, and with respect to the influencing by the colours behaves antagonistically to the tyrosinase, in that it attains in yellow its greatest, in ultraviolet its least effectiveness? This would correspond to the behaviour of the catalase (W. Ostwald, 1907), a possibility to which I pointed in the work of 1917, but which I have since not yet tested. There must still be carried out determinations of the catalase-quantity of the various pupal colour-types, as well as of the pupation-ripe caterpillars after keeping in the various colours.

The following, however, seems to me to speak for the first alternative.

With respect to the chromogen, much speaks for it being a matter of the same tyrosine-like chromogen which forms the basis both for the melanin and also for the green colouring matter, and indeed [for] the greening not only of the chromogen-solution, which might indeed be conditioned by the presence of a second chromogen in the solution, but also of the tyrosine. Just as it has a red and violet going-colour, so it has also a green one. Red, violet and green are indeed also the three characteristic colour-reactions of tyrosine on addition of Millon's reagent, respectively addition of sulphuric acid and ferric chloride (violet-Piria-test), respectively formalin and sulphuric acid and boiling of the mixture (green-Denigès-Moiner) (cf. Neues Handwörterbuch der Chemie, Vol. 8). Likewise Ducceschi too, in the autoxidation of tyrosine over the red-stage to melanin by means of potassium chlorate or sodium nitrite, obtained, on excess of these oxidizing agents instead of melanin-formation over the red-stage, a discolouration into yellow-green.

To this influencing of the formation by the yellow rays there might indeed also belong further the protection of the formed green colouring matter in yellow, and the destruction of the original green-colouring in the complementary colours and in the strong white light, raising the differences between the types and the action of the colours. It would be, then, for the explanation of the dependence of the green-colouring, both the colour-susceptibility of the green colouring matter according to the theory of Wiener and also the colour-sensitivity of the ferment that are to be made responsible.

In any case, further experiments in both directions, and indeed likewise in the sensitive stage of the caterpillars in comparison to the other stages, must be undertaken. For there has shown itself a striking diversity of the blood-colour in the various stages, as was described in Section I, A. The blood-colour of the fixed [caterpillar] — acid state — corresponds in colouring (blue-green) to the blood of the green pupae, whereas earlier it is more yellowish and after pupation, with all the other types except the green ones, likewise yellow-green.

Above all, however, clarity must be brought into the chemical constitution and the formation of the green, so that one may produce the components at will, just as for the melanin-formation, and may have it in one's power to establish the conditions for the formation at any time.

## III. Explanation of the colour-adaptation of the pupae and conclusions.

The experiments described in this part permit it, to sketch the colour-adaptation process in the pupae as a whole.

### A. Normal course in the pupation.

The still-feeding caterpillar in the last moulting-stage before pupation has an alkaline reaction-state; its tyrosinase conditions a weak rose going-colour, and the colour-sensitivity is not considerable, since within this stage the reaction-state remains alkaline also further on and no turning-over of it supports the light-sensitivity.

The caterpillar turns away from the food and begins to wander, it enters the "sensitive" stage, its reaction-state changes in the direction of acidification, the tyrosinase gains in effectiveness, goes strongly red, and since chromogen and tyrosinase are together in the blood and oxygen too is in any case present in the caterpillar-body, the "red-stage" is run through in the body itself; the blood appears no longer yellow-green as in earlier stages, but rather more reddish-yellow. Also in the gut, where the oxidation-conditions are perhaps more favourable than in the intermediate tissue, the red-stage is formed from the chromogen and the tyrosinase, which colours the still-remaining food-residues, so that the gut-contents now have, instead of the green, a red colour. The last-secreted faecal pellet is therefore red. Within this stage a strong acidification takes place, the tyrosinase now has no longer a red, but rather a violet going-colour, and conditions an abundant formation of precipitable melanin.

With the secretion of the red faecal pellet the caterpillar enters the "fixed" stage, within which the acidification still increases. The blood-colour is no longer reddish-yellow, but rather blue-green. The formed red-stage has been secreted partly with the gut-contents, partly has gone back in the blood (cf. the parallel hereto in Przibram-Dembowski 1919: going-back of the red-gone Halimasch-samples through air-free melting-in). At the places more accessible to the access of oxygen, that is, in the skin, around the pore-canals, the red-stage formed there from the chromogen present here, under the action of the tyrosinase in the "red" stage, persists, in order, as soon as the caterpillar-skin is stripped off, at the sudden access of oxygen at the places of strongest oxygen-supply, namely around the pore-canals and in varying degree also in the anastomosing canal-system of the new pupal-envelope spreading out from here, under the action of the tyrosinase rapidly to run through the further stages of the oxidation. — With a fresh, still-green pupa the marking indeed becomes first red, then violet and finally black, and the melanin is deposited in solid form.

With respect to the distribution of the melanin in the envelope — both macroscopically and also microscopically visible marking (cf. Part I 1917, p. 90) — it is here clearly a matter of a distribution of the pigment conditioned by the structure of the chitin (as well as the oxygen-supply), in that melanin-free places too show the same branched network of transparent canals, in whose nodal points the pore-canals are situated, and not a matter of a precipitation-form of the melanin, which in fact, as is regularly to be observed in the test-tubes with melanin-formation under the influence of acid tyrosinase, is likewise a branched star-shaped one; and also not a matter of chromatophores in the proper sense, with which the microscopic picture of the melanin-distribution in the envelope likewise has an outward resemblance (cf. Brecher 1917, Plate VI). Even the individual hexagonal melanin-flakes, which at the less dense places are clearly distinguishable, are no proper form of the melanin, but rather conditioned by the more intimate alveolar structure of the chitin, which everywhere, also at pigmentless places, remains the same (1917, Part I).

At the more deeply situated places, that is, in the hypodermis and in the blood itself, which are not so directly in contact with the oxygen, the red-stage has evidently gone back entirely and has made room for a more intense green-colouring.

If, out of this — fixed — stage the tyrosinase is extracted, it goes very strongly, but no longer red, rather violet. The abundantly formed melanin precipitates at once, which indeed coincides temporally with the deposition of the melanin in the fresh pupal-envelope. After this stage of acidification and most active melanin-formation the tyrosinase is exhausted. Therefore it is much weaker in the freshly formed pupae. At the same time, with the accomplished transformation, the strongly acid state too has again decreased and the pupal-tyrosinase is relatively more alkaline, its melanin does not precipitate; yet this tyrosinase normally never goes red any more, but rather violet, perhaps because its tyrosinase has already once run through the red-stage in the caterpillar-body (cf. the conjecture of Dembowski in Przibram-Dembowski 1919) and the process is not reversible under normal conditions. — On the action of warmth, however, every *Pieris*-pupal-tyrosinase is turned over from violet-going into red-going (Brecher, 1917).

To these processes in the colouring-out process, which ends with the colouring-out of the pupal-envelope, and in the change of the going-colour of the tyrosinase at the transition from the caterpillar- into the pupal stage, there exists a complete parallel in those observed by Dembowski (cf. Przibram-Dembowski 1919) on the air-free enclosing of little tubes containing tyrosine-Halimasch-tyrosinase:

If little test-tubes (Eprouvetten) containing tyrosine-Halimasch-tyrosinase were closed off air-tight with a cork-stopper, then the samples went weakly rose and then remained standing at this stage. If he let an air-bubble enter under the cork-stopper, then very soon the ordinary blackening began (p. 263).

If a tyrosine-Halimasch-tyrosinase mixture was melted in entirely air-free, then the rose-stage indeed appeared right at the beginning as a consequence of the oxygen still contained in the tyrosine, but this soon went back. A going-back of the colour to colourlessness occurred also after air-free melting-in of already red-gone samples. If such samples that had gone back in colour were again opened to the access of oxygen, then they went much more rapidly than the first time, but no longer red, rather directly violet (p. 266).

The course of the colouring-out process of the chromogen under the action of the tyrosinase around the pore-canals in the skin during the pupation-stages — in which the at first persisting weak red-stage, at the sudden access of oxygen after the stripping-off of the caterpillar-skin, rapidly runs through the further colouring-out stages — red, violet up to brown-black — is probably to be compared with the course in the little tube closed with a cork-stopper, containing tyrosine and tyrosinase, which reaches only a rose-stage and remains standing at it, and only at the access of an air-bubble rapidly shows the ordinary blackening-stages.

The receding of the red stage in the blood before fixation of the caterpillars — from which stage onward the tyrosinase, as soon as it is extracted, that is, comes into contact with much oxygen again, no longer produces a red but a violet onset-colour of the tyrosine — has a parallel in the receding of the red-turned Hallimasch-tyrosinase–tyrosine mixture sealed entirely air-free into little tubes, which on the admission of oxygen at once turns again, but no longer with a red, but with a violet colour.

It would remain only to clear up a few points concerning the extracted tyrosinase of the transitional stage (before fixation):

If one prepares the tyrosinase from caterpillars in a somewhat more advanced wandering stage, or by slow preparation of the tyrosinase from "red"-wandering caterpillars, then during the preparation the red stage will already form in the blood from the chromogen present therein, and as a consequence of the unavoidable admission of oxygen during preparation it will rapidly proceed further and already precipitate as melanin on the filter, so that the tyrosinase, as soon as it is dissolved and brought together with tyrosine, since it has already passed the red stage, will already produce the violet onset-colour, as was observed in the communicated experiments in the corresponding cases (cf. I, A and B).

## B. The influence of the coloured surroundings on the pupal colouration.

If a caterpillar in the "sensible" stage, that is, in the stage with active red-onset tyrosinase, which as a consequence of the physiological processes at pupation undergoes a sudden reaction-change into a strongly acid state that is to lead to active melanin formation and precipitation in the new pupal envelope, comes under definite light conditions, then the known action of the colours, which consists in definite changes of the reaction-state of the ferments, will influence this normal acidification process in a definite way and thus determine the end result, the development of the pigments.

White, as well as warmth-rays, act hyperalkalescently; therefore the acidification process is altogether retarded, the tyrosinase remains in its alkaline state, or is perhaps set back into the earlier, still more alkaline state which it has as a feeding caterpillar. It is therefore at first little active, develops the red stage in the envelope, which is also fixed at this colouration-stage by the hardening of the chitin. Therefore the pale pupae have so little diffuse melanin in the envelope, and that only in the closest vicinity of the pore-canals, and only in a pale-brownish stage (cf. 1917, Pl. VI, Fig. 1). At the places of stronger oxygen supply, such as accumulations of several pore-canals represent, the process may also have gone further, up to the end stage, hence the black spot-marking that is conspicuous in the pale pupae, in contrast to the weak developmental stage of the melanin in the rest of the ground colour.

In accord with this action of white stands also the red onset-colour of the tyrosinase of the pale pupae, in contrast to the violet of the other pupal colour-types, which can regain the red onset-colour only through the action of warmth. It is also explicable why precisely the tyrosinase of the pale pupae onsets most rapidly and most intensely in the test-tube, and over an intense cherry-red colouration nevertheless still passes into an intense violet, and in comparison with the other types yields the blacker samples (1917, III, pp. 155, 157). The alkalescing action has precisely protected the tyrosinase so long that the acidification proceeding through the preparation and setting-up in the test-tube can now stimulate it to just optimal activity. Exactly the same holds also for the blackening of the tyrosine samples into which not prepared tyrosinase but blood had been dripped (1917, III, p. 168).

White surroundings cannot in any case favour the green formation through their alkalescing influence. On the contrary, the green which still passes with the blood into the fresh envelope is degreened by the strong white light. Added to this is also the deposition of white in the space made available through the absence of the green layer, which makes the envelope appear opaque, and so a colour-adaptation is finally achieved, in that on a white ground quite pale pupae with little melanin and little green, but on the other hand strongly white-opaque envelope, come about.

Ultraviolet rays, and accordingly black surroundings (1919, IV), act alkalescently; therefore the normal acidification process is somewhat held back, and thereby at pupation just an optimal onsetting of this tyrosinase comes about, so that the pupae produced in black show the strongest melanin formation. The green formation, too, is therefore presumably not favoured by the ultraviolet rays.¹) To this would be added the destruction of the green originally shimmering through in the fresh pupa, in the complementary colour.¹)

The white we do indeed see deposited everywhere where there is no green. In any case, however, the whitish-greenish ground colour is entirely concealed by the diffuse black pigment, so that the pupae produced on a black ground appear very blackish.

Yellow rays act acidifyingly, will therefore accelerate the normal acidification process, soon exhaust the tyrosinase and bring it to the limit of activity before it can come to pigment formation. Therefore in the pupa the very slightest melanin formation will occur only in the closest vicinity of the pore-canals¹) — and that in a dark-sepia-brown developmental stage as in the other types, with the exception of the pale ones — (see 1917, Pl. VI, Fig. 3). On the other hand, the green will attain its strongest development, be it that the formation of the green colour is to be traced back to the action of the tyrosinase under over-acidification or at the minimum of activity, or to another ferment that now comes into action. Moreover, the green that has formed is also still best protected by the yellow rays. (Preservation of the body-colours having the same sense as the light-rays.) The green pigment lies close against the envelope; there can thus be no deposition of the white. — Only when one frees a little piece of envelope from the adhering green layer does the envelope become white-opaque on drying.

There thus arise on yellow green pupae. This would yield no agreement of colour if yellow-green too — and indeed even better than yellow — did not produce green pupae. Since now in the open the pupation takes place on green leaves, which reflect much yellow rays (Poulton 1892), there is in fact established in Pieris a complete colour-adaptation.

Neutral surroundings in the light and darkness will not shift the reaction-states of the successive stages. The result will be a mean development of the melanin. In the dark the green pigment too is somewhat protected; the pupae have a somewhat greener tone, sometimes even a pronouncedly green colour, which however is distinguished from that of the yellow-influenced pupae by the simultaneous development of melanin. They are also less opaque than those pupated in the light. (See explanation, Part V.)

This question, whether a heredity underlies this variability of the pupal colourations in darkness, could only be decided by experiments in which the descendants of the two colour-varieties of pupae occurring in darkness were again brought to pupation in darkness. If for the side-by-side arising of greenish and non-green pupae in darkness the cause lies in a hereditary race, then the descendants of the green pupae should yield more green, those of the other colour-types fewer green pupae in darkness. If the induced colourations can be transmitted to the descendants, then the descendants of green pupae arising in yellow surroundings should, on pupation in darkness, yield only green pupae, whereas the descendants of white- or black-influenced, that is, non-green pupae should yield throughout non-green pupae in darkness.

The light thus acts, according to the kinds of rays, upon the chemico-physiological processes taking place in the body of the caterpillar before pupation, and determines, through its hyperalkalescing, alkalescing, or acidifying action, the working-capacity of the ferment and thus the amount and development of the black and green pigment. On the connection of the pupal colouration with the natural surroundings there exist statements of the earlier authors (Wood 1867, Meldola 1873, Poulton 1887–92): pupae of Pieris brassicae which were found in the open on black or dark lath fences had a quite dark colouration, on lime walls a pale-whitish, on grey stone walls a grey one, while those pupated on the food-plant were coloured green. We now know, from the carried-out analysis of the light-actions, that with the black laths, probably similarly as in the experiment with black surroundings, it is the ultraviolet rays which, reflected from black, promote the melanin formation, so that here dark pupae arise (cf. Part IV); likewise the white lime walls, as in the experiment with white surroundings, through the ultrared rays and the strong reflected white light, probably bring about the inhibition of the melanin, the strong opacity, and the strong degreening, thus yielding pale whitish pupae (cf. Part V). Grey stone walls represent a neutral surrounding, which yields the mean development of the pigments, that is, pupae with grey ground colour. Concerning the spectroscopic constitution of the green leaves, Poulton (1892) states that they absorb red and blue rays, but on the other hand reflect abundantly the yellow and orange rays as well as completely the green ones. From this property of reflecting the yellow and orange rays their production of green pupae is to be explained, whence in this case too a pupa coloured like the surroundings results.

The result is therefore an agreement of the pupal colouration with the colouration of the ground, a colour-adaptation.

In the experiment too it depends likewise on the composition of the light which the prepared surroundings reflect, what kind of pupal colouration will result from it. But it thereby also becomes understandable that on yellow and orange more beautiful green pupae arise than on green, since most of the usable purchasable papers do not reflect so much yellow as the green leaves do.

According to the view of Lenz (1917), however, the light has only a secondary significance for the colouration of the pupae, in that the lesser illumination during caterpillar life releases the tendency to the white black-spotted pupal type, the stronger illumination the tendency to green pupae, and in connection therewith also develops in the caterpillars the instinct to seek out the corresponding protective surroundings for pupation. Hence the pupae of Pieris brassicae of the summer generation are found on leaves of the food-plant and are green, whereas those of the autumn generation are found on trunks, and indeed chiefly on birch trunks, and are white with black spots. This explanation, proceeding entirely from the selectionist point of view, must be rejected. It is, in contrast to the assumption of Lenz, precisely the strong white light which brings forth the whitish black-spotted pupae, [and] the yellow light reflected from the leaves, in its total intensity in any case weaker, which brings forth the green pupae. As regards the whitish colouration with black spot-marking of the pupae attached to the similarly coloured birch bark, this finds, according to our experiences concerning the direct action of the surrounding-colours upon the pupal colouration, an unforced explanation.

We know that white lime walls too, and, as we have seen in the experiment, other uniform white backgrounds, bring forth the same whitish black-spotted pupal type. The carried-out analysis has shown us that this appearance is brought about through the specific action of the rays predominantly reflected from white and through a chemically determined reaction-capacity of the caterpillar. Hereby the black marking-elements on the whitish ground colour of the pupa are conditioned through the definite structure of the chitin and are a consequence of the stronger oxygen-admission at these places. There will therefore on a uniform white background always arise such pupae, although they are visible on the ground owing to the marking, and just such occur also on the birch trunks, which are more similar to them in appearance. Also in this latter case it is therefore presumably a matter of the direct production of the pupal colouration-type through the same rays reflected from the pale whitish birch bark as in the case of the white ground, and not of a protective imitation of the colouration-pattern of the birch bark arisen by way of selection.

One sees from this how speculations built up on nature-observation alone lead to quite insufficient explanations not in accord with the facts.

It was a concern to me, with this work of mine, to deliver a contribution to the idea that a direct causal connection exists between the acting factors of the external world and the reactions of the organisms, and that the way to its investigation lies not in nature-observations alone, and the speculations attaching to them, but in the critical experiment.

Thus in these six parts I have sought to pursue, in the pupal colourations of the cabbage white, the way that Nature takes in the colour-adaptation, and have both analysed the external light-influence (Part II 1917, Part IV 1919, Part V 1920) and also, from the chemism of the animal (Part III 1917, Part VI 1920), sought to understand the process which the surrounding-colour releases in the body.

Through analysis and imitation of the processes by means of experiments in the test-tube it was indeed possible to sketch this way of the coming-about of the colour-adaptation. An explanation, however, of how it has come about that the light-influence and the chemism of the animal are so attuned to one another that as an end result an agreement of the pupal colour with the colour of the surroundings arises, still remains to be given.

These general discussions shall be reserved until the experiments carried out on other forms shall have been published.

Concerning the question whether this colour-adaptation of the pupae to the surrounding-colour has a utility-value, there exists the view of Poulton that it is a matter of a protective resemblance to the colour of the surroundings in the struggle for existence, which withdraws the pupae coloured like the ground from the eye of the insect-eating animals. In order to prove this, Poulton and Saunders (1892) fastened pupae of V. urticae on nettles, bark, walls; they vanished where their form was easily visible.

On the other hand I would discern a different utility-value therein, that in the pupal colour-adaptation it could be a matter of an automatically established light-protection, in that the rays most reflected by the respective surroundings produce precisely the pupal colour which also reflects back these rays the most, and so the excessive absorption of one kind of ray is prevented. According to this conception a finality would lie in the process itself, and there would not need first to be one lying outside the process and in no genetic connection with it that was secondarily introduced.

At the close of the treatise I should like once more to thank Him who, as my teacher, most kindly stood at my side on the way pursued, through advice and guidance, and without whom my wish to be able to penetrate into these questions would probably not have been fulfilled.

## IV. Summary.

1. A. The caterpillars proceeding to pupation pass through several stages, which are characterized by a different chemism: the still-feeding, green-defaecating, pre-pupation-ripe caterpillars have yellow-green blood; their blood-tyrosinase has an alkaline reaction-state, a slight activity, and calls forth in tyrosine a rose onset-colour and the formation of non-precipitating melanin.

The caterpillars wandering away from their food, red-defecating and ripe for pupation, have reddish-yellow blood; their blood-tyrosinase passes from a still-alkaline into a strongly acidic state, is very active and, like that of Halimasch, induces a red onset-colour [Angehfarbe] of the chromogen and at first the formation of melanin that remains suspended, and, with advancing stage, of very rapidly precipitating melanin.

The fixed caterpillars have intensely green blood; their blood-tyrosinase has an acidic reaction-state, a strong activity, induces a violet onset-colour in tyrosine and the formation of rapidly precipitating melanin.

All non-green pupae shortly after pupation have yellow-green blood, a slightly acidic and weaker tyrosinase which — with the exception of the tyrosinase from white pupae — colours tyrosine violet and brings about the formation of melanin that remains suspended.

B. The tyrosinases of the various stages have a different colour-sensitivity [Farbenempfindlichkeit], which results as the resultant of the characteristic reaction-course of the individual stages and the specific action of the coloured rays upon the reaction-state of the tyrosinase:

Accordingly, the tyrosinase of the still-feeding, green-defecating caterpillars possesses an insignificant colour-sensitivity. Yellow rays at first somewhat promote the activity of the tyrosinase through their acidifying action. After longer pre-irradiation, however, the alkalizing ultraviolet rays (black surroundings), and still later the hyperalkalizing ultrared rays (white surroundings), have best preserved the activity of the tyrosinase.

The tyrosinase of the wandering, red-defecating caterpillars, which are in the so-called sensitive stage, shows among all stages the greatest colour-sensitivity. Under the shortest irradiation, yellow rays accelerate, and blue to ultraviolet rays retard, the activity of the tyrosinase. Under longer pre-irradiation — corresponding precisely to the duration of pupation — the action of the colours is the reverse. Yellow rays, through over-acidification, bring about the exhaustion of the tyrosinase and the least formation of melanin; the ultraviolet rays (black surroundings), through their alkalizing action, bring about a retardation of the normal acidification, hence the best activity of the tyrosinase and the strongest melanin formation; the hyperalkalizing white rays cause altogether an inhibition of the acidification process, hence a very slight activity of the tyrosinase and very weak formation of melanin; darkness does not essentially shift the course of the process from that which is normal under mean light conditions.

In the pupal colouration, only this second colour-action corresponding to the longer pre-irradiation (the reversal) comes to expression. The tyrosinase of fixed caterpillars is very little colour-sensitive; yellow rays somewhat accelerate the activity of the tyrosinase.

The pupal tyrosinase is likewise only little colour-sensitive. Here, already under the shortest irradiation in the colours, the reversed colour-action comes to expression, that is, acceleration of the activity of the tyrosinase under blue to ultraviolet irradiation, weakening of the activity in yellow.

2. The green pigment in the pupal envelope is de-greened [entgrünt] by strong white light. The formation of the green pigment is favoured by yellow rays.

3. Hence, the influence determining the pupal colouration — that of the various surrounding colours upon the caterpillars in the sensitive stage — is conditioned by the definite course of the reaction-state in this stage, the specific action of the kinds of rays upon the reaction-state of the tyrosinase, and the definite length of time of the sensitive stage up to pupation. These processes are so attuned to one another that a colour-adaptation of the pupae to the surrounding colours results.

Thus white surroundings act, through the hyperalkalizing ultrared rays, inhibitingly upon the melanin formation, as also de-greeningly through the strong light-intensity, so that the lightest pupae arise; black surroundings promote, through the alkalizing ultraviolet rays, the melanin formation and yield the darkest pupae; yellow surroundings bring, through the acidifying action of the yellow rays, the tyrosinase to the minimum of activity, so that the least melanin formation results, whereas in yellow and green the green is most strongly protected and green pupae arise. Darkness does not shift the normal reaction-course; the consequence is a mean development of all pigments, hence under these conditions mean pupae arise.

Since in nature there are neither orange-coloured nor bright-red, nor sky-blue or other coloured surfaces that do not induce in the pupae a colour-change of the same direction, in the open the colour-adaptation is a more perfect one; for under the surroundings that come into consideration — such as on white rock, birch trunks, and further on whitewashed limestone walls — through the reflected white rays whitish pupae arise; on dark trunks, brown rocks, likewise on black-painted laths, through the reflected ultraviolet rays blackish pupae arise; on green leaves, through the reflected yellow rays, green pupae arise.

## VII. Explanation of the Plate.

### Plate II.

#### Orthochromatic Photograph.

The plate illustrates the colour sensitivity of the tyrosinase of the wandering, red-defaecating caterpillars (Figs. 1 and 2) and of the fixed caterpillars (Figs. 3 and 4).

In all the figures:

- *W* denotes the white reflexion box,
- *R* the red one,
- *GE* the yellow one,
- *GU* the yellow-green one,
- *S* the black one,
- *D* complete darkness.

The colours used correspond to the colour-standards depicted on Pl. VII of Parts I–III of my work (1917).

For testing the colour sensitivity of the tyrosinases, these were, on the very same day of their preparation, partly melted in air-free into capillary-drawn-out little tubes (Z), partly set up openly, closed only with a cotton-wool plug (O), in the colours for pre-irradiation. After 2 and 4 days respectively, two tyrosine samples of 1 cm³ each were each charged with one drop of these pre-irradiated tyrosinases, and the melanin formation was observed. After the reaction had run its course, the two melanin samples of each individual colour-action of each experiment were poured together into one little tube, and the same was melted shut for the purpose of further preservation. The photographic exposures of these melanin samples were made 4½ months after the sealing-in, by Angerer and Göschl. Apart from the subsequent precipitation of the sample with the dark-tyrosinase as well as the slight sinking of the sample with the green-tyrosinase in the upper group on the left (Fig. 1), all the samples had remained unchanged as at the time of sealing-in.

The two groups on the left, Figs. 1 and 2, present the course of the experiment on the colour sensitivity of the red-reacting tyrosinase of the wandering, red-defaecating caterpillars.

Fig. 1 presents the action of the tyrosinase after two-day pre-irradiation in the colours, in air-tight sealed-shut little tubes, upon the first taking-out. Here at first no difference at all according to the pre-irradiation had appeared. All the samples were deep-black and did not precipitate. Only much later, after the sealing-in of the samples, at the time of the photographic exposure, did the sample which had reacted with the dark-tyrosinase precipitate, and the one with the green-tyrosinase also sink somewhat.

Fig. 2 presents the action of the same tyrosinase after renewed sealing-in and further two-day pre-irradiation in the colours, upon the second withdrawal of the same: here one sees the extreme differences in melanin formation called forth by the colour-action. Yellow and Green had already brought the tyrosinase to the limit of its effectiveness; the samples show an extremely weak melanin formation, which has also precipitated at once, so that the liquid is clear and colourless. The red- and black-pre-irradiated samples, as well as the dark-tyrosinase, on the other hand, brought about a very strong, quite deep-brown melanin formation that remained suspended. The tyrosinase from White gave only a very weak colouring of the tyrosine, but the melanin did not precipitate as with Yellow and Green.

The two figures on the right, Figs. 3 and 4, present the action of the tyrosinase of fixed caterpillars after pre-irradiation in the colours. One sees the slight colour sensitivity of the tyrosinase of this stage as compared with the tyrosinase of the "red"-wandering caterpillars.

Fig. 3 presents the effectiveness of the tyrosinases of the fixed caterpillars, pre-irradiated openly in the colours, after four-day pre-irradiation. The sample with the yellow-pre-irradiated tyrosinase is somewhat darker than the samples set up with the white- and black-pre-irradiated tyrosinases. Yet the differences are much smaller than those which came to light with the tyrosinases of the "red"-wandering caterpillars at the same duration of pre-irradiation and even melted in air-free.

Fig. 4 presents the effectiveness of the same tyrosinase, pre-irradiated in the colours but sealed shut, after four-day pre-irradiation and first opening. Here no differences whatever according to the pre-irradiation are to be noted.
